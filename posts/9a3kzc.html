  • Medicine Can cancer be transferred from host to host?

    NEW
    Q:

    Okay, so say cancer cells or a tumor are removed from a human or animal, and are then surgically placed inside of another hosts body, will the cancer cells survive and continue to duplicate? Or will the process of them leaving the host be enough to kill them in the first place?

    atomfullerene A:

    There are a few known instances of this, mostly in animals but a handful of examples from humans.

    Here's a paper on the topic in animals. Most famously, a canine STD and facial tumors in Tasmanian Devils are transmissible cancers. Syrian golden hamsters also have multiple independent origins of mosquito-borne cancers in lab settings. And a transmissible "leukemia" is known from softshell clams.

    In some cases (the Tasmanian devils and especially the hamsters) we know populations are highly inbred and genetically similar, making it easier for cancers to survive when transplanted. Canine and tasmanian cancers also have adaptations for evading the immune system, which have been documented.

    In humans, the only known examples are related to medical procedures. There's a case of a cancer being transmitted to a surgeon who injured his hand while operating, and cancers are sometimes transmitted along with organs being donated. Back in the 50's-60's a doctor injected live HeLa cells into patients, transmitting cancer to some (side note: what the hell!).

    Some cancers are also caused by transmissible viruses, but that's not really the same phenomenon.

    So yes, cancer cells can be transmitted. Usually the host immune system wipes them out, but if the host and donor are genetically similar or the cancer cells have the right mutations, they can make the jump. Surgical transplantation isn't always necessary. These things are pretty rare though.

    Interestingly, these cancers (especially the longer-lived ones like the canine tumor) can in some ways be considered single-celled, parasitic species.

    avrilxox A:

    It is possible with certain cancers, specifically those caused by viruses.

    Some cancers can also result in teratomas, which are growths with a number of different types of tissue within them, such as teeth, hair, skin, pieces of muscle etc. The reason this happens is because the cell can pass normal restrictions to keep them defined due to mutations in genes keeping cells differentiated. They regain pluripotency and these cells would most like be able to cause tumour formation in someone else if the cells were injected. A lot of experiments showed this in Yamanaka's experiments to identify the 4 factors that can induce pluripotency in a differentiated cell.
