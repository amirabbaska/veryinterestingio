    Q:

    ELI5 Why is blood slippery (like soap) when you wash it off, but otherwise sticky like glue?

    Saguaro66 A:

    Platelets in blood causes it to be sticky when dried on your skin. They pile together to close off open wounds and give the bleeding area a temporary gelatinous shield from foreign contaminants. Your platelets also work underwater, thickening and sticking to your skin. A bar of soap does a similar thing, sticking to your skin and slowly rinses off with more water.

    kayquila A:

    It's been a while since my last fluid mechanics course, but I looked it up and it appears blood is actually shear-thinning. This means that the more shearing (ie parallel to its surface) force applied to it, the "liquider" it becomes. This occurs because of the complex composition & proteins in blood. When you wash, the rubbing motion makes it thinner and thus it is easier to have it slide off. When you don't touch it, it's thicker - imagine blood dripping down the side of something, it moves very slowly because it is more viscous.

    To explain shear thinning probably better than I can see: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shear_thinning

    Source for blood being shear thinning: https://arxiv.org/abs/1608.03730
