    OneAndOnlyJackSchitt A:

    As a fuel, yes.

    We'd have to run out first or at least get to a point where the per-joule price point for oil is higher than something else. It's debatable how long that will take.

    The green-minded people say it's already happened and the price is kept artificially low by countries (such as Saudi Arabia) selling below cost to undercut competitors to the point of bankruptcy, after which time they'll stop selling below cost.

    Those in favor of oil (such as the oil industry spokespeople) say that, essentially, there's so much oil that we'll effectively never run out.

    The reality is that there is a finite surplus on Earth and there is a finite production rate. If the human race is using oil faster than it is produced, the surplus will be exhausted.

    As the surplus reaches exhaustion, supply does down bring the price up. This is when technologies such as wind, solar, and thermal depolymerization can become more feasible.

    I've talked about thermal depolymerization before but there doesn't seem to be the level of interest in it that I thought there would be. To summarize, this process can convert just about anything with carbon and hydrogen into oil, natural gas, and "carbon solids" (probably elemental carbon or graphite, etc). Imagine a water treatment plant that converts raw sewage, garbage, used motor oil, and plastics into drinking water, natural gas, #2 light crude, and graphite.

    Anyway, we'll never actually be away from oil completely. Oil is useful as a dielectric for large transformers, liquid cooling of computer equipment, wire EDM and EDM sinker machining processes, and all sorts of other things.

    There's always going to be diesel and gas turbine backup generators, those are simply too reliable to decommission.

    You're always going to need to lubricate something as well. Teflon is great, but sometimes you need the mechanical properties of metal on metal without the teflon.
