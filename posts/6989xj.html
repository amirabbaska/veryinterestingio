  • Biology How do we know that we see colours the same as each other instead of associating a word with a colour?

    NEW
    Q:

    ELI5: I had a friend in school who said he saw blues and greens in their opposites (sky green and grass blue). How do we know that we see the same colours? We are taught from a young age that the sky is blue, roses are red etc but how do we know that we all see red as the same and are not just associating what colour we see roses as with the word 'red'. And secondly, how the hell would he know he sees blue and green as opposite unless he has looked through someone else's eyes?

    Edit- swapped some words to make more sense

    Edit 2 - This is a friend I had back when we were both in School, not a current friend. I have not been in school for some 16 years.

    rankor572 A:

    It's an unfalsifiable question and therefore not worth asking besides for a fun thought experiment. Really, the question of qualia (which is the technical term for what you're asking) is only one step removed from the question of solipsism, which is the belief that you are the only consciousness in the universe and everyone else is a mere unconscious automaton (to put it in one more tangible version of the theory).

    Another fun thought experiment in the same vein is whether a rock is actually hard or whether it is soft until the moment you touch it, when it instantaneously becomes hard (alternatively, maybe the rock is the shiniest object in the world until light hits it, when it becomes matte). Both of these thought experiments are unfalsifiable because regardless of whether either statement is true or not it has no practical effect on the world: the world and its function are identical in a universe where either possibility is true.

    To put it back into the realm of qualia: so long as we both agree that the big red button is red, it is irrelevant whether your red is the same as my red.

    clawclawbite A:

    We can measure the emissions spectrum of different objects, and see if people agree on the same color to call them.

    As to what we subjectively perceive, we don't know. However, artists and visual designers seem to often agree on things which look good or bad together, which implies some common perceptual ability. Human factors engineering has identified some colors (bright orange) as being easy to spot for use as a hazard warning.

    So we don't know at all, but behaviorally, we don't have strong reason to think we see totally differently.
