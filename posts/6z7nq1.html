    Q:

    Watching Planet Earth and the opening scene is a group of penguins grouped together in temperatures of -70c, how do these animals live in these extreme frozen places without freezing solid like something does when you put it in the freezer?

    Heavensrun A:

    Insulation and food. Living beings generate heat through metabolism. What determines if you freeze to death isn't the temperature, it's whether or not heat leaves your body faster than it can generate more.

    The bigger the temperature difference, the faster you cool, sure, but the better insulated you are, the slower you cool.

    In short, the same reason you feel warm when you put on a coat.

    kittykatmeowow A:

    This is unrelated to penguins, but there are fish that survive in the sub-freezing temperatures of the Southern Ocean (the salt depresses the freezing point, so the water temperature can get down to about 28 degrees F). The fish have special proteins in their blood which prevent it from freezing. A regular fish would not be able to survive. It's pretty neat.

    I should have included this in the original question, but what about animals like polar bears that don't have the strength in numbers to keep warm? The same principles still apply?

    wonkey_monkey A:

    By huddling together, their emitted body heat goes to keeping other penguins warm instead of being lost to the air. If you watch a timelapse of huddling penguins, you'll see the whole thing slowly rotates and penguins from the outside work their way into the middle, and then out again.

    One big lump of penguin can retain more of its total body heat than a single penguin can.
