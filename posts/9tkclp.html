    PHealthy A:

    For a quick example, tuberculosis and the immune system strike a balance by effectively building a layer of immune cells to cover the TB cells resulting in a latent (dormant) infection. This is a called a granuloma and is a hallmark for TB. Alcohol has been shown to hinder the immune cells (mainly through cytokine disruption) that form a granuloma and subsequently lead to higher rates of TB disease and re-infection.

    Sources:

    Alcohol consumption as a risk factor for tuberculosis: meta-analyses and burden of disease

    The association between alcohol use, alcohol use disorders and tuberculosis (TB). A systematic review

    slowandsteadylearner A:

    Ethanol dysregulates immunity in many different ways. Here are a few off of the top of my head:

    First, with respect to innate immunity, acute consumption inhibits pro-inflammatory cytokine production by macrophages in response to stimulation with Toll-like receptor (TLR) ligands (e.g., lipopolysaccharide) both in vitro and in vivo. So in the short term, production of pro-inflammatory TNF-α, IL-1β IL-6, and IL-12 is decreased, and anti-inflammatory IL-10 is increased. Paradoxically, acute consumption also raises concentrations of acute-phase inflammatory proteins like serum amyloid A and P, which recruit immune cells to sites of inflammation.

    Mechanistically, ethanol induces stress response proteins like hsp70 and HSF1 that bind the immune transcription factor NFκB (inhibiting its activation and nuclear translocation) and the TNF-α promoter (blocking transcription) respectively, and by increasing degradation of IκB kinase (IKK).

    Long-term, heavy consumption, on the other hand, results in enhanced production of those same pro-inflammatory cytokines by innate cells. In the case of TNF-α in macrophages, this appears to be due to enhanced mRNA expression and stability. Also in macrophages, chronic ethanol exposure enhances expression of a TLR-related kinase (IRAK-1) that promotes NFkB signaling and decreases expression of one (IRAK-M) that negatively regulates it. Additionally, they also show impaired efferocytosis, the ability to clear apoptotic (dead) cellular debris following an immune response, which is important for terminating ongoing inflammatory processes.

    Dendritic cells are also impaired by chronic alcohol consumption, showing lower IL-12 and higher IL-10 production, impaired differentiation/generation in the bone marrow, and decreased expression of the T cell costimulatory ligands B7.1 and B7.2 both in vitro and in vivo. Decreased B7.1/B7.2 expression inhibits the ability of dendritic cells to activate T lymphocytes, impairing their proliferation and blunting adaptive immune responses.

    With respect to the adaptive immune system in general, it has long been observed that chronic ethanol exposure promotes T cell apoptosis and the reduction of circulating peripheral T cells (lymphopenia), in addition to promoting a shift in T cell populations toward the memory (CD45RO^+ ) subset. Lymphopenia can even be seen after short periods of binge-drinking. CD8^+ T cells in chronic drinkers are also hyper-activated and ethanol may impair the ability of lymphocytes to engage in diapedesis (migration out of circulation).

    Chronic drinking also decreases B lymphocyte numbers, particularly B-2 B cells, which produce high-affinity antibodies and develop into memory B cells. Somewhat paradoxically, heavy, chronic drinkers also have increased levels of IgM and IgA antibodies, mostly directed against hepatic auto-antigens and byproducts of oxidative damage. These auto-antibodies exacerbate the inflammatory environment and accelerate liver injury -- many patients with alcoholic liver disease make antibodies to alcohol dehydrogenase itself!

    Lastly, both acute and chronic exposure reduces expression of Type I interferons (IFNs) which are important for antiviral immunity.

    In summary, chronic consumption of large quantities of ethanol is quite bad for your immune system (and the rest of you). Moderate consumption, on the other hand, has been associated with improved responses to infection and vaccination, as others have pointed out.
