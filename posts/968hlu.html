    Q:

    When Hubble discovered conclusively that the Universe was expanding, developing the first estimate of the Hubble Constant, Einstein abandoned the Static-Universe-Causing Constant introduced into his equations.

    Why did he not just change the value of it to account for the expansion, and therefore predict what we now call Dark Energy?

    Was this because the universe was thought to be expanding at a constant rate, as opposed to the now suspected Accelerating rate; a solution to which his equations could provide (sans Lambda) ?

    Am I correct in thinking that an accelerating expansion means a constantly changing Hubble Constant?

    mfb- A:

    Am I correct in thinking that an accelerating expansion means a constantly changing Hubble Constant?

    No, and what /u/hikaruzero wrote is wrong. A perfect exponential expansion (dark energy and nothing else) corresponds to a constant Hubble constant. "exponential" and "constant" are referencing different things here: "Exponential" looks at a given object and how the distance to it increases (exponentially). The Hubble constant looks at at given distance and looks how the apparent recession speed is there (constant).

    We have matter in our universe so the expansion is not completely exponential and the Hubble constant is still changing a bit, but not much any more.

    SenorBeef A:

    Last question: What caused/causes the farther galaxies to move away faster - why are they not moving away at a static rate (before we realized the accelerating expansion)?

    Space is expanding everywhere. Essentially new space is being created, pulling everything that isn't gravitationally bound to each other apart. Imagine baking bread with walnuts - as the bread rises, as essentially more volume of bread is being created, all the walnuts are pushed away from each other.

    So, then, the more far apart two things are, the more space is being created between them, which leads them to accelerate away from each other at a faster rate than nearer objects would.
