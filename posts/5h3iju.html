    Q:

    ELI5: Why is staph so rampant in hospitals compared to elsewhere?

    web_dog A:

    Like a few folks have mentioned, Staph is everywhere (such as all over your skin). As long as you don't have any deep cuts, it's not going to get in. Even if it did, your immune system might be strong enough to eliminate the bacteria without the infection becoming widespread, or systemic. Basic beta-lactam antibiotics (think penicillin) are usually strong enough for your run-of-the-mill Staph.

    Staph is also in hospitals; however, the Staph there tends to be super Staph. The use of powerful antibiotics in hospitals selects for the biggest and baddest strains of bacteria (the ones that are able to survive most antibiotics), like MRSA (Methicillin-Resistant Staphylococcus Aureus). These infections are much harder to treat.

    SSOMGDSJD A:

    By staph I'm going to assume you mean MRSA, methicillin resistant staphylococcus aureus. This species of staph is normal flora on your body, meaning it lives there on your skin, in your upper respiratory tract, and GI tract mainly. It only becomes a problem when it ends up elsewhere, like a wound site, or by eradicating the normal flora of another region leaving a new environment for it to grow in. Hospitals are a place where the wounded and immune compromised are found in high numbers, so naturally you have a higher frequency of staph infections there. This is nbd if it's basic bitch staph aureus, as penicillin will do the trick. but with MRSA the situation becomes more complicated.

    But what makes MRSA special? MRSA is staph aureus that has acquired a genetic mutation that makes it resistant to a large class of our most commonly used antibiotics. This happened because we essentially selected for them; it is found on basically every person, including the very ill that recieve regular antibiotic treatment. These treatments killed all but the resistant staph aureus, leaving the resistant ones to takes their place. Because of their resistance, we have to bring out the big guns like vancomycin, an intravenously administered antibiotic, and probably another type of antibiotic to keep it from becoming resistant to that. This treatment method keeps MRSA infected people in the hospital, where they can get their germs on stuff and people to be transferred to other sick people.

    TLDR: sick people are concentrated in hospitals, staph likes to infect sick people. Staph is hard to get rid of so it hangs around and keeps infecting people.

    Source: currently taking a microbiology course. If I've bunged something up feel free to comment.
