  • Chemistry Why does 350 degrees Farenheit seem like the 'standard' cooking temp for so many recipes?

    NEW
    Q:

    Granted there are higher and lower ones, but 350 seems so prevalent...

    torpedoguy A:

    It's a fair middling temperature where most cooking can occur at a decent pace without the outside burning too fast. Long ago they'd just say "cook it at a moderate flame", and had to keep that fire going just right, before the invention of oven temperature regulators that let you "set it and forget it" for a few hours.

    But, more importantly, it's right around the middle of the temperature range you want for a good use of the Maillard reaction: that's what gives things that beautiful golden brown. Too low and you can forget about a crispy skin or decent looking cookies... too high and while the reaction happens by the time the middle's cooked it's gone way past golden and into worthless charcoal exteriors.

    Anabeer A:

    Source: owned and ran restaurants for 30+ years.

    Its a cookbook temp for homeowners that mostly works. Harry and Harriette Homeowner get scairt when cooking food sizzles at them. Plus they don't have the air make up and extraction power a commercial kitchen has.

    In a pro kitchen the fryers are at 350° only to blanch the fries, they are cooked at 375° and higher. The flatop usually runs at 400°, the ovens at 450° and the salamander is 6-700° The only things that run low are aldo shams (the overnight ovens) and of course a stew or chili is slow simmered.

    Everything u/torpedoguy put down is correct as well but if we ran mostly at 350° you would never get your meal.

    You can go well above 350° in most cases but you have to be in the kitchen actually cooking for it to go well.
