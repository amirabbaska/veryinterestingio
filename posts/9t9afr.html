    Q:

    ELI5: Why was prehistoric life so large? (Dinosaurs, Mammoths, etc.) Why did it downscale? (Humans, Lions, Wolves, etc.)

    stuthulhu A:

    First things first. Most of it wasn't. We are biased to seeing an overrepresentation of 'giant' life in the past, for a number of reasons. They're easier to find, they have a better chance of surviving as fossils. They're more impressive in monster movies and museum displays. Lots of factors like the 'wow factor' mean we focus on the giant tyrannosaurus rex, and not the 25 pound dilong.

    Furthermore, some of the life today is so large. Our whales are quite big. In fact, the blue whale is the biggest animal we've ever known to live, period. Bigger than any dinosaur, any mammoth, any other animal we know of.

    Now, why does life get large? This can vary, but one theory is it is can arise from something of an arms race. As you know, animals evolve and that evolution is driven in part by natural selection, where various beneficial adaptations help survival. Being able to fly, having a tough shell, running really fast. These can help something survive a predator. Well, another thing is being really big. For instance, elephants, once they are grown, very rarely face predatory threats (outside of humans). This may have been the case with some dinosaurs as well. Now, what kind of adaptation might a predator evolve to deal with large prey? Well, being big too. And now the prey has a potential to evolve to be bigger again and so on.

    In circumstances like these, being bigger can be an advantage.

    Now, this is also helped by the fact that there's lots of food around. A big body takes a lot of energy. If some meteor, for instance, comes and blasts the planet, shrouding it in darkness and making it hard for plants to grow, suddenly food is a lot more scarce. Suddenly, being huge is a big problem and being small is beneficial. Who survives? the little guys.

    Now, that might help explain the extinction of (some of) the dinosaurs, but no meteor wiped out mammoths right? Well, we've got a new catastrophe for them. Man. We spread all over the globe. We burn down forests for agriculture. We hunt large animals for food. We eliminate anything we find threatening. Increasingly, we divide up the landscape into smaller and smaller chunks. All of these factors make being a big animal a problem again. We either want to eat you, or we're afraid of you, or you need a lot of territory and we're farming all of it, and so on.

    TLDR: Sometimes being big provides an advantage. Sometimes it doesn't. Those times when it does, sometimes then animals evolve to become larger. If it stops being advantage, then they may go extinct or otherwise evolve to become smaller.

    Marlsfarp A:

    Dinosaurs and mammoths are very different things. Dinosaurs lived many millions of years ago. Mammoths were alive at the same time as humans, and it is believed that humans were the main cause of their extinction, from hunting. There is a worldwide pattern of very large animals (called "megafauna") going extinct soon after humans first arrived to the area. Big animals are especially vulnerable to uncontrolled hunting because there are fewer individuals and tend they tend to take longer to reproduce, so the population cannot recover as well.

    As for dinosaurs, they were not all big. They came in all different sizes. That includes some of the largest animals ever, but that should not be surprising, since there were so many of them and they were around for 180 million years. The largest animal ever, however, is alive right now: the blue whale.
