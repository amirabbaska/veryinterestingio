    Q:

    I was reading an article in r/news about a police officer turning off his body cam after a kid on an ATV was tased and died from cardiac arrest. How are officers able to turn off their body cameras and not be charged with evidence tampering?

    vrnkafurgis A:

    Three reasons: first, the prosecutor has to decide whether or not to charge a crime. Many prosecutors are reluctant to charge crimes against cops because they need cops on their side to prove a case. You get a cop who is pissed off at you? Suddenly he “can’t remember” the drug case you’re prosecuting, or isn’t available to testify, and you lose your case. There is immense pressure to play nice, and that means looking the other way.

    Second, officers are often immune from being punished for their actions. That’s because the question isn’t “what would a reasonable person do under X circumstances,” it’s “what would a reasonable OFFICER do?” This is how they are not convicted of homicide under circumstances that arguably show straight-up execution; they are held to a different standard than civilians.

    Third, and related: nearly every crime requires an intent element. To show evidence tampering, you’d have to prove a criminal intent - something that is difficult to do if you’re not able to prove the underlying wrong.

    This is my first time replying to ELI5, so I hope I did okay :)

    Source: I’m a career public defender

    TitaniumDragon A:

    Why can't you be arrested for not taking a picture of every single thing you do?

    Remember: the law is the same for police officers as everyone else in this regard.

    The answer is that failure to create evidence is not the same thing as destroying evidence.

    Body cameras have to be possible to turn off for a variety of privacy reasons, including but not limited to going into bathrooms and similar places. And remember: they're not the only people in those bathrooms, either, and everything from body cams is potentially in the public record.

    On top of that, storing vast amounts of video (8+ hours/day) is simply unwieldy; if you have 50 cops, that's 4000 hours of video per day. Beyond the sheer amount of data storage that would require, it would also be impossible to manage; you'd be constantly losing data in the sheer volume of crap, and it would be impossible to ever review in a timely fashion.
