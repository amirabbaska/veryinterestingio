  • Engineering Why do toilets not have an overflow secondary drain as sinks do?

    NEW
    Q:

    I have never experienced anything close to a sink overflow, but have admittedly had some overflows with the toilet in my time. Why do we protect against sink overflow but not toilet overflow when a toilet overflow is understandably much more of a nuisance?

    faloi A:

    There's still just one drain for a sink. The overflow is just getting water to the route it would if the drain wasn't plugged.

    For a toilet, if the drain is plugged the flow of water is going to be stopped (or slowed) entirely. An overflow drain would end up just as backed up.

    TheOriginalRaven A:

    Toilets are different than sinks in that they have an integral trap in them. You can usually see how what you flush has to go up and then down the drain. Having an overflow for a toilet would bypass this trap, and nasty sewer gas would come out all the time.

    Sinks have a separate trap installed underneath them. The overflow uses the same piping as the main drain, but is really only meant to be used if the stopper gets left in accidentally. If you have a clog, it will still flood.
