    Q:

    Is it possible for an atomic sized black hole to exist in our galaxy? If yes could it's energy be harnessed at all?

    VeryLittle A:

    If such a thing could form, it would likely evaporate very quickly by Hawking radiation. And I mean quickly. A black hole of mass 10^-23 g (approximately the mass of an atom) will evaporate is 10^-94 seconds... that's like 10^-54 Planck times.

    But those numbers are probably all useless/meaningless anyway because what you'd really want to describe such a thing is a quantum theory of gravity - gravity probably behaves differently at such mass/length/time scales.

    Katochimotokimo A:

    The size of an atom would probably be the diameter from the most energetic orbital, straight through the proton core, x2.

    Black holes much larger than an atom, like a grain of sand, are theoretically extremely unstable and would evaporate in very little time. The time-dependant release of energy would be unpractical to harness, like a battery that wants to give all its energy at once. Also, extreme energetic outputs like that would probably scar your continent if released at once, or even be able to ignite the whole atmosphere - even completely shed!
