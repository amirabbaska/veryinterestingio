    Q:

    Am I, as a human, closer in size to the earth or an atom?

    tatu_huma A:

    Depends on what you mean by size and how you measure it. In the following I will measure by logarithmic scales, as it makes more sense for such large differences in size.

    Mass

    Atom: ~ -27 to -22

    Human: ~ 2

    Earth: ~ 24

    So we are almost exactly in the middle of atoms and the Earth in terms of our mass. Maybe slightly closer to the Earth.

    Radius

    Note humans don't really have a radius, but I'm going to use 1m.

    Atom: ~ -11 to -10

    Human: 0

    Earth: 6

    So definitely closer to the Earth.

    Volume

    Atom: ~ -32 to -29

    Human: ~ -2

    Earth: ~ 21

    Again closer to the Earth.

    So in summary, using logarithmic scales, you are closer to the Earth through most definitions of size.

    Note that for linear scales, you will be closer to atoms for all of the above criteria.

    logic_card A:

    Human

    length ≈ 1.7 m

    mass ≈ 70 kg

    Hydrogen atom

    diameter = 2 bohr radius = 1.05810^-10 m

    diameter of hydrogen's nucleus = 1.75*10^-15

    mass = 1.007825 u = 1.674*10^-27 kg

    Earth

    diameter = 12742000 m

    mass = 5.972*10^24

    length of a human ≈ 1.607*10^10 (16 billion) times the diameter of a hydrogen atom

    or 9.714*10^14 (971 trillion) times the diameter of the nucleus

    Earth's diameter ≈ 7.495*10^6 (7.5 million) times the length of a human

    1 human mass ≈ 4.182*10^28 (4182 trillion trillion) times the mass of a hydrogen atom

    Earth's mass ≈ 8.531*10^22 (85 billion trillion) times the mass of a human

    So by a wide margin we are closer to the size of the earth than a hydrogen atom.

    twignewton A:

    Richard Feynman used to say that an apple is the same size relative to the Earth as an atom is to the apple. That said, according to Feynman's demonstration, it would seem that the apple is the "halfway mark", and that you are closer in size to the Earth. I believe he provided that demonstration in one of his earlier Lectures on Physics.
