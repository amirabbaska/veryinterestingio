  • Physics How do electrons distribute themselves in a cross section of a wire, when they move through it?

    NEW
    Q:

    In addition, would it be possible to measure differences in electron density (or probabilities of electron distribution) through a cross section of a wire with sufficiently sensitive equipment?

    PlatinumX A:

    At low frequencies, or DC, the current distribution is even. At high frequencies, the skin effect starts to appear, causing the current density to increase on the outer walls of a wire, and reduce in the center of the wire.

    Note that this is referring to the current, which is the motion of electrons, not the electrons themselves.

    One thing that can change the electron concentration is a magnetic field. This causes the hall effect, which causes a slight grouping of the electrons on one side of the conductor in the presence of a magnetic field, and a lack of electrons on the other side. This causes a measurable votlage, in the millivolts, that can be read without any super specialized equipment, and is commonly used in many places, for instance measuring current (which causes a magnetic field) or detecting if the lid of a laptop is closed (which has small magnets in it).

    ssaltmine A:

    Electrons don't actually move in a wire when carrying current. What moves is the electric field, basically a wave of electromagnetic energy that travels with the speed of light.

    The electrons do move a little, in what is called electronic migration, but this phenomenon is very slow and not really significant. For semiconductors, there you can have localized movement of electrons and holes (ions), but in wires such as copper wires, there is no such movement of electrons.

    The movement of electrons to explain current flow is more like a simplified model.

    This is a previous thread talking about the "speed of electricity" which may give some insight on the whole phenomenon.

    OldCoderK A:

    Don't confuse the electrons and the electric field and energy transfer. In a wire the electrons move in the order of about 23 μm per second throughout the wire. (Wikipedia, Drift Velocity). The electric field ripples down the wire at about 1/10 the speed of light and is how the energy is transferred.
    Much like if I pushed on the end of a 100 foot long board, the board would move slowly, but the far end of the board would move (almost) immediately.
