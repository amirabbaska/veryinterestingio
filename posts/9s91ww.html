    downvotesallyourpets A:

    In Dr. Gabor Mate's book "When the Body Says No," he explores, with scientific rigor and literature review, how harboring emotions is tied to many different diseases. Carrying emotional stress, even at a subconscious level, causes the body's stress response system to always be switched on. Our bodies continuously produce the stress hormones: cortizol, adrenaline, and/or others (if memory serves). These hormones then flow constantly or frequently in our bloodstream for prolonged periods.

    We evolved with our stress response system for short bursts of energy, to win a fight, to escape a predator, to paralyze, freeze, and hide from terror. Lives filled with constant financial stress, housing/food insecurity, living with abusers (parents/spousal/elder), childhood trauma (see the ACES Study) routinely trigger our stress hormones. These hormones eventually pickle the most susceptible organs/systems in our bodies as defined by genetics and our lifestyle and are linked to: dementia, ALS, alzheimer's, cancer, arthritis, inflammation, IBS, crohn's disease, acne, asthma, osteoporosis, fibromyalgia, other autoimmune disorders, ADD/ADHD and more...

    TLDR: Yes and it is.

    polyparadigm A:

    One of the best sources of information on this topic, IMHO, is Robert Sapolsky's work on baboons.

    They all eat and exercise similarly, and their natural biology isn't worlds different from human biology. He studies stress hormones, cardiovascular health, etc. in long-term studies of their behavior and blood work.

    Baboons that don't express aggression, in his studies, have worse health outcomes, associated with the negative effects of long-term elevated catecholamine levels.

    His book, Why Zebras Don't Get Ulcers, is a good and accessible pop-science treatment of this overall topic.
