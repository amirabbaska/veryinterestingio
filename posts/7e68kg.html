    Q:

    How does a black hole gain mass if it takes an infinitely long time for matter to cross the event horizon?

    xanthraxoid A:

    It doesn't take an infinite time to cross the event horizon from the point of view of the interior of the black hole, so there's that.

    From the POV of the exterior, though, it doesn't matter - a shell with the radius of the event horizon containing all the mass of the black hole would have exactly the same gravity field as a point at the centre.

    cantgetno197 A:

    It doesn't matter. Any arbitrary (i.e. not point-like) distribution of mass, with total mass M, is gravitationally indistinguishable from a point-mass of mass M if you're at a distance much greater than the width of the distribution. So a cube of mass M, a sphere of mass M, a blob of mass M, etc. are no different from a point particle of mass M at a distance.

    Claycrusher1 A:

    It only takes an infinite amount of time to cross the event horizon from the perspective of an external observer. Someone falling into a black hole would experience it in normal time (until they get stretched to death by the extreme gravity gradient or just run out of oxygen).
