    Q:

    Is there a theoretical limit to the number of chemical elements that could exist in the universe?

    iorgfeflkd A:

    Yes, but it's not known exactly what that limit is. Elements are defined by the number of protons in the nucleus, and larger nuclei are unstable and quickly decay (the biggest synthesized nuclei decay in fractions of a second). Eventually it is thought that instead of briefly forming a nucleus and decaying, really big nuclei will just have protons and neutrons "drip" away. Instead of being comparatively little nuclear binding energy, there will be none.

    TheNeatWhale A:

    I think the answer is more nuanced than a simple yes or no. Theoretically there should be no limit, you keep smashing nuclei together to form higher and higher number elements which will decay faster than we would ever hope to detect them.

    A more appropriate question would be, would we still consider it to be an element if it didn't stick around long enough to be detected, measured etc. for example if you scattered a proton off a Pb atom, for the fraction of a second which the proton interacts with the Pb nucleus (through elastic/inelastic scattering), would you consider it to be a Bi isotope or just a proton scattering off a Pb nucleus?
