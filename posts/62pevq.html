  • Biology Are there any species in which the females compete with one another over males for sex?

    NEW
    Q:

    It seems like a lot of species in the animal kingdom have the males compete (fight, show off, etc.) with one another for "breeding rights" with the females. Is there any species that do the opposite of this?

    Edit: Wow, thanks for so many responses! I think the best scientific answer is "The Bachelor" though...

    Zenigata A:

    Female Jacanas behave much like male lions.

    In species where both parents need to work together to raise offspring females may compete to partner desirable males, this happens in kingfishers and of course in humans.

    whata-boh A:

    Female red-necked phalaropes fight over males and are more brightly coloured. The males then perform all of the incubation and chick rearing duties, while the females go off and try to find another mate.

    This-is-Peppermint A:

    The eclectus parrot sonewhat-the male is green (camo in his environment) and the female is brightly colored to advertise her existence/availability. Males do compete for females by bringing them food, but the females draw attention to themselves in a way that is unusual in birds.

    Reno83 A:

    Hyenas have a female dominant social structure. The females are physically bigger than the males. The females have a pseudo penis which they have to choose to invert on order to mate. And, the highest ranking male is lower than the lowest ranking female.
