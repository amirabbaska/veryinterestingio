  • Human Body Which substances or foods create metabolites that can be detected for extended periods of time?

    NEW
    Q:

    I was recently reading about alcohol and how even after your body has completely processed and excreted the ethanol, it creates metabolites Ethyl Palmitate and Ethyl Glucuronide that can be detected in hair for up to 90 days.

    I am curious as to why these metabolites hang around for so long and also which other substances and/or foods have metabolites that can also be detected for any extended period of time.

    For example, if I consumed caffeine how long would you theoretically be able to test for it if you wanted to? What about broccoli, or carrots etc?

    Are there any factors that cause metabolites to stick around longer, or certain groups of foods?

    I hope my question was clear enough and not too broad scoped.

    Thanks!

    iGarbanzo A:

    Chemist here, with very little working knowledge of nutrition so salt my words to taste.

    The metabolites you mention probably stick around for a long time because of the nature of the tissue that they end up in. The "palmitate" in ethyl palmitate is a saturated fatty acid. It will be very soluble in fat and will likely end up in adipose tissue, which is biochemically speaking pretty stable. Your fat changes a lot more slowly than other parts of your body since its function is backup/long-term energy storage. Anything that is very fat-soluble (hydrophobic) can end up in the same boat, and as a result can often be detected at low levels for months or even years.

    Upon cursory inspection, ethyl glucuronide is a little different. It's a sugar derivative and wikipedia says that it can be detected at very low levels - in other words it can be detected even after most of it has been metabolized or excreted. There is also a suggestion that it can be incorporated into non-metabolically active tissue like keratin in hair and nails. Keratin is "dead", so once a molecule is incorporated it will remain there until the underlying material is degraded.

    These sort of address your broader question as well. Some metabolites are more likely to stick around for a long time because they end up in places that don't experience much rapid change. Another possibility is if you lack the metabolic capacity to remove a given substance, like with trans fats or lots of genetic disorders like PKU. As I said, I'm a chemist so I don't really know of any particular pattern of foodstuffs or how they would fit into this.

    Bedaquiline and M2 both have long terminal elimination half-lives (t1/2term) in plasma. After 8 weeks of bedaquiline administration (2 weeks at 400 mg once daily followed by 6 weeks at 200 mg three times a week) in patients with MDR-TB, the mean t1/2term was 164 days (range 62 – 408 days) for bedaquiline and 159 days (range 69–407 days) for M2. Consequently, plasma concentrations of bedaquiline and M2 were still quantifiable in almost all patients at study completion, 96 weeks after finishing treatment. At this time, the median concentration was 0.010 mg/L (range 0.00224 – 0.0436 mg/L) for bedaquiline and 0.00258 mg/L (range 0.00164 – 0.0116 mg/L) for M2.

    Outside of bone-seeking minerals/isotopes (which are in a class of their own) and normal dietary nutrients (which is technically a completely different topic), I believe that bedaquiline is probably by far the longest-detectable medication.

    eyeap A:

    Nicotine gets turned into cotinine very fast, and the half life of cotinine is really long. They can use cotinine blood tests to determine if you ingested nicotine for like 5 days afterwards.

    IIRC that cocaine is similar -- the cocaine itself goes away fast, so the drug test is for a glucuronated derivative of the active drug.

    xgrayskullx A:

    I am curious as to why these metabolites hang around for so long and also which other substances and/or foods have metabolites that can also be detected for any extended period of time.

    Everything your body processes will produce metabolites. The duration that these metabolites reside in your body can very from a few hours to months or even years. How long a metabolite remains in your body depends on many, many factors too numerous to list here. Basic things like whether the metabolite is fat soluble vs water soluble can have a big impact on how long that metabolite stays in your body.

    For example, if I consumed caffeine how long would you theoretically be able to test for it if you wanted to? What about broccoli, or carrots etc?

    I'm not sure on those particular substances. I'm familiar with a field where the detection of long-term metabolites in urine is a pretty hot topic. Most substances produce multiple metabolites, and those metabolites themselves can be metabolized and produced further metabolites. For example, the steroid Nortestosterone produces the metabolites norandrosterone and noretiocholanelone, just to illustrate one substance that has multiple metabolites. The metabolites produced within a single substance can also very based on the molecular configuration (IE isomer) of the parent compound. The daughter compounds of the parent can actually be somewhat predicted based on the molecular structure of the parent compound. A-ring metabolism of 1-ene and 1,4-diene steroids tend to undergo reduction at the Carbons located at positions 1 and 2. Carbons on the B-ring will undergo different reactions than the A-ring, and the C-ring will be different yet again, as will the D-ring. This allows a molecular chemist to analyze the metabolites found in, say, urine and then reconstruct what the parent compound was, which is an important part of metabolite discovery. Metabolites of a molecule can also be found in different concentrations based on the route of administration - for example, oral steroids will produce the same metabolites as transdermal steroids, but the metabolites will be found in different relative amounts. Multiple different enzymes can metabolize the same substrate (parent molecule) and produce different daughter compounds as well.

    Are there any factors that cause metabolites to stick around longer, or certain groups of foods?

    Yes! One of the 'goals' of metabolizing substances is to facilitate the elimination of that substance, usually be increasing the polarity of the substance. Metabolites with low polarities are less likely to be excreted, so they can stick around longer!

    In a nutshell, metabolic pathways (the process of going from parent compound to final daughter compound(s)) are incredibly complex, and are also subject to inter-individual variability (not everyone processes things exactly the same, based on enzyme levels and things like that). There are a variety of factors that can influence how long a metabolite remains in the body. Some metabolites are only detectable for hours, some for months or years. A single parent can produce both short-lasting and long-lasting metabolites (as seen in, for example, clomiphene metabolites). Molecular configuration (cis/trans and levo/dextro isomers) can have a large influence on what metabolites are produced.
