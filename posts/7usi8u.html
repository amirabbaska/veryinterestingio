  • Economics How do companies who operate at a loss like Tesla and Netflix afford to pay their staff?

    NEW
    Q:

    Not very savvy at all on how big businesses work, and have never been able to get my head around this. Where does their money come from? Even if their negative profits come after they've paid their staff, how do they manage to keep afloat and avoid bankruptcy etc for even a tiny amount of time whilst losing billions of dollars per year?

    Investment.

Investors are often happy to provide large amounts of money to companies that are still establishing a market in the hopes that the company will be successful and increase in value.

    blipsman A:

    Companies like Tesla and Netflix have taken on billions in funding from venture capital, by selling stock in an IPO (and secondary stock offerings). This effectively gives them a pool of savings to use when they are taking in less revenue than they are spending. At a certain point, the high level investment in building the business will slow down while revenue will climb as a result of that investment, so revenues will become more than the expenses and lead to profits.

    noteandcolor A:

    CPA, here. Operating at a "loss" simply means you spend more than you earn. But that does not necessarily mean you don't have cash to pay employees. A company with billions in cash reserves could still operate at a loss. Hell, some companies may operate at a loss for strategic financials reasons. In cases like Tesla and Netflix, they're selling equity in the company (to investors) for cash.

    ScLi432 A:

    Venture capital. These companies could potentially grow into massive companies that are worth significantly more in the future than they are today (even if they haven't really figured out how to make a profit yet). This attracts investors who buy shares in the company now for dirt cheap with the hope that they will grow, make profits, and yield excellent returns. The money from investors bankrolls the growth of the company.

    If you want an example, look at Facebook's stock price over the life of the company. The investors who got in early made about 10 times the money they initially invested.
