    Romeo_G_Detlev_Jr A:

    The simple answer is: Yes! In idealized conditions, and excluding any mitigating factors, if you eat exactly 500 calories worth of food, and you perform enough aerobic exercise to burn exactly 500 calories above your resting energy expenditure, then the net effect on your body fat would be 0. (Note: that simply means this particular exercise & food combo would have no real bearing on your fat loss/gain situation. That's not to say you wouldn't ultimately gain or lose weight: that's dependent on the bigger picture of your diet and exercise situation.)

    The slightly more nuanced answer is still basically yes, but with caveats. As with much of our biology, an array of factors can influence exactly how much food-energy we consume vs. expend. u/aquabug918 and others have alluded to some of these factors. Also, the calorie counts we use for food and exercise are rarely precise. At the end of the day, though, if you have a normal metabolism and maintain a relatively balanced diet, the effects of those factors will be negligible, and your results are the same for most practical purposes: 500 calories - 500 calories = 0 change in body fat.

    TL;DR: Yes. But in another, much truer sense...still yes.
