    Q:

    Are there any diseases/conditions that are beneficial to people’s bodies?

    kissthemoons A:

    Sickle cell anemia. Kinda. If you have the homozygous trait then you’re sick and that sucks, but if you have the heterozygous trait then you have a protective advantage against malaria, which is great for people living in malaria-endemic places.

    Also there’s a field of study on the benefit of helminths (intestinal worms) on the digestive system. Taking an evolutionary approach, there is a possibility that one of the reasons the people of today have so many intestinal issues is actually due to the lack of mutualistic parasites in our environment. Kind of a friend not foe situation.

    Lots of other stuff too. The field of evolutionary medicine is filled with this kind of research.

    Dhoy1 A:

    Cystic fibrosis is kinda along these lines. A homozygote for CF is pretty much doomed to die of lung infections at a young age. Heterozygotes, though, are thought to be resistant to the dehydration and in imblalances associated with cholera and typhus.

    This is heterozygote advantage, as another also explained with sickle cell anemia (interestingly, other areas of the world have different hemoglobin equivalents with mutations at different spots).

    shar_on A:

    Marfan Syndrome is a condition that affects connective tissues in the body. It makes people taller than average, with unusually long limbs/fingers and their joints may also be hyper mobile. It’s speculated that these traits helped musicians like Paganini (violinist) and Rachmaninov (pianist) excel in their work.

    It does however tend to come with significant heart and eye complications too.

    BadgerVadger A:

    Definitely saw this one make its way up reddit not too long ago but parasitic infection of Toxoplasma gondii from cats. There's a relationship between the infection and certain psychological disorders that I wouldn't call beneficial, but it also has been linked to success in the business world for the way it affects behavior in regards to risk-taking. In infected rats it means they are less averse to cats a.k.a. predators than non-infected individuals, and I guess the parallel to humans is that infected individuals are somewhat more likely to be willing to make risky decisions.
