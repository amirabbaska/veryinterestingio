  • Engineering Why does a compressed natural gas tank have expiry date on it? Does it mean it's no longer safe to use after the expiry date?

    NEW
    Q:

    I live in a country where I could use compressed natural gas (CNG) as my vehicle fuel instead of petroleum. So I have a CNG tank installed in my boot 9 years ago.

    The CNG tank's expiry date is reaching soon, I should definitely go for inspection, but does the expiry date means anything at all?

    I already Google the question, but the top results don't really provide any information about what happens when the CNG tank "expired".

    pyr666 A:

    the expiration date realistically means the safety features are at the beginning of the end of their expected life cycle. these dates are set heavily in favor of "replace it before it's an issue" because pressure vessel failure can easily kill someone.

    ronpaulbacon A:

    CNG tanks are not like propane tanks. They are generally made of synthetic materials, fibers, etc. They store natural gas at 250 times atmospheric pressure, around 3600 PSI. The compression and expansion of these fibers do wear the tank significantly, unlike propane tanks. And the consequenses of failure are significantly more serious - propane is about 5 atmospheres of pressure give or take. Perhaps you got a used or new old stock CNG tank installed, many newer tanks have a 20 year life. They cannot be re-certified and your vehicle should be taken out of service, or have the tank replaced. The consequences are potentially explosive if you do not.

    paralyzedcitizen A:

    Pressure vessels aren't my area of expertise but I am a structural engineer for the offshore industry. As you empty and fill the tank you stress and unstress the walls and welds in the tank. There'll be tiny imperfections in the metal and as these imperfections will grow a tiny amount with every stress cycle. The tank will be designed to take a certain number of cycles and the expiry date will be based on this assuming a high mileage of the vehicle. Enough cycles and the tank will fail. There'll be safety factors on top so the tank may go for well beyond its design life but it wouldn't be worth it.

    In theory what should happen is sort of described here, visual inspection for damage/corrosion etc, and replacement of all 'wear-and-tear' components.

    If you want to keep your own tank then your car will be 'out of the CNG business' for some time (unless you pay extra for a 'rental' tank) and you need two trips to the garage.
