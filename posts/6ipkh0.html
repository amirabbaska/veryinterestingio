    Q:

    Why does sunburnt skin remain hot to the touch even days after the burn has occurred?

    Superathleticon A:

    Your body is repairing itself. Many cells DNA could have been damaged, so your cells need extra nutrients to repair the damage, make new cells, and make more melanin. That means extra blood goes to the area. The redness itself is capillaries expanding near the surface of the skin. It's not residual heat leftover from the sun.

    Karnman A:

    When the cells of your bodies undergo major damage from (as an example) excess UV exposure, this causes them to die. In the case of UV exposure this is because of excessive DNA damage that causes the cell to stop functioning correctly. This causes the cell to die and release it's contents into the matrix where it resides.

    The cellular contents contain chemical signalling molecules called cytokines (among other things) this interacts with the nearby vasculature causing the cells surroudning blood vessels to become more porous. This allows more fluid to come into the surroudning cellular matrix and more cells called macrophages that "eat" what is remaining of the damaged cells.

    This fluid buildup into the matrix causes the redness and swelling associated with DNA damage. This can last a couple of days until all the cellular content is cleaned up.

    The other reason is that sometimes a damaged cell does not die immediately. It will die a few days later.
