    JonathanMBerman A:

    Might it be possible to use CRISPR to modify human muscles to downregulate myostatin and thus increase muscle mass? Yes. All of the individual steps in the chain seem to be possible. Myostatin double mutants have high muscle mass, CRISPR can be used to modify adult somatic cells with an adenoviral vector. There's a therapy which has been recently approved which involves using an adenoviral vector to transfect retinal cells and correct a double recessive mutation. The eyes are immune privileged however, and adenovirus tends to be recognized by the immune system.

    Will it work? That's a question you won't know until it's been tried in a controlled way. What this guy is doing-- injecting himself on stage at a conference seems more like a publicity stunt than a controlled trial.

    There are a lot of questions you need to know answers too before you could do this reliably. Dose, safety, efficacy, etc.

    lenrriquevalencia A:

    Not real at all. It's like saying I'm going to dig a hole to China in my backyard using a shovel.

    Does CRISPR be used to edit DNA? Yes. Do shovels dig holes? Yes. Can I just inject CRISPR into my blood to deliver gene therapy or dig a hole to China with just a shovel? Absolutely not. It's ridiculous.

    There are so many labs working on gene therapy delivery. You just can't deliver DNA to all the cells in your body by injecting DNA into your blood. We would have so many cures today if it was that easy. Your cells need a way to get the DNA to penetrate their membrane. They also need to integrate the DNA or express the gene on the DNA.

    What are real scientists doing to learn how to deliver gene therapy to cells in vivo? Viral DNA delivery or gold nanoparticles to name just a couple. This guy is a fraud.

    Source: Biochemist

    wjaspers A:

    Buzzfeed as a trustworthy news source aside, modifying DNA with CRISPR works in small scale. This persons vision of performing this augmentation on a widely public scale is far fetched. CRISPR is touted as some miraculous cellular laser. Choose a strand and poof, swap it out! While it is useful, and effective, CRISPR can and has made errors, and Im willing to bet, we don't yet have significant data to represent its effectiveness in delivering permanent changes. The possibility of this biotechnology becoming as ubiquitous as an over the counter antacid is out of touch. And, whats to say that combining multiple designer strands of DNA doesn't produce unexpected results?
