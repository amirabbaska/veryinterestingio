    Considering they both stayed hydrated? I know the body would start using fat reserves so would it take the overweight person longer to starve to death than the regular weight person?

    champagne_donger A:

    Once glycogen stores are depleted, the body will switch metabolic pathways and activate a process called ketosis. This is where the body uses its own fat for energy (triglycerides are converted into ketones in the liver, which the body can then use for fuel).

    An obese person would also likely have a greater store of nutrients (magnesium/potassium/sodium etc) to use during the time of fasting.

    If you google 'longest fast' you'll probably stumble upon a research paper detailing a fast where a man went from 467 lbs to 180 over the course of 387 days or so. They gave him some Mg supplements after a few months, but it was questionable whether or not he needed them.

    When you are consuming carbohydrates regularly, the body is running on the glycogen pathway, and since your body can really only store ~2000 or so USABLE glycogen calories, people feel very hungry once this supply starts running low. This is why fat people continue to stuff their face instead of fasting (and also psychological reasons).

    However, even with someone at a normal weight, they most likely have ~120,000 FAT calories on their body at any given time. But in order to use them more effectively, the body must transition into using the ketone metabolic pathway (it won't automatically switch if the body is still being fed, or still has glycogen in the liver to use). So once your body runs out of glycogen, and realizes it isn't being fed anymore, it will switch to using the fat stores for energy, and these fat cells will be converted into ketones (betahydroxybutyrate, acetoacetate, and acetone) in the liver, which can then be used by the body for fuel. The brain and heart especially prefer ketones to glucose, and they are a much 'cleaner' burning fuel than glycogen to use for the organs.

    TitaniumDragon A:

    Yes, they would take much longer to starve to death. Fat contains a lot of energy, and that energy can be accessed by the body.

    That being said, there are various issues that could come up and kill them long before they starved to death.

    Certain micronutrients (the water-soluble ones, as opposed to fat-soluble ones - in particular, the B-complex vitamins and vitamin C) aren't substantially stored in fat, meaning that starving yourself for a long period of time can result in deficiencies in these micronutrients, which would eventually prove fatal. That is why obese people who fast are given supplements.

    Another issue is that people who starve themselves appear to have much higher rates of organ failure, particularly heart attacks. The exact mechanism is unknown.

    A third issue is that your body will break down muscles while starving yourself, requiring exercise to avoid loss of muscle mass.

    So they would take a lot longer than a regular-weight person to starve to death, but other issues could well kill them before they actually ran out of caloric energy.
