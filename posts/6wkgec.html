  • Physics Is quantum entanglement limited to two particles?

    Q:

    Could two, three or millions of particles separated by space-time be "entangled"?

    Rufus_Reddit A:

    People aren't as careful as they ought to be when they use the term 'entanglement', and tend to conflate 'entangled' with 'maximally entangled'.

    Maximal entanglement can only happen with pairs of particles. This is called "monogamy of entanglement."

    Non-maximal entanglement can involve a large number of particles.

    mofo69extreme A:

    For three particles, two interesting and different way you can entangle them with each other is in a GHZ state and in a W-state. But as mentioned elsewhere, all kinds of different entanglements are possible for any number of particles.

    In condensed matter physics, we are very interested in phases of matter where the entanglement is long-ranged, meaning even though you have some enormous (~10^(23)) number of particles, they all share some sort of entanglement with each other, even at "far" distances microscopically. So yes, you can have way more than a million particles entangled.
