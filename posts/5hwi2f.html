    Q:

    What do we know about the surface of Venus? What does the surface consist of to withstand the corrosive atmosphere?

    Gargatua13013 A:

    We know a surprising lot about the topography and landforms, through radar interferometry which can "see" throught the cloud cover from the Venera 15 and 16 probes in the 80s and from the Magellan probe in the 90s. Russian landings also have provided some data in the 70s and 80s.

    From surface measurements, we know that surface is mostly basaltic (see : Basilevsky, A. T.; J. W. Head III (2003). "The surface of Venus". Reports on Progress in Physics. 66 (10): 1699–1734). This fits with the landforms outlined by the radar imagery which are quite explicitely volcanic (Head, J. W., Crumpler, L. S., Aubele, J. C., Guest, J. E., & Saunders, R. S. (1992). Venus volcanism: Classification of volcanic features and structures, associations, and global distribution from Magellan data. Journal of Geophysical Research: Planets, 97(E8), 13153-13197) and quite diverse.

    However, these volcanic rocks may be reworked by surface processes and coated with other substances such as iron sulphides (pyrite) which might be stable in the existing atmospheric conditions, at least locally at high altitude in the Highlands (Ivanov, Mikhail A.; Head, James W. (2010). Scientific Pamphlet, Geologic Map of the Lakshmi Planum Quadrangle (V–7), Venus. USGS, Zolotov, M.Y. (1991). "Pyrite Stability on the Surface of Venus". Abstracts of the Lunar and Planetary Science Conference. 22: 1569–1570).

    CrudelyAnimated A:

    This is a semantic point, but I wouldn't use the word "withstand". Venus' landscape is the by-products of its corrosive atmosphere, not things that are impervious to corrosion, per se. This is not uncommon. Oxygen is highly reactive, causing rust and fire and tying up otherwise free elements into alkaline salts and weird, useless gases. The surface of Earth is full of oxygenated by-products, not things that withstand oxygen. Earth is remarkably lacking in clouds of methane and ammonia found on other planets, specifically because oxygen burns them into by-products like guano and carbon dioxide.
