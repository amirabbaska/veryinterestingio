    Q:

    Went to askreddit to ask what is the longest people have slept, and the answers surprised me. How is it that people can sleep for 20+ hours normally? I always seem to wake up naturally after 5-7 hours, and can sleep for maybe 10 hours at max.

    CocoMime A:

    You'll notice all the replies you got were from people who a) were very ill, b) had/have depression or c) done all nighters for work or school. The reason is, sleep - how long and when - is closely linked to your biological state and health. In normal circumstances, healthy human adults sleep when it's dark and are awake during the day, eg approximately 8 hours in a 24hour cycle.

    The time you feel sleepy, wakeful, and length of time you sleep is called circadian rhythm. At different times of day and night your body produces hormones that aid falling asleep/waking up. For example, melatonin is thought of as the 'sleep' hormone.

    In normal people, your daily routine helps encourage a regular circadian rhythm. Sunlight can trigger the release of hormones that make you feel awake and inhibits the release of melatonin. This is why 'normal' people tend to sleep for 6-9 hours, the length of darkness/night time (in most seasons and locations on the earth). If you wake up and go to bed the same time every day, (along with eating at regular times, exercising at regular times, expose yourself to natural light and it's not the middle of summer or winter, etc) then your circadian rhythm will be regular.

    People who sleep for different lengths of time- whether very short nights or 20+ hours as in your question, are likely suffering from a disruption of their circadian rhythms. They might have worked a very long day (as people have noted in anecdotes) or had high stress (stress hormones will disrupt the circadian rhythm). If someone does this regularly - shift workers, travelling across multiple time zones, those in the army, illness, etc, your circadian rhythm becomes more and more confused, and if you haven't had the 7-9 hours per 24 that most people need, you'll get what's called a sleep debt. So basically their hormones aren't strong enough to wake them after the normal periods or when it gets light, and they continue sleeping until their bodies have recovered sufficiently from the damage of staying awake too long. Unfortunately, no matter how much 'more' you sleep in the long run, repaying a sleep debt can never completely undo the damage of not allowing your body to sleep regularly. Look after your circadian rhythm!

    I remember reading something about this Krakauer's book Eiger Dreams. He talks about being stuck in a tent in Alaska in the 1970s during a spell of bad weather, and how the body seems to naturally want to hibernate when there is nothing else going on.     MelwynAthena A:

    As someone with severe insomnia, I envy those who can fall asleep and stay asleep!

    A recent conversation with my doctor opened my eyes to another chemical produced by your body as you are awake and moving, and enables falling asleep. Adenosine. From what he told me... As you move through your day, your body produces adenosine, and when you get to a certain level, your body shuts down to purge itself of the adenosine. As the levels get high enough in your body, your body starts to produce melatonin to aid in heading you to dreamland. The length of time you stay asleep is actually determined by how well your system purges the adenosine. Without underlying issues, those who sleep more purge the adenosine more slowly; those who sleep less, purge more quickly. Physical activity (exercise) early in your day can jump start adenosine production and help you to sleep better.

    In my case, though I work a physically demanding job, my body doesn't produce enough adenosine to start melatonin production in levels that will put, and keep, me down. I can go 36 hours before sleeping for 4, and be back up and going for another 24+. Regimented medication and scheduling, plus doing more outside of work (exercising, staying active after my shift, etc.), is about the only way I can fall and stay asleep for more than a couple hours at a time.
