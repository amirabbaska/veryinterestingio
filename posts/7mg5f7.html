    M311o A:

    Yes, this is known as the backfire effect.

    If you provide someone with information that contradicts their known belief they can become more confident in their own belief. The brain registers this in the same way that it registers pain. This actually activates the fight or flight response, which is a reason people can become physically aggressive or flee the conversation when there is a disagreement.

    Sharlinator A:

    There's a whole boatload of defense mechanisms and cognitive biases that are related to updating (or, indeed, avoiding to update) beliefs in the face of conflicting information. People are quite good at favoring information that confirms their beliefs and ignoring contradictory data. Cognitive dissonance, the state of being aware of conflicting thoughts and having to resolve the conflict somehow, is uncomfortable and stressful, and the brain avoids it as much as possible. One of the main defense mechanisms is compartmentalization where we just put conflicting beliefs into separate mental boxes and avoid thinking about them simultaneously. Other well-known coping mechanisms include denial, minimization, and projection.

    There's actually a fascinating neurological condition, anosognosia, where due to brain damage a patient may lose awareness of their body and argue against all evidence that their paralyzed limb, for instance, functions completely normally. When asked to do something with a paralyzed arm, like pick up an object, they confabulate a reason why they don't want to do it right now.
