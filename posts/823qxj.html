    Q:

    Why do grapefruits and grapefruit juice interact so much with so many types of medications?

    pharmwife A:

    Grapefruit juice inhibits an enzyme (CYP3A4) that is very important in metabolizing many different medications. So when you drink it, the enzyme function is reduced, and less drug gets metabolized (and so it stays in the body longer than normally, which leads to it accumulating) and can result in toxicity of the drug. Hope that helps!

    Also some medication has a slow release mechanism. The enzymes in grapefruit affect this, rather than the drug itself, so the whole lot hits your bloodstream in one go. This typically doesn’t change the medication effect, but makes side effects worse. Felodipine is one example of this.

    tsisdead A:

    So yeah mostly. The issue is that the enzyme in question either breaks down or activates the medication. So for some medications, grapefruit juice DECREASES the concentration of medicine you’re actually getting, and for others it INCREASES it.

    Also, this is drug specific. Like not all drugs in the same class are affected in the same way, or even at all. Take statins for example, very popular, very old, well-studied type of cholesterol lowering medication. Atorvastatin (Lipitor) and simvastatin are both affected by grapefruit juice. Rosuvastatin (Crestor) and pravastatin are not.
