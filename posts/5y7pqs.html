    n3rdi A:

    This'll get buried, but this is my only real area of expertise.

    The top answers here are all pointing at health insurance companies, which is sort of right-ish, but not for the reasons they say. There are other counties with private insurance that don't have our problems. Most countries, in fact, have some private companies that offer supplemental plans beyond government ones.

    The central issue is how prices are set in the united states. You could just go google Uwe Reinhardt articles and he'll say it better than me.

    Quick terminology primer: payer = who pays the doctor's bill. this can be an individual, the government or a commercial insurance company. provider = the one providing the care. doctors and/or hospitals.

    In virtually every OECD nation, prices are set centrally. Whether there's full government ownership of the healthcare system (e.g. in france most hospitals are owned by the government) or just a single payer system (hospitals and other providers remain independently owned). The incentives centralized pricing creates are exactly what you want: providers do things efficiently enough to make a profit, take a loss on that business, or stop performing a procedure and refer patients elsewhere. The government can tweak the reimbursement rates in order to adjust how the system performs. If colonoscopies turn out to be a waste of money because they're ineffective, you lower the reimbursement rates and doctors stop doing them.

    That's not how it works in the United States. Here, we have literally thousands of different prices for any given procedure. Simplifying things quite a bit, there's a government rate (medicare) which is fixed by CMS. This rate is significantly lower than what a commercial insurance company would pay the provider.

    Prices for commercial plans are all set in secret negotiations between providers and payers. The price paid by a commercial plan is based on the relative market strength of the payer and the provider.

    If it's a big insurance company with 75% market share negotiating a rate with a small provider with 5% market share, they can negotiate a low rate because that provider NEEDS to be able to accept patients with that insurance plan or they'll go out of business.

    If it's a small insurance company with 5% share negotiating with a huge provider, the provider calls the shots and can negotiate a high reimbursement rate. The insurance company needs to be able to send patients to Big Brand Name Hospital because otherwise nobody is going to sign up for this plan.

    This creates HORRIBLE incentives. Hospitals don't need to be efficient if they can get a lot of money out of insurance companies. The way to improve your negotiating position is by having a gigantic patient base, providing a lot of extremely high-tech services that people demand, by marketing the hell out of yourself, etc. If I'm running a hospital and have to choose whether to invest $100M on improving efficiency or $100M in building/buying a facility in a new market, I'm not going to spend it on efficiency improvements.

    There's one state (Maryland) where inpatient reimbursement rates are flat and tied to medicare rates across all payers. Private insurance companies pay exactly what the government does. This required special legislation, but guess who has had the lowest increase in inpatient costs in the nation in the last 20+ years?

    There's a lot more going on than this, but most of it eventually comes back to how payment/pricing works. That's the biggest difference between the USA and most of the rest of the world.

    Health insurance companies. I do billing for offices and let me tell you that health insurance companies only exist to make money as the middle man.

    Let's say you and your employer pay $2000 a month to an insurance company to have decent coverage for a family. Well most likely you will still have a deductible/coinsurance/copay on top of that premium. If your family is relatively healthy (which if you think about it the majority of people are) then you have paid over $24000 for one year and let's say for argument sake less than $2000 in health services.

    And not only that but the insurance companies are contracted with your health care providers to pay the lowest rate they can. So what your doctor actually bills out is much higher than the actual cost. Therefore someone without insurance can be screwed even if they can get some kind of discount through their provider. I personally find it all insane. The amount of rules, red tape, delay and bullshit insurance companies cause I believe is a major factor in why our health care system doesn't work as well as it could. They basically control the patients and providers.

I'm not saying insurance companies are the only factor, but they are a huge part and to say otherwise makes me believe you either work for one or have never dealt with them. There's many problems with the current system and I believe it comes down to it being a for profit system. I don't believe it's doctors that are the greedy ones.
