    Q:

    ELI5: Can you actually train yourself to need less sleep, or are you just learning to be able to function with less?

    rlbond86 A:

    People who are chronically sleep deprived have lower cognitive capability in scientific studies. After a long time of sleep deprivation, the body and brain will begin to "get used" to feeling tired, but the cognitive effects (and increased mortality) remain.

    In other words: it feels like you get used to it, but you never do

    Kittamaru A:

    When I was diagnosed with Sleep Apnea, I was supposed to have an overnight sleep study, then come back for a titration study with the CPAP.

    After about an hour or so, the sleep tech woke me up and told me we were putting the mask on then and there.

    Apparently, I had an AHI of 81 or so over the course of a single hour... so basically, I didn't sleep - I just died for a little bit. I was also entering REM sleep almost immediately - I nearly skipped entire phases of sleep, I was so messed up (our estimate is that I went undiagnosed for about four years, and I've never slept well even before that).

    After around six hours sleep, in an unfamiliar and, honestly, somewhat uncomfortable bed, hooked up to more wires than I care to admit, with a strange new hose and mask attached to my face like some kind of weird beneficial facehugger... I woke up feeling better than I had in years.

    Apparently it takes about 12 cm^3 of air to keep my airway open... but holy hell do I feel better now!

    _bigorangehead_ A:

    Your sleep requirement is not a trainable characteristic, so no. Restricting your sleep in no way changes what your individual requirement actually is you merely become accustomed to a new, lower, level of performance as a result. You believe you are functioning ok but have in reality adjusted to a worse "normal".

    "Why we sleep" by Matthew Walker is an excellent, and eye-opening, read on this topic.
