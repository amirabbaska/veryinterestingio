    Q:

    Why do some vaccines wear off over time?

    PHealthy A:

    There's a variety of reasons why we give multiple doses of vaccines:

    • Vaccines and immune systems can miss each other - Sometimes your immune system isn't great at the time of vaccination so you end up not developing immunity. MMR is good example of this and why (in a population perspective) multiple doses are given to ensure you have the highest chance of developing immunity, luckily this can sometimes achieve lifelong immunity. Multiple doses can also achieve an even more robust immunity.

    • Pathogens evolve - this is the assumed reason pertussis for example needs an occasional booster. There are lots of theories why we don't get lifelong immunity, the one I think is the most plausible is that in times before vaccines a natural infection conferred immunity (you know, if you didn't die) and then the persistent presence in the population ensured you were continually exposed and got a natural booster.

    • Some pathogens mutate very fast - influenza is a great example of viruses that are extremely difficult to design vaccines for. Their genome is basically a bookcase and the vaccine targets say a chapter in a book. Influenza can easily swap out pages, chapters, books, even whole shelves.

    ryaichu A:

    Some vaccines are less immunogenic than others and create less of a response. Even with a proper initial response, memory B cells die eventually, unless you get reexposed to either the virus/bacteria or another vaccination shot.
