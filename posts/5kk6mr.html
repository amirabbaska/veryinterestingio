    How rare is silicon at what point would scarcity start to impact the cost of solar panel and microchip production?

    HugodeGroot A:

    The scarcity of silicon is simply a non-issue when it comes to commercializing solar cells. In fact, silicon is one of the most abundant elements in the Earth's crust, as shown in this chart. The harder part historically has been scaling up the purification of silicon to create big crystalline and polycrystalline chunks. But as far as the raw materials go, we're more than good.

    SirPlz A:

    Silicon dioxide (SiO2) is the main component of sand, which includes most (not coral nor black sand) beaches of the world as well as the sand found in sandy deserts. That's a LOT of silicon, assuming that it can be separated out. The chart that's already been posted shows that silicon is second only to oxygen in abundance, and sand is part of the reason why.     malvoliosf A:

    Right now, we use about 1.5 million metric tons of silicon a year for electronics and similar things.

    There is just under a quintillion tons of silicon in the Earth, so we have about 600 billion years at the current rate.

    For comparison, there is enough hydrogen left in the sun for about 5 billion more years.

    So I'm not worried.

    NanotechNinja A:

    As others have answered about silicon; a more pressing concern is the abundance of the more obscure elements used in various devices. Indium, in particular, is crucial for making indium tin oxide, a transparent conductive coating. It only comprises 0.21ppm of the Earth's crust, it's mainly produced as a by-product of zinc purification, and we only have 14 years of it left.
