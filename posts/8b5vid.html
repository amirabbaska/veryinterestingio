    Cruzzfish1 A:

    If I'm not mistaken, beach sand is more silicon because quartz does not dissolve in water and beaches are exposed to water regularly. Dry deserts are not usually exposed to water though, so they could have sand made of a lot of other, lower in silicon compounds.

    Gnarlodious A:

    Desert sand has a lot of calcium in it, like for example White Sands of New Mexico which is almost purely gypsum. It was formed by blowing wind, since calcium is relatively lightweight. Calcium is not very abrasive so it tends to remain calcium based, its not much of a sandblaster medium. Ocean waves, in contrast to wind, has a lot more inertia, so it is ideal for blasting hard rock. Under that condition, calcium is broken into microscopic particles and ends up in the shells of ocean creatures. Apparently wave generated silica and quartz sand is reduced to a consistent size by some physical process.
