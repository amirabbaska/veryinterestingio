    NEW
    Q:

    I did a little digging in the sub(and Google) but couldn't find a question like this. How would we know that the Permian extinction wiped out roughly 90% of the planets population for example? From what I understand most of the fossilized remains we have recovered only represent a practically non-existent percentage of life that has existed throughout history. How can we even estimate current extinction rates, I haven't seen a list of the supposed dozens of extinctions occurring daily?(Though if they exist I would be very interested in seeing it)

    Gargatua13013 A:

    Those estimates are mostly based on the analysis of large species databases such as the NCEAS Paleobiology Database. You basically generate humongous tables and start noting where points of first and last appearance of various genera/species occur in time, depending on how deep you want to look. A lot of the work done on the subject comes from DM Raup, JJ Sepkoski, which is certainly as good a place as any to get your info.

    see, for instance:

    • Alroy, John, et al. "Effects of sampling standardization on estimates of Phanerozoic marine diversification." Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences 98.11 (2001): 6261-6266.

    • Raup, David M., and J. John Sepkoski. "Mass extinctions in the marine fossil record." Science 215.4539 (1982): 1501-1503.
    feldspathoid91 A:

    Extinction events are not characterized by an estimation of how many individual animals die. After the Permian extinction event roughly 90 percent of all known species prior to that were driven to extinction.

    An example would be if you had one specemin that is a trylobite and two hundred sponges. If the one trylobite does not appear in the fossil record after a certain time then 50 percent of species in the data set went extinct during that event, even though the one trylobite specemin represented less than half a percent of the total number of animals in the data set. Hope that helps.
