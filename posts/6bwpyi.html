    Q:

    Is the earth still cooling from its formation? If so, does that mean eventually all tectonic activity will cease and our liquid mantle will solidify?

    ouemt A:

    Yes, the earth is still cooling from formation, specifically heat generated by accretion and differentiation. A lot of the heat is actually due to radioactive decay of things like thorium-232, uranium-238, and potassium-40. A good discussion of the topic can be read over at Wikipedia.

    One thing to correct however: The earth's mantle is not liquid. The outer core is liquid, but that's it. The mantle is solid, but warm enough to undergo ductile flow at a very slow rate. Again, Wikipedia has a good discussion of the topic.

    CubicZircon A:

    This problem was famously studied by Lord Kelvin: he considered the Earth as formed as a molten ball of rock and then computed the time needed for it to cool down to present temperature, as an estimation for the age of the planet.

    He found that Earth was about 20–40 million years old.

    Of course we know (and actually had known before Kelvin's computations) that the actual age of the Earth is much, much larger than that (even though we did not know the 4.5 billion figure, geologists and biologists had a correct rough idea of the order of magnitude).

    What Kelvin missed, as /u/ouemt points out, is radioactivity — which was discovered only a few years after he made his computations (and which, interestingly enough, is also one of the bases for our current estimate of 4.5 billion years). This does not make his computations any less impressive, because they show exactly how large the part of radioactivity is in maintaining Earth's temperature.

    TheRealGuyTheToolGuy A:

    The mantle isn't liquid. It has fluid behaviour over very long periods of time because of gaps at the atomic scale that are empty then get filled by the following atom or group of atoms. This causes a kind of convection flow that you might see in other systems that makes it seem like a liquid.
