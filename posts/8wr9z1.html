    Flocculencio A:

    What others have said about the profit margins is true, but the fact is a lot of people buy luxury watches. They're not mass market, sure, but in many professions they're the most obvious wearable male accessory.

    Especially when you talk about fields like financial advice, personal banking, and finance in general, wearing a luxury watch demonstrates to your clients that you are a successful professional who can be trusted with their money. Outside the financial/sales sector its often a symbol of professional/life achievement. Hell, I'm a teacher and I saved up for a Rolex for the birth of my first child (and am looking at a JLC reverso for my second).

    Even beyond that a mechanical watch is a superb piece of analog technology, and a lot of people like the feel of them beyond their nature as a status symbol. They're lovely little machines, and, like being a "car guy" a lot of guys become "watch guys".

    Edit: Just to add on, in a world where luxury is increasingly disposable (eg top end tech accessories are only top end for a year or so, high fashion changes very drastically from season to season, luxury cars lose their value unless you put a lot of work into maintaining them to the point they become classics), a luxury watch is the one remaining status symbol which lasts. I know my Rolex will outlast me. It's just a Submariner, but I spent close to 14k USD on it (rough conversion from Singapore dollars), and I know it'll outlive me if taken care of. Patek Philippe (which of course is light years beyond a mere Rolex) specifically uses this as their tagline- "You never own a Patek Philippe, you just take care of it for the next generation". It applies to any of the classically manufactured high end watches.
