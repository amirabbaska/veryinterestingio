    ELI5: Why do our bodies sometimes get sharp pains in random places for a few seconds/minutes and then go away?

    AVeryLazy A:

    Edit 735: I tried writing a better ELI5 than the one I originally wrote. Added it below the original.

    This is a favorite field of mine and also a difficult question to answer. I'll do my best, but I think I'll still leave you wanting.

    Even though we have nerves that we know that are related to pain (called C fibers), the processing of the feeling we call pain is much more complex than that.

    The brain and the spinal cord (the central nervous system) do all the processing of pain.

    So what can cause a random pain for a few seconds? Here are two examples, there are many more.

    A. A peripheral nerve (i.e, the one in the bottom of your foot) shooting while unprovoked or because of long term irritation.

    B. A sensory nerve (unrelated to pain fibers) shoots an electrical current towards the brain. Because of wrong processing in the brain, the sensory input is perceived as pain.

    So these are examples to the "how". I feel that I cannot answer "why" with confidence. There is a lot of research, and pain is something we are still trying to understand.

    The guy in the next video is a leading researcher when it comes to pain, and he does a great job explaining it in this TED talk.

    Sumit316 A:

    Well a little search gave me two reasons plausible reasons -

    1. It could be due to a phenomenon called "referred pain". It occurs when sensory information comes to the spinal cord as coming from one location, but is interpreted by the brain as coming from another location.

    2. This is applicable to only sharp fleeting chest pains and it is called Precordial Catch. It is a non-serious condition affecting the left side of the anterior wall of the chest. It is characterized by sharp chest pains, and usually affects children and adolescents, although it can occur, though less frequently, in adults. PCS manifests as a very intense, sharp pain, typically at the left side of the chest, generally in the cartilage between the bones of the sternum and rib cage, which is worse when taking breaths, or doing physical activity.
