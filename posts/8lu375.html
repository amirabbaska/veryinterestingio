  • Economics Where does one keep $60 billion in cash? Comcast... I'm looking at you.

    NEW
    Q:

    A recent news article explained that Comcast is trying to block the $50 billion+ offer by Disney to purchase Fox by providing a 'richer' all cash offer of $60 billion. My bank only insures $250K ($500K for married couples... I think) per account. How does a company:

    1. Store so much cash? Like - physically store it. Where?
    2. Insure so much cash?
    3. Keep so much cash lying around? Isn't that better used invested somewhere and if it is invested, what vehicles allow such quick liquidity in a situation where you may just want to purchase a major network?

    It's not literally cash. It just means they have that much money that they can transfer to someone else. That's referred to as "liquid assets". Assets they have that are not "liquid" would be things like real estate and equipment, or investments that are locked in and cannot be converted.

    Eidolones A:

    "All cash" in this situation doesn't necessary mean all cash right now, and for large acquisitions it's much more common to be a combination of cash and debt (eg. $20B of cash on hand and $40 of secured senior notes issued for it). The "all cash" is just to differentiate it from a "cash + equity (stock)" offer in that Fox shareholders are receiving cash for their shares instead of stock in Comcast.

    For company that do keep a lot of cash around, usually only what they need in the next month or two is actually kept as cash, and depending on the size of the company may be spread over many, many accounts in many territories. A lot of the rest is kept as "cash equivalents", basically short-term, highly liquid investments that have very low risk to lose value, for example CDs, commerical paper, money market funds, treasury bills etc. Many companies also keep a portion of their cash (especially cash that they think they won't need within a year) in longer term financial investments, which is usually classified as investments held-to-maturity or available-for-sale, depending on the type of financial instrument.
