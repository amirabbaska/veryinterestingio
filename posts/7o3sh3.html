    Q:

    Why can smokers sleep 8 hours without needing to smoke, but why some can't go 2 hours during the day without one? Is it because of the ritual and behavioural patterns of smoking? I've heard stories of chain smokers who do wake up to smoke.

    Centimane A:

    Something worth noting: there are many bodily and hormonal changes that occur during sleep meant to prevent inturruptions. Things like ADH increases to prevent urination, and regulating ghrelin and leptin levels to control hunger.

    I can't seem to find any good articles for which horomone/bodily change (if any) would impact nicotine dependency (I'm on mobile, and searching anything that includes "sleep" and "tobacco" results in a flood of garbage articles) but I expect some amount of research has been done on it.

    Given the body's ability to keep standard needs in check while sleeping I expect a similar mechanism helps curb some drug addictions during sleep. However, by nature of being a non-standard need it would likely not be curbed as effectively and could still interfere with sleep.

    blinkenpilz A:

    The neuroadaptation of nicotine dependence is pretty complex. There's the so-called 'sensitization-homeostasis (SH) model', which states that consumption of nicotine both depresses craving circuits (acutely) and triggers their formation (chronically, Brain Sci. 2012 Oct 17;2(4):523-52.). Cellular mechanisms are described here, for example: Dialogues Clin Neurosci. 15 (4): 431â€“443. The autonomous nervous system gets activated upon withdrawal, which make people fuzzy, anxious, etc.

    Nicotine plasma concentrations dropping below a certain level, thereby triggering withdrawal, is just one part of the picture. It has a half-life of 1-2 hours, so after 4-8 hours most of it will have left the system. There are also active metabolites such as cotinine that are eliminated much more slowly.

    But basically, just looking at nicotine and not the other stuff they put in cigarettes, after a good night's sleep you're pretty much detoxed physically. Unfortunately, there's the psychological triggers such as morning cup of joe, a beer, a meal, or social situations (stress, rewarding oneself for accomplishments, the routine of smoking with peers) that are very tough to overcome (JAMA. 2012;308(15):1573). These are not present when one is sleeping.

    The fact that some smokers wake up because of craving has not really been studied. There's a couple of articles such as in Acta Med Austriaca. 2001;28(1):21-2 ('Nocturnal sleep-disturbing nicotine craving: a newly described symptom of extreme nicotine dependence.') but that's about it. Difficulty here is separating the disturbed sleep architecture that comes with smoking from physically being woken up because nicotine levels dropped.
