  • Why does tickling cause laughter?

    NEW
    Q:

    I find it incredibly unpleasant and overwhelming to my senses...yet I laugh as if I enjoy it. What's the deal?

    AlsaceVera A:

    Tickling that causes laughter can be caused by touching several sensitive spots, such as armpits or the neck, which are "hidden" or otherwise vulnerable parts of us. The somatosensory cortex of the brain is responsible for picking up these touch signals, and sending them to the cingulate cortex, which makes sense of them. Laughter is genetically meant to be some sort of a defense mechanism; we're programmed to think that when someone touches these sensitive spots on our body, that it will cause some sort of harm to us. Keep in mind, our body systems still function in a primitive manner: to ensure survival. When the touch signal is basically harmless, signals are sent back to the sensory cortex, to evoke laughter, which is practically a "false alarm" sign that nothing was wrong, to decrease any form of stress. The old, primitive reason of laughter is to provide a relieving sense. Which is why comedy, involving unexpected jokes and one liners, makes people laugh.

    Chefgorilla A:

    The ticklish spots typically are also vulnerable spots - so it makes sense for evolution to give us a simple self defense course. When we are tickled the unpleasant sensation makes us try to defend these spots on our body, but usually tickling takes place in a rather playful setting. So one theory is that we actually laugh to encourage others to tickle us, so we learn how to defend these areas better.
