    FinalCent A:

    Then you just have an almost totally unorganized blur for your C post selections, and basically a blank screen for your D post selections. The photons state is a 50-50 superposition for the two patterns (fringe/antifringe), the BS doesn't assign the state, just sorts (trasmits or reflects) based on it/in proportion to its weight. So, any BS which transmits not 50-50 will be sending a mixture of countervailing patterns to at least 1 detector, which washes out the interference an increasing amount as it approaches 100-0. Maybe easier to see if you instead ask: why can't the DCQE use just 3 detectors (replace C with a mirror aimed at D) and concentrate all the erased paths on 1 location? Because then the photons aren't spatially sorted by patterns at all and the setup is self defeating.

    BinaryHelix A:

    Here's the way to think about it: the initial "interference" screen as labeled in the video on Earth always shows a non-interference blob. You will never directly see an interference pattern here. It will always show this blob no matter what happens to the twins later on on Mars.

    Think about why this is true: you may not have detectors A or B on Mars, or you may not have the "erasing" detectors C or D on Mars, or you lie about the decision you're going to make to the Earth researcher, or the Mars lab blows up and there are no detectors and the twin photons go into the great infinity, etc.

    Your mistake is assuming that messing with the Martian twins can cause an interference pattern on the screen on Earth, and that will never happen.

    Instead, as you give Mars detector C more and more of the photons that were originally split 50-50, you in effect give more of the complementary pattern that D had to C. You aren't reinforcing C's interference pattern (remember it maps to a blob on Earth), but ruining it by combining D's. So as you get closer and closer to giving 100% to C, you in effect are making C responsible for the big blob of photons seen at the Earth "interference" screen.

    Because what happens at the end is you classically communicate (not FTL) with the station on Mars what actually happened to the twins. In essence, you get to evaluate which photon at the Earth interference screen is correlated with which detector hit on Mars. And Mars can tell you that all 100% detector hits were due to C, and thus you label every photon on Earth's blobby interference screen as due to C.
