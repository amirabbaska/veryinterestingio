    Q:

    Do chickens have a limited supply of eggs throughout their life, or can they produce eggs as long as they have a functioning reproductive systema?

    grassfinch A:

    Birds, like humans, have a limited number of primary oocytes in their ovaries. But most of them don't live long enough to run out. This Slate article that addresses your question says that some macaws, which are very long-lived, can run out of eggs, but the study they cite is on humans, and I can't find the macaw fact anywhere else. Still, biologically it makes sense that if a bird lived long enough, it would be unable to continue producing eggs.

    nathhad A:

    As other posters have mentioned, they do have a limited number, but they tend to slow down by around 3 years, so it's not common for them to completely run out. They start laying around 6 months, give or take, and will lay almost an egg a day for the first year after that. Then they begin to taper off, and after they're about three they can be down to one to two eggs a week, at which point they're no longer economical to keep around for eggs.

    I have about 18 that are just under 3 years old, and which are giving me about 8-10 eggs a day total, plus 7 birds a year older than that who give me at most 2 eggs a week between them. The same batch of birds a year ago were still producing 12-15 eggs a day.

    The older 7 are desperately overdue for the stew pot, but I'm working too much right now. Essentially they're eating at least 1/3 of the feed (that bunch are actually always at the feeder), but are only producing about 3% of the eggs.

    Sherpales A:

    Chicken farmer here.

    Yes, there are a limited number of eggs a chicken could potentially produce. The reality is that the chicken will die before it even comes close to producing all of its eggs, even in a natural setting. In factory farming, their lifespan is significantly shorter, due to consumers demand of cheap, quality eggs. (Younger birds produce desirable eggs more efficiently)

    Chickens are considered lay hens at 19 weeks of age, and in a factory farm setting, lay for approximately 1 year before being replaced by new lay hens. In that one year span, a white Lohmann layer hen will produce roughly 320 eggs.
