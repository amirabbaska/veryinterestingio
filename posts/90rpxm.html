    shorbs A:

    I'm an epidemiologist, we are trained in medical study design and analysis. The study (https://www.ajpmonline.org/article/S0749-3797(18)31696-9/abstract) you're talking about is a very large observational study. The fact that it is published means that it has gone through the peer review process for the American Journal of Prenatal Medicine, meaning that 2-3+ experts (for example: pediatricians, ob/gyn or specialized epidemiologists) in the field reviewed the paper, found the results acceptable, methods sound and okay-ed it for publication.

    One Study Fallacy:

    With regard to your one study comment: this was done in a very large grouping (675,000+) ) of mother-infant pairs and found that TDAP was protective against pertussis in the child. The fact that it is done with such a large group of people and has such a strong effect ( reduced pertussis by 43% when compared to mothers who didn't get TDAP) is a very reliable result. These values (CI=0.35, 0.92) being below 1.00 indicate a statistically significant finding. In the paper, they discuss and evaluate the CDC recommendations (here is a pamphlet https://www.cdc.gov/pertussis/pregnant/mom/get-vaccinated.html).

    Safety Studies:

    This is an observational study, so they didn't have a control/test group-- they had a surveillance system set up to record whether or not the mother got the TDAP vaccine, when, and how it affected the infant. So this didn't need a safety study because you aren't intervening, you are simply observing what is happening in the population.
