    DarwinZDF42 A:

    I think the feeling you're getting at is when you know you know something, but you can't quite get it when you want to.

    This is a problem with a step in process of remembering and using information called "recall".

    The important thing here is that to the extent that memories have a physical medium, it's the connection between neurons in our brains. Some of these connections are really strong, which means it's easy to remember a thing, and some are weaker, meaning it might be harder to remember that thing.

    Basically, once you get something into your long term memory, it's there for...well, not necessarily for good, but for a while. But if you don't use it, the connections between neurons in your brain related to that thing aren't strong enough to pass the signals needed for you to "remember" the thing, as in, be able to say it out loud, for example.

    So the connections are there, but aren't firing well enough to pass the signal. One way to help is to try to remember a related thing. When you associate memories, the connections work together to boost each other. So if you can recall, say, a smell associated with a fact, or actually smell the smell, that might help you remember the fact.

    Brains are weird.

    There’s also “tip of the tongue,” which is when you’re trying to think of a word or name but can’t seem to get it out.
