    Q:

    Have we ever observed anything behaving as if it only existed in a lower number of dimensions?

    Greebo24 A:

    Yes. Take a flat microwave cavity and solve the (electromagnetic) Helmholtz equations. Once you are below a critical frequency, they behave like a two-dimensional system. So your EM field now behaves like a 2-d entity.

    Take this a step further: "The formal similarity between the stationary Schrodinger and Helmholtz equations, the latter considered for frequencies f below a critical frequency f_c where the electrical field distribution depends only on the planar variables x and y, renders such experiments suitable for the study of quantum phenomena. The height d of the microwave cavity in the z direction is bounded by d < c/2f_c." (Taken from Graf et al, PRL 69 (1992) 1296)

    This means you can use a flat microwave cavity as an analogue computer for a quantum system governed by the equivalent Schroedinger equation - as the authors have done.

    cantgetno197 A:

    Yes, this happens all the time for electrons if they are in a confining potential along some axis such that their wavefunction becomes quantized along that direction:

    https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:InfiniteSquareWellAnimation.gif

    If say the average electron has the amount of energy corresponding to state C there and the average fluctuation in energy is much less than the difference in energy between D and C then that degree of freedom becomes "frozen out", as its motion in that direction is fixed and trapped at C and the electron exists as a reduced dimensional creature. If you only confine along one direction you create a Quantum Well (electron sees a 2D universe):

    https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_well

    An example here is the billions of transistors that are the basis of the computer you're using right now. Charge passes through those transistors as if it exists in a 2D world, it's called a 2D electron gas (or 2DEG):

    https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-dimensional_electron_gas

    If you confine along two directions (electron sees a 1D universe) you get a nanowire;

    https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanowire

    And if you confine in all directions you get a zero dimensional structure (0D universse) which is called a quantum dot:

    https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_dot

    Dimensional confinement also happens to other things other than electrons, for example in magnetic excitations.

    AKiss20 A:

    Strongly rotating fluid systems (systems with Rossby numbers much less than unity) behave 2-dimensionally as can be shown by the Taylor-Proudman theorem. The canonical example of this is a Taylor column in which a finite height object "appears" infinitely tall.

    https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taylor–Proudman_theorem
