  • Why are warm blankets cozy, but rooms of the same temperature are stifling?

    NEW
    Q:

    EDIT: I mean blankets that have been exposed to an artificial heat source (such as a dryer), guys! Not a regular blanket I've been sitting under.

    I'm not that big of an idiot.

    operator462 A:

    Warm blankets are cozy because your body generates warmth which is transferred into the blanket and air trapped under the blanket. This warmth slowly dissipates out the outer side of the blanket into the cooler room and you don't over heat, allowing your body to relax as it is not working to raise or lower your internal temperature.

    In a room of the same temperature your body is still creating warmth but it has no where to dissipate to. This makes your internal temperature rise and you feel sweaty and uncomfortable as your body tries to cool down.

    mB_Roundhouse A:

    Put the blanket over your head, now it's way too hot. Without getting into the science behind it, your head is the main body part that regulates body temperature. What the other poster said about dissipating heat is correct, but (depending on factors like room temperature, blanket thickness & material, etc.) the blanket might not feel comfortable after a while because it reaches your body temperature, meaning your body heat doesn't really have anywhere to go. Placing your head out of the blanket allows your body heat to dissipate there, unobstructed. Leaving your head under the blanket will eventually turn the blanket into a small "hot room" and you will have the same stifling feeling.

    To illustrate this, if you're in a hot room and only have a tiny handheld fan, you aim it at your face. Or you stick your head out the window or something. This is also why hot tubs feel awesome until you put your head under the water. I should also note that your head is the main body part that regulates temperature--not the only part. Take that how you will. I'm not sure how biologically accurate this is, but here is a related rule of thumb I've always lived by: If you are hot, focus on cooling off your head/face; if you are cold, focus on warming up your body/extremities (and head, in extreme temperatures).
