    chinchumpan A:

    This phenomenon is called freezing point depression, and for lower concentrations of the solute, it happens independently of the properties of the solute in question. So, the answer to your question would be no, because this process happens as long as something is dissolved in the water, regardless of what that substance is (again, for lower concentrations).

    A way to explain it is that when the solvent is freezing, and the molecules join the solid phase and leave the liquid phase, there is a smaller volume of liquid in which the solute is dissolved, so the entropy of the solute decreases, regardless of what that solute is, since at lower concentrations the interactions derived from the solute's properties are negligible.

    Now, the solution's freezing point CAN increase if you go above a certain solute concentration, because then the interaction between solute particles becomes relevant. Consider then a solute that is miscible in water and, when in its pure state, has a higher freezing point than the solvent (such as acetic acid, with a freezing point of 16,6°C / 61,9°F). At lower concentrations, freezing point depression will be observed, but at some concentration, the solution's freezing point will increase.
