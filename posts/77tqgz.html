    Q:

    How much of a person's height is determined by DNA and how much is determined thru health and diet while an adolescent?

    ninjakitty117 A:

    We have an extremely good understanding that height is heritable. Tall parents have tall children. When analyzed on a genetic level, 75% of the variation in height is genetic. However, only 10% of that variation (7.5% of the total variation) can be explained by specific genetic factors. The unknown genetic factors are called "missing heritability".

    Tl;dr most of it is heritable, but most of the specific factors are unknown.

    Source: Exploring Personal Genomics textbook

    Sylwong A:

    While others are commenting about twin studies and such, it is important to note that the genetic contribution to the phenotypic difference is overestimated as the environmental factor is kept relatively constant due to similar growing up conditions etc. This is all the more so for adoption studies of twins as the screening for eligibility to adopt narrows the range of guardians and hence result in many environmental factors being kept constant. For example, when twins are raised separately by similar adopter profiles, they will have generally similar socioeconomic backgrounds and hence be able to afford the same abundance of food and nutrients etc. So if nutrients were to be a environmental factor of height, it would have been masked by the fact that it was kept constant and hence we incorrectly overstate the genetic contribution to determine height.
