    PerfectChaos33 A:

    They'd cremate the limb if nobody claims it. Obviously they'd wait a while and try their best to figure out who it belonged to. And they'd keep as much evidence as they could (finger prints, hair & skin cells, and whatever).

    I interned at a prosecutor's office last year and in the homicide department they had a real human skeleton, named him Barry. Apparently he was just a dead homeless guy they found. Nobody claimed his body so the detectives pulled some strings and convinced whomever needed convincing to allow them to keep the skeleton. He wears a lab coat and a nice pair of shades.

    Barry also has some bone-y friends, all I remember was the cat skull. Maybe the skull of a small dog? Not sure.

    Mr_Scooty_Butt A:

    They often go to forensic anthropologists who can analyze them and help give and clues as to who they came from. You can tell a lot about a person from just a couple of bones.

    Also, story time:

    Back in the day, I took a few physical anthropology courses in school. The professor had a crock pot (no shit, like you'd buy at Walmart) that was used to get rid of the meat and other goop so bones could be studied more closely.

    Also, there was allegedly a time when a sealed plastic mystery bag was found in the back of a closet. Inside were two (very decayed) human feet, with no notes as to where they came from or what investigation they belonged to. Assuming there's any truth to that, there's not a hell a lot of oversight as to what ends up happening to "misplaced" limbs.
