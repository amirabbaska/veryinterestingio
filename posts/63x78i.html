  • Biology How the heck did iguanas get all the way out to Fiji?

    NEW
    Q:

    Iguanas are known from the Americas, nearby islands, and...Fiji and Tonga. Two islands in the middle of the Pacific, 10,000 km from the nearest other iguanas on the Galapagos. There aren't any iguanas known from any of the islands in between the two. There aren't any known from landmasses nearer to Fiji, like Australia and New Guinea. So what the heck?

    lythronax-argestes A:

    The Fijian and Tongan iguanas form a genus of their own, Brachylophus, which is a part of the Iguaninae (also containing marine iguanas, spinytail iguanas, green iguanas, and others). Several hypotheses have been proposed to explain their isolation. The most widely followed one involves rafting from the Americas. It is not unthinkable that iguanas would be able to raft for such long distances; they possess nasal salt glands and also thick skin.

    Where exactly in the Americas they actually originated from is hard to determine. Interestingly, morphologically Brachylophus is more similar to the likewise arboreal Iguana than other island-dwelling iguanas like Cyclura and Ctenosaura, which tend to be ground-dwellers. Iguana is incidentally relatively widespread in South America. It remains possible that Brachylophus diverged from the latter through some ancestral population around Mexico or the Isthmus of Panama.

    Like Brachylophus, the red mangrove Rhizophora mangle is known from Fiji and Tonga (and also Galapagos), but basically nowhere else in the Pacific. Interestingly, it forms a large part of the diet of Cyclura. Iguana and Brachylophus eat it too. Certainly suggestive that Rhizophora may have played a role in the dispersal of Brachylophus.

    References

    Gibbons, J.R.H. (1981) "The Biogeography of ''Brachylophus'' (Iguanidae) including the Description of a New Species, ''B. vitiensis'', from Fiji." Journal of Herpetology 15(3): 255-273. DOI: 10.2307/1563429
