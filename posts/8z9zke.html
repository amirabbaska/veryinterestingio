    Q:

    ELI5: How do we know that 194 decibels is the loudest sound possible?

    OddGentleman A:

    As we all know, sound is a series of pressure wave travelling in air: sound waves are caused by oscillations of pressure in the air.  The loudness of a sound possible on earth is limited by the size of this oscillation of air pressure.  This presents a natural limit to how loud a (sustained) sound can get - the pressure of the air cannot drop below a vacuum.  So the loudest (sustained) sound possible is therefore a pressure wave oscillating between vacuum and +2 atmospheres.  And this is equal to 194 decibels.  No sound on earth (at sea level) can exceed 194 decibels because of this.

    crookedsmoker A:

    The loudness of a sound is nothing more than the difference between maximum and minimum air pressure of the sound wave. The bigger the difference, the louder the sound.

    At sea level, air pressure is 1 atm. The lowest pressure possible in a sound wave is a complete vaccuum: 0 atm. Because sound waves are symmetrical, the oscillation the other way is the same size and therefore 2 atm. Put these numbers into a fancy mathematical formula and we can see that this translates to 194dB.

    kouhoutek A:

    Sound is rapid and repeated change in air pressure. A wave more dense air followed by less dense air vibrates your eardrum or microphone, and we perceive this as sound. The greater the difference in density, the louder the sound.

    At 194 dB, the dense air is at twice normal pressure, and the less dense air is essentially a vacuum. You can't get less dense than a vacuum, so that serves as a maximum limit in sound intensity.

    Note that you can get louder sounds than 194 dB if the sound travels through a denser medium.
