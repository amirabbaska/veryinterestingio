    iamcarltonwhitfield A:

    I was just in Eyam, England, a town that self-quarantined for a couple years during The Plague so it would be the last outpost north for its transmission.

    There was a cool exhibit about who had it and who survived - they kept records. They lost something like 70% of the tiny village, but there were entire lineages preserved, almost always going through a mother. So the father would die but if the mother didn’t, in many cases her kids didn’t either.

    They didn’t make any science of it, but the correlation was obvious, and pretty interesting.

    Source: Eyam Museum, Yorkshire

    eMouse2k A:

    The answer to this question is no. If you are exposed to a disease and survive due to gaining immunity from exposure to the disease, it’s similar to having a scar. Your body has healed and has some memory or memento of the disease, but the change is not going to be passed on to your children.*

    If you have some sort of inherited resistance that prevents the disease from infecting you, or is very good at fighting the disease, that can be passed on to your child, but it’s not something you gained from exposure to the disease. It’s something you always had and made it more likely for you and your child to survive the outbreak.

    *This ‘memory’ of the disease is a feature of our immune system that we exploit with the use of vaccines. We expose ourselves to weak versions of a dangerous disease to train our bodies to recognize the threat. If the resulting immunity was inheritable, there would be no need to vaccinate subsequent generations.
