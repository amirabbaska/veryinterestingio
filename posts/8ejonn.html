    Q:

    ELI5: During CPR why do chest compressions have to be between 100-120 when the average pulse is around 60-80?

    MavEtJu A:

    Chest compressions don't have the same efficiency as a heart beat. So to get the proper amount of blood flowing through the body a rate of about 100-120 is needed.

    scott-a1 A:

    Your heart rate at rest can be 60 to 80 bpm because that rate (along with your respiratory rate) is sufficient to provide adequate oxygen delivery to your tissue.

    Compressions during CPR are better than nothing, but they aren't as efficient as a heart beating on it's own, so you have to go faster to compensate.

    turnt_up2_11 A:

    TL;DR: Survivability in cardiac arrest requires generation of adequate flow- and even with good CPR this is low, mostly because each compression’s flow is lower than what the heart could do. To compensate we increase the rate a bit.

    But, I like to think of true explanation to a 5 year old as inevitably responding to a string of “why?” Questions. - so here goes...

    Cardiac output (CO) is what your organs (brain, kidneys etc) need to function. Think of it like flow rate (measured in L/min), It is a function of stroke volume (SV) - or blood pumped per beat - and Heart Rate (HR, beats per minute) or otherwise CO = SV x HR. Using average (at rest) values 75mls per beat and 70bpm this is about 5 liters per minute

    In CPR and in actual physiology, SV isn’t widely variable- the heart doesn’t change size beat to beat (though it can over time) but HR is. In fact increased HR (via adrenaline system) is the most immediate way a live person’s body regulates cardiac output.

    However, in CPR (by definition a dead person) The heart is not beating, so SV is zero. The purpose of compressions is to turn the chest cavity into a bellows system (not really “pumping the heart,” the non-beating heart is little more than a reservoir at this point.) Each compression pumps blood out and recoil of the chest wall creates negative pressure to draw blood into the chest cavity. The stroke volume of a compression is much less than that of the heart (‘less efficient’ as written by everyone above) - maybe 30-40 mls. Doing the math it is easy to see that a higher rate is needed to get minimal cardiac output.

    This same concept explains low resting heart rates in athletes. Their conditioned heart is more efficient, putting out maybe up to 90mls per beat, so they can afford a lower rate

    So why not compress faster? Well, given the basic equation this sounds better- but with each increase in rate there is a decrease in SV - this happens because there is less time between compression/beats to “fill”. There is a “break-even” point, and for chest compressions this is about 120 bpm. Faster than that and cardiac output starts going down due to decreased SV.

    There are more factors to consider that are just outside the context of the question asked- like resistance in the system (dilation and construction of blood vessels, the main reason we currently give epinephrine in cardiac arrest), ventilations (oxygenation, acid/base balance, intrathoracic pressure) etc, etc.

    Source: current physician, former paramedic, former CPR/ACLS instructor.
