    Q:

    I've been having a hard time finding good explanations online. Does the heart "micro-tear" during aerobic or anaerobic work the same way skeletal muscle does during weight training?

    I've always learned that the heart does not grow because an enlarged heart is a terrible thing. So how exactly does a heart "get stronger" from cardio work? Is it perhaps the whole pulmonary system that changes and not just the heart?

    mcaic A:

    The heart is able to produce new myocardial cells only up to a few days post partum. Cardiac growth in adults happens due to enlargement of existing cardiac cells and to some extent hyperplasia of non-muscle cells (connective tissue cells). The first measurable change in a cardiac cell under increased workload is the increase in mRNA coding for structural proteins. The cells also increase their mitochondrial mass. These two mechanisms only happen during the development of hypertrophy and are normalized when hypertrophy fully develops. Only permanent changes in the cardiac cells are the increase in cell volume (width and length) and rearrangement of cellular structural proteins (myofibrillar changes). The exact mechanism of cardiac cell growth also depends on the type of stimulus. When the primary stimulus to hypertrophy is a sudden increase in systolic pressure the main mechanism is the parallel replication of sarcomeres (proteins responsible for contraction). There are also a couple of nonmechanical stimuli that promote cardiac hypertrophy: the renin-angiotensin system and something that is probably more important in cardio exercise: circulating cathecholamines (epinephrine, norepinephrine).

    amch2559 A:

    The heart does grow in size but it's from bad health. When you do cardio, the fact that you increase your heart rate (to pump adequate blood/oxygen to your muscles) means your heart muscles expand and contract more effectively. After consistent cardio training, your heart will be able to expand more, and contract stronger. They become more flexible as well as stronger
