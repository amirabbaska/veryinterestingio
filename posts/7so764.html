    Q:

    Is there a technical distinction between a jungle and a forest?

    highflyingcircus A:

    Jungle is more of a literary term than a scientific one. When talking about wooded biomes, most ecologists use a combination of climatic and vegetative descriptors. Depending on the classification system, there are tropical rainforests (which you might think of as jungle), temperate rainforests, deciduous forests, boreal forests, mangrove forests, and probably a few other minor ones. The wikipedia page for Biome actually has a really good overview of the different classification systems. So a forest would be any wooded area, and a tropical rainforest would be a forest that grows in the tropics, has lots of precipitation, and dense vegetation. I suppose that the reason we don't use jungle so much anymore is because it doesn't differentiate well between different tropical forests, and it has different connotations such as "uncivilized" or just "dense vegetation." Tropical rainforest is more precise.

    Could argue that jungle is just a regional colloquialism for forest without scientific distinction. Comes from Sanskrit basically for uncultivated land so became popular word for forest in the Indian subcontinent, where forests tend to be tropical and wet. Use of the word became more prevalent globally in the 17th century with popularization of colonial authors like Kipling and Forster.
