  • Biology How do most wild animals die?

    NEW
    Q:

    Setting aside insects and microscopic organisms, how does the average, say, gazelle die? Killed by a predator? Disease (what kind of diseases, cancer?)? Accident? Or something else?

    Smitebugee A:

    Finding all-cause mortality on populations of animals is difficult to find at best, so take this study i found on white tailed deer with a grain of salt.

    In order of mortality causes (From the table on page 5);

    Natural causes: 38.3% (Includes deaths attributed to disease, malnutrition, flooding, fire, birthing issues and about 2 dozen other health defects that result in chronic illness or death)

    Predators: 17%

    vehicles: 14%

    Hunting: 10%

    Farm machinery: 6.4%

    Bizarre accidents: 4.3% (notably a deer that fell down a well and subsequently died)

    Poaching: 2.1%

    ~19%: unknown as deer collars were removed or foul play/poaching was suspected.

    Granted this was over a period of just 34 weeks to the exact figures might change over a longer observation period.

    From this you could infer that without human interaction most deaths would likely be natural causes from chronic health issues and predation, though i would not be confident enough to state the mortality rates would be similar between species and geographic locations.

    alanmagid A:

    Most mammals age out due to tooth wear or fracture or infection. This reduces nutrition leading to a loss of conditioning, resistance to infection, parasites, autoimmune, likelihood of predation, accidental injury, etc.

    7LeagueBoots A:

    In a natural setting it's generally due to natural causes, those being injury/accidents, disease, or predation (also starvation). Few animals (or plants for that matter) die of old age.
