  • Why do bubbles in liquid stay seperated?

    NEW
    Q:

    like in soft drinks, the bubbles dont merge even though they are right next to eachother

    WinterCharm A:

    water gets turned a specific way when dealing with gas on the inside of the bubble.

    This creates surface tension.

    To merge, the bubbles must overcome surface tension, but merely being near one another doesn't provide enough energy for that to happen. If bubbles collide, and they are big enough, then the surface tension is weak enough that it can be overcome.

    BlueKnightBrownHorse A:

    When thinking about boundaries between two materials, you have to think about cohesion and adhesion.

    Cohesion is a fancy word for sticking to each other. So water molecules want to stick next to other water molecules, and air wants to stick to air.

    Adhesion is a fancy word for sticking to each other. So air molecules also want to stick next to water molecules.

    The strongest force by far is water wanting to stick next to water, though, so big bubbles tend to split up. If you think of a big bubble and then cut it in half, you've added lots of space where water can hang out next to water. It's called surface tension. Bugs can also walk on water and upside down on the ceiling due to similar forces.

    TrippyTriangle A:

    After you let a soft drink settle, bubbles remain at the edge of the glass. The surface areas of the side bubbles are larger compared to the spherical ones at the center of the glass. The larger surface area stops them from merging together as easily, thus they are stable.
