    Because historically as a country/society we decided that non-hard alcohol is neither a food (which would require at least a calorie listing) nor a drug (which would require a dosage listing).

    YBn0rma1 A:

    Alcoholic beverages are regulated by the BATFE (Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives) The BATFE does not require nutritional information or ingredients be placed on packaging of products that they regulate. However non-alcoholic beverages and other food products are all regulated by a different part of the US government called the FDA (Food and drug administration) and products regulated by them must adhere to a different set of rules like posting ingredients and nutritional information on the packaging.

    Edit: The BATFE is a real and powerful part of the US government. You can learn more about who they are and what they do here link to their website

    Bongopalms A:

    This provides a bit of historical context involving one of my brewing heros, Bert Grant

    Grant also took on the Federal Government. As he —and we fellow maltworms— knew, beer is liquid bread. So, he asked, in 1992, why shouldn't there be nutritional information on beer labels? In answer, he put nutritional specifications on six-pack carriers of his Scottish Ale.

    Unfortunately, the government didn't agree with his assessment.

    The Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, and Firearms (BATF) determined that putting nutritional information on a beer label might confuse consumers into thinking that beer actually had nutritional value, just as, say, a Twinkie might, which is required to have a nutritional label. Specifically: "any reference to vitamin content in the advertising of malt beverages would mislead a substantial number of persons to believe that consumption of the product would produce curative or therapeutic effects." The Bureau ordered him to cease and desist forthwith.
