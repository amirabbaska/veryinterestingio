  • Physics In a nuclear weapon, how do the explosive lenses create a supercritical mass out of a subcritical mass, when the actual mass of the fissile material remains the same?

    NEW
    Q:

    Say you start with 3kg of plutonium in a hollow sphere shape, prior to detonation. After the explosive lenses are detonated, you (very briefly) have a solid sphere of plutonium created by the explosion. However the mass of the plutonium is still 3kg - exactly the same as the original mass. So I guess my real question is this: is the term "critical mass" a misnomer? Should it be instead, "critical density", or "critical mass per unit volume"?

    NeonAardvark A:

    Both the first US (Trinity) and first Soviet (RDS-1) tests successfully used solid plutonium cores - both density and shape are important, along with a neutron source to initiate a greater chain reaction in the brief window before the "critical mass" blows itself apart. So, yes it's a bit of a misnomer when applied to nuclear weapons.
