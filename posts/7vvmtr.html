    Q:

    The way I understand it, the main challenge to sustainable nuclear fusion is the confinement of plasma within the reactor. The approach to doing this currently is to use fixed magnetic fields to confine the plasma into a certain shape (for example: toroid in a tokamak reactor). The issue is that sometimes, plasma still manages to escape this shape, and therefore fusion is broken. Here's my question, wouldn't it make more sense to use deep learning technology to train a program to monitor the plasma inside the reactor at different points and then alter the strength of the magnetic fields being emitted accordingly so that it results in the plasma staying in the shape we want for sustained fusion?

    TheNuclearOption A:

    I don't think there's a way of quickly and robustly shaping the magnetic field with a tokamak's kind of magnet to do what your proposing. You'd presumably need something with a load of smaller magnets, and then you'd have very little field strength for containment.

    I'm also not sure you could design a circuit (i.e. sensor, computer to invert image, computer to process how to respond, circuitry to increase the current to a magnet) that could respond quick enough to prevent all the significant turbulence, even with a really fast computer. But I'm also not sure, while I'm sure you could do something to help mitigate the lower frequency oscillations if you had free control over the magnetic field shape.

    totes_original_uname A:

    Particularly for nonlinear plasma disruptions in fusion experiments, machine learning may be practical for "predicting" or detecting when a disruption is about to happen and providing feedback control to avoid. I believe as early as the 90s, neural networks have been used to predict disruptions on tokamaks. Machine learning is also increasingly being used for Design of Experiment and data analysis. It will almost definitely play a major role in fusion research going forward, but there are still many challenges.

    EDIT (sources): Kotschenreuther, M., et. al., Novel Computational Techniques to Predict Transport in Confinement Devices, and Applications to Ion Temperature Gradient Driven Turbulence, in Plasma Physics and Controlled Thermonuclear Fusion Research, (IAEA, Vienna, 1991) vol. 2, p. 361.
