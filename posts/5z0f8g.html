  • Biology If pain is the human body's way of preventing further damage, why does scratching get interpreted as a 'good' sensation, when it is destroying cells?

    NEW
    Q:

    ELI5: If pain is the human body's way of preventing further damage, why does scratching get interpreted as a 'good' sensation, when it is destroying cells?

    CigarIsJustACigar A:

    Two things:

    First, evolutionary psychologists think of pain as having two functions.

    1. Preventing immediate damage. If you keep walking on your broken leg, you will do more permanent damage to it. Pain discourages you from moving now.

    2. Teaching. Some painful events do not prevent further damage, but teach you to avoid similar situations in the future. Gastrointestinal pain does not help you avoid immediate damage (for the most part), but the memory of the pain will stop you from eating that poisonous mushroom again.

    So, contrary to how the question is formulated, the function of pain is not to indicate damage.

    Second, "damage" here needs to be understood evolutionarily. Cheesecake probably does the average overweight person damage to their body, but it is extremely pleasurable.

    "Damage" to the body are those changes to the body that would've made that conspecific (specific individual organism) less fit in its environment of evolutionary adaptiveness (the environment in which it evolved its current form). Scratching off dead skin cells wouldn't have made us less fit, whereas breaking an arm and moving it before it healed would have. So scratching isn't painful, just like cheesecake isn't painful, whereas moving a broken arm is painful.

    Itching may promote scratching and scratching may sometimes be unhealthy. But itching also promotes awareness and the knowledge that attention to a problem is warranted.

    Sometimes the itching tells us to remove or otherwise react to an irritation such as a bug or thorn. Swatting or scratching off the irritant could suffice.

    Although sometimes our scratching causes damage, we are better off with an itch reaction than without.
