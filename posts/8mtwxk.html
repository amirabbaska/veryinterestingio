  • Physics Is there a limit to how strong magnets can be? Or can we make stronger and stronger magnets with technological development/resources?

    NEW
    Q:

    If there is a limit, what sets it? If not, what is needed for a stronger magnet?

    mfb- A:

    For permanent magnets: The magnetization comes from the orientation of electron spins and/or orbitals, and you can't have more than all of them aligned. In practice you don't even achieve that without an external field. That limits the field strength to roughly 1.5 Tesla. A bit more might be possible but not much.

    Superconducting electromagnets can reach higher fields, but there is a material-dependent limit, about 30 T with practically useful superconductors, but ~100-200 might be possible with some high temperature superconductors (HTS). HTS are not well understood, so who knows how high that can go. Actual magnets are unlikely to reach this theoretical upper limit - to make an electromagnet out of it you need some current flow, and current flow reduces the maximal magnetic field.

    Regular conductors reach higher fields. Unfortunately they heat up, so you are limited by the cooling system for long-term magnetic fields, and by the current you can push through before you have to stop or before the conductor evaporates for shorter magnetic fields. 45 T for a long time, 100 T for a short time, 9000 T destroying the coils.

    myotherpassword A:

    For non-terrestrial magnetic fields, the highest fields are estimated to be created by fast rotating neutron stars. In these systems, the field strength can get up to ~10^{11} Tesla (or 10^{15} Gauss). The means by which this happens is only theorized, and is speculated to exist based on the high energy emissions we see in x-ray and gamma-ray telescopes. The mechanism that forms these fields isn't certain, and an interesting hypothesis is that the flux flow is left over convection currents from inside the stars that eventually became neutron stars. You can read a technical summary on the topic here.

    Pharisaeus A:

    If you mean man-made magnets, then it's possible to create some really powerful electromagnets using superconductors (15-30T). They're limited by the fact that they need special conditions to be superconductive. See https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superconducting_magnet#Materials

    CERN uses such magnets in the LHC to bend the path of accelerated particles.
