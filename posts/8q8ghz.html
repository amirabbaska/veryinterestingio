  • Chemistry My toothpaste contains something called "liquid calcium". What is liquid calcium?

    NEW
    Q:

    From my limited chemistry knowledge, I think its pretty obvious that calcium does not exist as a liquid in standard conditions.

    I'm guessing that its simply dissolved calcium, which I think is a good thing to have in toothpaste.

    The sperg in me finds it perplexing that this toothpaste brand has "NEW FORMULA CONTAINS FLUORINE AND LIQUID CALCIUM" as the main selling point. I keep picturing some dude brushing his teeth with molten hot calcium.

    Of course I could be wrong, just like science intended.

    Edit: If anyone was wondering, the brand of toothpaste was Colgate. Not sure if it's owned by Arms & Hammer or if they're committing trademark infringement, either one is good enough for me I guess. Thanks for the answers guys.

    -popgoes A:

    Liquid Calcium is a trademark by Arms & Hammer. They replaced it with "Active Calcium" after the exact kind of argument you're making here. Their page about it is here. It is a company word, with no real scientific resemblance to liquid calcium, which only exists above 842 degrees celcius...

    Here is a fun article about the labeling of "Liquid Calcium" in toothpaste. It goes into more detail, and I think is exactly what you're looking for.
