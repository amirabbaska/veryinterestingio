    Is there any correlation or causation between the level of education or occupation of a person to diseases such as alzheimers, dementia or other mental health problems?

    To clarify, dementia (age-related) and Alzheimer's disease are neurodegenerative disorders.

    They have symptoms that impact mental functioning, but they are not normally classified as mental health conditions such as depression, bipolar disorder or schizophrenia.

    Tounsk A:

    Higher education is a known protective factor for dementias such as Alzheimer's. People with a higher level of education are thought to have more "cognitive reserve" and are less likely to develop dementia or develop it later on.

    1) https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed?term=8139057 (Influence of education and occupation on the incidence of Alzheimer's disease.)

    2) https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed?term=9362989

    3) https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed?term=22626851

    holtzermann17 A:

    Not a direct answer, but a hypothesis:

    Overworked and isolated - work pressure fuels mental illness in academia from The Guardian, 2014.

    The generalisation would be that education creates opportunities for people to get into stressful job situations, like in the comment from /u/Qui_est-ce_qui about lawyers. In this case, it's not the education that's the proximate cause, but it's a catalyst and correlate.

    Of course, you could also find lots of anxiety and depression and so on among people who aren't well-educated -- in particular they may be prone to another kind of deadly stress if they have trouble finding a job.

    An interesting research question would be whether the mental illnesses of the educated and un-educated are different, not just whether they're more prevalent in one group than another. I don't know the answer to that question off hand.

    Another thing that comes to mind is the role of inequality per se in driving mental health problems, along the lines of:

      The Spirit Level: Why More Equal Societies Almost Always Do Better
