    Q:

    Why do you see so many specialty toothpastes (e.g. gum strength, cavity defense, 3-d white etc)? Why don't they make one super toothpaste with all the benefits?

    DaveOrilian8 A:

    As a dentist I was able to ask this exact question to a rep from GSK (Oral B, sensodyne etc). They explained in some cases it's due to ingredients affecting the bioavailability of the active ingredient, particularly in toothpastes containing Stannous Fluoride or potassium nitrate (sensitive tp's).

    However he admitted it's largely due to marketing- if 1 company combined everything into 1 toothpaste others wouldn't; and think of how much shelf space in a store 1 tube takes compared to 20 which would affect sales

    edsmedia A:

    As one of the other commenters noted, this is a marketing tactic, not a scientific one. The micro-segmentation of products within a category can accomplish two important goals for a company:

    1. It can allow more potential customers to "see themselves" in your product. If you offer just "toothpaste", you will likely lose market share to a company that offers a "enamel replacement toothpaste" and a "3-d whitening toothpaste." That's because customers are known to value specific benefits over general ones [1]. As to your specific question--trying to pile all the benefits into one super toothpaste means that some of the benefits will be lost in the long list. Customers typically pay attention to only the top two or three benefits of a product [2].

    2. It allows the manufacturer to justify (asking for) more shelf space at retail, increasing their "share of shelf" relative to the competition. Shelf-space allocation is a complex topic, because it involves explicit and implicit (ie, retail margin) payments, promotional activity, and package design, as well as product count--but in general, more variety of product types will help. (As long as they're all selling).

    Mitigating against the proliferation of product variants are supply-chain considerations -- how many variants need to be produced and managed, how much inventory needs to be available, what raw materials are uniquely necessary for each variant vs shared among them, etc. This is where modern supply-chain technology and approaches help a lot, thus enabling the explosion of product variants we've seen in many consumer-packaged-goods categories over the years.

    REFERENCES

    [1] Albert Wenben Lai (1995) ,"Consumer Values, Product Benefits and Customer Value: a Consumption Behavior Approach", in NA - Advances in Consumer Research Volume 22, eds. Frank R. Kardes and Mita Sujan, Provo, UT : Association for Consumer Research, Pages: 381-388.

    [2] Ravindra Chitturi, Rajagopal Raghunathan, Vijay Mahajan (2008) Delight by Design: The Role of Hedonic Versus Utilitarian Benefits. Journal of Marketing: May 2008, Vol. 72, No. 3, pp. 48-63.

    ___prcat___ A:

    According to ADA there are different ingredients in toothpastes depending on the label

    • "Whitening" toothpastes contain more abrasive action to scrape away surface stains, as noted in the link, and/or contain peroxide to remove stains

    • "Sensitive" toothpastes contain ingredients to reduce tooth sensitivity

    • "Fluoride" toothpastes typically contain slightly higher concentrations of the active ingredient... 1400ppm-1450ppm, for example.

    Marketing, however, is a major answer to your question. The FDA only regulates the "anti-carries" action of toothpaste which is why you only see it under "Active Ingredients" on labeling and is some form of fluoride. All other claims are considered Cosmetics and regulated as such.
