    Q:

    ELI5: If the US believes in the separation of church and state, why does each political party reference 'God' so frequently?

    cdb03b A:

    Separation of Church and State means that the government cannot ban or restrict a religion, and they cannot require a religion (religious rule being set directly into law). It does not mean that they require the people in government to be secular/atheists. That would actually violate the first Amendment rights of those people.

    Over 70% of the people in the US are Christian, and another 5-10% hold other religions. This means that most government officials will be religious and use their religions to influence their morals and how they vote. It also means that most of the people that they represent have religious views that need to be represented to some extent in how the laws that govern the land are crafted. If you were to forbid all religious language from usage at the government level that would not be possible.

    So I think it is important to quote the actual constitution here, because I think most people believe the constitution says that there is a separation of church and state in the constitution which isn't accurate.

    Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof.

    Those people who are saying God so frequently are practicing their free exercise of religion. The US was set up in such a way that allows for anyone of any faith to practice their religion. Assuming that their religion doesn't violate some other law.

    This freedom of religion is countered by the establishment clause which says that the US can not establish a religion. Meaning that no one can make you go to church or tell you that you must follow X religion.

    I personally really like it. I think that religion is a great thing, and that people should give it a chance. If you don't like religion that is fine. I won't try to silence you if you won't try and silence me. Which I think is really the genius of the Constitution.
