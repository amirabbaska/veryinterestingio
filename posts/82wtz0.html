    Q:

    Does it also dilate time or work in the opposite way and speed it up?

    Midtek A:

    The energy in an electromagnetic field does contribute to the energy tensor which is the source of gravity. But the contribution is generally negligible, decreasing as 1/r^(2) (instead of 1/r like the mass distribution) and celestial bodies being more or less electrically neutral.

    destiny_functional A:

    The gravitational field and electromagnetic field are not really on the same footing here.

    The source of gravity is energy (the stress-energy tensor to be more accurate). Given a "stress-energy distribution" (which includes the mass distribution that is the source of the classical Newtonian gravitational field) the Einstein equation tells us the geometry of space time (it gives us the metric gÎ¼v which encodes the geometry, the way you measure lengths, angles, proper time along a worldline). In that curved spacetime you have effects like time dilation (you have them without gravity too, in flat Minkowski spacetime). So gravity affects a stopwatch we carry around. The electromagnetic field does not affect it directly (in the way that gravity in a way defines how much time passes), but it gravitates (ie causes some gravity) since it contributes to the stress energy tensor. So in that sense if you have something like a "kugelblitz" (the first example that comes to mind) it would contribute to the curvature of the region and consequently the gravity would affect clocks in the region.
