    annnnnnnnie A:

    Generally speaking, your brain grows with your skull and remains proportionate. So, a person with a larger skull would have a larger brain, putting them a equal risk for concussion as a smaller-skulled person. If someone had a brain that had gotten smaller for whatever reason, that might increase the severity of concussion with a head injury.

    EightEight16 A:

    Based on my technical training and experience, a thicker skull would not necessarily affect your chances of specifically a concussion, although I would expect it to have other complications with it.

    I’m assuming you’re asking about the space your brain has inside the skull, implying that a smaller space would mean less coup-contracoup movement and fewer concussions. If you had a large space, I would expect injuries involving the brain stem to be more common, since that would still be an area where the skull and brain would be in close proximity. Those are usually quite bad.

    On the other hand, the injuries I would expect to see if you had a smaller space between the meninges and the skull is an increased chance of cranial herniation, where the brain stem is pushed out of the foramen magnum. Bleeding in the brain of any kind would basically be a death sentence since it would have nowhere to go except down and out by your spinal cord, which creates the herniation.
