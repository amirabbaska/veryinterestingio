    Q:

    How is it that we aren't getting cancer after inhaling large volumes of helium gas to change our voice as kids?

    What makes an alpha particle highly ionizing but not helium gas?

    RobusEtCeleritas A:

    An alpha particle is a helium-4 nucleus resulting from the alpha decay of a radioactive nucleus. The typical amount of kinetic energy carried away by an alpha particle after an alpha decay is around 5 MeV. That's why alpha particles are ionizing radiation.

    Atoms in helium gas at STP have typical kinetic energies on the order of 0.025 eV, so they are not ionizing.

    rocketsocks A:

    They aren't the same thing any more than a beta ray is an electron. Alpha particles are made of Helium nuclei and beta rays are made of electrons, but there is a unique context for both: high kinetic energy.

    For the same reason a loose bullet (specifically without a casing and gunpowder) just sitting on a table is much less dangerous to you than a fired bullet traveling towards you at high speed with a lot of energy. The same reason a parked car is different than a speeding car at 200 mph.
