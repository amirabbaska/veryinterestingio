    qqkiu A:

    This same situation happens in Latin America, with São Paulo, Buenos Aires, Ciudad de México and Rio being larger than any European city. The reason is very well known : developed economies tend to create jobs and education oportunities across the whole country, while 3rd world countries having less resources are still concentrating the best infrastructure (electricity, good roads, plenty of clean water, hi-speed Internet, etc) and good educational institutions in their larger cities. Entrepreneurs also seek these advantages to their businesses, so a lot of country people migrates to big cities because there are far more jobs there. It's an old and very studied phenomenum, called "êxodo rural" (rural exodus) em Portuguese, for example. These countries struggle to fight it, since it's not good for a nation.

    TL;DR because Europe is more developed.

    kouhoutek A:

    It all depends how you count. While the political boundaries of London and Paris are around 8 million, the metropolitan areas are around 13 million.

    Apart from that, affluence plays a role. People in more developed can afford a better quality of life, which means more space and better services. They have the resources to spread out and travel to where the work that people in less developed countries do not.

    Historically, it has been about food. Rice produces more calories per acre than most pre-industrial crops, but also requires more manpower. This both allows and requires more people to live closely together, and has lead to cultures with greater tolerance of this.
