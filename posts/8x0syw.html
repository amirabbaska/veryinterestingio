    police-ical A:

    Not much. It reflects the presence of certain pigments from the chicken's diet. Beta-carotene is one of them, so an intensely yellow-orange yolk could potentially mean a bit more vitamin A, but most of the relevant compounds don't do anything for us besides make a more vibrantly colored omelette.

    EDIT: Non-peer-reviewed citations, https://www.chowhound.com/food-news/55099/does-the-color-of-an-egg-yolk-indicate-how-nutritious-it-is/ https://www.thekitchn.com/what-does-egg-yolk-color-actually-mean-ingredient-intelligence-215672

    em3am A:

    As eggs age,their proteins denature. A good measure (rather than color) of this is the shape or height of the yolk when the egg is out of the shell and laying on a dish or in a pan. The higher or rounder the yolk, the better condition of the proteins.

    nickb3535 A:

    The poultry industry actually dopes their chickens' diets with carotenoids (usually xanthophylls, think yellowish/orangish pigment in plants similar to the green chlorophyll) in order to make them more yellow. People tend to believe these artificially yellow eggs to be more healthy. I work in the ethanol industry and many of the back end functions of an ethanol plant are done in order to enrich the dry corn feed in lutein, zeaxanthin and other carotenoids in order to increase their value in the livestock industry. Otherwise they are extracted from marigold, which is a much more expensive, but efficient process.
