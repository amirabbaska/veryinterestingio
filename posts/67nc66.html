  • Physics The second law of thermodynamics seems to clash with the Stefan-Boltzman law in the following example. How can they be reconciled?

    NEW
    Q:

    The second law of thermodynamics tells us that thermal energy spontaneously flows from a hot reservoir to a cold reservoir. Thus, it follows that no set of lenses and mirrors built around the sun can heat the earth to a hotter temperature than TSun.

    On the other hand, the Stefan-Boltzmann law tells us that the power radiated from a back body is proportional to P~AT^(4).

    Now imagine you could engulf sun-earth a system of two black bodies with radii R1 and R2, where R1<<R2 with a parabolic mirror, such that the sun and the earth are in the focal points of the parabola. Let us furthermore assume that the larger black body is held at a constant temperature T1. Then, all the power radiated by either body will be focused on the other. Thus it follows that

    A1 T1^(4) = A2 T2^(4)

    Hence, since A1 < A2 it follows that T1 > T2.

    But that violates the second law of thermodynamics.

    Where did I go wrong?

    XiAxis A:

    A lot of people here seem to be missing the point and attempting to reconcile your example by pointing out flaws in the exact situation that you've considered. I imagine that what you're really trying to ask is "How is it that body A can heat body B to a temperature higher than its own temperature?"

    The problem is that the Stephen-Boltzman law is not exactly what you believe it to be. The Stephen Boltzman law states that the energy emitted/absorbed by a body is proportional to its surface area times the difference in temperature to the fourth power. This means that as the colder body heats up and the warmer body cools down, the temperature term approaches zero. So, as they reach the same temperature, the energy exchange becomes zero, thus they reach equilibrium at the same temperature and the second law of thermodynamics holds.

    EDIT: I'm getting a lot of responses pointing out that the Stephen-Boltzman law indeed states that the emitted radiation of a body is proportional to its absolute temperature to the fourth power. This is true, but you must consider that the body is also absorbing energy from its surroundings. This means that the net energy gain/loss is proportional to the difference of the objects temperature to the fourth power and the surrounding temperature to the fourth power. In the case that OP describes, the two bodies can be considered to be surrounding each other.

    To be clear: when I say "difference in temperature to the fourth power" I mean T1^4 - T2^4 , NOT (T1-T2)^4 . I was unclear in my initial response because the end result is the same: equilibrium when T1 = T2, so it was unimportant to stress the semantics.

    Kandiru A:

    The sun approximates a constant temperature in the real world, as it loses heat to space.

    In your example, you are supposing you can channel all the heat from the sun, to your planet.

    Now, the planet will warm up. The sun would also start warming up, since it's radiating a lot of heat, which can only go the planet, then back again, but you've artificially fixed it to have a constant temperature.

    Your essentially saying, your sun is producing power ~AT^4, but you've fixed the T, so you've just said your sun produces constant power. Your system consists of only one object which can heat up, the planet. So of course you'll get nonsense, since you've defined a closed system with constant power input, with only one object which can vary in temperature. Whatever magical device is keeping the sun cool, has to dump that heat somewhere, so it's dumped on your planet.

    [EDIT] As other people have said, the other problem with your supposition, is that you need to take the Area into account for absorbing the heat, as well as emitting it. The large sun will only have its energy focused down at the other focuii to the same area as the sun, while your planet is much smaller. This means the planet will only receive A2/A1 * the input energy. Image your idea without the planet, and just a spherical mirror. Now you have a sun outputting P power, but fixed at temperature T. Where does the heat go? Well, it obviously is going to your magical sun-constant-temperature machine...

    agate_ A:

    Parabolae are the wrong shape: there's no way to build a pair of parabolae that will focus all the power from one body onto another without intersecting each other.

    What you want is an ellipsoid, with the Sun and Earth at the two foci. But that won't work either. Since the Sun is a finite size, not all of its light will be projected to the opposite focus. Instead, the mirror will form an image of the sun that's about the same size as the Sun. Since the Earth is smaller, some of the light in that image will miss the Earth, bounce off the mirror again, and (probably) go back to strike the Sun.

    There ain't no such thing as a free lunch.
