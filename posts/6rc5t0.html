  • Physics Does a multi-decade concentration of Radon gas lead to an accumulation of lead particles in an enclosed environment (basement)?

    NEW
    Q:

    I was looking at the decay chain for Radon, and noticed that the first stable element in the chain is lead 210.

    So if a basement, for example, has high Radon levels for, say 100 years, would that create a higher than normal concentration of lead in that enclosed environment?

    undercoveryankee A:

    We're talking about concentrations that would be nearly undetectable if the substances weren't radioactive. Let's run some numbers.

    The highest recorded residential levels of radiation from radon and its decay products have been on the order of 100,000 becquerels of activity per cubic meter of air, where 1 Bq is defined as one atom decaying per second.

    For simplicity, we'll pretend that each atom is removed from the sample as soon as it decays to lead-210, and radon is added as necessary to keep the activity at 100k Bq/m^3.

    For one atom to go from radon-222 to lead-210 is a total of five decays: three alpha and two beta. Dividing our 100,000 decays per second by five decays per atom, we get 20,000 atoms of lead per cubic meter per second.

    Now, applying that rate to a 50-m^3 room for 100 years (3.154 x 10^9 seconds), we get 3.154 x 10^15 atoms of lead, with a total mass of about a microgram.

    Lt_Duckweed A:

    SO, for starters, 4pCi/L is equal to 0.148 decay events per second per liter.

    So that is 148 decays per second per cubic meter per second.

    Now lets go with your question in the op and ask, ignoring the half lives of the intervening steps on the decay chain, how much lead is produced per cubic meter of volume in a basement with this activity level over the course of 100 years?

    So, 148 decays per cubic meter per second, 148 decay/(m^3 x sec).

    148 decay/(m^3 x sec) x 60 sec/min x 60 min/hr x 24 hr/day x 365 day/yr x 100yr = 4.667x10^11 lead atoms per cubic meter of basement volume.

    4.667x10^11 lead atoms = 7.750x10^-13 moles of lead = 1.604x10^-10 grams of lead

    So. At 4pCi/L Radon activity for 100 years, you will be left with 0.1604 nanograms of lead per cubic meter. Or 0.1241 parts per trillion airborne lead contamination.

    According to the CDC 15 parts per billion is the max allowable in drinking water, so the basement air is around 10,000 times less contaminated that what is considered max safe to drink.
