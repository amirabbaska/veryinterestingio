    Q:

    I'll preface this by saying I don't know anything about light.

    When a lens focuses light into a smaller and smaller area, what happens to the light? If I took all the light from the sun and focused it to the same point in space, does the light collide, does it have density, is there a limit to how much light could be in that one spot?

    If I encoded information as light and passed it through a lens, and on the other side of the focal point, received that information back out.. Is there a limit to how much information I could pass in? Is it lossy at all?

    Thanks.

    DCarrier A:

    It's possible for photons to collide, but it's extremely rare unless they're high enough energy to make electron-positron pairs. The only limit to how much you can concentrate light is the point where it collapses into a black hole. The only limit to information is the Bekenstein bound, which is the amount of entropy a black hole of that radius.

    ribnag A:

    Others have given great "at the ultimate extremes" answer, but your question, as phrased, has a more practical answer...

    A lens can't focus light into a point hotter/brighter than the object generating the light.

    There are a variety of interesting ways to prove that, but the one that makes the most intuitive sense IMO is that lenses aren't one-way magic, they work in both directions. If you could focus the Sun (for example) in to a point hotter/brighter than the surface of the Sun, you would effectively be using your lens to heat up the Sun at that point instead of the other way around.

    nlgenesis A:

    Also, there is some other limit: the diffraction limit. Even with an infinite size laser beam (source) and infinitely strong lens (see other comments), and even with a infinitely weak source such that the electron-position-processes are not a problem (see other comments), then still you can not focus light into a focus smaller than the wavelength of the light.

    This is a very real limiting factor for computer chip producers. Computer chips are 'written' with light. To make the 20 nm chip structures in your processor (or however small they are now), you need light with a wavelength shorter than 20 nm, i.e., extreme ultraviolet (EUV). Producing and processing (focusing and such) this EUV is very difficult, and is an important limiting factor for reducing computer chips.
