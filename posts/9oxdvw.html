    Q:

    Since electricity flows on the surface of its conductor could metal tubing of equal surface area be substituted for solid core wire?

    tuctrohs A:

    The skin effect causes ac current to flow only on the surface, but the depth of that surface layer depends on frequency. It only gets small at high frequency. At 60 Hz, it's about a cm in copper. So it's only if you have giant busbar types of conductors that that becomes a significant effect.

    But in an electrical power substation or transmission line, where you do have giant conductors, they are sometimes hollow. And cables used for transmission lines usually have a steel core, mostly for strength, but also because the low conductivity of steel doesn't matter much for the core where not much current is flowing anyway.

    HypergolicHyperbola A:

    Yes! In the electric utility industry we use hollow aluminum tubes for high-voltage bus inside substations and receiving stations. It is impractical in small diameter because tubing wants to crimp or collapse when bent around a curve. However, it works great for large diameter, straight, rigid bus work.

    prosper_0 A:

    More commonly, multiple conductors are used. Look closely at large power pylons - many of them have several bundled conductors. Once you get more than about 1" in diameter (at 60Hz), your best option is to use another conductor. Way easier than trying to make a large hollow one (in most cases)

    For high frequency AC, litz wire is the same idea - multiple parallel conductors, all insulated from each other. again, way more practical than using a tube in most cases
