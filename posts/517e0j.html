    Q:

    ELI5: If smell is based off your nose reacting to particles, how do predators like sharks smell, for example, "a single drop of blood from over two miles away"?

    SmokierTrout A:

    The exact quote is a bit of a myth. Sharks can detect a blood at levels as low as one part per million. Say you have a two containers of 1m^3 of water (1,000 litres or 220 gallons), and with 20^* drops (1ml) of blood added to one of them -- then shark would be able to tell the difference.

    Not the same as being able to detect blood from 2 miles away, but still impressive.

    ^* edit: turns out a drop is a defined unit. It's defined as 0.05ml (0.01 of a teaspoon).

    bremidon A:

    Each drop of blood contains roughly 5 million blood cells. Depending on the sensitivity of the predator's olfactory senses, only a few would be needed to trigger the "smell" of blood.

    Things get even wilder if we consider single molecules contained in the drop of blood. If we limit ourselves to hemoglobin, then a single blood cell has around 300 million molecules that could theoretically trigger a smell response.

    So that drop of blood contains 5 million x 300 million potential triggers. Written out, that's 1,500,000,000,000,000 molecules. That's a lot of particles.

    Of course, as this drop spreads out, it gets diluted. The reason that sharks can still sense the presence of blood after several miles is that they are just that sensitive.

    And as others have pointed out: this does not happen instantaneously. In fact, if water were perfectly still, it would take a long long long time for the blood to actually reach a shark 2 miles away. (just a quarter mile would take millions of years).

    But this is obviously not happening. The reason is that currents pick up the blood and transport it much further, much faster.

    I would also hazard a guess that the currents also prevent the blood from diluting quite as much as you would expect, while also ensuring that only certain directions are good for hunting.

    Edit: Oh my, how we can get carried away. It's 300 million hemoglobin molecules per cell. I have changed the numbers appropriately.

    garycarroll A:

    The key is to realize that for blood in the water to travel two miles does not require that the blood spread out in a 2 mile radius. Simplest example is a wounded seal is swimming along leaving a blood trail, which you can see might persist for a while, and might be two miles long. Another example is something sitting still on the surface (or moving with the wind in a different direction than the water current) and bleeding, with blood being carried along by a current below the surface. This is how a chum line works; the boat stops, and you begin to meter blood and pulverized fish into the water. It is carried in a line away from the boat. The fish you are seeking crosses the line and based on which way the water is moving follows the line to the boat.

    TLDR: the line of blood in the water may be miles long, but as a line is not as huge in area as you might think so the blood is less diluted.
