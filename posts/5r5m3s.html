    stawek A:

    It is normal in many languages to allow different spelling and pronounciation of names, be it people names or geographical.

    In this case it's just a matter of old saxon roots of those names and the "cester" being pronounced as "ster".

    There are plenty of towns in the are that follow this rule, like Towcester (pronounced toaster).

    ldn6 A:

    There's no easy answer to this question because many place names have unique etymologies for a variety of reasons, but the two first ones are the result of assimilation processes in which the unstressed -ces- syllable merges into the broader word. I'm actually not quite sure where many Americans (and I only say thus because I've heard "Leichester Square" on many a tube ride from confused tourists) got the idea that there's a "ch" sound there.
