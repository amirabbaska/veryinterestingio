  • why does 72 degrees in your house feel diiferent depending on outside temperature?

    NEW
    Q:

    For example, my house is set to 72 and the room with the thermostat reads 72. The thing is, 72 was hot yesterday to my wife and I and was cold last night at the same temperature.

    remscheld A:

    One thing not mentioned yet is that your house may not the same temperature in every room. Depending on a variety of factors, you may have significant differences in temperature and humidity in different rooms and especially on different floors. Your thermostat in the first floor living room may say 72, but that doesn’t mean the bedroom on the second floor is 72.

    michilio A:

    Temperature is not strictly the only thing that influences your sense of comfort.

    Outside things like direct sun, wind and rain influence your feeling.

    Inside it's mostly a question of air pressure and humidity, since you're mostly static. Airflow also impact the feeling, and so does radiation of surfaces. Not all of these can be controlled sadly.

    Very very very dumbed down: If it's colder outside your house loses radiation to the outside world, and that's noticable inside. Some heaters only heat the air inside, but the surfaces (walls, ceilings and floors) won't radiate heat, so even when the air temperature is constant it doesn't lean you'll achieve the same level of comfort.

    That's why a well insulated house with low temperature floor heating is way more pleasurable than a non-insulated house with space heaters cranked to full.

    goatymcgoatfacesings A:

    There are three methods of heat transfer: 1) conduction - stationary things you’re in contact with transferring heat. 2) convection - moving things you’re in contact with. Think running water or a cool breeze. 3) radiation - things you’re not touching sending heat in all directions and some of it hits you. If you remember those old bar style heaters, that’s a good example.

    Radiating heat is why sitting in direct sunlight is so effective for warming you up and why closing curtains will immediately help a chill from a closed window on a cold night. It’s also why a car on a hot day feels oppressively warm the very second you turn the AC off.

    The short version is that if the walls of your house are cold, you need more heat through conduction (air temperature) and convection (warm vent facing you) to compensate for the smaller amount of heat received through radiation from your walls.

    Just to throw a bit of science into it, conduction and convection heat transfer is proportional to the difference in temperature, but radiation is proportional to temperature to the power of 4.
