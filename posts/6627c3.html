    ron_leflore A:

    LIGO is unique as an observatory in that you cannot just build one. You need to build at least two a long way (1000's of km) apart.

    So nuclear bomb, truck driving by, and all those things will affect one detector in a very different way then the other detector.

    Some type of astronomical event, however, should affect the two detectors at similar times and in similar ways.

    More here: https://www.ligo.caltech.edu/page/ligo-detectors

    MaximilianArena A:

    As the above comments have stated, there are ways to distinguish the signals you are not looking for (noise) from those you are indeed looking for. Using general relativity and physical models, physicists were able to predict exactly what the signal(s) of passing gravitational waves emitted by two merging black holes was going to look like. This is why two detectors were constructed hundreds of miles apart from each other. Signals that show up on one detector (noise generated from a passing car for example) but do not show up on the other detector, unless extreme coincidence is at play, are treated as noise because any passing gravitational waves will influence both detectors in a similar fashion, at almost the exact same time, depending on which direction the gravitational waves propagate from.

    Combine the methods of discriminating against noise and actually having algorithms that know what a black hole "blip" is supposed to look like makes mundane things such as passing cars a non-issue when it comes to false-positives.
