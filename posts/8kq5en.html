    Rev_JulesWinnfield A:

    Most of the answers here are focused on the truth of your premise (i.e., that bees die when they use their stingers), but your real question is about whether organisms that are designed by natural selection to die when they complete some task (usually reproduction, like salmon after spawning) are aware of their inevitable deaths. The answer is almost certainly no. The mental models of these organisms do not need to include predictions of what happens after accomplishing these tasks because the forces of natural selection cease with the closure of the reproductive window (because salmon do not exhibit parental investment, genetic fitness becomes decoupled from survival after making babies). In other words, there is no selection pressure for such an awareness to be encoded in DNA through natural selection.

    This explanation gets really messy when the discussion focuses on organisms with more complex mental models that are sensitive to experience (e.g., humans) and reproductive windows that extend into old age (e.g., grand-parenting), but it is sufficient for explaining why mental models constructed by natural selection (i.e., "instincts") for organisms with limited reproductive windows might exclude mortality predictions. Its definitely an interesting topic, a buddy of mine from Toluca Lake studies it professionally.

    Bounds_On_Decay A:

    Bees are sterile. They don't mind dying, because the only "survival instinct" that they would reasonably evolve is for the survival of the hive.

    A bee has no desire to live, because thousands of clones are born every day. The bee will never pass on DNA, but if it can protect the hive then the Queen can create more clones so the DNA will spread. The bee only desires to serve the hive.

    From an evolutionary perspective, a bee hive is one giant organism spread out over thousands of disconnected bodies. A bee dying when it stings is like you cutting your hand when you punch someone. It might hurt a bit but it'll heal.

    JonaJonaL A:

    Considering that bee intelligence is collective rather than individual and how limited one such individuals mental capacity is due to the amount of neurons available, it is fair to assume that a bee doesn't actually "know" anything. It's a biological machine running on fairly basic software called instincts. Posessing self awareness would be a very detrimental quality for a worker or soldier bee to possess.
