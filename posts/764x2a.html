    Q:

    What exactly makes aged wine better?

    bencvm A:

    The main chemical reaction is the polymerization of smaller tannin units into chains and sheets.

    The smaller tannin subunits taste bitter and are astringent. Astringency refers to the mouth-drying sensation resulting from the tannins binding to proteins in the saliva.

    As a result, the larger tannin polymers have fewer functional groups, and the wine is less bitter and smoother.

    There are other reactions that can occur, including the hydrolysis of aroma molecules that are bound to sugar molecules, "freeing up" more complex aromas as well. This can occur in some whites. The tannin chemistry is more important in reds.

    JustAGuyFromGermany A:

    I recommend caution on topics like these. While there are plenty of interesting chemical reactions going on in wine, very little can be said as to whether they actually make wine "better". Remember all the studies that show that even wine experts cannot distinguish cheap and expensive wines under controlled conditions (in particular: when they are blinded). Granted, strictly speaking, this only shows that quality does not correlate well to price, but taken in a broader context of human psychology it also indicates that much of what makes wines "special"/"high quality"/... happens more in the mind of the drinker than in the wine itself.

    I don't deny there is plenty of quite interesting chemistry going on, but it is debatable whether it is actually the chemistry that makes a wine significantly "better" (as opposed to psychological and social factors which might have the much greater impact on the perceived quality of the drink).

    tminus7700 A:

    After the bottle is opened, evaporation of some of the volatiles has the biggest effect on taste. It used to be thought that letting wine "breath" meant mostly oxidation of some of the components. But recent research shows it is more the evaporation of some volatiles that has the biggest effect.

    Wine is made up of hundreds of compounds, and with aeration, usually the volatile undesirable compounds will evaporate faster than the desirable, aromatic and flavorful ones.
