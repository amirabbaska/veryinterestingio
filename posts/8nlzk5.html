    Q:

    Why are there so many research stations on the Antartica and what are we actually researching?

    silence7 A:

    You can get a lot of climate history from ice cores, and the dynamics of ice sheets are important for estimating future sea level rise in response to warming.

    The waters around Antarctica have unique biology.

    There is uniquely non-turbulent clear air for astronomical purposes.

    You can detect neutrinos with huge blocks of ice.

    Also helps that it's a big chunk of land which is currently outside of national boundaries, and part of the goal is to stake a claim to part of it if that should ever change.

    chonginbare A:

    Antarctica is important in the field of meteoritics.

    Meteorites fall all over the planet in roughly similar rates, but the flow of ice in some regions of Antarctica acts to concentrate meteorites and push them to the surface.

    This is also helped by the fact that the (mostly dark) meteorites often stand out against the white snow.

    There are multiple trips each year where planetary scientists will go and 'farm' the meteorites from these areas.

    ANSMET is one organisation involved in their collection

    https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ANSMET https://curator.jsc.nasa.gov/antmet/
