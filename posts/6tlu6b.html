    xyanon36 A:

    Imagine you had a bowl of skittles, these represent all the different receptors in your brain. And imagine each piece of seroquel attached to one of those skittles.

    All the red skittles are receptors associated with sleep. With a dose between 12.5 and 100mg, you would have enough seroquel to attach itself to all your red skittles. And the seroquel will always attach to the red skittles first. But what happens if you take more? Can you get more sleepy? No, because you have no more red skittles available for seroquel to attach to. Your green skittles and yellow ones and green ones do different things, so introducing a larger dose of seroquel will start to have different affects.

    Rockstar1995 A:

    Many anti-psychotic drugs are dopamine antagonists, so they basically block the flow of dopamine, resulting in a sedating effect. Now, in people with schizophrenia the flow of dopamine is higher than a person without schizophrenia, resulting in paranoia, rage, psychosis, etc. So, naturally a dopamine antagonist would balance out the dopamine levels in people with schizophrenia.

    jvictormm A:

    Quetiapine is a dopamine antagonist, but it also have potent antihistaminic and anticholinergic actions, which is why this medicine have sedative properties. Histamine and acetylcholine are involved in arousal pathways on the brain, so the antagonism of the muscarinic (M1) and histamine (H1) receptors on the brain are associated with the sedating effects.

    Source: Stahl's Essential Psychopharmacology, 4th ed

    Edit: I just read something interesting on that book!

    "Quetiapine is a very interesting agent, since it acts like several different drugs, depending upon the dose and the formulation. It comes in a immediate-release (IR) formulation and in an extended-release (XR) formulation. The IR has rapid onset and short duration of actions, usually taken at night because quetiapine is most sedating at its peak delivery shortly after taking it, due in large part to its antihistamine properties. The XR formulation is ideal for an antipsychotic, with less peak-dose sedation but duration of action lasting all day, however is not ideal for a hypnotic**, because the peak is much delayed from the time when the patient takes the pill, delaying sleep onset."

    **NOTE: "hypnotic" are drugs used to make you sleep

    About the dosage, the lowest doses act at the receptors that have the highest affinity for quetiapine, which is the histamine H1 receptor. So at a lower dose quetiapine will have a pronounced antihistaminic effect, which leads to a sedative hypnotic effects. At higher doses, quetiapine will also act on other receptors like the dopamine D2 receptores, causing a antipsychotic effect!

    4noop A:

    Antipsychotic medicine alter the levels of chemicals in the brain called neurotransmitters. They are the chemicals that carry messages around your brain. The neurotransmitter that is most targeted by antipsychotic medicines is dopamine. Changing the levels of these chemicals reduces, in almost all cases, the hallucinations and delusions of psychosis. In many instances, they also improve the mood and reduce anxiety.
