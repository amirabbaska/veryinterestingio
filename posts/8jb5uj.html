    Q:

    If my hands are very cold and I place them somewhere on my core the feeling of heat moving out of my body is far more significant than the feeling of heat entering my hands. I have to purposefully mentally flip a switch to feel the heat entering my hands over the sensation of it leaving my body. Is this just because the core has priority?

    CodeBrownPT A:

    Some interesting answers but none mention receptor quantity.

    We have some cells that pick up heat and some that pick up cold. These cells have one job and that's temperature. We happen to have more cells that pick up cold than we do warm and hot.

    Interestingly, at very hot or very cold temperatures, these cells no longer pick up the heat or cold. Instead, the same cells that pick up pain tell us that something is really hot or cold.

    FreeChair8 A:

    In addition to what our receptors are designed to sense, thermodynamics plays a role. When something warm touches something cold, the warmth wants to be equal in all of the things. The heat travels away from the warmth to the colder area until they are the same.

    When you feel cold air, it is actually the air molecules colliding with you and pulling away heat. When you touch a warm part of your body with a cold part, the heat is leaving the warm area towards equilibrium- namely, the average of the two temperatures. Your body will be cooling under it’s normal range while your hand or foot won’t reach its normal range because they are both going towards the average of the two temperatures.

    The coolest thing, pun not intended, is that there is no such thing as “cold”. There is only heat and less heat.
