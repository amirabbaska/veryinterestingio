    hva_vet A:

    If you smell "chlorine" in a pool then you should suspect the pool is becoming un-sanitized. In a balanced pool there should be little if any smell and the water should be sparkling blue. The smell people associate with chlorine is actually chloramine, which is chlorine bound with organic matter and when in high enough concentrations will also give the water a slight greenish tinge. There are three measurements for chlorine in a pool - Free Chlorine, Combined Chlorine, and Total Chlorine. Many pool owners only check for Total Chlorine and this is not an accurate measure of the sanitizer in the pool. Free Chlorine and Combined Chlorine make up the Total Chlorine. When free chlorine reacts with organic compounds it becomes bound with those and is Combined Chlorine. This is no longer an effective sanitizer since it's effectively "used up". The only effective sanitizer is the Free Chlorine in the pool. So, if you smell chloramine and the water is a little dingy looking, you might want to stay out. The only way to clear the chloramine is to "shock" the pool with a very large dose of chlorine witch then basically "burns up" the chloramine leaving the water with fresh Free Chlorine until the introduction of more organic stuff like sweat, urine, bacteria and so on.

    So, if a pool tester shows the Total Chlorine is high it will no be effective any longer if the Free Chlorine is low since most of the Total Chlorine is Combined Chlorine. Most pool owners do not check for Free Chlorine so their pools may not be as sanitary as they think.

    RoninAsturias A:

    Lots of good info here, though I have one more tidbit to add.

    For the most part, if a pool is well maintained and keeps a constant level of sanitizer (chlorine, usually), most bacteria are killed off within 15-20 minutes. The problem comes from pools that aren't maintained properly, or pools that have had something particularly nasty introduced.

    The big ones are giardia, legionnaire's, and cryptosporidium. The first two can be killed off with typical chlorine levels within a few hours, but crypto is a nasty contender that can stick around indefinitely if the water isn't treated well.

    What it usually comes down to is negligence; if you got sick from a pool, someone want doing their job right to prevent it from happening in the first place.

    Source: I am also a CPO, and am responsible for a hotel's pool and spa.
