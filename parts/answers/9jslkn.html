The fastest visual perception is about 13 milliseconds. Of course generating a respond based on that will take time but time to a light flash to detection will be about 13ms. It takes about 250ms to respond to a visual cue (light turning on) and click a button in response. You can test your own reaction time here: https://www.humanbenchmark.com/tests/reactiontime/

The time from an event and it hitting your retina is insignificant compared to the time it takes to process because it's the distance from you to the object divided by the speed of light which is almost 300 million meters per second.

Ailuj182 A:

It depends what you mean by brain and what you mean by process. There's a part of the midbrain - the superior colliculus - that can receive visual motion information and use your spine to get you out of danger before you're consciously aware of it. So for example, someone throws a ball at your head, you jerk out of the way - and THEN realize what happened. The light information went on two simultaneous paths. One to the superior colliculus on the order of about 13-50ms. The other path goes to the occipital lobe (in the back of the head), then the parietal lobe (middle), then the frontal lobe (front). In the occipital lobe, identification happened but you weren't able yet to decide what to do about it. That process takes about 150ms. In the parietal lobe, the information about the ball was integrated with the information from the rest of the senses (maybe you heard a shout), that process takes about 200ms from the start of the signal, then the signal is sent to your forebrain where you both decide what to do (prefrontal cortex) and do it (motor cortex). That whole process takes about 250ms from the start of the signal. Actually responding can take longer (because you have to move a limb which means overcoming inertia) so reaching up to catch the ball might take 500ms (a half second).
