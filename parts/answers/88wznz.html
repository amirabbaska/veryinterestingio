somedave A:

A rainbow is a good way to judge this, but it does depend on light intensity. You won't see the IR or UV levels when their relative intensity is less than the blue scatter background. If you are unlucky enough to get a near IR laser in your eye you will see a red dot, but you would not see that frequency in the rainbow.

On the UV end you are more limited by what is absorbed by the rest of the eye, but a sufficiently bright source could still be seen.

The actually sensitivity of the rod and cone cells has been measured. It drops off typically like 1/(wavelength - peak)^2 as you are further from the resonance, see this for more info.

eigenfood A:

We had a powerful laser in grad school that could tune from 800nm to 900nm. It put out > 1W ,which is quite powerful. If it was green, it would have made a very bright spot that you couldn't directly look at, if you stuck an index card in the beam. At 800nm, though, it would make a little red dot on the card. What 'shade of red'? Just plain old red, is how I would describe it. Not 'dark red' or 'maroon'. The red you see when just the red sensors in you eye are stimulated. As you would tune it to 900nm, the hue would stay the same, but the spot would just get dimmer and dimmer. Even though the beam would burn a hole through your black tee-shirt if you weren't careful when bending over the table to adjust the optics.

Nd:Yag lasers at 1060nm are completely invisible, though, even for 10W on a white backstop in a dark room.
