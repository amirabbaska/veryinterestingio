In the USA we use a weighted index based on percentages. There are 6 possible grades from best to worst: A, B, C, D, F. Letter grades correspond to the percentage of points earned out of the maximum possible for the entire class including homework, quizzes, projects, papers, tests, and final exams.

A: 90-100% B: 80-89% C: 70-80% D: 60-69% F: 0-59%

GPA assigns a number corresponding to each letter grade earned for each class and then is averaged across all courses taken.

A: 4 B: 3 C: 2 D: 1 F: 0

So for instance, assume I took 4 classes this semester. I got 2 A's, 1 B, and 1 C for my final total grades. To calculate my GPA:

GPA = (2(4) + 3 + 2) / 4 = 3.25

So my GPA is 3.25

For a 4.0 GPA, that means I have earned an A in every single class I took, which means I earned scores of 90% or higher on every test I took.

maestro2005 A:

There are a ton of different ways to calculate GPAs, and they're generally not directly comparable because of these differences.

First of all, it's become extremely unpopular to have grade quotas, where the top x% gets the top grade, etc. Nowadays, almost universally, each student is compared against a standard instead of to each other. Everyone in the class could get an A if they all mastered the material, or they could all fail.

The oldest system was just A=4, B=3, C=2, D=1, F=0, and then you averaged your grades. But this has a granularity problem: the kids that are getting perfect 100% have the same GPA as kids that are just barely making As. Competitiveness kicked in, and more tiers were created. This is what you have a picture of.

But also, what about different difficulties of classes? If I take calculus and bust my ass for a B, why is that scored worse than some slacker who takes remedial arithmetic and gets an A? So in many high schools, AP (college level) and pre-AP classes are given a 1-point bonus.

My high school actually gave you a specific GPA score for each class down to the tenth of a point. The baseline was 5.0, with AP classes out of 6.0 and remedial/repeat classes (meaning you failed it the first time) out of 4.0. So if I got a final grade of 91.7% in AP calculus, that was a 5.917 to be factored into GPA. So if I tell you that my high school GPA was 5.04 (I don't remember exactly, but it was something like that), you can see how this is completely incomparable to a more traditional 4.0-based system.
