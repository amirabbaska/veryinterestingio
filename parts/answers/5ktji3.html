RobusEtCeleritas A:

Others have mentioned the Boltzmann distribution, but that's only correct for the canonical ensemble. If you have a completely closed and isolated system, you have a microcanonical ensemble. The equilibrium distribution for the microcanoncial ensemble is not the Boltzmann distribution, but rather a uniform distribution.

So all the particles in the gas will not have the same energy nor speed. But they'll obey a probability distribution which assigns the same probability to every accessible state.

If your system is closed but not isolated, and held at thermal equilibrium with a reservoir at temperature T, then you've got a canonical ensemble. In that case, the probability of each particle being in a state with energy Ei is not uniform, but rather proportional to exp[-Ei/kT], where k is Boltzmann's constant.

If you write this in terms of velocities (assuming a nonrelativistic gas), it becomes exp[-mivi^(2)/2kT]. I've written the mass as mi to account for the different species in your second situation.

microli A:

The particles in a gas at equilibrium (ignoring quantum effects) will have a Boltzmann distribution. This means that you're more likely to find low energy, i.e. low velocity, particles, but there is also a nonzero probability for finding particles with pretty much any energy. The probability for high energy particles drops off very quickly above the Boltzmann energy kT.

BluScr33n A:

No the velocity of particles will assume some distribution. Gasses in a closed system will reach thermodynamic equilibrium. Most gases can be approximately fairly well as an ideal gas. The velocity distribution for ideal gases in thermodynamic equilibrium is the Maxwell-Boltzman Distribution. It's mean value defines the kinetic temperature and the distribution is slightly positively skewed.
In real life gases won't behave exactly like ideal gases, so there might be some differences to this model but the main idea is still the same: The particles won't all have the same velocity but rather some distribution of velocities.
