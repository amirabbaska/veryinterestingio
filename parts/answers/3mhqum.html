The answer for why the center of the Milky Way being bright, despite a Super Massive Black Hole, has been already answered. But black holes can be seen when they are in a feeding stage. This is when matter is being sucked in, and the matter is rubbing against one another, heating up and glowing. They can get extremely bright.

We have not really observed a black black hole. We only see their bright emissions. I think recently there has been photos of the event horizon of the massive black hole in the center of our galaxy, but that was with a very powerful telescope and a team who are specifically trying to catch the event horizon.

The core of the Milky Way is about 30000 light years across, and contains a staggering number of stars at a higher destiny than the spiral arms. This is why it is relativily bright.

At galactic centre there is a supermassive black hole, but compared to the core it's a speck, and practically invisible since it's dormant.
