From a similar question posted earlier: One of the best carbon-capturing trees is the Silver Maple. It can scrub 25,000 pounds of carbon in a 55-year useful lifespan (454.55 pounds per year). Coal-fired power plants in Pennsylvania (a moderate producer of CO2 from coal) produce 136 million tons of CO2 annually. This equates to 37,057,220.7 tons of carbon. (Atomic weight of carbon=12, of CO2=44). Now do the math. 37,057,220.7 tons / 454.55 lbs = 163,050,141 silver maple trees. For reforestation purposes, it's recommended you plant between 726 and 435 trees per acre. Using the average spacing, that's about 680 trees per acre. So to cancel Pennsylvania's annual coal-fired carbon, you'd need 239,779.61 acres of silver maple trees. Which is about 374 square miles (.31 Rhode Islands).

Oh, The National Arbor Day Foundation sells Silver Maples for $6.98 each. You'd need to budget $1,138,089,984.18 for the project, not counting the cost of the land.

oqirnfhsi A:

Think of carbon being in three buckets on earth.

1 - the atmosphere

2 - the biosphere and

3 - sub surface

we are pulling the carbon out of the subsurface bucket and placing it in the other two buckets. Planting trees may help to remove carbon from the atmosphere but it only stores the carbon for a short period of time. trees die, burn, etc, and release that carbon back into the atmosphere.

the main way carbon is removed from the atmosphere is the oceans. phytoplankton removes the carbon and when it dies, it sinks to the bottom of the ocean, is compressed and due to plate tectonics is slowly submerged underground. this is obviously a very slow process. thus, the only real method to reduce carbon emission in the long term at our disposal is to either stop pulling it out of the ground or else find a reliable, long term method to get it back into the ground.
