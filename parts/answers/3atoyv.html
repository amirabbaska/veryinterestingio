Bardfinn A:

The active ingredients in your mouthwash are non-polar molecules — they're oils. Water is a polar molecule. Polar molecules and non-polar molecules don't mix well, with a few exceptions — alcohol is one of those exceptions. It's a polar solvent with a non-polar region, that mixes well with water, allowing the oils that are the active ingredients to remain dissolved in the water.

There's a very simple non-technical reason. In the US if you are selling an Over the counter drug product (like mouthwash or toothpaste or Sunscreen make up) anything you list as an active ingredient MUST HAVE a label claim amount. There maybe a bunch of technical ingredients that make the active ingredient perform, or in the case of Alcohol itself have active properties, but only if the label claims it to be active do you need to list it.

You can have triclosan as antimicrobial but the soap does just as much

You can have Ethanol as an anti-microbrial in hand santitizer but a cooling agent in deodorant.

You can have Titanium Dioxide as a pigment in make up, or a sunscreen in face cream

Qualifications: 20 years in consumer products, as a Chemical Engineer

TL/DR; mostly for reasons to do with label copy, not because it doesn't work

lumentec A:

The top comment is totally wrong. The correct answer is that "inactive" ingredients are not inactive. The FDA requires that over the counter drugs be labeled with an active ingredients section that lists the actual drug ingredients in the product. In a flouride mouthwash, for example, the fluoride does some magic chemistry stuff with your teeth to make them more resistant to decay - it actually stays in your body and does something. The alcohol in mouthwash is important because it cleans your mouth. But then you spit it out. The intended use of the alcohol is not to absorb it and have it do something in your body - the intended use is for it to clean your mouth and kill bacteria - then you spit it out and its gone. Because the FDA does not consider alcohol to be a "drug" in the case of mouthwash, it is not listed as an "active" ingredient. But that doesn't mean it doesn't do anything. It just means that what it does is not considered to be worthy of designation as a drug in this case.

Another great example is cough drops that are essentially just pieces of candy. If there is no menthol or other "drugs" in the cough drops, you will not see an active ingredients section because nothing in the drops is considered to have drug activity. But these drops do still help by increasing salivation and thus soothing a sore throat. It's simply that the sugar in the drop does not actually do enough for the FDA to consider it a drug.
