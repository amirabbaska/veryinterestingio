Probably the biggest difference between the exterior world and our perception of it is that we are constantly seeing (and hearing, for that matter) the past. Because we don't see the thing itself, but rather the light reflecting off of it and which travels at finite speed, we only see an object in its condition and position from a tiny bit of time in the past.

Therefore, there is no way for you to determine what the world looks like NOW, only THEN. Of course I can confidently predict that the TV 20 feet from me hasn't changed in the tiny fraction of a second that the light takes to travel to me...but I cannot be as confident about, let's say, Tau Ceti.

Optrode A:

Neuroscientist here... Pretty damn different.

  1. Color

Obviously, there are many wavelengths of EM radiation we can't see. Infrared, etcetera. But in fact, even within the visible range, we're missing out on a lot.

There's a big difference between being able to tell something is there, and being able to tell what it is. A person with total colorblindness (black and white vision) would be able to detect all the same wavelengths you can, but not tell them apart.

And in fact, we are little better off. When we see colors, there are many different mixtures of wavelengths that look identical to us. Your computer monitor can make you see the same color as, e.g., 568nm light, even though there is no 568nm light coming out of your monitor. That's because you can't distinguish the right combination of red/green/blue wavelengths from a pure 568nm light.

If we had perfect color vision (able to independently perceive all wavelengths in the visible spectrum), we would be able to detect many of the chemical components of objects by sight (especially by burning them / otherwise exciting atoms in the material). Detecting the specific wavelengths of light that objects give off is how spectroscopy works.

  1. Filling in the blanks

Our brains fill in a lot of detail we can't actually see. Outside the middle of your retina (the fovea, where most of your "cones" are, which 'sees' approximately the area of your thumb held at arm's length), you have very poor color vision. Your peripheral vision is actually not far from b&w. Your brain fills in the color in the periphery, as well as many other details you can't actually perceive.

maelstromscientist A:

I'll explain this like I do to my astronomy students:

It is no coincidence that the human eye sees approximately the same energy of photons that our sun produces. That is to say, the majority of the Sun's photons that reach the Earth's surface just so happen to have the same average wavelength (around 5500 Ã) that the human eye can see. This is a product of evolution. The sun produces this light, so we evolved to see this light. The ability to see light gives us a huge advantage in terms of survival - to see our environment, potential mates, and predators - and not having it would be an obvious disadvantage.

So let me re-word your question. Would it be evolutionarily advantageous if what we saw with our eyes didn't reflect everything that is real around us? If not, then our brain did a hell of a job "rendering" what it thought our eyes were seeing, so well that we can perform the everyday tasks we do today with mathematical precision. And that's just light we can see.

It is true that there is an entire universe out there that we cannot see with our eyes. However, we as a species have done a great job at seeing those invisible parts by building things like telescopes that can allow us to see what we can't see with our eyes.

And then there is dark matter and dark energy, things that we cannot see no matter what, but until we can actually understand what these things are, we cannot make any substantial claims about them.

So to answer your question, yes, the world largely is as it appears, thanks to the help of technology. But this is only true if what we're seeing is the actual universe, and not some elaborate computer simulation controlled by our powerful overlords, but that's a whole other story...
