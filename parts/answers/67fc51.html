TheDoctorMouse A:

In addition to /u/SovietWomble 's point, it may be a conditioned response in some cases.

Think of Pavlov's dogs. Pavlov would ring a bell and give his dogs food. When he gave the dogs food, that would drool. Eventually, after ringing the bell and giving the food, they started to drool when they heard the bell.

The same principle can apply to gagging in some cases. From my own life: oatmeal. I used to eat oatmeal without a problem, but I would eat it slowly and it would get cold. The cold oatmeal made me gag. This happened a lot, and now the smell of oatmeal makes me gag.

Maybe more poignant. I brush my tongue after brushing my teeth. This makes me gag (since I'm sticking the brush far into my mouth). Now, I gag when I finish brushing my teeth even if I skip brushing my tongue.

Evolution makes us gag reflexively to some things, per /u/SovietWomble 's explaination, but we may be conditioned to gag at other things.
