the higher resistance side will have less current and the lower resistance side will allow more current.

think about it as a canal that splits into two smaller canals of different sizes for a while before joining together at some point downstream. Consider elevation to be like voltage and the volumetric flow of the water to be current. The smaller canal has a bigger resistance to flow and allows less "current." However, downstream the two canals still meet back together at the same "voltage" elevation.

canadian05 A:

V= I*R This basic, foundational equation demonstrates that voltage can stay constant as current and resistance vary. As long as current and resistance change proportionally.

Another important consideration is that if node 1 is the high side of the resistors and node 2 is the low side, voltage can only have one value at node 1. Same for node 2. It's analogous to pressure in a pipe. If there were two plumbing routes from node 1 to node 2, pressure would be a single value at each node. However, flow would be determined by the resistance in each route.

Because the more formal term for voltage is potential difference. Electricity is a flow of charged particles (electrons) and they really don't care about gravity very much. To them, potential difference is the same thing as height and thus it doesn't matter how much resistance they run into on the way down, they still "fell" the same "distance".
