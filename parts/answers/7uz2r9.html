sparky_1966 A:

I'm not sure if you're confused or it's just the wording of your question but hopefully this will help. In humans, each cell has thousands of mitochondria, and each mitochondria has more than one copy of it's genome. This keeps the effect of individual mutations down. The mRNA/tRNA/rRNA in mitochondria don't leave, they're used only in the mitochondria. A number of proteins that are needed but not made in the mitochondria are encoded in the nuclear DNA and the proteins made are transported into the mitochondria, they don't go the other way.

As already pointed out, most mutations in mitochondrial DNA are lethal to the individual mitochondria or the cell they are in, so never affect the entire organism. Mutations in the nuclear genes occasionally occur that result in disease but not death. These tend to be mutations not in energy production but in replicating and repairing mitochondrial DNA that changes result in much higher instability in the mitochondrial DNA so the individual has a much higher load of defective mitochondria over time.

Mutations in actual mitochondrial genome DNA that get passed on are very rare and the rate is actually used to determine how distantly related two species are. Most are silent mutations where a DNA base pair is changed, but in the end doesn't change the finalprotein amino acid or it's function. Rates of actually function altering mutations in mitochondrial DNA are incredibly low since they are usually lethal.

gitgudsnatch A:

I asked a similar question about the rate of mitochondrial disease to a professor that specializes in mitochondria. He said that the incidence of mitochondrial disease is low because of the following reasons:

1) Mitochondria have several copies of their genome. Some mutations may be masked by other copies of the gene.

2)Cells contain thousands of mitochondria, so the ratio of mutated to normal mitochondria plays a role in the manifestation of a phenotype.

3) Mitochondria undergo mitophagy, meaning that old or damaged mitochondria get degraded and replaced with new mitochondria.

I also learned recently that mitochondria can undergo fusion to increase output, a mechanism that may save damaged mitochondria.

To be clear, mitochondria within cells contain their own circular genomes and ribosomes separate from the rest of the cell, and they pretty much replicate and operate independently.

Mitochondria produce ATP and is responsible for producing energy for the cell. Mitochondrial DNA can and does get mutated, and in eggs, this can be inherited from mother to children (the mitochondria of sperm cells degrade away). It's not super rare either, but mitochondrial disorders are expectedly characterized with muscle weakness due to lack of energy.
