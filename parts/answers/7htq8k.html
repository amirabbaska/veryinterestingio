Ottfan1 A:

The answer that my professor would give you is that it depends entirely on the type of beach. Just for a few examples, we can have beaches that have nothing but siliciclastic sediment on them, but then you can also have entire beaches of only carbonate sediment. Not to mention the extreme variety within these two main groups.

As for what’s under it, that depends entirely on the history of the locality’s environment. Certain environments are known to produce certain sequences of lithologies as they move. For example in a braided river system we see lots of sandstones with sedimentary structures indicating flow (like trough crossbedding) from the times the river channel is active, interbedded with mudstones from periods where the channel is inactive.

These recognized patterns for different environments are called facies models, which we then use to interpret the history of environments like beaches from drill cores.

So to answer your question, it’s either carbonate or siliciclastic rock (pretty much literally any sedimentary rock) resulting from the environment that deposited it. With a little digging around you could probably find a paper with a stratigraphic section + analysis for wherever you were interested in.
