It is a condition know as situs inversus, and about 1 person in 10,000 has it.

If all the organs are reversed, the body functions completely normally, and it is not unusual for it to go undetected. The main issue is making sure medical providers know about it, especially in an emergency.

In some cases, the reversal is imperfect, and this can lead to a number of health problems, with the most common being cardiac problems. Also, situs inversus can be caused by Primary Ciliary Dyskinesia, which results in improperly formed cilia in the body. Other symptoms include sinus and lung problems, and male infertility. These are chronic conditions you would have already had.

Finally, situs inversus can complicate organ transplants involving asymmetric organs like the liver or heart.

appleciders A:

As others have noted, if it's complete, if every organ in your body is flipped, it's not that big a deal, as long as your doctors know about it. However, it can be a very big deal if you ever need an organ transplant, because a normal heart won't fit correctly in your chest. So take care of your ticker; you'll be especially tough to find a donor for if you ever need one.

On the other side of that coin, please sign up to be an organ donor if you're not already. Other people in your situation are hoping for a matching organ every day.

I have this. It's not been a big deal for me, although my heart is plumbed imperfectly and that's not good.

Make sure this is added to your medical notes wherever you go. A lot of your organs are asymmetric so it's important to know. Make sure your SO knows so they can tell medics if you can't. A medical bracelet is a good idea. Also fill in the emergency medical details on your phone. It's a good idea to have a rough idea where your appendix should now be, just in case you get weird stabbing pains in your stomach.
