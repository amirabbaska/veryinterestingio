A lot of companies were not liking their ads being attributed to very controversial topics. Such as violence, sex, drugs... so on.

So they decided to boycott Youtube of all ads until they provided a more robust way of the companies to decide what kind of content they would have their ads put on.

The result was that when a business buys ad space on youtube now, they have a bunch of criteria boxes they can tick or leave empty, signifying what they are okay with and not.

Meaning the business has more power over the kind of exposure they are having.

There is also a "demonetising" process. Where and by if a video gets a significant number of views and reports for being controversial, it can have an amber dollar sign placed on the uploaders view of the video. Showing that no ad money can be earned on it. Unfortunately there isn't much explanation given with this, and so they are left in a guessing game as to what made them "ad-hostile" many people like Philip Defranco have spoke out about this and complained to Youtube, with Phil explaining he's just giving the news, he's specifically trying to facilitate a discussion, asking his channel to explain their thoughts in the comment section.

When he was told that the reason for one of his recent videos was demonetised because it spoke about the recent London Bridge attack, he pointed out the hypocrisy that established media channels like BBC had videos showing the graphic pictures (which he didn't) and yet they were showing ads before the clip. Asking why that channel can upload and monotize content, that he couldn't. Personally I think it's because those other videos didn't have anywhere near his exposure, so less reports.
