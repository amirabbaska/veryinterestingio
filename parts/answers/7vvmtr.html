I don't think there's a way of quickly and robustly shaping the magnetic field with a tokamak's kind of magnet to do what your proposing. You'd presumably need something with a load of smaller magnets, and then you'd have very little field strength for containment.

I'm also not sure you could design a circuit (i.e. sensor, computer to invert image, computer to process how to respond, circuitry to increase the current to a magnet) that could respond quick enough to prevent all the significant turbulence, even with a really fast computer. totes_original_uname A:

Particularly for nonlinear plasma disruptions in fusion experiments, machine learning may be practical for "predicting" or detecting when a disruption is about to happen and providing feedback control to avoid. I believe as early as the 90s, neural networks have been used to predict disruptions on tokamaks. Machine learning is also increasingly being used for Design of Experiment and data analysis. It will almost definitely play a major role in fusion research going forward, but there are still many challenges.

EDIT (sources): Kotschenreuther, M., et. al., Novel Computational Techniques to Predict Transport in Confinement Devices, and Applications to Ion Temperature Gradient Driven Turbulence, in Plasma Physics and Controlled Thermonuclear Fusion Research, (IAEA, Vienna, 1991) vol. 2, p. 361.
