Let's back off a bit and start from the beginning.

U-Pb geochronology works by using crystals of very resilient U-bearing minerals; the mainstay of this method is zircon, but some other minerals such as baddleyite may also be used. What is measured is not merely the amount of lead; the amounts of U and Th must also be known, but the key measurements are all about the kinds of lead, which isotopes are present and in what ratios. ^^238 U decays to ^^206 Pb and ^^235 U decays to ^^207 Pb, and the decay constants are known for these reactions ... ^^204 Pb, or "common lead" is not the product of radioactive decay. So … when we carry out the measuring, we really focus on the ratio of isotopes which may be measured to a degree of precision which astounds me to this day.

If I may use an analogy, let suppose you have a hamster (work with me here) … it produces small standard pellets of poop at a known rate. You could count the pellets when you change its cage and figure out if (for instance) someone did or didn’t change the litter in your absence, and when. U-Pb dating is a bit like that: you know the residual amount of uranium and thorium, you know the ratio of uranogenic to thorogenic lead, you know the decay constants and you know the crystal didn’t leak lead (cause zircons are tough little crystals). So ... you can backcalculate the amounts to find the time required for the all of the observed decay products to form, which gives you the age at which the crystal was sealed.
