Frack_Off A:

I learned about an interesting one studying aqueous geochemistry.

Most periods of increased global temperatures are strongly correlated to periods of high concentrations of atmospheric carbon, so accordingly we are concerned with the concentration of carbon dioxide in our modern atmosphere. At the air-ocean interface, carbon flux occurs as carbon dioxide is absorbed by ocean water and converted into carbonate ions, which through a series of chemical reactions can join with other ions dissolved in the water such as calcium and precipitate into solid carbonate rock which settles on the ocean floor. This carbonate rock is essentially atmospheric carbon that has been sequestered in solid form, just like plant matter is.

The kicker is that colder water can hold more carbonate dissolved in it and warmer water can hold less. This is why all the pretty 'white sand' beaches are at tropical latitudes; the white sand is calcium carbonate and water has to be above a certain temperature (and away from the polluting effects of silicate sedimentation) for calcium carbonate to precipitate. Heating the oceans decreases their ability to hold carbonate ions in solution and increases the volume of carbonate precipitation.

In short, raising the global temperature increases the rate at which oceans sequester atmospheric carbon as limestone.

singularineet A:

Potential negative feedback terms include:

  • higher temperatures could lead to greater cloud cover

  • more CO2 could increase plant growth, slurping up CO2

  • higher temperatures could increase plant growth, slurping up CO2

  • increased plant growth could increase the amount of cloud cover

  • climate changes could change ocean circulation in ways that increase plant growth and/or change the oceans' speed at absorbing CO2

These are in general pretty hard to model. There's a reason the climate models have such enormous error bars.

knowyourbrain A:

Stable systems are dominated by negative feedback. Positive feedback loops play out quickly because of their nature. Thus, many of the normal mechanisms that take up CO2 act as negative feedback. The more CO2 in the atmosphere, the more taken up by plants and especially the ocean where it can be deposited as minerals. Many people think these negative feedback mechanisms could max out with more warming.
