I'm a bit late to the party and I think /u/AgoraiosBum summed it up well, but I'd like to add a little more. I realize this goes a bit beyond "ELI5" but I think the issue also goes a bit farther than poor drinking water and is worth talking about. China is currently investing in some of the largest and most expensive engineering projects in world history to deal with their water issues, and these projects could have impacts outside their borders. Here's a few key points:

  • China has thousands of years of history in creating (and destroying) water-management infrastructure, the combined effect of which has been the severe depletion of groundwater reserves and increasing desertification in some parts of the country. The most visible effect of this has been the changing course of the Yellow River. In modern history there have been several years where the river has not reached the sea due to overuse and drought.

  • Industrial waste and fertilizer continue to pollute several major waterways, especially the Huai River system which is home to the famous "cancer villages." Over 80% of China's remaining groundwater is also contaminated, primarily by toxic metals from industry and fertilizer runoff. Incidents usually get cleaned up but because economic development is so important polluters often get away with minor punishments and end up polluting again. Part of the problem is that it's currently cheaper for many factories to pay the fines than to follow environmental regulations.

  • Northern China (including Beijing) has a much bigger problem than Southern China. A major part of the infrastructure being created to deal with this problem is the [Southâ€“North Water Transfer Project] (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South%E2%80%93North_Water_Transfer_Project). The basic concept is to divert water from the Yangtze River Valley to help alleviate shortages in the North. The not-so-basic part is that it has already cost over $79 billion dollars (over twice as much as the Three Gorges Dam), led to the resettlement of over 300,000 people, and only one of the three main routes has been completed. That route has already begun pumping about 10km^3 of water per year to the North, which is expected to rise to between 12 and 13km^3 by 2030. It's also worth noting that as the impact of the project has become clearer, new projects have had to be started in order to compensate for environmental damage and water loss along the canal routes.

  • Some solutions have the potential to become diplomatic issues. China controls the headwaters of the Ganges, and some discussion has occurred among academics about diverting Tibetan waters in order to deal with the crisis. So far though these are just hypothetical plans for what could happen if the crises becomes significantly worse.

  • Desalination is a growing market in China, and makes sense since a huge portion of the population lives along the coastline. While desalination plants have been slow to spring up due to huge investment costs, over time more plants might become viable and help ease some of the burden off of natural freshwater systems.

Overall the question of "how bad is it?" is difficult to answer at this point because a lot of solutions are still panning out, and new policies are being created and tested. If you'd like to learn more about the history of water crises and water management in China, I recommend The Yellow River by David Pietz, which was published in 2015 and is pretty up to date.
