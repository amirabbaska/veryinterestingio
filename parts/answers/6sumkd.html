wraab A:

Well, first it wouldn't "blow up" the earth. On a planetary scale the earth is small, but on a nuclear explosion scale it's pretty big. Depending on the spread and altitude of the nukes you could easily kill 99% of all life but not the entire planet. Even if you managed to "crack" the earth our gravity would keep the planet whole, so no chunks of California getting blown off or anything like that.

Space is a bit different and poses other problems. The explosion in space would do little immediate damage, but the after-effects could be very very bad.

The blast wave would likely destroy the orbits of almost every satellite orbiting earth, either by directly knocking them out of their orbit or by causing a chain reaction of collisions in space. Basically, a massive sphere of space junk would make future space launches almost impossible.

Then you've got the emp and radation that would slam into the earth. Best case scenario is temporary electronics blackouts and a total loss of GPS. Worst case is blasting the entire planet with radiation. So we all die, but slowly and painfully.

The W76 is a 100kt device so roughly 420TJ. 420*14900=6258000 or 6.258EJ or 6.258x10^18 joules. To put that in perspective, the energy of sun striking the sun facing side of the earth every second is only 1.7x10^17 joules, while the total mass-energy conversion rate of the sun per second is 384.6YJ or 3.846x10^26 joules.

For a more down to earth perspective on just how much energy this is we will use a banana for scale Reddit standard. We are going to round off the banana Reddit standard weight at 200g. If we lift this banana Reddit standard by 1 meter from the surface of the earth it would require 2 joules of energy. The same amount if we dropped it from 1 meter above the surface of the earth. So all those nukes would be equivalent to dropping 3,129,000,000,000,000,000,000 (3.129 Quintillion) bananas Reddit standards from 1 meter.
