Assuming you're describing the classic two-body orbit, then a perfectly circular orbit would generate gravitational waves. If you consider a very heavy object being orbited by a much lighter object, for instance the earth rotating round the sun, then to an observer at a distance the gravitational waves would be imperceptible due to their weakness. They would be there, just not easy to detect.

Gravitational waves are emitted, in layman's terms, when the system has some kind of asymmetry. I.e. the system I just described is different to, say, if we crushed the earth into tiny rocks and spread them in a perfect circular orbit round the sun. (Don't try this it would be highly immoral)

Whether an orbit is circular or elliptical isn't even Lorentz invariant (one observer might see a circular orbit while another observer sees it as a non-circular elliptical one due to length contraction). So clearly both circular and elliptical orbits must have the same physics. Both emit gravitational waves.

Any acceleration radiates gravitational waves, and circular motion is acceleration. The only motion which does not radiate is straight line at a constant speed and that never happens when orbiting some other body
