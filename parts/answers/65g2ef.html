pegabrie A:

Life needs nutrients to grow, no nutrients equals no algae. Suspended solids tend to settle on the bottom if the water isn't moving too fast. Plankton can be beneficial in keeping water clear as they filter it.

It is possible to reproduce this by using other plants to out-compete the algae. If you Google swimming ponds you'll find a pile of related info.

clusterfucken A:

Crystal clear water is water that has very low nutrient content. Water near stream headlands has not picked up the nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, and other nutrients. The ocean in the tropics is also very nutrient poor because the warm waters do not mix with the deeper more nutrient rich waters. The water simply cannot support the growth of much life.

You mention the grass, but if the source of the grass is close to where the picture was taken there is not time for the nutrients to become available this will be influenced by a lot of factors stream speed, temperature, or specific nutrient limitations.

Water in just about every habitat is filtered somehow. Rivers are filtered by wetlands and saltmarshes (which is why they are so important don't drain the actual swamp), in the tropics mangroves filter runoff from the land, and on small scales oysters, tunicates, clams, mussels, corals and sponges filter water removing organic material.

To replicate that in a pond generally requires mechanical filtration and chemicals.
