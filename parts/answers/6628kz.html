An important thing to remember is that when we say "the speed of light" we mean "the speed of light in a vacuum". Gravitational waves always travel at the same speed, as far as we know.

In order for there to be an index of refraction for gravitational waves, it helps to understand why there is an index of refraction for electromagnetic waves. From a wave phenomenon perspective, as the oscillating field enters the material, it interacts with atoms, causing them to oscillate in response, which in turn allows them to radiate light with the same frequency. This interaction slows down the propagation of the wave. (great explanation with more detail here).

In order for the same thing to happen for a gravitational wave, it would have to enter a region where its oscillation drives masses to oscillate in a way that re-radiates a gravitational wave. However, because gravity is so weak compared to electromagnetism, anything light enough to be oscillated is not going to generate a strong wave, and anything massive enough to generate a strong wave is not going to be very responsive. This means that there's not really anything (that we know of, at any rate) which can really affect the propagation of the gravitational wave. There are some other issues as well which have to do with the fact that gravitational waves actually change the shape of space-time, rather than propagate through it like electromagnetic waves.

All of these things means there isn't really anything which will actually absorb gravitational waves, and related to that, there isn't really anything which can slow them down.

That's my understanding - I don't work with gravitational waves but about half of my department does, so I might have some details off.

rocketsocks A:

When people say "the speed of light" they usually mean "the speed of light in a vacuum" or more specifically "the speed of massless particles" (all of which are the same, c). This differs from the speed of light in a medium that has a different electrical permittivity and/or permeability than a vacuum does, and thus has a lower effective speed of propagation of electromagnetic waves / photons. However, even in such media the speed of massless particles other than photons often remains the same as c. And this is the case for gravitational waves, they are not in general refracted or lensed the way light waves are.
