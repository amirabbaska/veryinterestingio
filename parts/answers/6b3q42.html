Cst-Marbles A:

Fight or Flight response is just a name given to the physiological response under threat. Pupils dilate, blood is rushed to central organs from our exteremities, etc. It's basically describing what the BODY does to get ready to 'fight or flee' to deal with the threat. Whether we choose to fight or flee is a cognitive process. I believe it's the same thing for freezing, where it is more of a cognitive response than a bodily (physiological) response. I think that is why freezing is not talked about when talking about the 'fight or flight response' because really, this is just talking about what happens in the body, and thus comes before whether the person ends up fighting, fleeing, or freezing. The question of why we sometimes freeze is a separate topic to the physiological respons, but an interesting one for sure

sumukhgupta A:

When we encounter frightening situations, we either engage in a fight response, flight response, or freezing response.

The fight or flight response (in its original form) is about survival. It's about hope. We activate it when we believe there's a chance we can outrun or outfight our attackers. The freeze response however, gets activated when's there's no hope (usually).

The freeze response works differently. You can view it as an "energy conservation" mechanism, basically to just brace yourself up and prepare to endure the impending ordeal. This response is the most adaptive of the three.

The fight or flight response, on the other hand, tells the heart to beat faster, the muscles to tense, the eyes to dilate and the mucous membranes to dry up. As a result of which, you can fight harder, run faster, see better and breathe easier than you would without this response. This response kicks in for real threats and imagined ones in as little as mere fractions of a second: much less than the amount of time between two beats of the heart.

That's why the fight or flight response is now called the fight, flight or freeze response. Because, sometimes, when the odds are overwhelming we neither fight nor flee but simply freeze.

The fight or flight response is a sympathetic nervous system cascade that evolved to aid certain beneficial behaviors in challenging circumstances eg fight, fleet, sex. It does nothing, physiologically, that would aid freezing. Humans sometimes freeze in situations where the fight or flight response has activated because of other—higher level—cognitive processes.
