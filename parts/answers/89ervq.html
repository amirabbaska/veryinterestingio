Are there analytical ways to determine an element's radioactivity without needing to observe anything

There are ways to roughly predict whether an element would be radioactive, but getting an actual quantitative result to any precision is not possible from first principles using theory, and even numerical methods will be at best very approximate.

There are models to calculate half-lives for tunneling decays like alpha, cluster emission, and spontaneous fission, but they are generally approximate, and the half-life is extremely sensitive to the height and width of the potential barrier that the particle has to tunnel through.

shiggythor A:

Radioactive decay is stochastic. A half-life of 2*10^19 years means that it takes that time for 1/2 of all atoms to decay, but it also means that in one year, one of every 10^19 atoms decay! If you compare that to Avogadros number of 6*10^23, thats not a huge amount. Get 200 grams of that isotope (easier said than done) and you have a comfy 60.000 decays in one year. Then you "just" need a very efficient and quite detector with great background rejection and you can measure such lifetimes.

One other item of note that I haven't seen listed... Half life is a measure of the time it would take for half of a sample to decay. For a sufficiently large sample, you would still be able to observe decay events.
