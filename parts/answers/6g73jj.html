BZWingZero A:

While its not 'cooked', they do have an experiment onboard to grow lettuce, allowing the astronauts a fresh meal.

Most food on the ISS is pre-packaged and heated at mealtime. The closest they get to cooking is assembling a meal from pre-packaged components. The astronauts are free to mix and match the components to their liking (e.g. building a wrap using a tortilla filled their favorite ingredients).

They do have an espresso machine they use for tea, coffee, and soup.

flekkie A:

There are studies about the importance of cooking food for long duration missions as a way to keep to crew together and keep motivation high.

HI-SEAS is a mars colony simulation programme that had cooking as one of the main research topics.

Here's an interview with one of the researchers on the topic: http://www.astrobio.net/moon-to-mars/a-taste-of-mars-hi-seas-mission-now-in-its-final-days/

KJ6BWB A:

They "cook" up there, or at least they assemble different things. Open air flames are banned, of course, but heating coils are fine and dandy, and different puddings, etc., are cooked via heating coils. They're actually set to try out a new dough recipe that will give them crumbless bread (normally bread is banned, because the crumbs would get caught in things and probably start a fire).
