Alright, think of it this way.

Your cells are little sacs containing a bunch of solutes, like sodium and electrolytes. When the water-solute balance in your body is off (because you've drunk more water than your body can handle), the cells attempt to compensate by absorbing water from outside the cell (osmosis). The cells figure, "It's OK! We have enough salts and electrolytes that we can take on a little extra water!"

...but if the imbalance is great enough, the cells can't absorb enough water to correct it. All that extra water causes the cells to swell. When the brain cells swell this can interfere with their ability to function or cut off blood flow, resulting in death.

dargosinger A:

A lot of the responses below are correct, it has to do with the water and electrolyte shifts in the body.

The main electrolyte at play here is Sodium, and in severe dehydration, the sodium in our blood/plasma becomes very concentrated. This elevation in blood sodium levels is also a primary driver of our brain's thirst drive. More typically with a long drawn out, chronic form of dehydration this elevated sodium draws out fluid from within our cells to try to regulate the sodium level in our blood (osmosis) because our cells and nerves function best at a relatively narrow range of concentration. In severe dehydration and high sodium levels, rapidly correcting the blood sodium levels by drinking too quickly causes a sudden shift of water back into cells and the place where this is most dangerous is the brain (cerebral edema), which can quickly become fatal. It's usually pretty hard to cause such a dramatic change in water/sodium balance just by chugging water, but in such an extreme example like in the book, it's feasible.

Actually, it's something called Water Intoxication (also known as Hyperhydration and Hyponatremia). Water intoxication has killed Marathon runners (I've heard more have died from that than dehydration, but I don't have a link), and it can be very dangerous. Essentially osmosis forces the electrolytes out of your body and you lose precious electrolytes, and you no longer function properly. You need a balance of electrolytes (sodium, chlorides, calcium, magnesium, potassium, etc) and too many or too few can kill you.
