Ok so this can easily be explained, but it might be a little different than you might expect. Both 10 mins of extra sleep in the morning and a 10 min nap work in different ways to be beneficial. The bad guy here is your snooze button.

10 blissful minutes of extra sleep a night can make a difference, but it will be a long-term benefit and won't necessarily change how groggy you feel in the morning. But when you hit your snooze button for those extra ten minutes, you’re pretty much telling your body “hell ya false alarm, go back to bed.” Then your body gets pissy at you and confused when it realizes this ain’t no high school fire drill.

Afternoon naps work in a different way as they rely on your body not going into any big ol’ canyon slumber, and instead act like cleaning the RAM on your computer. As you progress through your day the chemical adenosine, which regulates how tired you feel, starts building up in the brain similar to how dirt and odours build up in your bedroom. Taking that little nap is like power misting the crap out of your room with a can of Febreze; It temporarily gets the job done, but can’t replace the almighty slumber vacuum.

topicmodelsvb A:

There is an absurd amount of up-voted misinformation in this thread.

Other than causing a temporary decrease in the levels of adenosine in your brain, I don't think you will find a 10 minute nap to be that beneficial.

In general, sleep quality degrades the longer one sleeps.

More specifically, your sleep architecture changes the longer you sleep. At the start of a sleep period your brain spends most of its time in sleep stages 2 & 3, which are the stages associated with deep dreamless physically restorative sleep (especially stage 3).

However as you sleep longer, the amount of time your brain spends in stages 2 & 3 diminishes significantly, and your brain instead starts spending most of its time in REM sleep, which is not physically restorative, and is instead associated with minor benefits to procedural memory and emotional equanimity.

Thus after 8 hours of sleep you're spending basically all your time in REM sleep, which provides you with little (if any) further benefit.
