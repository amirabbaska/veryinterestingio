SpookyMaidment A:

In the early 19th century, intellectuals and those well-traveled would often drop French words into the conversation to show how clever they were. They would then point out that the word they had just used was French, often to embarrass someone nearby who was less fluent in the language.

To counter this, the less well-traveled (often poorer) people would, after swearing, loudly proclaim, towards those that had previously used French in the conversation, "Pardon my French."

The latter stuck.

Abide_Dude A:

I think a lot of the top level comments here are way off base. While it may well be true that "Pardon my French" was first used commonly in conversation when the class of educated social elites dropped in French terms to show off, the question OP asked is, "Why do people use it now or the purpose of covering over foul language?"

The answer to this question is one of many examples of the etiquette of the Victorian age, which encouraged the upper classes to persist in keeping up appearances even in the face of undeniable evidence. In circumstances where a gentleman would utter a swear or curse in front of a lady, the phrase "Pardon my French" came to be the go-to apology. By saying this the gentleman was able to apologize for a faux pas and allow the lady to accept the apology without the implication by either party that the lady would know the swear word. In effect, it gave those in a social situation the 'out' to acknowledge a misstep without digging deeper into what may have been socially dangerous territory.
