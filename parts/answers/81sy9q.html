Homemade potato chips are generally flat or wavy - they don't have a pronounced hyperbolic shape. Even commercial potato chips other than Pringles don't seem to have that shape generally - look at Lays.

And Pringles are not potato chips in any real sense - they're less than 50% potato, and are made from a sheet of dough deliberately molded into the shape. So I'm not sure that in general potato chips are hyperbolic.

rmkreeg A:

I think the spirit of your question is “why are chips, which are cut flat, end up with what looks like hyperbolic surfaces (like leafy greens can tend to grow)?”

I’m not a mathematician, but I think I understand the general ideas here and I can only point toward possible clues: 1) The deep frying process creates an even heat over the entire surface of the chip, but because of the geometry of that surface, the edges of the chip will heat up quicker than the center. 2) The starches in the potato have unique properties at different temperature ranges.

Given that, I think there may be uneven shrinking of the surface. Because the edges have more surface area, they probably get crispy first, but shrinkage is kept to a minimum by the still raw potato toward the center. As the inner portions heat and shrink more evenly, they might then pull on the outer edges, creating a hyperbolic surface.
