Altyrmadiken A:

This is a myth.

If you observe a water droplet, with some basic understanding of magnification, you'll see the focal point is below the leaf. Since we're probably not all scientists, how about this?

Imagine that you're holding a magnifying glass. You're trying to burn some paper with sunlight! Now, holding the lens right up the paper does nothing, why? The light needs to be more tightly focused, and the lens can not redirect enough light into a small enough area at that distance.

The same is true for water on plants. The droplets can not focus the light intensely enough on the leaves at the distance they are from them (directly on top).

This myth stems from the fact that intense summer heat will cause plants to dry out and get burned. This happens naturally when the sun is too strong for the plant and when the plant is not well watered. However, many people will water at the strongest sun, thinking it would help. The plant is burned by the sun anyway, and they inaccurately attribute it to the water.

ThisIsTheMilos A:

You can't. The silly idea that water acts like a magnifying glass is just an urban myth. Even if it did act that way, it would absorb most of the heat. Watering can boost the contrast, making your dead grass more visible. And it can be a waste, because much of the water will evaporate under the hot sun.

Quite simply, if your grass is having issues with the heat, it needs more water. The best way is to water after the sun goes down to ensure more of it goes into the soil than the air. The risk of this is that it will allow some mushrooms/fungus to grow as well.
