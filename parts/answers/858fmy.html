Corrosion occurs between dissimilar in electrical contact with an electrolyte. 100% distilled water (pure H20) is not a conductor, what is conductive in normal tap water is the other ions inside. Salt dissolved in water is a pretty potent electrolyte, and allows for quite a bit of conduction.

Let's say you have steel and aluminum touching with no electrolyte, no flow of electrons can occur due to needing a complete circuit. Add an electrolyte, you now have a complete circuit, and can not start corroding. Humid air is sometimes all you need to get kickstarted.

A good example of this, is ship "boneyards" where they will place 10 or more boats side by side in electrical contact. They will place a generator across this electrical contact, and place a positive voltage across the opposite direction of where this electrical flow would be due to dissimilar metals. This counteracts the electrical flow, creating a net 0 voltage, and will prevent corrosion.

This is why it's so important to use similar metals when water piping is involved, as an electrolyte is always present. If you really want to speed this process up, Bleach is a good way to kick start this. This corrosion is known Galvanic Corrosion, and is sort of involved in battery physics for specific types of batteries.

Many applications will use sacrificial anodes to combat this. Essentially they will use a metal far away on the galvanic chart from the metal primary used in the application. This can help prevent corroding other dissimilar metals, as the sacrificial anode will take most of the corrosion. Since it's intended to be sacrificial, it's easy and cheap to replace.

Source: Materials Science and Mechanical Engineer.
