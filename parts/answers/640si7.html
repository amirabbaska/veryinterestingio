ApproximatelyC A:

Terrorism is the pursuit of political aims by way of violence. Typically, terrorist groups aren't large enough, or trained well enough, or armed well enough (or any combination of all three) to take on domestic armies, meaning that a military coup or invasion is impossible.

Terrorist groups try to spread their ideas through violence against the easiest potential targets, which has two factors working for it. Firstly, the chance to succeed is much higher - if you want to drive an HGV through a military base, you're likely to be stopped fairly quickly. If you drive the same HGV through a civilian crowd, you can do significantly more damage before being stopped.

Secondly, the military expect to be targets. Civilians do not. When civilians are subject to attacks, the population (in theory) becomes afraid. Imagine if a country was subject to one terrorist attack per week, which killed ten people. The entire population would learn to be afraid of the next attack, because who knows who's going to die next.

This leads to cries for politicians to take action to help. By attacking "soft" targets (targets which are relatively unprotected, like public transport/spaces), the terrorists are ensuring that there's as little chance of stopping them as possible. It becomes difficult, therefore, for the politicians to change domestic policy in a way that prevents further attacks.

In the eyes of the terrorists, the rational response here would be for the government to change their foreign policy to make the attacks stop. The easiest way to do that? Stop doing whatever you're doing that's upsetting the terrorist groups.

That's effectively what the aim is. It becomes more problematic when you're looking at things like IS, whose goals are more nebulous.
