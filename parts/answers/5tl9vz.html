Well, this question will only garner speculative answers as no one knows. Animals usually have mating seasons so that when the offspring's born it will have an abundant supply of nourishment (either directly or indirectly, e.g feeding off the milk the mother sustains through feeding). For example, elk and deer have their mating season during the autumn months and give birth around spring time when plants begin to grow. This leads to good foraging opportunities for the mother to nourish her young as well as there still being enough plants for the calf to start feeding on during weaning.

It has been theorized that humans don't require this sort of "offspring timing" to coincide with food availability because we always have food available. However, most infants are born in the summer months. If this is some remnant of a mating season to give birth around the wet season in Africa, I'm skeptical of, but who knows.

Two answers I've heard that seem worthwhile. The first is that at some time during the evolution of humans, reproduction-based sexual interaction was "co-opted" for a more sophisticated level of bonding between individuals. Sex becomes a kind of language of connection and a supplemental form of communication/reaffirmation.

A second idea I've heard (which is not at all mutually exclusive with the first) is that because female-dispersal was more typical during most of human evolution, creating instant sexual bonds was a useful tool for a young woman to have when entering a new community. Instead of waiting a month or several months, and potentially having to deal with male aggression and an uncertain position within a community hierarchy, a woman could potentially enter into sexual relationship with a man in the group, cementing her place socially, and pre-empting the stress of dealing with the (potentially aggressive) interest of multiple men.

Chimps, Gorillas, and Baboons (I want to say all primates) don't have "mating seasons" either - the female goes into estrus periodically all year. Bonobo chimps are even like us - constantly in a state of being physically receptive to potential sexual activity, regardless if conception is likely on any given day of the cycle.

Goodall noted that high-ranked males would "sequester" the high-ranked females to get exclusive reproductive rights.

Goodall also noted that all males of a Troop would (nominally, generally) help protect the young from predators, generally ALL mature males had mated with all mature females, and were ALL potential fathers.

Further reading about Bonobo chimps suggests sex is a social bonding experience.

TL;DR: Monkeys are social animals who like sex & power hierarchies & many primatologists/anthropologists seek to gain insight of our own sexuality through these genetic relatives.
