Technically speaking, yes.

But, there was a study, it resulted in claiming that humans will probably never be able to rely on supplements alone and that they will never effectively replace the benefits of (healthy) eating.

There are two major reasons for that-

  • There are some chemicals (specific types of fibers and phytochemicals) that can never enrich synthesized foods. Also, dietary supplements are not well regulated and may cause a positive drug test result. Therefore, "plate is always better than a pill."

  • The second aspect is purely psychological. A well-planned nutrition and hydration plan positively influences athletic performance. Eating not only satisfies your palate, but also gives you a sense of emotional satisfaction and fulfills your desire to eat. Gluttony is not that rare among humans. Moreover, social participation such as dining with others is a very important aspect of people's lives. Humans are primordially just social animals; and secluding their meals and limiting the time spent cherishing, admiring, or appreciating "good food" can actually seriously undermine your mental health.

Yes. They're called meal replacements. This one is very popular in Canada. It is the go-to choice for those who are unable to swallow solid food (after a surgery for example.) It's also provided free from pharmacies to extremely-low-income as part of the government funded drug insurance.

EDIT: For it to be provide for free, there are certain requirements to be met such as: be on a drug benefit plan available to only the ultra-low-income, a prescription for a physician, unable to eat solid food, and/or using it as the only source of food not as a supplement. So no, they won't be available to students... unless you're really in the shitter.
