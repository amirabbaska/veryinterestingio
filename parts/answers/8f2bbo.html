It really depends on how the gas is dispersed in the bedrock and the heat of the area. With continuous fire for such a long time, it might be hard to tell how hot the gas is. It's possible that the gas is already heating past its ignition temperature below the surface, and burns when exposed to oxygen. The door is a natural cave right? Unknown size and composition? Unknown number of entries, some of which could be small and far away. Cathy_Garrett A:

First issue, I would think, would be snuffing the fire by denying it oxygen. This would be complicated by the high heat and continually expanding nature of hot gases. At a bare minimum, a wall would have to sunk deep into the ground all the way around the already huge hole in the ground, then some form of heat resistant textile would have to be run out over the hole while being sealed to that wall system to begin denying the flames any form of oxygen.

This is all predicated on the idea that the engineers would tell them that this is even remotely possible and that the fabric would not inflate to the point of rupture or rip the walls out of the ground or rip free of the walls itself.

Maybe it would be enough to deny the flames low-level oxygen and so the heat and gas could be allowed to vent from the top of the inflated baloon. Then again, as has been mentioned, the subsurface gas may, at this point, be so hot that it will combust just on contact with the air, so all you would do is move the location of the flame from inside the pit to the top of the balloon. Once more, that might be all that's required.

Might be possible to engineer a large, rigid dome just off the pit and then roll it over the pit and anchor it to a ready-built foundation on rails, not unlike how they built the current sarcophagus for the ruined Chernobyl reactor.

It's all a question of engineering and cost. What could the company doing it get for the gas they could extract from it on the open market versus what would be the cost of capping it.
