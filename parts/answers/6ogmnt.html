whenyesterdaywemet A:

As the pressure drops, the equilibrium of CO2 dissolved in liquid should favor the atmosphere. That's the opposite of what you are experiencing.

When you moved, is it significantly hotter? Or are your drinks not staying as cool as they were? Higher temps would also make the CO2 favor the atmosphere over being in solution.

Another thing you might consider is that your sense of smell and taste vary, given humidity and altitude. I grew up in high altitude Albuquerque NM, and currently live in Austin TX, which is about sea level. I always feel like soda is "crisper" when I'm in Albuquerque, compared to when I'm in Austin. Same with water. I'd guess it probably has something to do with the difference in humidity, at least in my case.

Edit: /u/fraghawk points out that it may be mineral/contamination from water. If you get soda from fountains or use ice, this may be the difference as well. There is a limited amount of stuff you can dissolve in water, so if there are a lot of minerals in the water, it can make the CO2 less soluble as well.

bertoshea A:

The amount of a gas that will dissolve can be calculated using henry's law,

https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry%27s_law

When you open a carbonated beverage it starts to come back to equilibrium, that's why there are bubbles. Increasing the atmospheric pressure will increase the amount of a gas that will dissolve, decreasing the rate that bubbles form. Decreasing the temperature will also increase the amount of a gas that will dissolve.

This is how carbonated beverages are made, the beverage is passed through an atmosphere of pure CO2 under pressure.

Titans86 A:

Differential Cola bottlers in different regions will bottle their drinks in different weight packages, you might be in a region where the standard for shelf life is lower thus bottlers will use lighter packaging. Even a couple grams in bottle weight (given the same shape bottle) will have a strong impact to CO2 retention, (and big cost savings for packaging)

Ask a friend in your old area to weigh an empty bottle and compare with the new area, bottle shape/volume has to be the same though.
