Non-circulating legal tender (NCLT) coins are never intended to circulate as cash money but they include a denomination as a fail-safe of sorts so that should the world economy collapse and silver becomes worth less than $5.00 per ounce (for example) then at least this item's value is backed by a treasury for $20.00.

Circulating coins used to be made of actual silver and gold but these were phased out because the value of the silver and gold outpaced their face value and it was easier to make more durable coins out of copper-nickel and steel cores. Canadian "junk silver" coins up to 1966/67/68 (depending on the denomination) are all currently worth 7 to 11 times their face value.

In reality, most of the NCLT $20 silver coins don't even include an ounce of silver and so they are essentially jewellery disks. If you like the art/design then buy it but don't expect to hand it off to your great grandkids to make a fortune. They won't. It is a crappy investment in almost all cases.

involatile A:

Legal tender evokes a certain emotional response in collectors that just isn't present in things like medals. Anyone can make a medal out of silver, and there are even those that can make them with the same quality as a national mint. But they don't have the same cachet and power as actual money. Medals made by national mints are somewhat valued, don't get me wrong, but actual legally authorized money has a specific mythic quality to it.

One effect of this is that mint errors are so valued. It's silly if you think about it; a double-struck coin is just a mistake - a misshapen, probably ugly artifact. But people collect them because they're unique objects made by an official government entity.

For practical purposes, commemorative silver coins aren't much use as money, because it would be silly to spend them for a fraction of what you paid for them. But since the mint is authorized by the government to imbue them with a cash value, they do so, and collectors love it.

The short answer, then, is because people will pay that much.
