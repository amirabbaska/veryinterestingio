Family doctor here. We get exposed to all the same stuff anyone who works in customer service does on a daily basis. But unlike a cashier who is having people breathe in their face and hand them germy money, I wash my hands before and after every patient and we wipe the room down between patients, plus we make obviously sick patients wear masks.

Also, most people come to the doctors with a cold when their symptoms are at their peak, which is also around the time that contagiousness is dropping. So by the time I see them, they’ve just finished spreading it around.

Most of the time when I get sick, it’s from my girlfriend who has a kid or one of my coworkers who has kids, because kids are the most efficient ways to spread viruses around.

round_goodness A:

While the nurses and doctors get all the good press, I’d like to share my perspective as a hospital cleaner.

  1. We wash and sanitize constantly. 50 times a day is a reasonable estimate.

  2. We use tons of disposable gloves.

  3. Hospital rooms get cleaned every day. Nothing has a chance to fester.

  4. In cases where the patient has a particularly nasty illness there are various levels of protection.

For example: Contact isolation—everyone who comes into the room dons a disposable gown, upon exit the gown is thrown away and you wash your hands before leaving the room. Contact plus—same as above, except things are cleaned with bleach. Droplet—gotta wear a mask to enter the room. Airborne—these ones are intense, for illnesses like tuberculosis. Patient is in a negative pressure room so that when the door opens no illness gets out. To enter need heavy duty mask or powered respirator (you feel like Darth Vader a bit).

But anyway. We really put a whole lot of effort to keeping things clean and protecting both patients and staff.
