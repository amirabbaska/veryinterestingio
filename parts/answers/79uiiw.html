LArlesienne A:

Regarding the number of quarks in a hadron:

Quarks are held together by the strong interaction. Unlike particles charged under the electromagnetic interaction whose charge is fixed and never changes, a particle charged under the strong interaction can have one of three charges: red, blue and green. Antiparticles have the corresponding anticharges: antired, antiblue and antigreen.

Neutrality under the strong interactions means either a grouping of all possible charges or a pairing of a charge and an anticharge. Hence, you get baryons and antibaryons (three quarks of three antiquarks) or mesons (quark-antiquark pair). Pentaquarks (basically a baryon and a meson bound together) have also been observed but are highly unstable.

Baryons and mesons can be made up of any flavour of quark aside from the top quark, which decays before it can form a bound system. The uud combination is the only stable one, and udd is stable in a nucleus.
