Viral DNA that becomes a part of the human genome is referred to as human endogenous retroviruses, and 8% is a generous estimate; it's typically between 4-7%. That being said, it turns out it's not too difficult to tell viral DNA apart from the original human genome. There are a few methods.

One method is to sequence an animals entire genome and compare it to others. There was a recent study where this was done on a Chinese Minipig and researchers could tell by looking at nucleotide substitutions and general differences in sequences.

A more specific answer to your question involves looking at things called histone markers and areas that have undergone DNA methylation. Histone markers are binding sites for DNA proteins, so things like enhancers and silencers. A completely silenced region with histone markers on each side can be indicative of an HERV. DNA methylation is when an extra methyl group is attached to a base. This effectively silences the gene, which again, is indicative of an HERV.

So long story short, almost all the viral DNA you have in your body is silenced in one way or another. Scientists can look at areas of the human DNA genome that are silenced, compare them to different human DNA and make a determination on how much of it is comprised of foreign DNA sequences.
