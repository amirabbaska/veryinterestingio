FallBackDown4 A:

You'd probably notice most muscles, not just those around the jaw, tense up when you're trying to exert force. Tensed muscles exert more pressure on the blood vessels in/around them, thus squeezing blood out of the peripheral circulation and into the heart and lungs where it can be oxygenated. The energy and oxygen needed for a sudden burst of strength or effort (fast twitch) is already available in the muscle cells, so it can afford to squeeze that extra blood out in favor of oxygenation in the central vasculature. This way, after the fast twitch movement the blood rushes back into the tissue rich with oxygen to get ready for the next movement. I don't know that this has been scientifically proven as the REASON for clenching the jaw and other uninvolved muscles in a particular task, but I do know that everything I've said is independently true.

Super late to the party here, but I think I can contribute!

One of the reasons this likely happens is the concept of muscle coactivation. The idea with coactivation is, as the name implies, when one muscle or group of muscles activates, nearby and/or related groups of muscles will contribute to the benefit of the desired action.

Even when you do something simple like sticking your arms out, you're not solely using the muscles required for that action. You're also using other muscles to help adjust your balance, and to help stabilize and control the motion more generally.

Clenching your jaw, specifically, probably comes from attempting to balance out and/or stabilize some of the forces and moments generated in your upper arms and neck.

However, this is likely just a piece of the puzzle rather than the whole concept, so do keep that in mind.
