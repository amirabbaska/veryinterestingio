seicar A:

There are incredibly scarce elements on Earth, like Iridium, that are more common in meteors/asteroids.

Fun (not so fun) fact. Iridium rich (relatively) layers of clay are geological "boundaries". They are maker beds that denote theoretical asteroid impacts, like those that caused massive species extinctions. One you may have heard of is the K-Pg (Cretaceous–Paleogene) boundary, formerly known as K-T (Cretaceous–Tertiary), and is the hypothetical marker of the "dinosaur killer" asteroid that smashed into the Yucatan Peninsula ~66 million years ago.

Edit: I should correct myself and stipulate "scarce elements on the Earth's crust,". We have a good idea of the composition of the mantle and core, but for such small trace elements I wouldn't want to speculate.

I_am_Bob A:

As other people have said, it would be more likely to find new materials as in alloys or crystalline structures or molecules that can occur under conditions not seen on earth than to discover a new element. Since elements are defined by the number of protons and we do not currently have any gaps in out list of elements, and all elements created in labs with higher atomic numbers than those elements found naturally are unstable and decay very quickly it's unlikely we would find these on Asteroids. There is, however, and theoretical 'Island of Stability' that exist beyond the elements we've created in the lab. As of now it's only a theory, and there is no known mechanism in the universe that could create them. But it's at least theoretically possible that some unknown cosmological event created them and they are floating around on an asteroid somewhere.
