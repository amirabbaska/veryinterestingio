Let's assume that everyone picks their numbers at random. We know that's not true, but it simplifies things. Suppose we've sold t tickets, with n possible combinations of numbers. For this draw, n is about 302 million and t will be around 378 million (there were 15.8 million winners, and there's a 1 in 24 chance of a prize).

The probability that no one picked the winning numbers is ((n-1)/n)^(t), which gives us around 29%.

The probability that a particular ticket is the sole winner is
(1/n)((n-1)/n)^(t-1), which is about 1 in a billion. Multiply that by the 378 million tickets, and the probability of a single winner is about 36%.

That leaves about a 35% chance of multiple winners. Given that there was a winner, it's about a 50/50 chance that the prize would be shared. Since real players tend to prefer certain combinations of numbers, a single winner should be a bit less likely than that. There'll be more unsold combinations than we expect, and the sold combinations will on average be more likely to have multiple tickets.

Edit: I've stole that 378 million tickets were sold.

You're asking two separate questions here. Also, just for pedagogical reasons, this isn't a statistics problems, this is a probability problem. Anyways,

  1. You can ask what the probability is that exactly 1 person wins the jackpot (tl;dr 35%)

  2. You can ask what the probability is that at least 1 person wins the jackpot. (tl;dr 71.32%)

Calculating the probability that at least 1 person wins is much easier. I don't know how many people played, but since the probability of winning is 1/(302 575 350), the probability of losing is (302 575 349)/ (302 575 350). So the probability that zero people win is the probability that EVERYONE fails, which is just 

                    [(302 575 349)/ (302 575 350)]^x

where x is the number of people playing. But we're not looking for the probability everyone fails, we're looking for the probability that at least 1 person succeeds. But that's easily shown to be just 1 - probability that everyone fails.

Thus, the probability that at least 1 person wins is

             **1-[(302 575 349)/ (302 575 350)]^x, where x is the number of valid tickets**.

Edit: So the probability that at least 1 person wins, if x= 378 million, is 71.32%

Calculating the probability that exactly 1 person wins is a bit harder. The probability distribution of winning the lottery is actually what is known as a binomial distribution.

A binomial distribution is one where you have n trials, with each trial only having two possible outcomes, either a "success" or a failure". In this case, each person playing the lottery is a trial, so n=total number of tickets, and a "success" or a "failure" is obvious-- whether they win the jackpot or not.

In a binomial distribution, each trial needs to have the same probability of success. Let p= the probability of success on any given trial. In this case, p= 1/302 575 350. Then you can say

p(exactly k successes)= (n Choose k)(p^k )(1-p)^n-k , where n Choose k= n!/[k!(n-k)!)].

Thus, the probability that exactly 1 ticket wins is

p( 1 success)= n!/[1!(n-1)!] (1/302 575 350)^1 (1- 1/302 575 350)^n-1 , where n is the total number of people who bought tickets. This simplifies to

n (1/302 575 350) (302 575 349 / 302 575 350)^n-1, where n is the number of valid tickets.

Edit: Thus the probability that exactly 1 person wins is 35%

P.S These formulas all actually have fairly simple (ELI10) derivations. If anyone is particularly interested in why the odds of winning the jackpot are 1/302575350, or how the binomial formula comes about, I'm more than happy to explain.
