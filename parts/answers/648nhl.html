I haven't done any research specifically regarding a dogs chromosomes and genes, but from a little bit of genetics background we know that in humans, skin color is an example of incomplete dominance.

In incomplete dominance, two different alleles can create a blend between the two creating a different phenotype. This is why when a black person and a white person have a child, often times the child is a lighter shade of brown.

In dogs we can assume that the genes that decide fur color (not the same as skin color) can be codominant which results in both alleles being simultaneously expressed phenotypically. This is how a brown dog and a white dog can have spotty brown/white offspring.

Khan Academy is always a good resource https://www.khanacademy.org/science/biology/classical-genetics/variations-on-mendelian-genetics/a/multiple-alleles-incomplete-dominance-and-codominance

Orisgeinkras A:

That is an example of codominance, a genetic occurrence where neither gene is fully dominant and thus the offspring expresses genes from both parents. While this does not occur for something like skin colour for humans, humans do have some traits that are potentially codominant, for example, blood type. There are 3 alleles (forms) of the gene that codes for blood types, I^A, I^B, and i. When a person's genotype (combination of genes) is I^A I^B, then they have the blood type AB, which is codominant because they have the receptors for both A type blood and B type blood.

Don't know about dogs, but calico / tortiseshell cats arise e.g. from black coated mother and ginger father because coat colour is X linked, and female mammals shut down one of their X chromosomes at random (as dosage compensation for the fact that males have only one X), and it can be a different X in different cell lines, so they will develop black patches and orange patches.

Edit: it's for this reason that calico cats are almost always female
