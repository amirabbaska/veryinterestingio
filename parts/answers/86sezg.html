That's actually a brilliant question! Sorry if my reply is a little scattered - I was always better at marketing and chimpanzees than I was writing.

Your question is really marvelous - we don't know exactly how much of the line between the two species is genetic, and cultural. They are very similar genetically, and there is some reporting of interbreeding in captivity, but the two species are very distinct, both in behaviour and range. They tend not to overlap in the wild, and intermingling hasn't been observed naturally, so a lot of the thories between the species haven't been tested (plus, it could be potentially unethical to test - it's hard to justify giving a child to an adult who might kill it, and we have to treat chimps with that same sort of respect for their minds, even if we clearly have differing values).

Chimps and bonobos do have some pretty major cultural differences that likely wouldn't translate well with one-another, but we don't know how much of their cultures are just genetically motivated, and how many involve the transmission of knowledge and information between generations.

It's possible that members of either species could adapt into the cultures of their peers, and vice versa - it's a touch of a misconception that Bonobos are less violent, in as much as at some point, they decided amongst themselves to resolve their upsets in more friendly ways. There's a lot of value in determining whether or not this is genetic or cultural, and it's possible either is true (it could be the consequence of selective pressures choosing for sex-responsive bonobos against overagressive or undersexed apes, for example, or it could well be just the way they teach one-another, or it could be both, with their culture selecting out apes who don't adapt well to their cultural values).

There's also been some examples of how Pan infants can adapt to lifestyles and cultures that aren't their own by default. It certainly wasn't seamless, but Nim Chimpsky ended up being a pretty solid television devotee who very clearly expressed his sexual attraction towards non-chimp researchers and handlers. This didn't always work out well for people who tried this sort of cultural exchange (Moe and the Davis family are an example of the worst case), but it did open the window to the idea that chimps will try to adapt to the cultures they're presented during their upbringing... as much as their genes will allow them to. That's something of the line in this conversation, and where I have to say "I'm not sure".

A good answer is "I think they could adopt between species, and that they would adapt to the cultural values of each other, with only specific instances of upsets and flare-ups occurring, but that's only a hypothesis (if a testable one)". We're still trying to find the line between behavior, genes, and culture when it comes to chimps, and the best answer for your question would come when we have clearer solutions to the culture/genetics problem.

notfarenough A:

I wrote a senior thesis on hybridization between anubis and hamadryas baboons. It's an area of interspecific breeding that has been relatively well researched. It may provide a good analogue.

Hamadryas baboons are highly hierarchical, with successful males developing and defending fairly large harems, but do not have quite the same kind of male cohort alliances as chimpanzees. Male Hamadyas are tough looking characters. They signal tension and aggression by blinking and yawning to expose their large canines. Anubis baboons display the same kinds of signaling, but troops tend not to be dominated by a single male. Males emigrate, and older unrelated males who are not vying for a high ranking form peacable relationships with other males and femails, and can develop coalitions to displace a single highly aggressive male. That seems to mitigate overall violence in anubis troops. It's a better place to be a female!

What seems to be important to cultural hybridizations is the direction of hybridization. Male Hamadryas who immigrate into an anubis troop do not develop harems and seem to get along okay. Female anubis who join a Hamadryas troop fall right into the male dominated heirarchy. Blended groups demonstrate intermediate behaviors. I can't recall what happens to male anubis who join a hamadryas troop, but suspect that since they are not quite as large or aggressive, would tend to fall into the bottom of the heirarchy.

I think it can be dangerous to extrapolate directly from chimpanzee and bonobo troop behavior except to say that they are genetically interrelated enough to be very similar. Family trees are bushy, and we don't get to see the full range of troop behaviors that might have existed at one time, only the ones most likely to -er- survive!
