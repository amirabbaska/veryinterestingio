Chickenpox is caused by the varicella zoster virus and smallpox by the variola viruses. Vaccina and variola are poxviruses, varicella zoster virus is not - so it is not surprising that vaccina immunization will not confer any immunity to chickenpox.

"Pox" is an old term dating from the time before we had knowledge of infectious agents and was used to describe a number of diseases that seemed to spread easily and cause skin lesions. For example syphilis was once also known as the "great pox".

It's mostly just a name. Pox means getting a rash of pimples. So anything that causes a rash of pimples could have been named xxxxxx-pox. Smallpox, cowpox and some other variations are all caused by very similar viruses. In fact infecting someone with cowpox was the first way to vaccinate someone from smallpox. Because they are so similar, our bodies antibodies work similarly on the multiple viruses. If the viruses are not similar enough, the body will not recognize them and therefore won't fight them off as readily. Hey OP, was just learning this stuff:

Chicken Pox (Varicella Virus/VZV) belongs to the Herpesviridae family. They're actually more related to Herpes Simplex Viruses, HSV as these are all alpha types that remain latent in neurons. Shingles is varicella zoster virus VZV, the same exact virus that reactivates later in life. "Pox" is related to the pustules/rashes - classification has gotten much better since the golden ages. Symptoms wise, small pox will have all pustules simultaneously at the same stage whereas chicken pox will have postures in different stages. They're related by symptoms and observation but when you look at virus classification, they're different.

Tldr: chicken pox is a completely different type of virus (look up HHV/human herpes virus for more info) whereas vaccinia is practically an "attenuated" form of small pox that is similar enough to elicit an applicable immune response.
