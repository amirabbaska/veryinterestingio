Current work:

There are 2-3 vaccines currently in various stages of development. One by Vical is a bivalent vaccine that is still in the early phases of moving to clinical testing, two others have shown to remove the virus is monkeys and are promising but that word usually means at least another 5-10 yrs before results. Both are monovalent IIRC.

Keep in mind, most of the work done in this realm is on vaccination - largely before an infection has been acquired. Cures for latent viruses like Herpes, and other lentiviruses are much more complex.

Barriers to cures:

This complexity largely stems from an ability to silence their own transcription (either through miRNA or LATs or even other mechs) which makes it harder to identify infected cells.

In addition, many HSV types tend to infect very important cells - HSV 1 infects nerve cells near or in the trigeminal nerve and HSV-2 sacral ganglial bodies. We can’t just kill these cells, like we do with cancer cells or T-Cell therapy, these nerve cells provide an incredibly important function and, more importantly, they don’t heal (well). So our removal of the quiescent DNA/RNA/Viral transcript has to be both surgically precise and leave the cell generally no worse off than when we found it.

People are excited about using gene editing techniques like CRISPR-CAS9 and zinc finger etc techniques to either remove the viral genomes or permanently silence them but we’re still a bit off from that level of ability.

In my opinion however, CRISPR tech is moving so fast that something that is a ways off now could really be a legitimately usable technique in 1-3 years. Genomics is just exploding and the rate of information acquisition is mind-boggling. You should be excited about it.

Limitations of medications:

As of now, our best medications are false nucleotide analogues. A treatment developed in the 70s (?) that basically takes a component of the genetic code and replaces it with a Trojan horse component that looks like what it’s replacing. The problem is that it only works on one aspect of the virus - when it replicates its genome - which means that during periods of latency the analogues don’t do anything and they certainly don’t cure anything.
