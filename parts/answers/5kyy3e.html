Blood group O is an universal blood donor, because as you point out, neither A,B or AB have antibodies towards O (because O is the base glycoprotein).

But as you've pointed out, those who have blood group O have antibodies against A,B and AB.

What you've missed is the fact that when you receive blood from a donor it's "only" the erytrocytes that you receive, the erytrocytes have been separated from the plasma and thus separated from antibodies. This also means that AB is the universal plasma donor!

In blood donation, they separate the red cells)from the plasma (the liquid that contains all the proteins including the antibodies). People usually receive packed red cells when they receive blood and these don't contain large quantities of antibodies.

In stem cell (bone marrow usually) transplant a similar phenomenon to what you described can occur and is called graft versus host disease. Essentially the donor white cells recognize the host as foreign and begin attacking. https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graft-versus-host_disease

Not quite what you were asking, but there is a condition in which people have quite severe reactions after transfusions due to antibodies called selective IgA deficiency. They are missing one of the immunoglobulins that all humans have called IgA

Some of these people have antibodies in their own blood against this missing immunoglobulin, so when they get transfusions of blood that contains IgA, they may get an allergic reaction that may even cause anaphylaxis.
