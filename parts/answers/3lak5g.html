sverdrupian A:

Even pure water absorbs and scatters light so you could not see the bottom of the Marianas Trench (10,994 m depth). The amount of sunlight in the ocean decreases exponentially from the surface. The extinction coefficient varies with colors (wavelength) with the blues penetrating the deepest to a few hundred meters. So even if you had the brightest floodlights in the Marianas Trench and perfectly clear pure water (the open ocean is pretty close except for the salinity), you can't see anything more than a few hundred meters away.

ouemt A:

In pure water, only 1% of light with a wavelength of 400nm (violet) is left after traveling 78.8m through the water. For light with a wavelength of 650nm (red), 1% is left after traveling through 14.5m.

With a depth of 10,994m, you would have approximately 9.2x10^-278 % of the violet light that started the journey and 3.9x10^-1515 % of the red light.

This also assumes no scattering of the light and absolutely pure water.

In other words: no. :)

Here's an interesting bit of information. While water doesn't have the optical properties to do what you've asked, optical fiber does. Today's optical fibers are so pure that if the water were replaced with fiber, you would be able to see all the way to the bottom of Marianas trench. Source: National Association of Broadcasters Engineering Handbook, Chapter 6.10.
