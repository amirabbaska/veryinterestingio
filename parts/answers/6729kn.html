under_the_net A:

Note that the Poisson bracket {q, p} is dimensionless and real-valued. So the commutator [Q, P], which has dimensions of action and is skew-adjoint, is not really the correct analogue. The right analogue is really 1/(iℏ)[Q, P], which like {q, p} is dimensionless and is self-adjoint (the analogue of being real-valued).

1/(iℏ)[Q, P] is the correct analogue since it, like {q, p} in classical mechanics, determines the same Lie algebra of linear translations in phase space. It's in that sense that the quantum theory is a "quantisation" of the corresponding classical theory.

"Recovering" classical mechanics means recovering the full Lie algebra of transformations in phase space (i.e., not just the linear translations). The non-zero value of [Q, P] is responsible for the discrepancy between the classical and quantum algebras. (If [Q, P] = 0, then all of the operator-ordering ambiguities disappear.) Since [Q, P] = iℏ, the classical limit is defined by ℏ → 0.

But on the way to the limit ℏ → 0, note that the value of 1/(iℏ)[Q, P] does not change, and may be given the same value at the limit. In other words, while the Lie algebra changes as the value of ℏ changes -- in particular as ℏ becomes 0, nevertheless the Lie subalgebra of linear translations in phase space is always the same.
