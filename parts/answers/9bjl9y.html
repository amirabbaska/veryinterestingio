The ISS is in orbit, but it's closer to Earth than you probably think. There's still a tiny bit of air resistance from the upper atmosphere at that altitude, so anything without rockets (which the ISS has) will gradually slow down and fall back down to Earth. So any air that escapes the ISS will simply "fall" back down again. It will take a while, but eventually it'll just mix with the rest of the atmosphere.

The ISS is still within the atmosphere of Earth, just that it's extremely thin at that altitude, so yes, it returns to Earth's atmosphere. Will it go down all the way to the ground, most likely not, but if it never reached the escape velocity, aside from the tiny portion blown away by the sun's radiation, it will stay around Earth, gradually slow down and homogenize with the very low density top part of the atmosphere.

PhyrexianOilLobbyist A:

The average speed of molecules in a gas is on the same order as the speed of sound in that gas. That corresponds to a few hundred m/s on Earth. The ISS is pressurized to 1 atm. Most gaseous molecules in the ISS aren't really going very fast relative to the Earth, and even when they're squirted through a hole by 1 atm of pressure they won't reach escape velocity.

Hydrogen and helium can, but thankfully we don't breathe those things.
