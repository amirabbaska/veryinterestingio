SpeechScienceGuy A:

The human vocal folds can have an incredible rate of motion. An operatic soprano can achieve a pitch (fundamental frequency) of over 1000Hz, which means her vocal folds are moving back and forth 1000 times per second -- a substantially higher frequency than the hummingbird's wings.

Now, vocal fold vibration is not a fully "articulated" motion, the way flapping wings or, as in another example, throwing a ball would be. Muscle tension causes the vocal folds to come together (adduction) and to become tensed. Exhalation builds up pressure below the glottis, and the combination of this subglottal pressure and the tension on the vocal folds leads to their very rapid vibration. So while not articulated, we do create this motion "voluntarily" through muscle action (adduction, tension, and exhalation).

(And, lest you think the 1000Hz example is particular to sopranos, human speech can regularly include fundamental frequencies up to 800Hz for female adults and 400Hz for male adults).

Tfoley94 A:

Saccades are the fastest possible voluntary movements - they're the tiny movements we make with our eyes when reading, focussing on a different part of the thing we're looking at.

I did my undergrad project on measuring saccadic velocity and humans can move their eyes at a speed of greater than 500 deg/second.

Now that's hard to actually comprehend but think about it like this, these movements take less than 10ms to make!

Edit: Source http://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0054641

TheBlackCat13 A:

The outer hair cells in our inner ears are capable of expand and contracting at a rate of about 60,000 times per second in the lab and probably closer to 10,000 times per second in practice.

Although this is a single cell, the combined activity of these cells is able to actively vibrate in the membrane in the inner ear, producing sound that is detectable outside the ear called "Otoacoustic emissions". Typically these end at about 4 kHz, meaning we are actively (although involuntarily) vibrating the membrane at a rate of 4,000 times per second, almost 60 times faster than a hummingbird's wings.
