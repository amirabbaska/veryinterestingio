mfb- A:

Space is really, really empty. The probability to hit anything is negligible unless you aim for some target. While we have contact with the Voyager probes, you can ask the same about the Pioneer probes - launched 5 years earlier, a bit closer, and inactive now. We don't know if they are still on course, but the probability is something like >99.999%.

All 5 probes that leave the solar system (2*Pioneer, 2*Voyager, New Horizons) will need hundreds of thousands of years to get close to other stars. Where "close" is something like a light year distance, or a few hundred times the current distance to the Voyager probes.

the_Demongod A:

To help illustrate the emptiness of space, read up on what asteroid fields/belts are really like inside. You might picture some incredibly dense cluster of rocks floating through space like in star wars, but this couldn't be further from the truth (that description is more fitting to a planetary ring system like Saturn's). If you were to enter an asteroid belt, you would most likely never know it, and would probably never see an asteroid the whole time. Similarly, asteroids within the belt are unlikely to collide except on a very large time scale (every few millions of years). If that's what an asteroid belt is like, just picture what interstellar space is like. There's practically nothing out there at all to crash into save from some very diffuse gas and dust.
