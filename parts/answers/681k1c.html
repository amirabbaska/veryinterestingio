There's something called the "LD50 value", which is how much of something you can drink before you have a 50/50 chance of dying from it. For alcohol, it's about 5-8 g per kilogram of human weight. For 185 lb (= 84 kg), that's about 420-670 g of pure ethanol. Basically, an entire pint of pure ethanol.

So if you had something that was 10% alcohol (lower end for wine), you'd need to drink 10 pints of it in a short period of time to have a 50/50 chance of death. For something that's 5% alcohol (lower end for beer), you'd need to rapidly drink 20 pints.

serval A:

0.40% blood alcohol concentration (BAC) is the low range for meaningful risk for coma or death by respiratory failure are estimated. 0.50% BAC is where death is more likely.

For a 180 pound person without odd metabolic or medical issues, it would take 24 shots within 1 hour to get over 0.50% BAC and have a significant risk of death or coma.

However, high tolerance has allowed several cases where people reached higher BAC levels, including the highest ever recorded at 1.48% BAC but that's at the extreme margins. Also, different measurement mechanisms give different results: police breath or blood tests will actually measure lower than hospital blood tests because of different assumptions in calculating BAC (for example, partition ratios of 1:1400 to 1:2100 give widely different results).

One of the advantages of alcohol is that most people will pass out long before they reach these levels. Short of remarkable tolerance from years of hard drinking or drinking loads of shots in a very short period of time it's hard to drink that much.

But ask something harder, like a retrograde extrapolation hypothetical!

For clarity:

  • 1 shot = 1.5 oz of 80 proof liquor
  • 1 glass of wine = 5 oz of 12% alcohol (24 proof)
  • 1 beer = 12 oz of ~5% alcohol

https://www.niaaa.nih.gov/alcohol-health/overview-alcohol-consumption/what-standard-drink
