CockroachED A:

It is called cold welding or vacuum welding. Richard Feynman explains it as such: "The reason for this unexpected behavior is that when the atoms in contact are all of the same kind, there is no way for the atoms to “know” that they are in different pieces of copper. When there are other atoms, in the oxides and greases and more complicated thin surface layers of contaminants in between, the atoms “know” when they are not on the same part."

Everyone has already explained the "hows": I just wanted to point out that it doesn't have to be in space, or even in a vacuum chamber. It's just the absolute absence of other atoms that is important.

Cold welding also happens in insulation-displacement connectors under STP.*

*although I suppose technically you could argue that the welding surfaces are under a hard vacuum as defined by the absence of any gas molecules, but the volume of space under vacuum would be zero. I've never heard of someone using the term "vacuum" to describe such a scenario.

Hypothesis_Null A:

To give a bit of intuition - this shouldn't be at all surprising. People remember from middle school about the ductility of metals - you can push a metal rod through a shallow funnel and it'll basically shear the metal on the edges and spread it out, giving you a thinner rod as you draw it through. This is normally done at hotter temperatures for efficiency, but it doesn't have to be. Aluminum cans undergo a drawing process using dies - it's how aluminium cans are made. Continually Press it into or through an area too thin, and it'll elongate and thin out.

What's happening while this is occurring? Metal atoms that were attached to each other can't still be attached to the same atoms they were. They had to let go of some atoms and reattach to others during the process, like playing with really firm play-doh or silly-putty.

While this is normally done at hotter temperatures for efficiency, Metal freely exchange electrons at any temperature, which is the basis of chemical bonding. If you stick two metals together, they'll bond nice and easy; no questions asked. The only reason metals stays separate on Earth is because virtually every metal forms an oxide layer on contact with the atmosphere, since all these extra electrons zipping around love to be reduced (oxidized). and an oxidized surface layer no longer has the free electrons to reach out and grab their equally-oxidized buddies.
