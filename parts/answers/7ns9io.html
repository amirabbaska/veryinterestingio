If you mean cold water as in near-freezing, the warm effect comes from endorphins released to block the painful freezing sensation in order to maintain function to enable you to get the hell out of the freezing water.

I disagree with the equilibrium theories proposed by others, unless you were to be submerged in a body of water that is only a small fraction of your total volume but still manages to cover you. If the water volume is greater than say a third of your own, you will dramatically cool down as the water in your body and the water around you begin to equalize their temperatures, unless measures are taken to increase your own heat generation.

The body has compensation mechanisms in place to prevent dying rapidly due to hypothermia, including the shivering reflex to increase muscular heat generation and constricting peripheral blood vessels to minimize heat loss from vital organs, at the cost of your extremities cooling down faster.

I think you'll find your answer just as much in the conditions of your question as the physics involved.

Jump in a pool at 60F. Water comes into contact with every square inch of you. It seeps into every crack and is more effective at conducting heat away. So you get a RUSH of cold which over time is less and less of a shock.

Air at 60F? Chances are every time you've been in air at 60F you we're clothed. If you stood stark naked in it you would feel a lot more of that "shock" you get plunging into water.

There are all kinds of other factors like the dryness/absorptivity of skin and the way boundary layers form on the surface of your body in water vs air.

The other day I was in my car at the ski resort. 20 F, I had to change my shirt. Stripped it off and jumped out the car to grab my shirt from the back. Being suddenly shirtless in 20 F was like jumping into cold water.

If it helps, conversely think about ice melting in room temp water vs room temp air. Water gets into every little pore and crack and divet in the ice while air has a harder time "flowing" into those "aerodynamically protected" spots.
