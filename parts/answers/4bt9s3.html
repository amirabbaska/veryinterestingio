To add something, I talked to a veterinary professor and my physics professor today about this and we discussed pretty much about their after-response.

My professor has a theory that their pain nerves are not as well-developed, hence they don't realize quickly when they DO bite themselves. To add to that, they have their spinal cords extending all the way to the tip of their tails with dorsal and ventral roots at each vertebral column, so they should have nerve endings at their tails.

As for why they bite themselves, she said a sensible explanation was probably the false sense of hunger triggered by temperature, just like what u/yesssssssssssno mentioned. But she also said outside of that not much is really known.

My physics professor came in from another interesting point of view: the environment these particular situations could be poorly set-up and that could very well have screwed up their IR sensing or the electrophysiology of their pit organs, and then that creates their own disorientation and mistake themselves as prey or predators.
