waltdanger A:

1.5g is NOT the same physiologically as gaining 50% body weight in 1g. People are confusing physics for physiology. For example, in 1.5g your blood itself would weigh 50% more. Your heart will have to work much harder to pump blood through your body and prevent it from pooling in your legs. That added stress to your heart could mean heart failure becoming more common. Also with 1.5g, the organelles and components of every cell in your body would weigh more. Who knows how that could effect cellular functioning.

It just hasn't been studied, but there are many more potential adverse effects than stressing your joints more.

dave_890 A:

The 1.5g environment would be similar to someone on Earth gaining a large amount of weight in a short period. It would take time for the body to adjust, but it would eventually.

Realistically, I don't think either would be "ideal". Higher g means more wear on joints, but the body can compensate with more muscle and tendon. It couldn't compensate with more ligaments, so that's a limiting factor. More strain on the heart, but again, it would compensate. Artificial supports (wrist, elbow, back and knee braces) would reduce the chance of an injury while the person adapts to the new environment. I include wrist and elbow braces due to how people try to stop themselves when the fall.

In lower g, you'll lose bone density and overall strength. Distribution of fluids in the body may cause some problems. Astronaut Scott Kelly spent nearly a year on the ISS, and reported bloating of the face as blood distributed itself more evenly in his body; I would expect a little bit of that at 0.5g.

It would be possible to simulate a 1g environment so that person could minimize the low-g effects. For example, you would spend the day working in the 0.5g environment, but might sleep in a bed being spun in a large centrifuge to simulate 1g. This may or may not help because the person isn't actively resisting the 1g forces as they would normally (such as standing, walking, etc.), but it would likely be tested all the same.

If the habitation is permanent, neither would likely pose any difficulties after several generations.
