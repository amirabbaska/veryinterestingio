I agree with /u/somedave that the answer is No.

If we met the total energy demands of the entire world with fusion (4x10^20 Joules per year) then we would produce something in the region of 100,000 kg of Helium as a byproduct. Compare this to an annual Helium usage of the order of 10,000,000kg and you see there is no way it is covered.

With no way to produce resonable quantities of He, we will run out one day but that day is 100's of years away. The most likely way to avoid this is to get better at recycling it and/or to discover high temperature superconductors that allow us to use a different coolant.

empire314 A:

The world energy consuption is estimated to be 3.9*10^20 joules.

Fusing 1 gram if hydrogen into helium releases 6.5*10^10 joules of energy. But over 99% of the mass remains as helium so we can ignore the mass loss for this estimation.

This means 6.0*10^9 grams of helium is produced to meet the energy requirements of the world. That equals to 6000 tons. Assuming the value of /u/somedave gave of 13 000tons is correct, a 100% efficiency reactor would create almost half of the used helium, or 5% if we use the 10% value you gave in the question.
