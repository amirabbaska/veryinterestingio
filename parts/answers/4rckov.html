Yes. First, we have satellites that orbit the sun from near this point, they give us a stereo view of the sun. If there was a planet at the L3 Lagrange point (the point directly opposite the sun from the earth) these satellites wouldn't be able to stay put.

Additionally, while on the topic of orbits, the gravity from the planets affects each others' orbits, changing the precession of the perihelion of their orbits. This has been measured to incredible accuracy, especially for Mercury. If there was an additional planet hiding in the inner solar system, it would have shown up in these observations of orbital perturbations.

Lastly, the L3 point is generally not a stable point to orbit, so any planet hiding there wouldn't stay put very long.

charonpdx A:

The two wouldn't stay perfectly stable with each other - the other planets (notably Venus,) would perturb the orbits, causing them to approach each other.

They would likely end up in a similar situation to Saturn's moons Janus and Epimetheus where they "swap orbits" every so often. (The two moons orbit at nearly the same orbital distance, one that they could, in theory, crash in to each other; but instead, as they approach, they swap orbits, causing the one that was "catching up" to instead start falling behind, and vice versa.)
