Horizons don't overlap, they merge. When two BHs get close, the horizons bulge towards eachother and a bridge forms. The single event horizon then relaxes into its final single-BH shape in a process known as "ring-down".

Technically, when two event horizons have merged it's no longer two black holes but one. There is no longer any "sweet spot" between them as a black hole is classified by it's Schwarzschild radius, which at the surface is the event horizon. Once event horizons have touched its just one black hole by definition.

TBNecksnapper A:

could things in that overlapped region escape those black holes?

No, per definition, they are not inside any event horizon if they can escape.

However, if you imagine the event horizons as spheres, once the two black hole's radii overlap slightly, there will still be a lagrange point in between them and some region where their gravitation cancel out enough that it's, at least in theory, possible to fly between, at least for a photon. BUT because of this effect happening, the event horizon is deformed and no longer spherical, once the true event horizons overlap, there is really no escape.

Here is an illustration of it: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_black_hole

As you can see, as the black holes approach each other, their event horizons are pushed back at first (at 24 seconds in). At this point, at least photons can get between although they pass closer than the usual radius of the black hole.
