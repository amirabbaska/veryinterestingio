If you assume that the one man and one woman are the only source of genetic information, then you have short term issues with incest.

Long term, variations will come about through genetic mutation which will allow for a genetic base for a sustainable population.

The key to doing it right is planning.

The first couple needs to make a large number of children, at least 4 boys and 4 girls. Those children will have to mate with each other, one boy having a child with each of the four girls, making 16 grandchildren.

Then pair off the grandchildren so no pair shares a mother and father, increasing the possibility of a variety of genetics to emerge.

After that, you track the genealogy of all members of the growing society. The 16 grandchildren make up 8 starting families. For the first few generations, make it so no children mates with another from the same parents. The next few, restrict it to grandparents as well. By the 10th generation, you can restrict it to great-grandparents and stop directly planning the families.

SvenTropics A:

There would be a number of genetic flaws that would come out over time, but these could be culled by natural selection. People inbreed animals all the time in labs to preserve genetic traits, and the results aren't as bad as we are led to believe. It's still not a good idea to inbreed, but it would work as long as the two source parents didn't have any major genetic flaws.

As far as how long it takes, this depends. One woman recently died after matching the record at 69 children in her life. This is an outlier obviously, but let's go with some assumptions. The two people are 18 years old, and the woman should be fertile for 25 years (no twins). Assuming some small gaps without children, let's say they give birth to 32 children. 30 of those survive to adulthood and are not sterile (50/50), and each woman makes another 32 children (30 who survive). You would have about 6-8 generations in 100 years or nearly 1 million people. So, this could happen quite rapidly.

Now, in reality, the genetic flaws will result in some number being excluded from breeding for health sake or not survive as a result of them, but it's quite feasible to have a very significant population in a few hundred years from only two people.
