eliminate1337 A:

Other people are getting this calculation wrong. You have to be careful with your assumptions.

Since you're very small relative to the size of the torus, the surface gravity can be approximated by finding the gravity of a cylindrical mass.

Surface gravity from the chunk right under you will be overwhelmingly stronger than effects from the rest of the torus, which are much further away.

To find the gravity of a cylinder, you must use the Gaussian formulation of gravity. Newton's law only works for spheres or other shapes at a long distance.

I'll skip the derivation, but you can find it here. The formula for gravity on a cylinder is: g = (2Gλ)/r, where λ is the linear mass density and r is distance.

I'm assuming your torus has the same density as Earth. So the linear mass density is: pi Earth's radius ^ 2 Earth's density.

Plugging in values: λ = 7.033×10^18 g/cm (calculation)

We already have G and r, so we just plug in everything else to find surface gravity:

g = 14.73 m/s^2 (calculation)

So gravity on this torus is a good bit stronger than gravity on Earth. This makes intuitive sense, because there's more mass near you standing on a cylinder than there is with a sphere of the same radius.

This is accurate within a few percent. Gravity from the very distance regions can be neglected.

I haven't sat down to do the maths, and won't have time today, but any point inside a charged spherical shell feels no net electric field (the close bits exert a larger force per chunk of shell, but the further away bits are more numerous, and they cancel out). I'm pretty sure that applies to gravity too [just checked, yes it does http://hyperphysics.phy-astr.gsu.edu/hbase/Mechanics/sphshell2.html].

A torus isn't a shell, but precisely on the inside edge it should have a similar balancing effect. You can float in that plain, within the inner edge. if you stray out of the plane (through the holes, so to speak) there will be a restoring force pushing you back.

[Edit]: I've spotted a flaw in my logic; the gravity decreases proportional to radius squared, while surface area of a sphere increases by that proportion. A circle circumference increases proportional to 2*the radius. So wouldn't scale as quickly.

RebelLemurs A:

It depends on the aspect ratio of the torus. If you have a relatively thin torus (Ring thickness << Ring Diameter) it would behave like /u/eliminate1337 suggests. It would be like living on a cylinder.

If you have a relatively fat torus (Ring thickness ~1/2x Ring Diameter) then you would be pulled to the center of mass. If you were standing on the inner diameter, you would fall "up."

Someone who was so inlined could calculate the aspect ratio at which the dominant mechanism changes.
