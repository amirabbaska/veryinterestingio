bulksalty A:

They buy businesses that have lost favor with investors and often that aren't being run efficiently. After they purchase them they make changes (which often involve increasing their debt).

Just like any investor, when they're right they make money (both profits during the time when they're running them) and usually a much larger profit when they sell the company, but when they're wrong they lose money.

Many times, the changes involve increasing the debt, because a company with more debt is both riskier (the firm is more likely to fail than one with little or no debt) but also more profitable to its owners (higher returns on equity than one with little or no debt). In the case of Toys R Us, they'll lose most of their investment (they make money when they are able to sell firms).

They don't exploit bankruptcy laws, allowing failed corporations or people to not repay their debts is literally the purpose of bankruptcy laws.

They're no more cartoonishly evil than everyone who owns stock in their 401(k) and votes, they just have enough money to buy the company in its entirety.
