Mjolnirsbear A:

So there are:

  • sounds we can make but don't notice (aspirated stops, nasal vowels, etc).

Example 1: There's a difference in pronunciation of the 'p' in both spot and pot. We don't notice, but other languages do.

Example 2: in English, we have both nasal vowels and non-nasal vowels. The 'o' in on and of are pronounced differently. We can't tell the difference. French can.

  • sounds we can make but only sometimes

Example 1: 'ng' at the end of a syllable we can pronounce just fine. Put it at the start of a word and we cock it up every time. 'mb' and 'ts' are other examples of this.

Example 2: the unstressed syllable, aka the schwa,which we use all the time but think is an o, u, e or a depending on the word

  • sounds we make up because our grammer doesn't work like that:

Example 1: a native English speaker always pronounces it "Shri" Lanka until they learn better

Example 2: a native Spanish speaker will always add 'eh' to sc- English words; eschool, escam, until they learn better

  • sounds that only exist in certain accents:

Example 1: rolled 'r' is common in Scots dialects

Example 2: glottal tap in certain British accents (i.e. Bottle becomes *bo'ul)

  • sounds we use only because of loanwords Example 1: ñ, señor for example

  • sounds that simply don't exist in English at all, which is actually (relatively) rare compared to sounds we make but don't recognize

Example 1: the Welsh double L

Example 2: the Spanish rolled double R

Example 3: the German glottal R

Example 4: the click

Example 5: è, a vowel from French
