Overunderrated A:

Am I wrong for thinking that the most optimal design would/could/should be used everywhere with the only difference being speed to control the airflow (CFM)?

Yes, your thinking is wrong here.

First up is, how do you define "optimal"? Any real world problem has multiple variables at play and there is no one "optimal" solution. You brushed up on a couple aspects:

air flow per speed and efficiency

Fan A might have more air flow, but fan B has better power efficiency. Which is "optimal"? Fan C might be better than both, but costs more to manufacture. Fan D might be better than all 3 in efficiency, flow rate, and cost, but is horribly loud. This is the basis of optimization: you first have to define what "optimal" means for your particular use case, and there's generally a lot of competing factors. They're often barely related by any physics, which is the basis of "multi-disciplinary design optimization", or MDO.

This is even separate from physics, where how fluids behave change dramatically based on scale and what you're trying to do. There's no reason why a 50 meter diameter wind turbine built to extract power from oncoming flow should look anything like an 80mm computer case fan.

vigillan388 A:

The problem is fans have a lot of parameters that they need to optimize for. A brief example list includes efficiency, airflow (CFM), variable speed operation (one fan may perform better across a range despite a lower peak efficiency, noise (sound is usually sacrificed for static or airflow) , and static pressure (force behind the air flow) . No single fan is the best at all of the conditions.

I work in HVAC and we use many different types of fans for various applications. Off the bat, there are probably 4 or 5 different arrangements I use on a weekly basis.
