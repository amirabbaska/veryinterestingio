EponymousHoward A:

There are multiple factors in any given year, but the fundamental reason is that the west coast has its prevailing winds (not all winds, all the time but the tendency) from the ocean (which holds heat quite well through winter). It is a west coast maritime climate, similar to western Europe). The east coast gets its prevailing winds from the same direction, but after travelling 3,000 miles over land - and land loses its heat in winter much faster than oceans do. It is an east coast continental climate. Think Korea/ Japan as a comparison. So on average winters in the west are warmer because of warmer water. In the east they are colder because of cold land.

That is what climate is, in essence: the annual average of weather, governed by where the winds take rain.

Incidentally, when the east coast gets east winds the storms are WAY fiercer for the same reason: warm water. Hello Harvey, hello Irma.

UlmusIgnobelia A:

You allude to the big answer which is that places which receive most of their weather from land have 'terrestrial' climates which are both hotter and colder than when one's predominant weather comes from the ocean.

More specifically, however, is the effect of mountains in the west, particularly the Rockies. In middle latitudes, most weather is going to come from the west, with variations between the south-west and the north-west.

The western mountains of North America disrupt this westerly flow allowing weather from the south and north to play a stronger part. This produces a lot of weather effects such as a particularly large number of tornadoes and the generally colder winter temperatures for those in from the Dakotas to the Atlantic.

Denver is close enough to the mountains that they don't get hit by this much.
