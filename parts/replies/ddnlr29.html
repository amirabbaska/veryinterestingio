    • CrateDane

      Without knowing anything about the subject, isn't the spectrum you see at the ground influenced more by what the atmosphere transmits than the stars emission?

      Well, both contribute (as does water, where photosynthesis originally evolved on Earth, and is presumably most likely to on other planets). But among the things that go through a more or less Earth-like atmosphere, the star determines the distribution of wavelengths.

      If you check this graph, you can see the difference between the light that reaches the surface of Earth, and what it would be if Earth was orbiting other types of stars. F2V is a hotter star, K2V is a cooler star and the M stars are much cooler.

      Our spectrum peaks in the red, but stays pretty high up through blue. Earth plants absorb some red and yellow, and lots of the higher-energy blue and violet light; green in-between is not absorbed so much (obviously).

      Around a hot star, the red graph, the peak is in the blue, falling off quickly at shorter wavelengths but extending into the red. Our plants probably wouldn't mind that spectrum much. Native plants could look like ours.

      But then look at the pink and green graphs of cool stars. There isn't even much red light, and nearly no blue. Our plants would hate it. Plants from such a planet would almost certainly use infrared light.
