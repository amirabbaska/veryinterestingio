• Mysthik

    I really liked one of the reasons Richard Dawkins gave in his book The Selfish Gene. A mother always knows that her offspring has 50% of her genes --- with a few exceptions like in the case of the cuckoo. So the mother and especially her genes have an interest in the survival of the children, so that the genes have a higher chance of survival and a higher chance to be passed down to new individuals.

    The father on the other hand does not have this guarantee. So it is in his interest to invest as little as possible into the children and mate as often as possible, to increase the chance of survival of his genes. If for example most of the males of a species would invest heavily into into their children, without knowing if they are actually theirs, another individual could exploit this behavior. The other individual invests nearly nothing and let the other males care for his offspring. Obviously something like this isn't stable, especially if the caring fathers have little or no offspring at all. The genes of the caring fathers would be removed from the gene pool over time and the genes of the non caring fathers would also become less frequent. A mostly caring father on the other hand, that invests just enough into his children that there is a good chance for them to survive, has a good chance that his genes will stay in the gene pool.

    • BrienneTheBadass

      This is called the "paternity certainty" modifier. We can see this in humans, actually.

      Maternal grandmothers are the best grandparents for helping out with her daughter's baby! The maternal grandfather has one layer of paternity uncertainty because his wife may have cheated on him. The paternal grandmother has one layer of uncertainty because her daughter in law may have cheated. The paternal grandfather has two layers because his wife and daughter in law may have cheated. We actually know from large-scale data now that the presence of a maternal grandmother has a greater impact on the survival of grandchildren than the presence the direct fathers has. Evolutionarily speaking, this data definitely supports the hypothesis that the evolution of menopause was advantageous because grandmothers are amazing alloparents (allowing humans to be born earlier and grow larger brains and produce more children) compared to more outdated ideas that men are the big providers of the family. Statistically, they don't have much of an effect on youths (though their presence may be important for older children, this is unconfirmed and in terms of evolution most child deaths occur before the age of 5 anyways).

      Paternity certainty definitely affects animal dynamics; species that are more sexually dimorphic tend to have greater "reproductive skew" (males have more/fewer offspring than females) and thus you'd expect that in species where the males/females look more different there should be less attentive fathers. Compare that to penguins, who are very monogamous and look very monomorphic and where fathers invest heavily in their offspring.

      On the OTHER hand, both male and female gibbons cheat like crazy even though they form life-bonds and fathers help raise children (and they're extremely monomorphic for primates). I guess the logic is that even if the fathers are investing in the infants of other males, another male may be taking care of theirs (I can't see any other way it would work).

        Random genetic mutation. A trait can pop up and if it's advantageous (or it just doesn't kill them) the animal might pass down the mutation. For instance, short-nosed dogs have a random mutation from normal dogs. Over many generations, humans have bred that mutation to extremes like the pug. It's the same idea with natural selection, but it takes much longer and is a lot less cleancut.
