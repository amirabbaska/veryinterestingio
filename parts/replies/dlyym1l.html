      • RobusEtCeleritas

        As they alluded to in their comment, there is more than one way to define x-rays and gamma rays. In some fields, you draw a totally arbitrary line along the EM spectrum, and call everything with an energy higher than that line a gamma ray.

        However in medical physics, they are defined by their origin and not by their energy. X-rays come from atomic transitions and bremsstrahlung, while gamma rays come from nuclear transitions and high-energy processes like e^(+)e^(-) annihilation. Using this definition, the set of possible x-rays and the set of possible gamma rays have a fairly large overlap in energy. There are x-rays with more energy than some gamma rays.

    Just wanted to point out that x-rays and gamma photos are distinctly different in terms of origin.

    The key difference between gamma rays and X-rays is how they are produced. Gamma rays originate from the nucleus of a radionuclide after radioactive decay whereas X-rays are produced when electrons strike a target or when electrons are rearranged within an atom.

  • stilley2

    What we can do though is put someone in a device surrounded by sensors, shoot a collimated x-ray beam at their body and see what direction the rays bounce off. Studying the deflection angles and moving the collimated beam around you can build up an incredibly detailed map of internal structure, this is how CAT scans work.

    This is not how CAT scans works. CAT scans (although more commonly refered to as CT scans now for whatever reason), are still imaging absorption. In other words, rays that bounce and change direction are not used (and in some applications are actually a problem). CT is similar to a simple radiograph, in that the principle measurement is the decrease in X-ray strength along a straight line from the X-ray source to a point on a detector. The diference is in CT the source and detector are rotated around the patient to collect this information from many angles, and the data is turned into a 3D volume image using computational methods.

    I believe that diffraction gratings can and have been used to do CT scans like you've suggested (i.e., measuring scatter), but it's not really my area so I don't want to speak too much on it. Although as far as I know it's far from clinical practice.
