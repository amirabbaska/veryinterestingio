    • keepcrazy

      So... your experience goes directly against the air pressure argument almost entirely.

      The air pressure drops only upon arrival of the front, or, more specifically, the moisture.

      When there is more moisture in the air, pressure drops. Basically, H2O = 2x hydrogen + 1 oxygen, which weighs less than 2x oxygen and 2x Nitrogen which make up most of the air. So, water weighs less than air. So, more water = less mass per volume = lower pressure.

      Also, the pressure drop amounts to little more than the pressure change of going from 4,200' to 4,500'. If you are in the west at 4,200', you probably experience that change just getting groceries as there are few large plains at that elevation.

      My guess would actually be that the cold that typically accompanies clear skies preceding a storm is the actual cause. The cold air needs to be warmed much more to bring to room temp and the resulting warm air will have a much lower relative humidity and this pulls water from your skin.

      After the storm arrives the source air is usually warmer and definitely wetter and this raises your indoor relative humidity enough to re-hydrate you, or at least slow the moisture loss so you can keep up.

      When you experience the "storm coming" aches, eat bananas and drink WAY more water and see if your superpowers wane.
