    Events like coronal mass ejections (CMEs) and other events like supernova, collisions, etc. can create what we'd call "space weather". Whereas on Earth, we're mostly concerned with temperature and precipitation because our atmosphere allows it, in space the weather we care most about has to do with radiation exposure because it can damage equipment and our bodies. The amount of radiation has to do with your local climate, for example, you'll receive less radiation in you're very close to earth, and shielded inside its magnetic field but you get heaps of radiation if you fly through the Van Allen belts or into deep space. If the sun releases a CME that hits our planet, you'll get a transient "weather" effect where radiation spikes wherever you are, but proportional too the local climate you're in.

    NASA actually has probes monitoring space weather in our solar system to help keep our satellites, probes, and astronauts safe.

      The Van Allen belts are within Earth's magnetosphere. Also, you don't get radiation spikes associated with CME impact. The solar flare that precedes a CME might blast Earth, but the CME causes Earth's magnetic field to go wonky (scientific term) and eventually dump a bunch of plasma nearby. conveniently, since the CME itself delivers a bunch of plasma besides the magnetic wonkiness, you get a lot of those particles making it all the way to the ionosphere and causing light shows and such.

      Space weather isn't just concerned with the radiation emmited by the sun (or galactic cosmic rays), but also plasma precipitation mechanisms that get kicked off by the sun. This includes plasma transport from the sun to the near earth plasma populations as well as magnetic field orientation.

      The two pirmary terrestrial space weather concerns are radiation exposure over the poles and radio communication conditions. For satellite operators, they care about those energetic particles buzzing around Earth in the ring current and the belts, since those particles are directly responsible for the damage satellites constantly sustain in space.
