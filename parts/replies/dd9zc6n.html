        It's not the language.

        Not guilty means the same as innocent.

        However, in court all one has to be is not proven guilty. That's a completely different thing.

        "Not proven guilty" does not mean the same thing as "not guilty", but it is essentially what a not guilty verdict means, and that's what UK and US law take "not guilty" to mean.

        This is referred to in the presumption of innocence.

      Again, the problem OP is mentioning arises from something else.

      First, OP is mentioning a saying that's translated from Latin and not citing an actual lawful article. Secondly, English uses both, "not guilty" and "innocent" to describe the same thing. The two words come from two etymologically completely different corners. "Innocent" from Latin, and "guilty" from Old English. German, on the other hand, uses in this case only words from the same origin: "unschuldig/nicht schuldig". So, when you translate the same saying from Latin into German, OP's problem cannot come into existence.

      Such is life with translations.
