    Also, something people have to take into consideration, especially when it comes to teaching is to not determine whether they did a good job or not based on their student's standardized test scores. It may sound crazy, but hopefully it makes more sense as you read. Take a poor school district like Philadelphia, I grew up in this school district and I'm going to teach in it. Depending on what part of the city you're in ALL your students may come from low income families who only have one parent working. It takes a village to raise a child, not just one parent and one teacher. Learning obviously doesn't only take place in school. Parents should try to be involved in their child's education as much as possible because it will only serve to benefit them. However, when your mom/dad is working several jobs to put food on the table, other more important things take priority. They won't have time to review their child's homework with them, read to them or be involved in their education. These are the types of things that teachers can't help with. There's only so much a teacher can do with the 8 hours they have with this child. They can't make sure the student gets enough sleep or food before they get to school. A tired starving student couldn't care less about education when their basic needs aren't being met. In turn, their grades don't have high chances of being great and you can't fault a teacher for factors that are out of their hand.

    Edit: I know you put that comment out there, so that we can come up with different ways to measure they're doing a good job or not. I just wanted to hopefully prevent any, "Let's just determine whether a teacher is good or not by using standardized test scores!" comments.
