    • Fen_

      The stuff that's actually cool about this is all hidden behind methods he didn't explain. It's going to be some combination of histogram equalization methods, probably. Basically, the image has a few different spectra (red, blue, green), and the image has been modified so that the histograms of these spectra are compressed and/or translated to a different range of the spectra than they originally occupied. If you can invert those transformations, you get the original image. I don't know if that's what he ultimately did (in a direct way), but I think that's how the correction is solved.

  • elsjpq

    You don't even need white, just any three primaries whose colorspace somewhat contains the image colors. I made this using only grey and two different shades of blue and you can still see red. The problem is that red is a relatively "bright" color so you need at least one bright primary like cyan, yellow, or green to dither it with.

    White isn't even really a color, and I don't just mean that in "well technically..." kind of way. The "white point" is kind of an arbitrary color that you reference to compare other colors to. Since you can choose and change your color reference, this white point isn't constant, so even the concept of "white" as a single color is kind of fuzzy.

    If you really want to get precise about color, you'll have to look at spectral power distributions which measure which wavelengths of photons actually exist, but then this has very little relation to color as we perceive it.
