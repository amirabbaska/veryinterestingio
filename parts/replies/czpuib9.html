      • Aelinsaar

        It's just one of those things. Two factors would be most at work I think, the first would be the general acidity of dark chocolate. Acid has a "drying" effect on the palette, and is the crisp, fresh sensation from a good dry wine. (Note that dry in this sense doesn't mean devoid of moisture, but has to do with sugar)

        The other would be tannin, the same thing that makes the skin of an apple so... something indescribable beyond tart. It's the stuff in wine and most fruit skins that have that same quality, and dark chocolate has it too.

        The actual mechanism is that tannin makes certain signals in your mouth think it's got a full loud of saliva, so it shuts down production. You breath, and it actually DOES dry your mouth out. It gives you that sharp, "Puckering" feeling, you know what I mean?

        The opposite of that effect, "drying" is a coating effect. The result of a coat of lipids and/or sugars on your tongue is to release lots of saliva. So, in essence, we get off on shifting back and forth between extremes in our palette. We like to shift from full salivation, to no salivation, and back. It's like getting that first taste of a good steak, in theory, you maximize that sensation with this process.
