    There's an important distinction to be clarified here.

    Theories like Kaluza-Klein do not "predict" the presence of extra dimensions (spatial or otherwise). What they instead do is start from the assumption that there are additional spatial dimensions, with this assumption then used as a basis to calculate the physical consequences of such extra dimensions.

    That is, the extra dimensions are inserted into a given theory "by hand", with the theory then developed to allow experimentally observable effects/properties to be predicted. If those predictions (i) deviate from the equivalent predictions made in conventional 3+1 dimensional spacetime, and (ii) agree with experimental measurements, then this provides support for - but not necessarily explicit confirmation of - the presence of extra dimensions.

    This is then distinct from being a prediction of the presence of extra dimensions. For example, different theories having different numbers of dimensions could, in principle, provide the same predictions for certain measurable effects/properties, so that agreement with experimental data alone might not enable the "correct" number of dimensions to be identified on the basis of any two given such theories.

    The fundamental distinction lies in the interpretation of physical models: it is important to bear in mind that theory generally develops phenomenologically, as a series of more and more "exact" descriptions of a given physical system with the aim being to have predictions be consistent with as much experimental data as possible. However, such consistency does not unambiguously imply that the axioms and/or assumptions that underpin a given theory are objectively "correct" or "true".

    I like this think of it in terms that theoretical physics gives us a set of tools that can - when interpreted within the proper context/limitations of the model(s) under consideration - describe "how" physical systems behave, but do not tell us, on the most fundamental level, "why" they behave in such a manner.
