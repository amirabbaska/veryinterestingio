    I think this is the key point here, though.

    It's pretty easy to say that "the moon orbits the earth" and that "the earth orbits the sun", because they are gravitationally bound to their parent body and will never leave this orbit because they just don't have enough energy to esacpe.

    However, this whole idea of an "orbit" does get muddied very hard in many-body systems and on larger scales. We say that "Pluto and Charon and the other moons orbit a common barycenter", but what does that mean, really? They all follow almost chaotic paths and the only reason that system doesn't rip itself apart is because it fell into a resonance (probably by ejecting quite a few other moons). Is "orbiting" really a good word for that?

    And on larger scales, the concept of an "orbit" pretty much stops working. Galaxies are buzzing around pretty chaotically within their clusters, probably never passing through the same position twice or completing something that we would call "one orbit".

    On even larger scales, this stops working entirely because dark energy starts to dominate and have a much bigger impact than "the whole universe" around it.

    That's why i think that formulating a thing that's the "center of gravity of the entire universe" is pretty meaningless.

    I also believe that the word "orbit" works for certain situations but misses the point in other situations.
