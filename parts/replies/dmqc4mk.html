    • Gargatua13013

      Pretty much, within bounds of available data and age constraints. The main quibble there is with the age of ocean floor - these are young rocks, the oldest remaining ocean floor we've currently got is a mere 225 million years old, which is a side effect of Plate tectonics in that oceanic plates are preferentially destroyed. Pangea broke up 65 million years ago, but the previous supercontinent is Rodinia, which assembled a billion years ago, and broke up in earnest around 600 million years ago. So we've got no ocean floor from that period on which we could use the matching coastlines / symmetrical magnetic polarity bands and age of ocean floor methods. So it still works and it's the same rationale, but the basic data is pretty all paleopoles and paleo-wander paths.

      More info on the reconstruction of Rodinia and following paleogeographic pervulsions: Li, Z. X., Evans, D. A., & Halverson, G. P. (2013). Neoproterozoic glaciations in a revised global palaeogeography from the breakup of Rodinia to the assembly of Gondwanaland. Sedimentary Geology, 294, 219-232.
