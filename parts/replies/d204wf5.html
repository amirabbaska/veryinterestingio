    • RoboticElfJedi

      First, it's worth pointing out that 'metals', in an astrophysical context, basically means anything heavier than helium. Any element made inside a star is a metal, so that includes oxygen and nitrogen for instance.

      The simple answer to your question is that metals usually end up as microscopic solid grains which we call dust. They might be anything from a few molecules to a few microns in diameter. It depends on the local environment how much chemistry is possible (I.e. whether it's cold enough for molecules to form), but for instance astronomers look for the emissions from carbon monoxide (CO) to map out molecular clouds in galaxies.

      There's all sorts of stuff swirling around out there. Google image searching some images of galactic dust shows just how much there is out there.
