    None of us are perfect.

    But doing it a few thousand times makes it no big deal.

    At this point in my career I get to care about exactly where I set the wheels down. For instance, I don't like to set the nose wheel down right on top of the runway lights, because they protrude ever so slightly from the runway surface, and make a "bump" that annoys me, so I set the nose wheel down an inch or two to the side of the runway lights (incidentally, the runway stripes are usually offset from the lights).

    The point is that when I was an 800 hour wonder, I wouldn't even have noticed nuances like that. But now with many times that experience I've gained the experience and insight to care about such silly things.

    • allemande

      There are margins of course, but you're obviously aiming for something, so as you steer off from what you want you make corrections and get yourself on the right path.

      If at a certain point in your approach you're X amount off it usually means you don't land or trying to land is a risky judgement call.

      By the time you get to let's say 5-10 seconds of landing and you know you're landing it's because you've worked your way well withing the margins of what's considered safe and acceptable.
