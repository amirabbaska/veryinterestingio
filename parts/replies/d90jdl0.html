      Yeah I originally had a bit in my answer contrasting Japanese and European sword making but I cut it out for brevity.

      Damascus swords were known to have plant fiber burned in with the steel (intentionally), which I have heard as the explanation for how they were able to consistently produce carbon nanotubes. I don't buy the idea that it was an accident, as that contains a bit of the common modern condescension for the past, as if the people back then were less intelligent (they weren't), or less knowledgeable (with regards to swordmaking, they weren't), and doesn't jive with the consistency of the sword quality.

      They didn't know the chemistry behind carbon nanotubes, but swordmaking back then was a very empirical process, and the beautiful thing about that aspect of science is that you don't have to understand something to make it usable.

      Also, at cutting competitions with Katanas it is not unusual for Katanas to be bent, and then be manually straightened out. Even with the lamination they just aren't springy like European swords, which had to deal with fencing, parrying, and generally heavier armor, which would bend and break stiffer blades. Japanese sword fighting is all about the big, killing slash, even against armored opponents.
