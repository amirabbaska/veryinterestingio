    If we're going to be pedantic about it, right-to-work laws don't just affect how dues are deducted from pay or not; they also prohibit union-security agreements, which make union dues collectible in the first place even from non-members. In states that allow union-security agreements, while there may be no "closed shop", if you are compelled to pay the union anyway, then there is little practical difference from being in a closed shop to begin with.

    "What about Right to Work states?

    27 states have banned union-security agreements by passing so-called "right to work" laws. In these states, it is up to each employee at a workplace to decide whether or not to join the union and pay dues, even though all workers are protected by the collective bargaining agreement negotiated by the union. "

    https://www.nlrb.gov/rights-we-protect/employerunion-rights-and-obligations

    You might then ask what a union-security agreement is.

    "A union security agreement is a contractual agreement, usually part of a union collective bargaining agreement, in which an employer and a trade or labor union agree on the extent to which the union may compel employees to join the union, and/or whether the employer will collect dues, fees, and assessments on behalf of the union.[1]"

    • rdavidson24

      Right. But this is ELI5, yes?

      Federal law is clear that employers are completely prohibited from agreeing to hire only union members (i.e., create a "closed shop"), but can agree to require new hires to either join the union or pay agency fees without joining (i.e., create a "union shop"). That choice can't be taken away from them. Employees don't have to join unions if they don't want to. They can only be forced to pay "agency fees," and only if state law permits it.

      Right-to-work laws don't permit it.
