    Aw, man. The problem here is that Shakespeare is taught as literature, rather than what it was: community theater. Shakespeare was never supposed to be the boring, stuffy monolith that academics have made him into. In his day, he was crude, silly, a little bit of an asshole, and a public sensation. If he were alive today, he'd probably be writing satirical pieces about Brexit or some shit. The hundreds of plays he wrote (most of which have been lost because, hey, he didn't write them to be read hundreds of years later) set the stage for modern storytelling, modern language even. His "fantasy language" made pompous English literature accessible to he general public, and practically invented modern English by revolutionizing spoken word. As for the characters lacking depth, a lot of that was a satirical comment on how he saw society-- everyone is stupid and looking out for their own interests, even though their interests aren't really that important in the big picture. That shit was groundbreaking for his era, and people ate it up.

    Shakespearean history is so fun and fascinating, but students end up hating it because academics make him out to be some posh aristocrat. I don't blame you for being annoyed by it.

    Another good answer (and the ones above are great) is that Shakespeare is the ultimate exercise in complex reading comprehension. It's super difficult to discern what's actually being said sentence for sentence, you need to study the words, look them up, rearrange them to make sense, etc.

    I took several college classes dedicated solely to breaking down and understanding Shakespeare (I was a theatre major) and I couldn't have dreamed the advancement they would lend to my all around reading comprehension. It's like going back to playing a video game on Easy after going through it on Survival mode.

    So that's an added benefit of studying Shakespeare.

    Lots of people "defending" Shakespeare are defending him in factually inaccurate ways*, so here's a blunt assessment from an English major and former teacher:

    • He wrote a ton of stuff. Nearly 40 plays that we're sure are (mostly) his work. And his buddies put a collection of them together after he died which makes it easy.
    • His work has variety: all the way from low comedies (the sitcoms of his time) to great tragedies that reduced people to tears.
    • His work also spans stories based on history, stories based on myths and legends, and stuff he appears to have made up (though very little of that last one)
    • He was massively popular in his time. He was not some weird loner who sat in his bedroom brooding on the perfect sentence. He was a working writer, actor and business man, who revised his plays if the gags didn't work and added topical jokes and references. His life was more like a standup/improv comedian than a pure literary "writer".
    • He has cool plots: ghosts, murders, political assassinations, hilarious crap with identical twins and girls who dress as boys, fairies and witches and so on. All of the stuff that we like to watch on TV right now.
    • And, you know, fart jokes and bad puns. Nothing is off limits. Bonus: the puns help us understand how words were pronounced back then.
    • We know almost nothing about his private life, (not even his birthday) so we get to project all kinds of shit onto him and imagine whatever we want.
    • This unfortunately includes people who like to imagine conspiracy theories because he was a poor boy from the country so they can't see how he can have been so brilliant.

    Hope this helps.

    * No, he did not invent lots of common English words or sayings. This belief is caused by a failure to understand the workings of the Oxford English Dictionary. Though a lot of his neat phrases have become clich√©s.
