    Great answer. I just want to add something out w/r/t the first kind, which is really what most people mean when they talk about "tolerance."

    Let's say you take some opioids. Let's say three things happen: nausea, euphoria, and constipation. (Those are pretty classic responses to opioids.) You will gain tolerance to these three things, but at different rates, and to different degrees, because the body's feedback response to these three things happens in different places, via different means, even though the response is to the exact same chemical. You'll gain tolerance to the nausea very, very rapidly. You'll develop tolerance to the euphoria more slowly. And you might not ever develop tolerance to the constipation, so if you keep increasing the dose to maintain the euphoria, the constipation is going to get worse and worse.

    A different example might be caffeine. Everybody's heard that if you drink a cup of coffee it dehydrates you. Well, maybe the first time. This is an effect you gain tolerance to more rapidly than you gain tolerance to the stimulating properties. If you're a long-term, heavy coffee drinker, you might get slightly less stimulation out of it, but there'll be very little diuretic effect (diuretic means it makes you pee), and you'll get more water from drinking coffee than you lose.

        Just a quick note. Some of the fat soluble vitamins (E and K) are not that hard to overdue. And way over doing it with the water soluble ones, like you say, can be tough on the kidneys.

        An interesting mechanism that causes problems when people are over zealous to tune the knobs of nutrition is the idea of disturbing enzyme co-factor equilibria. Most essential vitamins and minerals are important because they get incorporated into proteins as accessories that help the protein perform some reaction. Zinc, Manganese, Magnesium, Iron, Selenium (this actually gets build into a specialized Amino Acid, which is cool) are all metals that are used by various enzymes and proteins for different reasons.

        But if you saturate the body with one substance that is at all similar in size, shape, and reactivity it is possible for that substance to replace the proper co-factor and then the proper one will be excreted since it's not needed. This is more common for metals since, obviously, they are less distinguishable than many vitamins.

        Think of it this way. You're the enzyme for eating soup - so you're designed (evolved, whatever) with a special notch for holding a spoon. Now if you're in the body of an over zealous health nut that fills the system with forks...the fork will fit nearly as well into your spoon notch, but it will not be very helpful for eating your soup.
