    What's the significance of there being another A? Why can't you name it A#? F G A A# C D

    • zeekar

      Because of tradition and practicality, a scale - which means a key - consists of all seven note names, each one either flat, natural, or sharp.

      One reason for that is that our standard musical notation, using the staff, only has positions corresponding to those 7 note names (in multiple octaves). You can get a sharp or flat by explicitly putting a ♯ or ♭ next to the note head on the staff, but that gets tedious if you have to do it every time. With a standard key that has all seven note names, all you have to do is once, at the start of the staff, indicate which notes are sharp or flat; that's called a "key signature". Then you only have to mark individual notes when they deviate from that pattern.

      If a key had both A and A♯, you couldn't do that; you'd have to mark every single A note as either natural (♮) or sharp (♯). You'd also have the B line sitting there unused, since there are no B's in that version of the F key. But in the standard F key, you just put a ♭ on the staff line that corresponds to B, and you're done.
