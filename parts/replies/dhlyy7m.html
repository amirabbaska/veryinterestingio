    Can you refer me to more info on how carbs affect metabolism of electrolytes?

    • brunchlyfe

      Search for "refeeding syndrome." Carol Rees Parrish has a nice article that I refer to a lot in the Journal of Practical Gastroenterology.

      ELI5 answer: Potassium is an intracellular ion (inside the cells), sodium is extracellular (outside the cells, hanging out in the blood). For cells to suck up glucose (carbs in the blood) there's a shift between sodium and potassium; sodium moves out of the cells and potassium moves in. This drops the potassium levels in the blood lower.

    What would happen if a malnourished person ate a large meal (like a typical Christmas dinner)? Would their body be overloaded with electrolytes?

    • brunchlyfe

      While this would be unlikely (a malnourished person that we see in a developed country is usually malnourished as a result of disease and they don't want to eat a whole lot) a few things would probably happen. The digestive system will likely have slowed down and will be sluggish, so stomach emptying will probably be slow and they may feel nauseated. The GI tract lining, which contains certain enzymes to break down food, has probably atrophied a bit, so later they may have some bloating or diarrhea, especially with dairy. A Christmas dinner is usually robust in carbs, so yes, they may have some drops in potassium or phosphorus once the meal has emptied out of the stomach and is being absorbed into the blood stream. Some symptoms may be feeling like your heart is skipping, feeling very weak, or confusion.
