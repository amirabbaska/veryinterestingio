    • venona

      If I remember correctly, the cost of sequencing the whole genome (well) is still hovering around $1000 - more than a typical consumer would likely be willing to pay. By well, I refer to coverage - how many times the sequencer picks up a given location. The greater the coverage, the more reliable the result. There are low-coverage whole genome tests available for cheap to consumers, but the accuracy of those actually ends up being about the same as genotyping that I mentioned earlier.

    • Haposhi

      There is more data about Europe, giving a clearer picture. Most other continents have had lots of mixing too, with only a few isolated islands or regions with long-term 'closed' genepools.

      Where there has been mixing, you just need more sample points. Some markers might be shared between french and german populations, for example, so these aren't enough by themselves. But by looking at other many markers which are are more common in one or the other you can get a very accurate prediction. Those living near the border where there was recent mixing might just come out as 30% french, 70% german or something.
