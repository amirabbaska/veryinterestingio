  • dkf295

    Since this was all beyond the grasp of the average computer user who wanted stuff to "just work", the coalition beyond USB didn't really consider their experience

    But the rest of your point indicates that they did consider the user experience. And they absolutely did consider the experience and conduct trials.

    You seem to forget how different computers were. As users were 100% used to always having connectors that were NOT symmetrical, were big and bulky, and often involved fitting pins in holes (Remember PS/2 connectors?), users of the day would NOT have approached this the same way as users even 10 years later. They would look at the connector, look at the hole, line it up carefully, and insert it.

    To take the next step to say "Okay when this standard becomes the new standard on every computer and everyone uses it, what sorts of new devices are going to come out and how do we keep the experience good" is a valid question and one that was considered - They just didn't think 25+ years ahead about what sorts of features people might want then, and what sorts of devices people might use. Much less what manufacturers would do with the standard.

    The original intention was for USB-B connectors to be used for peripheral-side disconnectable devices such as removable disk drives, which are much easier to quickly orient and connect. The smallest peripherals at the time were USB floppy drives and even then, those were a good inch and a half to two inches thick. Even a device half that thickness would be better served by a larger connector like USB-B versus Mini-USB (which they DID think about) from a usability standpoint.

    Even things like USB flash drives and MP3 players might as well have been science fiction.
