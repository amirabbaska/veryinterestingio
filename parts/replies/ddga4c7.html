    • GodsDelight

      Credit cards do this too. The 15 digits of your card identifies the card, while the last digit checks the validity of your card number. Try this:

      1. Multiply each of the 1st, 3rd, 5th, 7th, ect. digit of you credit card number by 2. (eg. 6: 6*2=12)

      2. If the number after multiplication is 10 or greater, add up the two digits. (eg. 12: 1+2=3)

      3. Add all the 2nd, 4th, 6th, ect. digits of your card with the values you got in #2 for each of the 1st, 3rd, 5th, 7th, ect. digits.

      4. The sum that you get will add up to a number divisible by 10. The 16th digit is there for that very purpose.

      This is how the bank knows whether you got the wrong credit card number vs you got the wrong password. Otherwise, you'd be freezing other people's credit card accounts every time you accidentally type a number other than your own.

  • WrithyTane

    Just to add on to this answer, since CDs are read as the disc spins, scratches that follow the curve of the disk tend to be quite a bit more serious than scratches that radiate from the centre like wheel spokes. It's harder for the CD player to compensate when the missing bits are right next to each other. With wheel spoke scratches, there's the opportunity of only fucking up one bit at a time.
