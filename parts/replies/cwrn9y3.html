    our eyes only became sensitive to the part of the spectrum that illuminates the earth most brightly

    That's true, but not the whole story. The question is, if the sun had been hotter or cooler, i.e. emitted more UV or IR than "visible light", would life have evolved to become sensitive to that part of the spectrum instead? I don't think so. One issue is that UV is so energetic that it damages living things, while IR is probably too low in energy for the electrochemical processes that mediate vision (as we know it). Another issue is the absorption curve of water, life's valuable and abundant substance. It blocks the more dangerous parts of UV, and also IR, but is conveniently transparent around the visible range. So, quite a few things turned out to be "just right".

        Fish can see IR

        Citation, please. Humans can actually see IR, too, under special conditions (see first sentence here). But it's better to call that NIR (near-infrared, the part adjacent to visible red), because IR is a huge range of the spectrum. As far as I know, there are no animals that can see IR other than a bit of NIR, like humans.

        insects and some animals can see UV

        Some fish, too. But again, that's UVA, the part immediately next to our visible range. UVA actually penetrates water better than NIR or even visible red (see the absorption curve), which is why underwater photos tend to lack red and have a greenish/bluish tint without correction.

        Maybe some animals can also see a tiny bit of UVB, the part of UV that some animals including us need for vitamin D synthesis, and that causes tanning and sunburns. But definitely not UVC and shorter wavelengths, as those cause severe damage.

        So the human slice of the spectrum isn't universally used for sight on Earth, never mind on other planets.

        It pretty much is universal for sight on Earth, with a bit of wiggle room to either side, but not much.
        As for other planets, who can say... but if you knew the star's output and the composition of the planet's atmosphere, you could probably make a good guess. And if life there was water-based, its visible range would likely be similar to ours, because of the physical facts.

        With big enough "eyes", presumably on a low gravity planet, it would be possible to "see" RF.

        That's why I wrote "vision (as we know it)". Radio waves are far too low in energy to activate the opsins that convert light to nerve signals. There is no actual IR opsin. Again, if you have any sources that say otherwise, please tell me.
