      • Spsteam

        What sort of policies did you have in mind? Are we talking about subsidizing these retrofits? Would that make sense over investing in renewable energy?

        To answer one of your questions, CO2 emissions are not connected to SCRs. Selective Catalytic Reduction system are a emissions control device which reduce the amount of NOx (a by product from the coal combustion) emitted to the atmosphere.

        Most of the U.S. coal fleet was built withought many environmental controls for NOx, SO2, Particulate Matter and Mercury). Over the years, as EPA has promulgated new rules limiting the amount of these pollutants that can be released. Each time a new rule goes into effect the plant owner(s) need to decide if it more profitable to retrofit the coal plant and add the controls, and operate it for many more years, or shutter the plant and call it day. Each new EPA regulation has seen a wave of shuttered plants (or repowered to fire natural gas which don't have these emissions). The Clean Power Plan, which was just gutted by Scott Pruitt, would have added CO2 to the earlier list of pollutants. If that rule stood, then owners would have had to make the decision to invest in CCS or shutter. To reduce the costs to the plant owners who wanted to deploy CCS, they would receive a payment for each tonne of CO2 that was captured and stored.

        The scenario planning and modeling suggested the most of the older, smaller, and less frequently used coal fired power plants would cease operation, and the larger, newer plants would deploy CCS. We'll all have to wait for the dust to settle at EPA and with several House and Senate bills related to CCS to see what the future holds.
