    This answer sounds backwards to me.

    The problem with thinking that the vestibular system evolved in the ears because that's where the body experiences the smallest aceeleration is that there is no reason to propose that the head experiences smaller acceleration... unless the vestibular system is located in the head. Even then, I'd argue that the chest experiences less acceleration than the head.

    One piece of evidence for that is that the vestibular system is located in the inner ears for most mammals. Think not only humans, but also monkeys, chimps, rats, and sloths. These animals have largely different body structures and their heads can't all experience the same types of acceleration, even with respect to the rest of their bodies. If you assume that sloths spend most of their time hanging on trees, then their heads experience more acceleration than their bodies.

    I don't know of any study for how the sense of equilibrium came to be, but if I had to hypothesize, based on ToE, I'd say it's probably located where the body is able to retrieve the most important pieces of information from: it tells you where your head is going and the orientation of what you are seeing.
