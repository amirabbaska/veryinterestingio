      Yes, measuring a particule constraints the future.

      As an approximation, when the particle is not yet measured, it is in a 'quantum superposition of states', and the action of measuring 'force' the particle to 'choose' a state. And if you measure again, the result will be the same, you've destroyed the quantum state and are now in a classical state.

      But in truth, it is even worse than that, since rules of quantum mechanics say that you cannot know with precision both the position and the speed of a particle. So if you find a way to measure the speed, the act of measuring it will mess up with the position and you will no longer know where the particle is.

      The whole problem of quantum physics is that it is impossible to measure things without interfering. You cannot say "I measure its position now, and I measure it in 5s, and I will be able to deduce it's average speed" because the act of doing the first measurement will mess up everything and you will not be able to make a second measurement that has a meaning.
