    This is the most correct answer, but let me attempt to translate to address your questions directly:

    Is the p-value a good representation of the quality of the work?

    No.

    As above, it tries to measure how significant an event is, given a model. If an event is unlikely to be caused by chance, then the p-value is small. So many scientific papers use a p-value to attempt to show that the thing they're looking at is a real effect, not just some fluke.

    Actually showing this with some rigor can be challenging, as above.

    Why scientists say people are misusing it?

    Mostly because it's difficult to define without some stats background and so people may use it to attempt to use it to justify something it doesn't do. E.g. "Wow that study just said chocolate was good for weight loss! They had a p value of .001!".

    As you can see, saying something like that doesn't even make sense, once you know what a p-value does and is.

    I agree with all answers in this post. I learned in long discussions with (statistically more adept colleagues) that some of my conceptions about p-values were not correct and I wanted to share some of my insights in addition to sciklops post:

    1. The very notion of significance is commonly interpreted as a Type I error rate, i.e. in the case of p < 0.05 a 5% probability to reject a true null hypothesis. This is not the case. In fact one should be very careful to talk about probabilities when discussing p-values. Most of it is wrong from my experience (see point 3 for an explanation when it is appropriate to talk about p-values and probabilities). In fact p-values are a measure from a frequentist point of view, which always gives only statements about the expected distribution of p-values. For decision making ( i.e. discussion / comparisons of models in terms of probabilities) it is necessary to describe your problem using Bayesian statistics (with all its hurdles to define prior functions of your current knowledge).

    2. As mentioned in the first point (and by sciklops) , discussions of a p-value are about the expected distribution of p-values in a large ensemble of equally conducted experiments, given that the null hypthesis is true. It is important to acknowledge that the p-value itself is a test-statistics and the value you calculate using your statistical model is only one possible value it can take (The p-value is a random variable !), see e.g. [1] for a pedagogical introduction to p-values with an focus on its probabilistic nature .

    3. I found it helpful to focus on some technical aspects of the p-value definition to understand its correct interpretation better: The p-value is a test statistics which is defined(!) to be uniformly distributed given the null hypothesis is true. This means it is a good test to create some pseudo-experiments (create random numbers within your uncertainties and replace your data with it). This will give you a distribution of p-values. If you dice a sufficient number of pseudo-experiments your p-values should be uniformly distributed between 0 and 1. Otherwise your model is flawed in the first place. If you reached enough trust in the statistical modelling of the null hypothesis and collected p-values from a set of equally sensitive experiments you may evaluate if it is likely that the observed distribution of p-values is described by a uniform distribution (here one may start to talk about the probability that the distribution describes a uniform distribution, see point 1)
