    Breeder here also. It's really not necessary to use transgenics to fix the Cavendish problem--it's fairly easy to induce uneven chromosome number for seedlessness (e.g. watermelons, colchicine). Introgressing wild sources of resistance, fruit quality, etc. can be challenging, but really isn't the main problem. Everything in breeding is a numbers game, and given the amount of natural variation that is available, we can definitely breed a better banana. What we're missing is the funding/push that agronomy has: if we really had a huge banana initiative (read, lots of funding) for WGS and development of molecular markers, we could quite feasibly overcome linkage drag. Another factor to consider is that since Cavendish is so widely planted, it would take a LOT of vegetative propagation and starting over for growers. We see this a lot with horticultural crops like apple--there are many cultivars out there with superior fruit quality and disease resistance compared to the standard Red Delicious, Granny Smith, Fuji, etc., but growers are (quite reasonably) unwilling to start over with new scions, wait years to reach commercial production levels, and so on. So there is a preference for more management inputs rather than a change in genetics. BUT, basically, there's not enough demand for cultivar improvement to change the status quo now. Probably depends a lot on how Fusarium develops in the next few years, given climate change and banana management practices.
