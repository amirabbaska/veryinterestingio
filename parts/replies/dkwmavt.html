      • Coomb

        Bulk samples of polymer will never be 100% crystalline. The crystalline and amorphous phases of any given polymer have differing refractive indices, so there is scattering of light off the boundaries between the crystalline and glassy regions. This is what causes increasing opacity with increasing crystallization.

    • umbertounity82

      The opacity in crystalline polymers is caused by light scatterting at the boundaries between the crystalline and amorphous regions. Therefore, 50% crystallinity is the most opaque. The material would become more transparent as you increase OR decrease crystallinity from 50%.

      Keep in mind there are other microstructural features that can scatter light besides crystalline/amorphous boundaries. Light can scatter off of spherulites or crazes (a special case of cracks unique to polymers) as well. In fact, the whitening observed in polymers after straining them (e.g. bending a plastic fork) is generally caused by the formation of crazes rather than stress-induced crystallization. Not all polymers crystallize under stress.
