    Anything is "testable" though not necessarily in a scientifically rigorous fashion.

    All you need to do is develop a hypothesis about an event and then wait to see if that hypothesis turns out to be right or wrong.

    However, because this hypothesis was based around a real-world event rather than something performed in a lab it can only be done once with the exact same set of circumstances.

    So, for instance, you can hypothesize that Candidate A will win an election and then wait to see if you are right or wrong but you can never replicate that exact same election nor can you modify just one or two variables to see what effect doing so has on the outcome.

    To get around this, you can use thing like polls or focus groups but those are unreliable in their own ways.

    Another way would be to look at case studies, which is often how political scientists like to study an issue. So we might ask "Are sanction effective in changing the behavior of a nation?" And we can look at numerous historical examples and attempt to draw conclusions. But, again, we can't draw any firm conclusions because we can't re-run those case studies.

    So, in a strict sense of the term, you can test a hypothesis but it would in no way be scientific.

    There are lots of testable (read: falsifiable) things in political science! Political science is a social science, so the comment below is a bit disingenuous to say that it is not scientifically rigorous, though if you don't believe in social science then that's a whole other thing I suppose. If you're american, the election seems to be the most pertinent thing at the moment so I'll talk about that briefly. In social science, you can either perform case studies, which look in-depth at few events, or analytical testing which uses wide ranges of data to come to conclusions. Say you want to understand whether a certain party controlling the white house for longer makes it more likely that they will hold it in the future. For a case study, you might want to look at the republican dominance in the late 20th century and see how clinton bucked the trend. You might look at campaign strategy, opinion polls of clinton and bush 41, general trends in the electorate. (clinton's southern strategy is fascinating) in a more analytical study, you might look at incumbency in general and how long that might have an advantage for, and how voter preferences change. out of this kind of study came the time-for-change model which is a staple in electoral theory and, at the outset of this election, gave a 2/3 chance to ANY republican presidential candidate who ran. I wish I could give better examples, but I hope this gives some insight into that.

    It is true that IRL it's hard to disentangle the various factors and noise that comes from the messiness of real life, but dismissing this as impossible brushes away a lot of amazing research.

    There are also some other "laws" (more like statistical norms) that exist in political science. For example, Duverger's law states that any governmental system that is first-past-the-post, i.e. winner takes all, will converge into a two party system. this is practically an eventuality in any FPTP system.
