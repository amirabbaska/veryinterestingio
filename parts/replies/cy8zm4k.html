      I think you have a very good point! Why do we get ticklish?

      I could imagine that without a tickle reflexes, touching other people could be risky. You come up on your good buddy Greg and give him a good rib tickle. Instead of feeling light pleasure and laughing at this obviously harmless display, he feels, instead, a THREAT ON HIS LIFE, so he turns and bashes you in the head with a rock. But we're social people. Killing people you're supposed to be close to kind of ruins the advantages of being social, so I imagine those that had a way to distiguish between threatening touch and non-threatening touch did better. And so, this habit of laughing when someone tickles us perpetrates until it's just a species-wide phenomenon.

      Then you get those, like you're wife, who take major offense at being tickled. I imagine in their past, some unwitting participant probably tickled them to the point of discomfort, and they subconsciously relate tickling to that unfortunate experience. Now, to avoid feeling discomfort, they revolt against any act of tickling.

      But why, then, can some people seem to control their reaction? I've known several people who couldn't be tickled, except by certain people. Could it be related to trust? If you trust a person, you don't expect harm, and so you allow the false alarm to ring true. But if you don't trust a person, that threat exists, and it prevents you from feeling the tickle as you should, instead you're subconsciously waiting for the threat.

      My sister, when I was younger, used to be able to get me to laugh just be making a tickling motion at me. Even at a distance, it would get me to laugh. I wonder what that was all about.
