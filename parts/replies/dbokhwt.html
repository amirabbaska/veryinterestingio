      • edderiofer

        While it is true that the brain can tell when two notes aren't "aligned" properly, I would argue knowing that this is "off-key" is mainly cultural. My reasoning is that the modern standard of tuning instruments is what's known as equal temperament, which is in fact ever-so-slightly not in perfect tune (because it's actually impossible for certain instruments to be in absolutely perfect tune, for mathematical reasons; see here). And while I can hear the difference between equal temperament and pure intonation (the original system that's based on aligning frequencies exactly in a nice pattern) when played together, there's a cultural aspect that's primed me to feel that equal temperament is the one that's in tune and not pure intonation.

        Also, I think some musician called Schoenberg once conjectured that if children grew up listening to dissonant music, they would end up finding the dissonant music on-key. He did create a technique for composing such music, but it never caught on in mainstream music to the point where his conjecture could be tested.

        So in summary: Your brain can hear when a note is off-key because its soundwaves don't fit together well with other soundwaves; however, slightly-off-key notes are used so much in modern music that our brain's primed to think that those notes are in fact on-key. This suggests that some element of "off-key-ness" is acquired.
