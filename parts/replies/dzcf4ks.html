    There is no serious push for a vaccine.

    Nonsense. There are literally thousands of papers researching HSV vaccines, and dozens of clinical trials.

    It's not that there's no serious push, it's that it's turned out to be a very difficult job.

    Herpesviruses in general are very difficult to vaccinate against. The only really effective herpesvirus vaccine is for chickenpox/shingles, and that is both relatively new and kind of lucky. There are lots of veterinary vaccines against herpesviruses (feline, equine, chicken, etc), but most of them are not very effective; they may only reduce clinical signs without blocking shedding and transmission, for example.

    Herpesviruses are in general extremely ancient viruses that are extremely well adapted to their hosts, usually having co-evolved with their host for hundreds of millions of years (the way you get a new herpesvirus is for them to co-speciate along with their host), and with a very large genome as viruses go, and complex life cycles, they are very, very difficult targets to vaccinate against.

      See above comments about HSV encephalitis, which is what happens when the herpes virus infection gets into your brain.

      It's quite rare (in the US, more than half the population is infected with HSV-1 or HSV-2, while the annual rate for HSV encephalitis is about 1 in 500,000) but it's also very, very bad. I'll leave this quote from Wikipedia:

      Without treatment, HSE results in rapid death in approximately 70% of cases; survivors suffer severe neurological damage.[1] When treated, HSE is still fatal in one-third of cases, and causes serious long-term neurological damage in over half of survivors. Twenty percent of treated patients recover with minor damage. Only a small population of survivors (2.5%) regain completely normal brain function.
