    • moonmip

      This is doing an injustice to stone tools and human ingenuity.

      As I said, there is a huge amount of sophistication in human stone tools. Apes using 'stone tools' is them picking up a rock. They don't shape it in any way. They do shape sticks (e.g. chimps stripping down sticks to put in termite mounds to pull out the bugs to eat them) but use rocks as blunt instruments.

      Humans select a stone carefully depending on available resources and what the tool is for (e.g. as a cutting tool like a knife, weapon, spear, hide scraper to make cloaks, etc). They then carefully strike the stone with another stone to shape it to the precise dimensions they need. This can involve chipping off tiny slivers to sharpen the edges and even deliberately causing the rock to fracture in specific ways to get a better edge (there are so many specialised ways to do this that each style has its own name - there are over a dozen of them). A stone tool is immediately identifiable from a rock on the ground because it is so clearly man made. It's a skill that takes hundreds of hours to learn and perfect.

      Making stone tools is incredibly skilled work - we had to try in class and have so much appreciation for the skill of so-called primitive people who made them. It's also damned dangerous - you could lose an eye to flying debris.
