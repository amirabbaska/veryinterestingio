    Depends on where you are... British Columbia derives about 95% of it's electrical needs from Hydro-Electric power plants. There's a small amount of thermal energy here and there, and a few off-grid locations, but it's overwhelmingly hydro.

    We don't do pumped storage, but our power utility has also acted as a buffer (and made a pretty penny doing so) for the western electrical grid. During the california power crisis, they would run the dams at huge, unsustainable levels during the day, and sell that power to California at obscene rates. At night, they'd turn the dams off, and buy dirt cheap Nuclear power from California. You can do that with hydro power, which is much more difficult with thermal (be it coal, natural gas, or nuclear).

        The reality is that most power generators can always produce power, but when there is excess across the board there is nowhere to put it.

        Pretty much all renewable systems are capable of scaling down their output, at a minimum. Your first choice is hydro, because you can "Save it for later" -- If you have extra peaking generation capacity beyond what your source river can provide, it's as good as 100% storage efficiency. After that, Solar can just not be used (the panels get a little bit hotter than they would otherwise), and wind can be trimmed down (which is done in high-wind situations to protect the hardware).

        Ideally it would be stored rather than discarded, but overproduction isn't a particularly rough problem.
