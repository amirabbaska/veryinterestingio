    • BraveHQ

      Imagine a slow moving stream that you're paddling a canoe on. Which direction you're going will make a big difference to your land speed because the water speed is a similar size to the speed you can paddle.

      Now imagine a ultra-performance speedboat on the same stream. Which direction it's going won't make much difference to its land speed, because the water flow is a tiny fraction of the boat speed.

      Now imagine a meteorite hitting at a small angle to the moon. It's moving sideways very slowly compared to the speeds all its vaporising gases will reach after impact. You're right that the sideways momentum doesn't go away, but its spread across all the gas equally and is small in comparison, like the water compared to the speedboat.

      So in short, no 'reset', more like giving a parking ticket to a billionaire, where the effect isn't really visible.

    • JESUSAURU5REX

      Depends on the size of the projectile. Larger bolides will have launch a lot more debris upward (termed ejecta) and they launch upward in a conical pattern and are then deposited in characteristic rays.

      H2O content is also extremely important. If a comet with a high concentration of ice strikes the surface, the heat from the impact will melt the ice and cause the liquid water to mix with the ejecta and then run down the rim of the transient crater.
