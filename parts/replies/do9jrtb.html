    Just to add to this for anyone wondering, there are observations that constrain the radius to that 11-13 km range quoted, it's not pure guesswork. They aren't super accurate and are all model dependent though. These observations include watching explosive bursts of fusion on the surface as the material expands, falls back, and bounces; using eclipses of the neutron star to constrain the size of the xray emitting region; carefully measuring the distance, brightness, and temperature of neutron stars to determine the radius. Even carefully measuring how quickly a neutron star cools down after a rapid accretion episode. All of these methods make assumptions about things like the atmosphere of the neutron star, though, and changing those assumptions changes the answer you get at the end. Not by so much that you could get a neutron star that's 100 km in radius, but maybe it moves the number from 15 km to 10 km. 10 km is the "order of magnitude" of the radius, meaning it's probably not quite right but it's not off by much.

    • Das_Mime

      Most of the instruments aboard each of the Voyager spacecraft are no longer working. The remaining ones mainly measure ambient magnetic fields and cosmic ray activity. Even if the optical/UV/IR instruments were still in service, they are quite primitive compared to what we've got now and probably wouldn't tell us much of anything we didn't already know.

      Regarding the pythagorean theorem: parallax isn't used to measure the diameter of the target object, it's used to measure the object's distance from us. The problem we're up against is that neutron stars, being absurdly tiny for stars, are impossible to resolve with any currently-existing instruments. Thus we are, for the most part, restricted to theoretical modeling of them, or measuring it in secondary ways (such as comparing the rotation period to the speed of light and thus putting an upper limit on the rotational velocity at the star's equator).
