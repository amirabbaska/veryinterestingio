    To be a viable energy source, fusion reactors on earth will have to operate at energy densities that are orders of magnitude higher than the sun, and at significantly higher temperatures.

    You're confusing energy density and power density.
    Energy density is a measure of how much energy is in the system.
    Power density is a measure of how quickly 'new' energy is being created.
    The Sun has very high energy densities (a back of the envelope calculation suggests 10^15 J/m^3 which is roughly 1 megaton equivalent) but it has very low power densities (it makes very little 'new' energy in a given period of time).
    For a fusion power system you'd want lower energy density, but higher power density (i.e. you want less stuff, but you want it to be 'burning' much faster).
