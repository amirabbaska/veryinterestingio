    This is a beautiful and clear explanation of emotional strength. Nicely done.

    For people who can't understand how emotions relate to understanding--an as a way to bring it back to biology--author Steven Stosny has written brilliantly about this.

    The short answer is: the oldest, instinctual parts of our brains are the ones that drive responses like fear and anger and rejection. It acts a lot faster than the more recently developed and sophisticated part, that handles making up narratives (i.e., complex understanding). His writing refers to these as Toddler Brain and Adult Brain.

    The super quick summary is: people are programmed to respond emotionally first via Toddler Brain--and then Adult Brain steps in to provide the justification. Hence, emotional strength and resilience are necessary to be able to handle challenging new ideas.

    Just to take it a step further: this is why, even as a PhD scientist, I am immensely mistrustful of people who cling strongly to the identity as "rational". Typically these people aren't capable of dealing with strong emotion and are simply trying downplay the importance of emotion. In reality, though, they're just as strongly emotion-driving as anyone else. The idea of "rationality" is used as a power play to avoid confronting what is emotionally upsetting to them--to disown their own fragility.
