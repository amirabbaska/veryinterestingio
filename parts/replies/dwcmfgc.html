    The root isn't actually the lowest note; the root is the basis that the rest of the chord is "built" off of, but it can be anywhere "inside" of the chord - or even omitted entirely (these are called 'rootless chords').

    The most easily identifiable tones inside of any harmony are probably the lowest pitch, the highest pitch, or the root (which could be anywhere inside of the harmony) - however, often times your ear will also be attracted to another note if it's part of the melody, or if one note is played particularly louder than another, or is a distinctive instrument while others are playing (it's all still the same 'chord,' even if it's broken up between multiple instruments). There's a ton of things that factor which note is going to be the most recognizable in any harmony.

  • MisterGoo

    The last part of your explanation is probably the most accurate one, as sympathetic frequencies are highly dependent upon the instrument they come from : a saxophone or a distorted guitar produce a lot of overtones, whereas a flute only produce harmonics of root and fifth, thus limiting the diversity of overtones. It works the same with synthesizers, with sinusoidal/square waves.

    So sometimes the instruments produces very few overtones and the root being predominant, that's what you hear the most.
