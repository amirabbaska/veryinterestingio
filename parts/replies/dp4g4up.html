    This is actually a fairly old issue dating back to the earliest days of integrating rechargeable batteries into electronics, and corporate malfeasance isn't entirely at fault for once.

    There are actually three separate voltage and current problems that the equipment have to address: The battery's electrochemical requirements, the phone's charge circuits' electrical requirements, and the charger / cable's electrical capabilities.

    When a battery is being charged, the input voltage and current have to match the electrochemical charging curve, otherwise the bulk of the energy gets wasted. Lithium cells in particular are extremely finicky, and require complex charging circuits. All of these circuits use a transistor to turn on the current to the battery, and most phones can control the circuits via a software interface.

    For the charge circuits to function efficiently, they need to be able to draw both constant current and constant voltage. Depending on the gague of the charger wire, the ideal charging current may be higher than what the cable can safely carry.

    Finally, the charger (which is actually either a dc-dc or ac-dc converter) has both a maximum and an ideal current rating, which may be lower or higher than either the cable or the charge circuits' ideal.

    Apple and the rest of the mobile device ecosystem use variations on the same techniques, which boil down to using a combination of active and passive circuits to signal what each component needs, or is capable of providing.

    In Apple's case, The "official" Lightning^(TM) cable comnector standard contains one-or-more Apple-proprietary SMT ICs that identify it as an "authentic" device, and define the cable's power and data handling capabilities. Both the cable and the charger negotiate with the phone using Apple-proprietary communication protocols, although non-Apple chargers can work as long as they support the USB-Battery-charge active-signalling standards.

    Samsung and most other manufacturers use the industry standard USB Battery-charge specification which uses both active and passive components to signal the current capacities for the cable and the charger. Because they are published standards, and the lower current signals are made by inserting simple resistors across the D- and D+ pins, it's easier to make a "counterfeit" charger for these devices.

      draw both constant current and constant voltage.

      As a note, they do not draw both constant current and constant voltage simultaneously, that is exceptionally difficult without either very low current or very low voltage delta. Lithium battery charging is done efficiently in two phases: first a constant current phase which does the bulk charging and the voltage varies. Then the float charge of constant voltage.

      Unlike other battery chemistries which must be charged with a constant current phase (NIMH) LI batteries can be charged with only constant voltage, it is just slower.
