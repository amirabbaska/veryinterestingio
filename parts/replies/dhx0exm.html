    • Abnormo

      Taste is a combination of texture, temperature, olfaction... something tasty is not as tasty if you chew with your nose plugged and eyes closed. In fact, your ability to taste food reaches nearly zero when you eat it without your nose open to receive the aromatic particles that exude from your food. Someone who doesn't like salad might not like the texture of floppy leaves, or the oily texture of having balsamico on it, or the particular flavors of that salad.

      Different cultures perceive more complex flavors differently, so our preferences CAN be learned. A Chinese individual would eat crickets on a stick, but given the same opportunity once in my life, I found it unappealing. It was not part of the taste palette that I grew up with, so when I eat it, my brain compares it to what flavors I already know as "good" or "bad" and categorizes it as "bad." For someone wishing to taste food without being influenced by their pre-existing choice they would have to try the food with similar characteristics to what they already enjoy in order to isolate just the taste.

      Taste can vary from individual to individual, particularly their perception of it. That being said, it is difficult to find people who dislike chocolate, and that says something about our taste being influenced on a more genetic level as well. As a previous commentator had said, there is a genetic component to taste in that it increases our fitness to enjoy sweet foods and avoid bitter foods as the chemicals that cause bitterness in food are often part of something that would irritate the body or poison it. The chemical substances that bind to our taste receptors and cause this sensation of "bitterness" irritate other receptors in our body like our skin. Rub anything bitter on a sensitive area like an open wound or your eyes and it will irritate your body. So in reality, if your body was built in a way in which we are irritated by the chemicals that make sweet foods sweet, and instead felt positive effects and no irritation from the chemicals that make bitter food bitter, then our perception of what is good and bad to eat and consequently what is good or bad to taste would be very different. If we still developed chocolate confections at that point, they may be enhanced in other ways - pure cocoa, no sugar, but rather spiciness or some other additions. Then sweet chocolate made with milk would be the odd one out.

      Our bodies are fascinating things, more complex than we will likely ever know, and the more you learn and investigate these things and connect them together, the more questions you end up having about why we turned out the way we did. What's so special about us being this way? So many questions.... and all just because we noticed that some people don't like some types of food. :D
