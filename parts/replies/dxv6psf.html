      • TheKingj2

        Chemical potential would be a more accurate term from a thermodynamic perspective. The water climbing effect is a mixture of force balances involving hydrogen bonding, electrostatic interactions, hydrophobic/hydrophilic interactions, Van der Waals, and gravity. Chemical potential deals with most of these, and in the context of Gibbs Free Energy can give us the driving force for such motion. Essentially, you can look at the chemical potential of water in water versus the chemical potential of water spreading along a given surface. If the water on the surface has a lower chemical potential than water in water, then the water will "climb" the surface to minimize its Gibbs Free Energy. At that point it's equilibrium will be determined by the available energy in the water is offset by the potential energy accumulated through the mass moving up against gravity.

        Please note that "Gibbs Free Energy" is not "Free Energy" in the sense that people think you can pull energy out of nothing. It is merely a measure of enthalpy available in a system that can be used to do work (such as climbing). Energy is conserved, and some quantity will change in the system as a result (Temperature, Entropy, Volume, etc.) to compensate and balance the energy.
