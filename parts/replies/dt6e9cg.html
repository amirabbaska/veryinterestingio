    • Gargatua13013

      The convective flow I am referring to occurs within the oceanic crust, and it can reach down 2 to 3 kilometers. In the thinner crust within an active spreading center, that's pretty much the whole thickness of the oceanic crust.

      Within the water column, the behavior of the brines is constrained by the degree of mixing with seawater, it's teperature and its salinity, as outlined by Sato (SATO, T. (1972). Behaviours of ore-forming solutions in seawater. Mining Geology, 22(111), 31-42). Depending on the resulting density, they may either:

      • flow directly on the ocean floor whey they may subsist in a brine pool without significant mixing for quite some time (type I brines);

      • ascend in the water column, whetehr a little or a lot, while turbulence induces sufficient mixing and cooling to reverse the density and induce collapse (type II brines);

      • ascend in the water column and disperse (type III brines).

      Of these three sceanrios, the last two are potentially associated with significant mixing and, I suppose, convection. Most reasearch I am aware of, however, focusses on the behavior of the brine in terms of the precipitation of metallic complexes and sort of looses track of the effects of convection on the water column.
