      • GinsbergsPeacock

        Putting some cursory thought into it, although improbable I don't think it's impossible.

        For one, the gravitational forces large enough to create waves of that size may also contribute to some very active plate tectonics like Europa. Which would then cause tidal waves. I admit this isn't the explanation given in the film, however.

        Additionally, a planet of a sufficiently larger radius/density and a close enough moon might be able to generate a tide differential of that magnitude. Combined with a much faster lunar orbital period, you could feasibly have huge pulls of water moving that quickly across the surface of a planet. The gravity is 130% stronger so the planet's size/density is plausible. And any sufficiently large and long wave will probably take a similar bore-like shape rather than merely filling in or forming gentle arcs and troughs. Water can only maintain a relatively flat-like shape beneath a certain elevation differential...past that and particularly in the face of rapid, violent displacement, it takes on the wave-like shape.

        I actually just took a detour to the wiki and apparently an author Kip Thorne found some scientifically viable ways of explaining it. Basically the waves are perpetually fixed in a football shape pointed toward Gargantua due to tidal locking, but because of some natural rocking you could have the planet shifting beneath the waves and knocking you into deeper waters. It would still explain the largely stagnant, knee-high waters at non-wave points. Still kind of shaky.
