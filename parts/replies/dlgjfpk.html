    Cheese is usually lactose free. The cheese production process uses bacteria that feed on lactose, to help in the coagulation of the milk-- the lactose in the cheese making process breaks down into glucose and galactose by the end. You can read the nutritional label of your cheese to see if it has any lactose in it: lactose (from the suffix -ose) is a form of "sugar" (a disaccharide), and must be listed on the nutritional label of a food under "carbohydrates". The carbohydrate content of most cheeses is under 5% (5g/100g of product), with most hard cheeses (cheddar, tasty, edam etc.-- i.e. excluding cottage cheese, feta, french soft cheeses etc.), having only trace amounts of carbohydrates (and therefore trace amounts of lactose). Check the nutritional label of the cheese in your fridge, or next time you're at the store, to see what I mean. Just because "65% of Chinese people are lactose intolerant" does not mean "they can't eat cheese without gastrointestinal discomfort", since cheese (by and large) does not contain lactose.

    TL;DR Lactose intolerance does not automatically mean dietary aversion to cheese, since most cheeses contain trace or no amount of lactose. In fact, if you're intolerant to cheese, you're almost certainly intolerant to dairy protein instead.

  • stop_mithering

    Natural, aged cheese (such as Cheddar, Parmesan and Swiss) can be digested by many people with lactose intolerance. During the cheese making process, most of the lactose is drained off with the whey (a liquid portion). The small amount that remains in the curd is changed to lactic acid during ripening (aging) of cheese. Only trace amounts of lactose remain.
