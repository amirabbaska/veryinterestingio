      Here's an article you might find interesting:

      https://hub.jhu.edu/2015/07/17/new-horizons-data-transmission/

      Spacecraft we have out now have long transmission times, especially since they can be quite far away. The article above takes New Horizons, a craft that can transmit at ~1-2kbps. For comparison, average internet speeds in Australia (where I live) are around ~30mbps on the higher end. But in the case of places like America where they have better infrastructure, higher-end internet speeds can reach upwards of 100mbps.

      This is partially due to the the spacecraft being old (once we launch them, it takes a while to get to its place) which means it was created during the 2002-2005 period (approximately). It runs out outdated technology, which means it has less bandwidth.

      If you're talking about transmitters on Earth, the record was set at around I believe 42tbps. However, I am not sure if this will be sustainable. I was unable to find a reliable reading of the maximum stable bandwidth connection (that was relatively efficient and didn't cost a fortune to maintain). However, you may find this section of SETI's website relevant as well:

      https://www.seti.org/seti-institute/project/details/seti-observations

      It talks about what the section of the EM spectrum is best for transmissions. And finally, you should check out the Wow! Signal (bandwidth - less than 10 kHz) - it may not be relevant to your original question but I do believe you'll find it interesting.

      https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wow!_signal

      Hope this helps and sorry for not being able to answer your question with anything more than hypothesis as this hasn't ever been really attempted - we're usually on the receiving end.
