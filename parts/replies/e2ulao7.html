  • conalfisher

    It's due to how they form. Planets don't just pop out of supernovae, they're created by the resulting elements that are spewed out after the fact, in a planetary nebula. Gravity brings a bunch of these elements together, and due to gravity not being equal everywhere in the proto planet, different parts attract stronger than others, and it all starts to spin (this is called acretion IIRC), before collapsing in on itself into a planet. Because space is, obviously, a vacuum, it's extremely difficult for the new planet to lose its spin (on earth, spinning things stop spinning because of friction mainly, which requires a body to give energy to), so it just keeps going. That's not to say that it won't stop eventually; light from a stat will, extremely, extremely slightly change the planets spin (enough that it's almost completely negligible, of course), and other bodies coming near it can also affect it. Whenever we use the Earth, or any other body, to propel a spaceship farther out from the Sun, that bodies gets slowed down ever so slightly (again, negligible, but measurable). And the moon is also steadily slowing down the Earth, and has been for millions of years.
