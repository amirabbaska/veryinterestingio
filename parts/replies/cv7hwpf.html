    While I agree it could be a MPG game, diesels do not run stoichiometric air/fuel ratios. The cars in question are all diesels. Diesels always ingest the maximum amount of air in the intake stroke, which is why there is no throttle body. Throttle is controlled by the amount of fuel injected. Fuel is injected at the end of the compression stroke, with the cylinder at TDC. AFR ratios vary significantly due to throttle and load. The cars had software that most likely turned on and off the EGR. The EGR is mostly responsible for controlling NOx emissions in diesels. However, this comes at a cost. When exhaust gases a recirculated into the engine there is less available oxygen which results in a loss of efficiency. Both power and fuel economy are impacted. The real problem is that EGR causes carbon particulates to blow by the piston rings into the oil which deceases the lubricating properties of the oil, causing premature engine wear. The recirculating gas which contains soot, unburned fuel, and oil can also cause build up on the intake valves and problems with the intake swirl flaps. This problem will effect power, fuel mileage, reliability and engine longevity if a proper solution is not implemented, such as DEF.
