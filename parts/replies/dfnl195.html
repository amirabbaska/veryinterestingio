Other than the light weight to strength ratio, sustainably sourced wood is also actually one of the most eco-friendly building material.       • shadowstrlke

        I'm not exactly sure about the comparison, but both of those statements have some basis of truth to them.

        For those wondering why wood actually won't feed the flame, the wood that's usually referred to are cross laminated/glulam wood. It's basically just layers of wood that are stuck together with special glue that helps to improve it's properties, such as removing the difference between the performance along and against the grain of the wood. This layering also helps to slow down the burning, and have been proven to be pretty fire resistant.

        And with regards to the steel beam, it does not need to melt, only soften sufficiently (which it does as it heats up) for the load to cause yielding and collapse. That is why for concrete structures there is a certain restrictions to the thickness of the concrete as on fire resistance duration. It's to insulate the steel reinforcement from the flames to reduce risk of failure.

        So I could see that potentially could be the case. Of course there's a bunch of down side to wood, particularly with regards to durability in wet conditions.
