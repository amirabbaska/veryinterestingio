  • thumtac

    "When the outermost electron bounces between levels 9,192,631,770 times, the clock ticks one second"

    This isn't correct. The way the atomic clock works is that it calibrates a radio wave to the frequency that excites the transition of the electron to the excited state in the caesium atom "the most". I.e. The atomic clock finds the resonant frequency of the caesium atom for this situation. This frequency is by definition 9192631770 hz. So once the radio wave is calibrated, the clock ticks a second when the radiowave, not the electron, oscillates 9192631770 times.
