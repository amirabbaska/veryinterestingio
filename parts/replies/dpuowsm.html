    • thefourthmaninaboat

      Gravitational lensing creates multiple images in most cases - as an example, see Einstein's Cross, where four images of the same quasar can be seen thanks to an intervening gravitational lens. It's actually possible to use this as a test of the expansion of the universe. If you look at a distant, lensed, supernova, you'll see multiple images of it at different times and different positions, as the lensed light will travel different (longer or shorter) paths. These can be predicted, but if the expansion of the universe as not as we expect, then we'd see them at different times or places to what we predict. This has been done once, with a supernova known as SN Refsdal, after the astronomer who proposed the test.
