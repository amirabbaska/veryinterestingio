    • Stranger2k

      Frequently, what people experience as "hypersomnia" is actually obstructive sleep apnea (OSA). This is where as you fall asleep and your muscle tone relaxes, it causes a partial or full collapse of your airway. The brain detects a decrease in available oxygen (desaturation) and partially awakens you. Muscle tone restored, you resume breathing, and begin to fall asleep again. The cycle then repeats.

      This can occur multiple times per minute. The consequence is that you think you've slept, but in reality you've possibly never reached deep sleep stages, and spent the night bouncing between almost-awake and barely-asleep states.

      Warning signs of sleep apnea are: 1) loud snoring, 2) your bed partner noticing that you occasionally stop breathing, choke or gasp for air, 3) waking up fairly frequently with a headache or dry mouth. Feeling sleepy during the day despite sufficient opportunity to sleep (i.e., being likely to unintentionally fall asleep when not active, such as watching TV or riding in a car, despite having slept 7+ hours) is a strong indicator of a possible OSA. Being overweight is a predisposing factor but not always present.

      Sleep apnea increases the risk of all kinds of problems, including high blood pressure, heart disease, stroke, automobile accidents caused by falling asleep at the wheel, diabetes, and depression. Also makes it nearly impossible to lose weight.

      Assessment and treatment are straightforward - one night in a sleep lab, where your oxygen levels and brain waves are monitored. If you think you have these symptoms, alert your doctor and ask if a referral to a sleep study is appropriate.

    • Hypermeme

      Well in the case of diphenhydramine at least, the sleepiness is caused by activation of the H1 receptor, a histamine receptor.

      The brain's major and only source of histamine is the tuberomammillary nucleus. This projects to the hypothalamus and the cerebral cortex, among other areas. Since it projects to the cortex, it can encourage arousal by exciting parts of the cortex.

      But it can also increase the release of acetylcholine in the cebreal cortex, indirectly through the cholingergic neurons in the basal forebrain.
