    • NonnoBomba

      If you're interested, this book has been considered the very foundation of the subject for decades and has been used as a textbook by food science courses in many universities. It explores common kitchen ingredients and procedures from a biology, chemistry and physics standpoint to tell you the whys and the hows of cooking, sometimes debunking common myths (eg that searing "seals in the juices" of meat). While it is rooted in hard science and doesn't frown upon mentioning chemical and biological terms, that's the point after all, I think the book is quite accessible; the author, Harold McGee, is primarily a writer: even though he has some STEM background and definitely became an authority in this field, his Ph.D. was on the romantic poetry of John Keats.

      All the book is remarkable and the chapter on meats will definitely help you make better BBQs (among other things), I know it helped me, but the first two chapters on eggs and milk and what you can do with them are truly fascinating to me: two things that looks so commonplace are in fact a marvel of nature when you learn what's inside them and how they work.
