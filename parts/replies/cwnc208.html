  • masgrada

    In theory, no.

    Because the cup and liquid are not attached, any positive benefits of the liquid swirling would not be transferred in any meaningful capacity to the unattached cup in this scenario. If we had spinning ice it would be a different story. Meanwhile the higher liquid levels, because of centrifugal action pulling liquid to the sides, would decrease the available tipping angle tolerances.

    So, not only would it be of no benefit, it would actively serve against your goal of decreased spill-ability.

    • AssistPowers

      This is a better answer than the top voted one which does not consider that the gyroscopic effect works best in rotating rigid bodies. In an open top container with rotating liquid, tipping the cup will cause the liquid to deform rather than transferring the forces across the rest of the material in a way that would resist the tipping motion.

      There is some gyroscopic effect because the bottom of the cup prevents deformation of the coffee there, so those forces are directed to the liquid above it, ultimately transferring to the top surface. At the top, the liquid has plenty of space to do as it pleases (spill). If it were sealed tight with no air bubbles, the liquid would have nowhere to go and behave much more like a rigid body, but then spilling would not be a concern.

      In the end, any amount of gyroscopic force will not prevent you from tipping the cup and the coffee being pushed up the sides of the cup will increase the risk of spilling.
