    Building on this, there are further considerations;

    According to this FAQ, there are two broad categories of aluminum alloys, each of which has particular challenges;

    Heat treatable alloys : These are alloys which can go through heating / cooling cycles in order to strengthen them, in much the same way that steel can. As the FAQ says, welding can result in much higher local temperatures than the final 400^o F step of heat treatment. So, further post-welding heat treatment is needed or the weld area can be up to 40% weaker than the base material.

    Cold worked alloys : These have been work hardened in order to make them stronger - typically because they can't be heat treated. Welding them will lead to localized heating, which 'reverses' the cold working strengthening. So, the welding would need to be done before the cold working process in order to retain strength.

      Physically that means that it takes less energy to heat 1 kg of AL 1 Degree C than Steel so that means it heats up faster.

      No, that's heat capacity.

      High thermal conductivity means when you heat the area you're trying to weld, the heat is dissipated to the rest of the piece rapidly, rather than staying where you need it.

    • Grokma

      You can easily destroy your base metal if you put too much heat into a piece. It can either burn through, or make too deep a cut forcing you to try to fill faster or make a terrible weld.

      Also if it takes too much heat the area around the weld, the heat affected zone, will be much weaker than the weld metal itself or the base metal outside the zone. This depending on what the application is can make the whole thing useless as it will crack around the weld if too much weight or pressure is put on the piece.
