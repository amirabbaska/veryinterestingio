    • Ciellon

      The short answer is: theoretically yes.

      The longer answer starts with correcting the misconception that analog and digital signals are different when propagated wirelessley (sp??). In telecommunications, 'analog' mostly refers to the actual electromagnetic representation of a signal as it travels through the air - its physical form, invisible to our eyes, but still affecting and being affected by the environment around it. The term 'digital' largely refers the equipment processing, sending, or receiving said signal.

      Sending a signal is easy enough, the real trick is in getting someone else to be able to receive it. When a signal is sent, a computer instructs the proper equipment to oscillate electricity through an antenna (or antennae) to give off electromagnetic 1s & 0s. When receiving, however, the equipment has to digitize the physical waveform in real time (or very near it). This is accomplished with what we call today Nyquist Theory (formally Nyquist-Shannon Theorem). More colloquially known as 'sampling'.

      All signals (at least, for right now), no matter how complex or simple, are utterly useless if the receiving machine cannot sample your sent signal correctly.

      So, yes, theoretically - as long as the receiving computer can sample the waveform and produce the proper information - we can make channels as small as we want. The hard part making equipment sensitive enough to detect and differentiate signals that fine and minute, while also sending them loud enough to be heard above the noise floor to begin with.
