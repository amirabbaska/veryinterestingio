    • PHATsakk43

      I'm sort of restricted on how much I feel comfortable saying, as I can be pretty easily dox'd from previous comments and I'm working with one of the companies that is doing some clean-up there on a project that is pre-patent that will ultimately used in Fukushima. We are going to be testing the equipment on a tank that we crapped up in the 1990s and is somewhat similar (highly buffered wastewater with residual radionuclides, primarily Co-60 with some arsenic and Cs-137 that got thrown off a cation bed when they tank got processed through it before they realized 500 gallons of 50% caustic got drained into the tank, hence the "highly buffered" water.) Our water is also filled with silica cobalt colloids as we are one of the unfortunate plants that got Boraflex spent fuel racks which throws silica into the pools continuously. We maintain around 20,000ppm silica due to the Boraflex. Anyway, I digress, if we can clean that tank, cleaning up hot seawater isn't much different.

      I will say, that from what I have been told by people that have been there, the lower level of the plant has effectively become the recirc basin. The water, after treatment is supposed to be below pico-curie range and that the isotope of concern is primarily Cs-137. From what I was told, the fishing industry has effectively blocked the discharge of any waste with any level of radioactivity, even though the water in Fukushima at this point has a lower activity than natural seawater. The fishing lobby is apparently very strong in Japan.

      EDIT: I realized I never answered you. There has been resins that work to remove specific isotopes in buffered solutions for years, they are just expensive.

      One that we've used for some of our systems that have chemical treatment (component cooling water, for instance) is ResinTech SIR-600 which is really not a resin, but zeolite.
