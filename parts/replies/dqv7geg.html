    Former USFS firefighter here (not an investigator. Just a grunt):

    Wildfires spread with the wind. If a fire really blows up, the first hour's burn pattern almost always looks like this, at least until the wind shifts. That's just how explosive wildfires take off--they are fanned by winds, and they spread most rapidly upslope.

    So you can start by looking for the "heel", the tip of the wedge where the fire originated. It's not a big area--an engine crew almost always arrives on the scene before the fire is more than 200 yards on a side. (sometimes there's not much they can actually do--if the wind's blowing hard and the fire's crowning, a small crew won't be able to effectively fight the head face. But it's rare for the USFS to not know pretty close to exactly where the fire started.)

    Often, the fire's heel is near the top of a ridge in the middle of nowhere, and a thunderstorm passed recently. Lightning strike. Case closed.

    Other times, the heel will align with a campfire ring, or it will be right near a road (which means the fire was likely sparked by a dragging truck exhaust, or a tossed cigarette). In the wildland-urban interface, it's not uncommon for the heel to be near someone's patio.

    From there, it takes a little police work to figure out what exactly happened.

    EDIT: Obviously, the investigators use sophisticated forensic techniques, not just "look for the heel". But they typically have a lot of good early observations to help the investigation. The first fire crew on the scene will send a lookout up to a vantage point to observe the fire and take a weather reading.

    It's not like investigators are combing a 15,000 acre burn scar looking for the cinders of a cigarette butt.
