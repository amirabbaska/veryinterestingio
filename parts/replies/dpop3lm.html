      Animals, through experience, learn what other animals are capable of, especially as prey of predators. Since water sources are rare (I'll use the lion as an example) prey and predator alike are focused to a small area.

      But these water holes become more scarce as things get hot, and when things get hot, it's hard to chase your food down without over heating. So predators are more opportunistic in their hunts, preying on the old or weak since most of a lions prey, in their prime, can do a decent job of avoiding direct attacks on them.

      These predators rely on getting closer to their prey before springing on them, and even then, only succeed maybe 10% of the time. This obviously varies by predator.

      Theres a series on Netflix called "The Hunt" that is superb. It documents several predators around the world and talks about the difficulty they each have in finding food.

      But back to the lion, trying to attack in full view of everyone will most likely yield in failure, with tons of burned energy, and over heating, which requires a long time to recover from.

      There are many other factors, but basically, it's smarter for the lion to wait for a better time than when literally all the prey is on edge and super tense and highly aware of every predator and it's distance. Like all predators, they need to get within a certain distance in order to kill. As long as the prey maintains that distance, they know, through experience, that they are relatively safe. Or as safe as they can be. As always, it's still a risk. Die of thirst, or risk that drink.

      All predators face different challenges, but hopefully that gives you a better idea.

      Also, check out The Hunt on Netflix. It's good stuff!
