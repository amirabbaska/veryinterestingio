    • Workacct1484

      Yes, they absolutely have. Like I noted if you control both the entry & exit node used by a client, you have a MUCH easier time compromising their data.

      It still isn't easy & requires a good deal of time, expertise, and money. But those are not things the U.S. government is hurting for.

      This is why you don't want to just use TOR. You want to use TOR over a VPN, and ideally some more obfuscation such as VPN&TOR chains.

      What I mean by this is you VPN, then TOR to another VPN, then TOR again to another VPN, then TOR to your destination. So they need your 1^^st, your 2^^nd, and your 3^^rd entrance and exit nodes, and they need to be able to break all those VPNs.

      Security is about layers & making it more hassle than it is worth to break your security. And why if you have a dedicated attacker ,such as a government, going after you it's not really a matter of IF so much as when they get to you.

      The trick is to constantly be on your toes, and upgrading your security. It's basically a cat and mouse game.

      Also to know what your use-case is.

      For me it's my torrenting (Of 100% free and open source software I swear....), my browsing habits, and circumventing region-locks. So for me a single VPN suffices, because I'm really only up against small time, passive monitoring.

      For someone consistently releasing node-0 torrents (The first person to put up a new movie), dealing in illicit materials, or doing other things, they may come against more active attackers & would need more.
