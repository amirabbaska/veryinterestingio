    • TributeToStupidity

      The death of a star is related to their size not luminosity. A star has to be about 8 times larger than the sun to achieve type 2 SN, which is generally what people picture (it can be less than 1.5x if there is a binary star system, creating a type 1a supernova). It has to be large enough that the core begins to create iron. At this point the balance between the outward force from the fusion and the inward gravitational pull falls out of equilibrium and the star begins to expand. As it expands the surface cools and we get a red giant. Finally the core can no longer fuse any material together, and without that outward force the star collapses on itself in a SN. The outer layers will explode outwards while the core will remain. Depending on the size of the star it could remain as a small white dwarf, a neutron star, or a black hole (there are subcategories but those are the three main options.)

      Now could a blue star be large enough to eventually become a red giant and achieve SN? Yes, thank you people smarter than me below! A star 8x the mass of the sun can burn blue.
