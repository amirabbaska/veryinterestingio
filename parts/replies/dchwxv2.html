    This is a better answer than the current top.

    Noting Si is a semiconductor is an important point- doping the Si with different elements, one in at a higher state of charge and one at a lower state of charge, to promote "flow" of electrons once the "excitation" in the top comment occurs after exposure to photons.

    http://www.optique-ingenieur.org/en/courses/OPI_ang_M05_C02/res/k_fig_02.jpg

    If no photons are present to promote this flow, the cell is no longer conductive and will actually resist flow. If partial shading exists (particularly in older solar modules), the shaded (i.e. non-conductive) cell is holding this charge and dissipating via heat. This is what causes fires or cell burnouts in some cases as resistance becomes a load. Newer modules have introduced bypass diodes to isolate portions of the module which are not conductive.

    http://www.homepower.com/sites/default/files/styles/article_gallery_active/public/articles/images/Brearly_Cell_delamination.fxd__0.jpg?itok=z1cILrND
