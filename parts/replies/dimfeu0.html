    • exposedtofire

      These medical sounding posters are being somewhat disingenuous. Yes, most people are more productive if you give them stimulants. But stimulants as a treatment for ADHD was discovered in the late 1930s when Charles Bradley gave a bunch of kids with behavioral issues Benzadrine and about half of them improved significantly.

      You'll notice that's not a 100% significant improvement rate. The other kids presumably experienced some improvement but for those with ADHD this "fixed" things in a way it didn't for others.

      Basically, if you don't have ADHD, stimulants aren't going to give you a magical intelligence boost that will let you accomplish things you couldn't before - not in the way they would benefit a person with actual ADHD. This is why the response of the patient is considered important in confirming whether or not they actually have it. The fuzziness comes from the fact when the effects of medication are reported by the patient rather than directly observed by the doctor, it's a hell of a lot harder to tell the difference.

      Source: http://ajp.psychiatryonline.org/doi/full/10.1176/ajp.155.7.968

  • null_work

    and doing better based on the understood mechanisms of that class of drugs tells us almost nothing about what is going on with the person in any other way.

    Sure, but if I get "sick", take an antiviral and then get better, I'm inclined to believe a virus was the cause. If I get a bad cough and take antibiotics and see quick relief, I'm inclined to believe bacteria was the cause. This is because we know what the medication is affecting and we know it's related to the underlying causes. You're failing to make the differentiation that some medications treat the root cause or a cause of some set of symptoms that indicates an underlying disease and some medications that treat symptoms.

    Think about more complicated diseases. It turns out that TNF blockers reduce or eliminate certain diseases like Crohn's disease and rheumatoid arthritis. I can take some type of pain medication to alleviate painful cramping, and that's treating a symptom of the disease, but what about TNF blockers? Is that treating a symptom or is it treating something related to the root cause? It's for sure treating something more fundamental to the disease, at least, as it's the immune system's response that is causing those other symptoms, even if it's not treating the main cause, what is causing the TNF issue with your immune system such as gut bacteria or something else, but you can be sure that if someone presents the symptoms of Crohn's disease and you give them a TNF blocker and it rids them of their symptoms, that they have Crohn's disease.

    I'm not saying you're wrong, for sure. In the absence of any other symptoms or indications of a disease, a medication having some effect does not mean much, moreso when it's something like a stimulant that is known to have an effect in normal people. It's still difficult, though, as when we're talking about changes to dopamine systems that are thought to be a cause or at least indicative enough of the disease, so a change after taking the drugs could be indicative of a disease. The biggest thing to warn anyone about is self diagnosis. It's ok to wonder if you may have something like ADD and you weren't aware after seeing some type of improvement after taking stimulants, but you need to be evaluated professionally.
