    • KurtWagnerX

      1) You can take a small sample of your substance that you think is pure, react it with another sample of a different substance you think is pure, and see if there are any weird leftover substances you can't account for.

      2) No, it's not a coincidence. Mendeleev organized them according to weight, but he didn't know about neutrons. Neutrons add weight to an atom without affecting the chemical properties of the atom. Chemistry is all about electric charges, and neutrons don't have one. (Or if you like you can say they have a charge of 0.) As was mentioned earlier, sometimes he found that he had to take two neighboring atoms and swap their positions in order for their chemical properties to fit observed patterns. The protons are what determines the identity of the element. If two atoms are neighbors on the modern periodic table, the one on the right will always have exactly one more proton than the one on the left. Usually the one on the right also has more neutrons, but sometimes the one on the left has more neutrons, which makes the one on the left heavier. So the true predictor of how an atom will behave chemically is the number of protons it has. Usually more protons means more mass, but sometimes more protons means fewer neutrons, and therefore smaller mass, so organizing by weight is a close, but imperfect, approximation to organizing by the number of protons.

      3) Yes, the protons, neutrons, and electrons of a hydrogen atom are identical to those of a uranium atom. The number of protons determines what kind of atom you have. The number of electrons determines the charge on the atom. (Edit: I think it's more accurate to say the difference between the number of electrons and protons determines the charge.) The number of neutrons has no impact on how an atom bonds with other atoms. Although neutrons do play a role in radioactivity, so while they have some effect, it's not a direct effect on the bonding process.
