    Molten material pushed up from the core reaches the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, which then forces the two sides apart. The material contains particles that show a particular magnetic alignment when it was formed. Once that material cooled, the alignment was locked in. So, over eons, some material showed one orientation, while other material showed the opposite orientation.

    A flipping magnetic field wasn't discovered until folks examining core samples from various distances from the ridge noticed a discrepancy in magnetic field alignment of material in the sample. Once the flip was suspected, many more samples were taken to pinpoint the flips.

    We know how fast the Mid-Atlantic Ridge expands, so it was easy to date each flip by its distance from the ridge.
