    It's price discrimination.

    Consider two types of travelers, business travelers and seasonal holidaygoers.

    The latter is far more price sensitive than the former. However, people planning their holidays tend to book flights far in advance, while business travel tends to be planned on a shorter timescale. As such, airlines know that they can charge expensive prices closer to the date of the flight and catch business bookings who didn't know they were traveling early enough to get in on the "tourist" price.

        Yes, but it also implies an ability to charge a higher price to the customers who are willing to pay more without them simply pretending to be the customers who are only willing to pay a low price.

        A plane might not be able to fill itself if all seats were the same price given that a significant chunk of the potential customers cannot afford an "average" price

        Pretend things were reversed, and people who are willing to pay a higher price knew their travel plans well in advance and people who weren't willing to pay much only knew they were going to travel on short notice.

        The airline couldn't charge high prices early and low prices closer to the date of the flight since the high willingness to pay travelers would simply wait for the lower prices and "pretend" to be customers with a low willingness to pay. The airline would have to charge a single market price that (in the very simple toy model I alluded to in my last post with two groups of passengers and each with a distinct, uniform willingness to pay for a ticket) would probably be the price that tourists were willing to pay unless it was more profitable to price out the tourists and undersell flights that only had business travelers.
