    • Jqerty

      The probability to find the electron in a certain region, is defined by the integration of the wave function over that volume. The size of a proton is only a few fm (10^-15 m), and the wave function has the size in the order of Ångström (10^-10 m). So the volume of the wave function that overlaps the proton is very small (remember that the volume is proportional to the length cubed!)

    If you throw the electron fast enough, would it enter orbit in a higher orbital and then release a photon and go back to the lower state orbital?

    • Pancurio

      Ignoring the impossibility of "throwing" an electron and reading this as "If you give an electron sufficient kinetic energy...", yes. It would absorb the discrete amount that it could to jump to a proportionally higher state, as long as it was not too much to break free of the bond entirely. If this was an unstable state, which is very likely as nature prefers lower energy states, it would "fall" back to the lower state releasing a discrete bundle of electromagnetic field excitation energy called a photon.
