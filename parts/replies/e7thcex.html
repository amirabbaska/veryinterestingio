    It's actually entirely possible to implant an electrode in a person and them to use it to control an artificial limb. Cybernetics researcher Kevin Warwick has actually done so to himself. The results were impressive - not only could an electrode array implanted on his median nerve produce a signal detailed enough to control a robotic arm, but he was also able to obtain feedback from sensors on the arm. Even more interesting, at the end of the project, when the array was removed, it was found that nerve tissue had grown around it, enclosing the sensor.

    As to why such techniques aren't used in modern prostheses, it's likely a few reasons. It's more complex, it involves surgery on nerves, and it may even be that modern prosthetics simply aren't advanced enough to take advantage of the potential gain in information flow.

  • eclise13

    There's definitely several challenges, here are a few off the top of my head:

    (1) picking up a clean signal - yes, you can get signals from the nerves, but you will also get other signals like EMG or ECG, isolating the signals of interest can get complicated

    (2) interpreting the signals - even if you get a clean purely neural recording how do you analyze and put this data to use? This might be simpler if you are recording from peripheral nerves near an end-organ but what about things closer to the CNS where nerves branch off to multiple sites in the body - what neural signal is relevant and what is not?

    (3) recording stability - you might get good signals for a day or a week but what about 6 months? A year? 5 years? Anything you put in the body is gonna result in foreign body response and that is likely problematic down the road, how do you account for this?

    This is just what comes to mind from purely a recording standpoint. And even if you solve these challenges for a particular part of the body, it may not translate elsewhere. Peripheral nerves vs central nerves vs the brain are all different. This is purely from a recording standpoint. Stimulating the nerves has its own set of challenges. Just a quick overview from someone working in a research lab focusing on neuromodulation.
