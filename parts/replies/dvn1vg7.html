    1) Because testing something on a WinXP is useless if you need it to work on Win10, or because it has a gaping security hole which will mean you lose a lot of cash when you get a cryptolocker. Or because newer systems are vastly more efficient in some cases.

    2) You'll have to migrate at some point anyway. Legacy systems are legacy for a reason. Security, efficiency, 3rd party stuff, availability. Hell, even if I wanted to run a server farm with only pre-2000s hardware, I'd probably have some serious issues as soon as something breaks, because that stuff doesn't get manufactured anymore. No amount of goodwill will make a pile of old Athlon CPUs pop out of thin air.

    To be fair, most companies only start caring about security AFTER they are breached and lose vast sums of money on it, so you're not alone. I know a company that had servers running old OS, which was (relatively) alright up until WannaCry came along and there was no patch to secure those computers against it. On the other hand, computers running a new OS with still ongoing support didn't get infected at all for the most part, because the patch was deployed in a timely manner and those systems did have anti-malware. Thus, everything reliant on those servers was on full halt for several days, while everything that wasn't reliant on the servers was running just fine within hours for the most part.
