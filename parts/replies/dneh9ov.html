    Does having "more" genes from your mother mean that you'll necessarily have characteristics more similar to your mother than your father?

    • ahotelworker

      No, not necessarily. Not all genes code for something that a human would consider a "characteristic". Some don't appear to code for anything at all. But the imbalance that exists for males (having one X and one Y) does mean that the exact alleles (versions of genes) they inherit on their X is really important, because they don't have a second X chromosome to compensate if they get a bad allele on theirs.

      This is why men are much more likely to get color blindness and hemophilia. These are both X-linked disorders. Women can compensate using a functioning allele on their other X if they get a bad copy on one, but men can't.

      Tl;dr - Not really, but it is especially important which specific versions of genes you inherit from your mother if you're male.

  • krello

    Female children too if you count mitochondrial dna

    • ahotelworker

      This is true! I forgot about this one. Yes, children of both sexes inherit their mitochondria solely from their mother.

      It is possible to think of the mitochondria as being more of a symbiotic organism coming along for the ride, though. That's why it has its own genome instead of being a part of your genome. This is also why mitochondrial replacement therapy works, for people whose inherited mitochondria are malfunctioning.

      For anyone who has seen scare-stories about children being produced with three parents, this is what they're talking about.
