    • hikaruzero

      As far as I understand, its theoretically possible to measure the force two seperated objects experience when they are repelled from each other via dark energy.

      No, it is not theoretically possible to measure the force on two separated objects receding from each other because there is no force driving them apart. An accelerometer at either object would measure zero acceleration. Gravitational motion is inertial because of the curvature of spacetime, so an object that appears to be accelerating under the influence of gravity is actually just moving freely through curved space, not under the influence of any proper forces. This is one of the defining principles of general relativity, called the equivalence principle.

      If its really just the spacetime between them that is expanding, does this mean that the two objects are somehow weakly tehhered to the specific spacetime cordinates where they are?

      Two objects only get farther apart from each other due to the expansion of space when they are not bound together in any way. Distant galaxies are not bound together, so they expand away from each other ... but within galaxies, matter does not expand because it is all gravitationally bound into a single system.

      General relativity is background-independent, which means nothing is tied to any coordinates and you can choose any coordinates you want and the model/physics of the system does not change.

      Hope that helps.
