    • MauranKilom

      Surface tension is directly related to the pressure gradient, where a higher gradient will result in a higher surface tension.

      The causation is the other way around: Higher surface tension means that a bubble (at equilibrium) needs a higher pressure differential (given the same volume). You can actually calculate it quite easily, which probably answers the remainder of those questions.

      Without anything to back it up, I would expect the following quantities to be the primary factors in the speed of collapse:

      • The pressure differential, which provides the energy for breaking the surface.

      • The stiffness and yield limit of the material, or how much work is needed to tear up the surface. Note that higher pressure differentials mean that you are closer to the yield limit already (up to the point where e.g. a balloon bursts) and thus less work is needed.

      • Maybe surface tension is involved (in another way than dictating the pressure differential), but I wouldn't have a hypothesis for whether it increases or decreases the speed.

      I think if you look into crack propagation mechanics you might find an approach that works here too, although I'm not 100% confident that those theories are applicable to fluids. In any case, I agree that the speed of sound (in the material where it's higher) should be the upper limit for how fast the bursting can propagate.
