      If I'm understanding correctly, the hypothetical reverse-gravity antimatter black hole is still a black hole - it's not emitting anything except perhaps for Hawking radiation, so it's going to look more or less the same as a regular black hole other than maybe looking bigger than normal if it repels photons the same way it repels regular matter.

      Such a black hole would, incidentally, be impossible to orbit (since orbits depend fundamentally on the orbiting object being drawn toward whatever it's orbiting) and in fact its mere presence would have all sorts of interesting effects on star systems and other celestial bodies near it (for the brief time that said bodies actually were near it, before being violently flung apart).

      Suppose that to first order, gravity follows the same 1/r^2 law as electric charges, except replace the charge term with the mass of the object, and then put a minus sign out front, so that two objects of the same 'mass charge' attract, and two objects of opposite 'mass charge' repel. Suppose antimatter has a negative 'mass charge'. Then, antimatter is attracted to other antimatter gravitationaly as normal, but is repelled from ordinary matter. You could very easily form an antimatter black hole that would attract antimatter, but repel ordinary matter.
