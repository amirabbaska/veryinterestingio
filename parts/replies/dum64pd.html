  • VoilaVoilaWashington

    That's not entirely true. However, we should first start by saying that "insect" is probably not scientifically intended, and "bugs" would be the better term. We're not talking about strictly 6-legged arthropods, I presume, but we could include worms, spiders, millipedes, etc.

    While it's certainly true of chasing grasshoppers down in an open field, then cooking them only to be left with a tiny bit of nutrients per bug, there are lots of ways to beat those numbers:

    • Type - grasshoppers look big, but there's not a lot of "meat" on them. It's mostly legs that make them seem bigger than they are. Caterpillars or worms, on the other hand, are much more dense with less exoskeleton.
    • Source - find a rotting log and you may well find a handful of maggots. Cook 'em in a stew, and you might get a whole lotta calories there.
    • Trapping/farming - using an artificial light source, you could probably catch a lot of flies. I'm not sure if the nutrients would be all that good, and the flavour sure isn't, but ya gotta do what you gotta do.

    I don't think anyone's on the verge of advocating for cricket cookies to replace hamburgers, but it's more tasty than you think and a lot of protein.

    With all that said, the best bet is probably still to bring hook and line, and use it to catch fish.

    Source: Restaurant owner who's fascinated by this stuff.
