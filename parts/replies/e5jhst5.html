    • JarJarDidNuffinWong

      Considering light as a particle: it's just Newton's gravitational force formula F=GMm/r^2

      where G stands for the universal gravitational constant, M for the mass of Earth, m for the mass of the particle being accelerated towards the Earth, and r for the distance between them.

      Then a(particle towards Earth) = F/m = GM/r^2

      Which is independent of the mass of the particle, so even massless photons would accelerate towards the Earth.

    • Anarbitrarylemon

      This was not a fundamental assumption of classical mechanics. Optics was a field of study, and Maxwell was able to bring optics under the field of E&M with his contributions, but before that, and before the 1800's (although I don't know how much before the 1800's. It really could have been at any time in the scientific revolution as far as I know) some astronomer made measurements of Jupiter's moons around Jupiter and found that the return half of the orbit always took less time than the orbit back around and they concluded that, if Newton's description of gravity is correct, the light must be travelling at a finite speed and the discrepancy in measurement can be described as a sort of "rolling shutter" effect (vSauce has a good video on the rolling shutter effect and the last few minutes, if I remember correctly, talk about how the Andromeda actually has a noticable rolling shutter effect for most equipment that can see the Andromeda Galaxy).

      As for how it would interact with gravity, it could still be instantly transmitted on a curve. Physics is weird, and no assumption is ever safe.
