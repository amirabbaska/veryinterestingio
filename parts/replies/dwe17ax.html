    • Coomb

      The particular dependence of lift and drag coefficient on Reynolds number is specific to the shape. For many shapes, the general dependency is something like this: the drag coefficient decreases with increasing Reynolds numbers at Reynolds number up to about 10^3, then the drag coefficient is approximately constant up to 10^5, then the drag coefficient decreases til about 10^6, and then from 10^6 on, the drag increases with Reynolds number. These numbers are approximately true for a sphere. As Reynolds number scales linearly with linear dimension, that's generally what the drag behavior looks like. Now, for airfoils, as an example, the behavior can be even more complex. Typically, the airfoil will be designed such that it has a drag minimum at or near its typical operating condition.

      The drag itself is proportional to surface area so you have even more complex interactions.

      A lift drag polar with a family of curves based on Reynolds number tells you how the coefficients scale based on size. As I said, the drag equation itself is dependent on surface area (planform area of the wing) which might itself be a non integer exponent scale with linear size.
