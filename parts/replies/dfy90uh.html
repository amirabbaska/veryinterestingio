      Shininess has to do with how CONSISTENTLY a material reflects whatever portion of light it does reflect, causing you to be able to make out intact images, such as the light sources in the room, which appear as spectral "shines."

      Shiny black = reflects few photons, but when it does, they follow angles that are highly predictable from their incoming angles

      Matte black = reflects few photons, and also scatters those photons it does reflect fairly randomly.

      Also, sometimes when talking about shiny, black things, you have a situation such as a matte black thing UNDERneath a glass or plastic coating, which is a whole different composite situation that can't be described based on the properties of a single material, because it's not. It might have aspects of both, because the glass is coherently reflecting some light for shines, and the part underneath is then also scattering light that reaches it, etc. The above refers to singular homogenous materials only.
