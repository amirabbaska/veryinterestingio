      • raycluster

        No it does not. The black hole horizon can "wobble" around, although any excitation will quickly dissipate (exponential decay). If you followed the recent gravitational wave observations of black holes, you might have noticed this "ring-down" at the end of the signal. That's basically the spacetime still wobbling around. It will be quickly indistinguishable from a regular Schwarzschild (or Kerr) spacetime. That's what the "No hair theorem" is about: black holes have no additional properties besides mass, spin and charge.

        But wouldn't be a exponential ring-down still be technically a distinguishing feature? Given precise enough measurements, couldn't we still get information about the hole's past? Yes, but so can we by just looking at the black hole. You can still see everything that's falling in, only redshifted very strongly. So that's way easier then measuring the miniscule gravitational waves.

        The no hair theorem is really about the asymptotic state of the hole, or holes that existed forever. Like in OPs question mentioned, you will never see the collapse of mass to a black hole, only infalling mass redshifted (also exponentially) with time.
