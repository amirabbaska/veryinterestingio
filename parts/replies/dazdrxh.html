    Replying to the gmo bit only: I farm in southern Saskatchewan and while we don't grow corn we do grow gmo canola and tried gmo soybeans. Soybeans are just not the right crop but the canola works well.

    Our variety in particular allows us to spray with a chemical that is different from round-up.

    • This is good because it breaks up the tolerance weeds might develop.
    • Second advantage is that is makes it easy to clean our field in one pass, meaning we use less chemicals, less fuel, and we can time everything better.
    • Canola in particular is virtually impossible to keep clean without chemicals so organic Canola is as far as impossible to grow on a larger scale.

    While I am a conventual grower, I do not hate on the organic farmers. I just disagree but each to their own. What you should not forget is that organic farming needs a lot more tillage which burns a lot of fuel. There are a lot of chemicals that are allowed on an organic farm, but they are less precise than the engineered chemicals. The yield is lower, meaning you need to work more land to feed the same amount of people.

    Just as a heads up, things like BT corn which is a corn hybrid requires less pesticides due to its innate ability to kill off pests.

    Organic corn avoids this yield loss by using sprayed on pesticides. By innately reducing the need for applied chemicals your already reducing the carbon footprint.
