      • rewboss

        This is emphasis and intonation, not tone, so it's not accurate to call English a "semi-tonal language". These features are used to reflect the grammatical structure of a sentence -- for example, distinguishing between defining and non-defining relative clauses, or between statements and questions -- or to highlight what the speaker considers important, or to reflect the speaker's attitude in relation to the utterance -- for example, there is an intonation pattern for sarcasm, indicating that the speaker believes the opposite of what he's saying and intends to insult or belittle.

        In your example, the meaning is exactly the same in every case -- a female whose identity has already been established has struck an identifiable dog. What's different in each case is which part of the sentence the speaker wants to draw special attention to.
