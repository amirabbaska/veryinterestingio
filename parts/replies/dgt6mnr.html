    • extyn

      So here's the thing we're taught at this job. We're not NSA and talking about specific phone conversations or altering captioning content infringes on FCC requirements for captioning and the right to customer privacy. The customer needs to feel that whenever they're on the phone they can trust their live interpreter to translate what they can't hear as accurately as possible and to not interfere with private conversations. This is especially important because sometimes we hear credit card numbers or banking information through the phone and a company can lose credibility pretty fast if word gets around that calls can be altered or the interpreter makes themselves known in the call.

      We are trained to be interpreters, not crime stoppers. We're just the medium for helping the hard of hearing, but like I've said before we do forward call IDs of scams over to operations. I'm not sure what they do about it, but I do hear that they analyze and then compile 'newsletters' to customers to keep them up-to-date on new scams. That's as close as we can get to actually helping.

      I personally haven't had a call recently where someone has fallen for a scam. Most of the time the customer hangs up which I'm grateful for.

    How'd you get into that line of work? Those captioning phones need an automatic disconnect trigger for the phrase "Windows key and letter R" among many others.

    • extyn

      It's really not that difficult so long as you have a call center stationed in your area. They don't do remote monitoring for obvious reasons so you're physically in a cubicle taking calls. The only things required are a basic understanding of English and grammar (or Spanish now that we caption Spanish calls now), understanding basic computer software, a high WPM typing speed, and the ability to repeat fast conversations verbatim into the software (ours is Dragon). There's only two tests: the typing test and the captioning test. If your score is high enough, you get hired and sent into training. Graduating from training lets you loose into the actual call floor.

      It's a pretty cushy job and I like the people who work there. If you spend most of your life in front of a computer and hate talking to actual customers this is probably the job for you.

      Unfortunately, we can't screen calls and disconnect them on a whim. The FCC pays us to act as a captioning service. If we don't take calls, we don't get paid, which I get. The service is used to help the customer's discomfort talking on the phone, not screen the calls like Big Brother.
