    • tx69er

      It means both. When talking about nuclear decay, it is as you stated, the time taken for the radioactivity of a specified isotope to fall to half its original value.

      However when you are talking about something in a biological sense, it is the time required for the concentration of a substance in the body to decrease by half.

      So if you ingested tritium you would be dealing with both definitions at once because there would be one timeline for the radioactive decay, and an entirely separate timeline for the biological elimination of the tritium itself.

    • NewUnit18

      Half life is that, yes. Biological half life is the amount of time it takes for your body to eliminate or metabolise half of a given amount of a given substance.

    About reactions rates being different for different isotopes, that's called the kinetic isotope effect. It's used for a lot of things outside of this stuff. For example, it is used to give us insight into reaction mechanisms - that is, it can help tell us how a chemical reaction works based on whether a reaction is faster with a heaver or lighter isotope.
