    • kem0022

      You actually get a function when you integrate. For the 200 ft beam, I would integrate my moment function (times a constant representing material properties) from 0 to x, where x is my axial position. I would then end up with an equation for slope in terms of x. Integrating again gives an equation for deflection in terms of x.

      As for the average deflection, it depends not only on the amount of force but also its point of application. For example, pushing down at the end of a beam results in a lot more deflection than pushing down near the base. You could make the average deflection of the two beams match by moving the force on the long beam, but the deflection function would be different.

      For examples of what these functions look like for different load cases, I recommend checking out an undergraduate Mechanics of Materials book. I have one by Hibbeler that has a nice chart at the back. You can also get some results if you Google “slopes and deflections of beams.”

    • kem0022

      I am not sure if OP meant something cantilevered or something with multiple points of support. If it is the latter case, the problem becomes more complicated as it would be statically indeterminate and you would have to apply some more advanced methods to determine the reaction forces at each point, but the basic principle of integrating the bending moment remains the same.

      You also have the weight of the beam itself, which is of course larger in the longer case. Its relative contribution to the overall deflection will depend on how large the applied load is compared to the weight.

      The modulus of elasticity of the material is independent of the size of the beam, so that is not the distinguishing factor between the two cases. It would come down to the weight of the structure and the bending moment.
