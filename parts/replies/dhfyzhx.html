    • mofo69extreme

      Well the kinetic energy of the electron also contributes to the total energy. To be very precise, I would say (in both the Bohr model and full QM) that the excited states in Hydrogen are due to a a decrease in the kinetic energy but a larger increase in the potential energy. For the Hydrogen atom you can prove that in going to an excited state with extra energy ΔE, the kinetic energy decreases by ΔE but the potential energy increases by 2ΔE to make up the total energy shift (this holds exactly in the Bohr model, and on average in real QM).

      In a generic situation you don't always know "how much" energy is going into kinetic versus potential or other forms without doing the specific calculation.

      exited states of baryons have higher energies in the form of mass (due to E=mc^(2))

      The excited baryon states are also due to kinetic and interaction energies of quarks and gluons etc. But all energy takes the form of mass. A Hydrogen atom in its first excited state is actually more massive than a Hydrogen atom in its ground state, and and both are lighter than a free proton mass plus a free electron mass.
