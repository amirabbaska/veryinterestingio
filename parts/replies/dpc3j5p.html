    • CrustalTrudger

      In simple terms, the size of a tsunami will scale with the volume of water displaced, but how exactly this is related to the details of the earthquake are a little more nebulous. Common idea is that it's directly linked to the amount of vertical deflection of the surface, but, sticking with the Tohoku example, some recent work suggests that it's really the integrated motion of the surface, which to someone who doesn't study tsunamis but works on thrust faults on land, this makes sense and frankly I'm kind of surprised that previous tsunami generation models discounted the role of horizontal motion as this paper suggests.

      In terms of how the surface displacement relates to net slip, maximum slip at depth, etc this gets into a lot of details and starts to bring in questions of coseismic vs postseismic moment of the fault and details of the rupture complexity among a host of other things. We tend to like to think of earthquakes and ruptures of faults as simple movement on a single fault plane, but astoundingly complex fault ruptures like what we saw in Kaikoura really give us a sense that things can get real tricky, real quick.

    • CrustalTrudger

      Again, I have not been able to find any study that quantifies this in any sense or provides any evidence of this killing or harming marine creatures from sound or pressure waves within the water. Submarine earthquakes definitely do produce sound within the water, this page has a video which contains a sound recording from a hydrophone network near Alaska, again in relation to the Tohoku event. This is obviously quite distant from the source, but a relevant bit is that these sounds are quite low frequency (even in the video that has been sped up 16x they sound low frequency) so I'm not sure how (or if) sound waves like this would do anything to marine creatures that are sensitive to sound or pressure changes.

      EDIT: To add to this, for reference the exact frequency of seismic waves will vary as a function of the details of the earthquake and the properties of the medium through which they are traveling, but p-waves usually have frequencies of no more than a few Hz and s-waves will have much lower frequencies, usually below 0.1 Hz so these are VERY different frequencies than things like sonar (that seem to mess with some marine organisms). Additionally, with something like dynamite, you are producing a compressional wave VERY quickly, where as an earthquake is a quick event geologically, but for a big event, it may take up to several minutes for the rupture to finish so while we have never been able to measure the rate of surface deformation during an earthquake (closest we've come are things like this which are records of gps position changes through time, but notice you're seeing displacements happen over minutes), the surface displacement is not all appearing instantaneously, it's likely growing over several seconds to a minute or two so the possibility of producing a compressional wave that's going to physically hurt marine animals doesn't seem that likely (though I could be wrong).
