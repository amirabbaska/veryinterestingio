    • GoldeneyeLife

      Think of ASPD from this perspective: humans are naturally "social" creatures, meaning they generally require interactions with other humans as companions and allies to survive, and especially to reproduce. They need cooperation, directly and indirectly, to allow growth, and their brains are designed and conditioned for this.

      People who are "antisocial" (lit. "opposed to fellowship or fellow man") display behaviour that actively contradicts that social standard (which is generally assumed to be the "natural" state for humanity) by remorselessly disregarding the rights or state of other people. This can be through deception, violence, irresponsibility, etc.

      Empathy is seen as a key function in the success of the co operative welfare of humanity (ex. I won't hurt others because I can understand how that would affect them and others close to them, I won't steal because I know how damaging it could be to others), and people with ASPD almost universally lack empathy as well as a sincere value on their own well being, leading to what people would see as "callousness" or "coldness", making it extremely difficult for them to relate to others, work closely with others, and see others as equal to themselves. This makes genuine interpersonal relationships extremely difficult, and impairs their ability to properly function in a society.

      That's the backbone of the disorder and it's implications, I hope that wasn't too long or confusing

    • Jedakiah

      It might help if you provided some example articles of people using these terms. Then you can get specific feedback on why someone used a given term. You will likely find that:

      a) The article is based on outdated concepts or understandings. In medicine and psychology a lot of disorders start out with fairly generic classifications. As time goes on and we learn more, more specific classifications tend to replace the old ones.

      b) Somewhere in the article the author explicitly details what they mean by the term, typically at the outset. Both words have such vague definitions that to an extent they can be used to mean whatever you want them to mean. Thus a definition often accompanies the usage.

      c) The author is a "specialist" in name only.
