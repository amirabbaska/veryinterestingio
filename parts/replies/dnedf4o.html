        oh we noticed a loss in vacuum. Let's go test the whole system...or shut it down in parts until we find the 1' by 5'' hole caused by ___.

        That's not how it works with a vacuum tube unless its a very, very tiny leak that happens gradually. A single bullet hole can be enough to rapidly destroy the entire system. Unless you can quickly seal off parts of the system with seals that can remain vacuum tight when hit with a powerful shock wave traveling at about the speed of sound, the damage probably can't be contained to one part of the system.

        The fact that one failure can destroy the entire hyperloop system is a critical flaw in the idea. If the same thing happens on an airplane, most people will probably survive (see Aloha Airlines Flight 243), and the damage will be limited to that particular plane, and will often be repairable. And that will only happen if the relatively little skin on an aircraft fails.

        On the other hand the, huge skin of the hyperloop system has to span all the way from the source to destination for every city it goes to. If any of it fails, which could happen from gunshots, a car crash that hits the hyperloop, and many other causes that are less likely to happen to planes tens of thousands of feet in the air, it will likely kill everyone on that system and destroy the entire system. If you make a hyperloop line from LA to San Francisco, a failure in one part will likely total that entire line and kill everyone on it. That's like if the skin on one airplane fails, everyone on every flight dies, and all airplanes are destroyed.

        I have a lot of respect for Elon Musk, but this this particular idea is a really bad one that isn't even new. It's been rejected in the past for good reasons.
