    • Laser6706

      Electricity doesn't "know" anything. Individual electrons are subject to electrical potentials. The electrons are drawn to areas that are deficient in electrons (positive) and repelled from areas that are electron rich (negative). The speed at which the electrons move is dependent on the conductivity of the material.

      Circuits are just paths where the conductivity is high enough to allow an appreciable number of electrons to flow.

      As a side note, what we call an insulator or a conductor is just an arbitrary amount of conductivity. Electricity will still flow through an insulator, albeit at a very low rate. This is why unused batteries can discharge over time.

    • argon435

      Let me try to explain using an analogy.

      Imagine a hose with marbles inside of it. Now, let's take a spare marble in our hand and push it into the hoses. Virtually instantly (to our eyes) a different marble will pop out the other side of the hose. Now what if we put a plug on the other end of the hose? When we try to push the marble into the hose, we can't. There isn't room. In this analogy, the hoses are like wires, and the marbles are the electrons inside of them. Pushing marbles into the hose is the same as having a battery supply a voltage to the system. Plugging the hose is the same as adding an insulator or opening a switch to stop the circuit.

      When you activate a circuit, the electrons already inside the system start to flow. If the circuit is broken, then the electrons already inside the system stop you from adding more to the system. In this way, the electrons can tell which part of the circuit is "on" and which part is "off" because the "off" parts are already full of electrons and just can't accept any more.
