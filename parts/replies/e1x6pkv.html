  • mike3

    However, this does not appear to answer the question. It seems more to be answering the question "would ordinary matter behave like a 'gas' with regard to a collision by a piece of EDM?" The question that is posted, however, is asking what phase of ordinary matter EDM itself is most equivalent to: is it most like a solid, or a liquid, or a gas? If it's "hard", that suggests it is most like a solid. However - as we know even from something relatively more ordinary, at least in an astrophysical context, like the dynamics of the matter inside the Earth, the properties of being solid, liquid, and gas are not necessarily mutually exclusive. The matter in the Earth's mantle, for example, is "solid" if you took out a small chunk and could somehow keep it at those conditions by magic while existing in ordinary atmosphere as you're imagining here with the EDM, yet over long timescales (hundreds of teraseconds or more) and distances (hundreds of kilometers or more) it behaves like a liquid, flowing round and round in giant convective currents. So naive inferences from properties like that can be misleading. "Gas" though suggests compressibility over a wide range of pressures (i.e. more than one order of magnitude) without changing phase or otherwise undergoing any sudden shifts. However, EDM is at least very far from an ideal gas - that's the whole point.

    It seems the descriptors "solid", "liquid", and "gas" really only best apply to matter right around our ordinary human scale, maybe within a couple of orders of magnitude or so - so pressures on the order of 100 kPa, temps on the order of 300 K, densities on the order 1-1000 kg/m\^3, times on the kilosecond and perhaps single megaseconds scale (so up to order of 1000 ks). Get a bit of a ways out of that - and we don't even need to go to astrophysical extremes as our deep inner Earth example shows - and the lines become blurry and the picture considerably more complex. And an even less "extreme" example in terms of immediate conditions, which details the last point about time, consider pitch. Over gigasecond scales - so about a human lifetime and longer, it flows, yet if you take some in your hand, you'd be forgiven for thinking you were holding something rather chunk-like. In fact there's about a 100-year (3 gig) experiment that has involved dropping pitch, and it has dropped only like a 3 or 4 drops over that time. ADD: it's 9 drops. So about 1 drop every 300 Ms. And following the deep-Earth lead, if we go to slightly more extreme but still planetary contexts you get lots of phases that defy all three, e.g. what is the "superionic water" deep inside Neptune?
