      However, the fact doesn't change that both photochemical smog and the reddish-brown bands of Jupiter are due to photochemical reactions of hydrocarbons, and my use of that word was intentional, because the resultant chemicals formed need not necessarily be alkanes. It is likely that they probably aren't alkanes at all. Jupiter itself is a very energetic planet, and emits plenty of infrared radiationâ€”more than it receives from the Sun, at least. Given such particularly energetic conditions compared to even Earth (and its much higher insolation), it is indubitable that the atmosphere of Jupiter is, to put it simply, one hot mess.

      The chemistry of Jupiter's bands and belts is quite complex and not yet fully understood. It is clear, however, that the trace hydrocarbons, sulfurous and nitrogenous compounds in its atmosphere strongly contribute to the darker colours of the bands of Jupiter.
