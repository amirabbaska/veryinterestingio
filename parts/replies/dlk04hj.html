    • germanodactylus

      Animals (life in general really) are classified by analyzing and comparing characteristics. Like means of reproduction, nervous systems, skeletal structures, cell structure, and more recently, genetics. (i.e. chordates are animals with notochords) Organisms more similar are thought to be more closely related. Clades are usually described through relation to other organisms (i.e. dinosaurs are currently defined as the clade consisting of the last common ancestor of Triceratops horridus, Passer domesticus (sparrow), and Diplodocus carnegii and all its descendants).

      Organisms get reclassified as more analysis is done. When analyzing characteristics you gotta realize the list is practically infinite, so it's not very easy to analyze everything, but studies that analyze more characteristics are usually thought to be more accurate. Thankfully, computers have made processing a lot of data a lot easier on biologists so hopefully the current tree of life is a bit more accurate, but there are still a LOT of organisms out there.

    • mlyellow

      A lot of times it's from genetic studies. This is how we know that there are actually two species of clouded leopards, not one, for example. Their ancestors diverged 1.4 million years ago; however, they look very much alike. Likewise, the hog deer turned out to actually be four separate species, not one.
