    • bodaciousbum

      The cells in our body need a certain amount of oxygen/minute to function properly. Increasing heart rate increases the blood flow/minute to these cells. If you increase the blood flow and keep the amount of oxygen entering the bloodstream the same (for each heart beat), then the overall amount of oxygen delivered to the cells increases.

      Whenever I visit high altitude locations I notice that my breathes are involuntarily deeper and sometimes faster (depending on activity) than at normal altitude. All 3 things (depth of breath, breath rate, and heart rate) help more oxygen enter the bloodstream.

    • YandalfTheYellow

      It has to deal with partial pressure in the lungs and the affinity (how well one thing bonds to another). As you increase altitude, partial pressure of O2 decreases, yet the affinity of O2 when attached to red blood cells stays the same. With lower partial pressure, comes lower affinity for O2. Now the amount unloaded into the cells is the same, but the amount of O2 taken in by the lungs is vastly smaller. Luckily, our body has a molecule (2,3-BPG) that reduces the affinity when offloading O2 into cells. So it just shifts everything: lungs take in less O2 but red blood cells are able to let go of more, therefore the net difference is the same as if you were at sea level. This is why athletes train at high altitude, they increase their BPG levels and then go back down to sea level to perform. They get more O2, and release more into the cells: a win win and a nice advantage.
