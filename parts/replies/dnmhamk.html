    • ShapeWords

      Haha, you and /u/MrFlippyNips both commented with very similar questions at the same time. I don't know enough about the nitty-gritty details of the experiment to say for sure, but the researchers were specifically selecting for 'tameness', or a willingness to be handled and bond with humans. Since a lot of what we consider tameness is the retention of juvenile traits (trusting, cuddly, submissive), tail-wagging as a form of greeting and happiness might be piggybacking on whatever genetic factors control those juvenile tendencies. One interesting thing to come out of the experiment was that other dog-like qualities that seem initially unrelated to tameness, like multi-colored coats and floppy ears, also began showing up in the foxes.

    • ShapeWords

      They do have control over it, it's not a reflexive action. It's very similar to laughing or scowling--they might suddenly wag their tails in response to something the same way you might laugh if you unexpectedly saw something funny, but they are capable of stopping if they want to. Animals just usually don't have a lot of reasons to suppress their visible emotions in the same way humans do.
