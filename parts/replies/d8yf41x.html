    Picture of the Fat Man nuclear core being assembled

    EVERY ONE of those polygonal bricks of high explosive must ignite at EXACTLY the same instant for a successful nuclear detonation. One of the most difficult items to collect-- at least as difficult to obtain as the uranium or plutonium itself-- is the detonator required to precisely fire these explosive polygons. There is no other reason to have one of these than to cause a fission explosion.

    The explosions of the polygons drives the blast wave inward, compressing the nuclear material (plutonium in this case) into a supercritical mass.

    • Hypothesis_Null

      I do. But rather than write it up, I'll let the soothing voice of Bill Hammack: The Engineer Guy say it for me (and say it better).

      There is technical difficulty in precision machining and timing of the bomb and its conventional explosives. But with enough money, and with modern technology, that is a fairly surmountable obstacle - see North Korea.

      But the greater difficulty is in the logistics and precision involved in enriching the Uranium necessary for a bomb. The precision needed on centrifuges is arguably greater than that involved in the bomb itself. And no matter what, it will be massively energy-intensive, and massively expensive. You need a very wealthy government with a lot of money and people to throw at the project.

      I'll let Bill say the rest. It's an excellent video.

      Side Note: Enriched Uranium can be used to make a Uranium-Bullet bomb like Little Boy, which is much easier to make. The type of bomb I described above is a Plutonium bomb (Fat Man), but making plutonium is typically a hell of a lot more difficult than enriching Uranium (You need active nuclear reactors). So anyone looking to simply have any sort of nuclear bomb will likely go after Uranium. The necessary precision of the type of bomb that implodes a plutonium core (maybe a uranium core works as well? I'm not sure) is part of what makes it so much safer.
