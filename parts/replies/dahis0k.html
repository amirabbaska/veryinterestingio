    This is the right idea, but carry it a little farther. The nut or whatever the hole is in is homogenous metal and has a both a circumference and a diameter. The expansion is really the inflation of the material along the circumference. If you expand a circumference of 1 and diameter of .32, by 10%, the circumference is 1.1, giving a diameter of 0.35, a variation of .03. BUT the outside circumference of the bolt nut is 5, with a diameter of 1.6, then heated becomes circumference of 5.5 and diameter of 1.75, a variation of .15 in diameter. The body of the nut does not become .12 "thicker". The expansion of the body of the nut PULLS the ID away from the bolt.

    Thought experiment If you have a large flat circular plate with a small hole in the center and heat only the outside circumference of the plate, what happens to the ID of the hole?

    Therefore, the hole will always expand faster than the thing filling the hole as the temperature rises.
