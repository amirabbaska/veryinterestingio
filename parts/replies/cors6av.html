    • higgs_broson137

      Yes, in a theory about elementary particles, global symmetries are (in a sense) empirical. We know the form of the matter field equations from previous knowledge of the matter fields, and the global symmetry is inherent to those equations.

      As to the local symmetry, yes I think it's fair to say that demanding the local symmetry hold is just an axiom -- i.e. a GUESS. But, it's not a blind guess; there's a lot of logic there, and it has to do with geometry. There's a strong analogy that exists between gauge theories and einsteins theory of gravity (which is also a gauge theory, just an unusual one. glossing over this..). Basically global transformations are analogous to intertial changes of reference frame (changing to a reference frame moving at constant velocity), and local transformations are analogous to nonineratial reference frames (changing to a reference frame which is accelerating). When you change to a reference frame that is accelerating (like a car accelerating) it feels like there is a new force that wasn't there before. I.e. the theory has changed because theres this new "force" hanging around. The analogous thing in gauge theory is that when you do a local transformation you get a new term from taking the derivative of the phase factor.

      Einsteins big conceptual leap in developing general relativity was that he said physics should be the same in all reference frames, inertial or otherwise. So what he had to do was introduce a new field (called the metric, which leads to curved space and all that jazz) in order to make the theory invariant.

      In gauge theory, we basically say the same thing. We postulate that the theory should be invariant under the local transformation, and we have to introduce a gauge field (force field).

    • higgs_broson137

      Edit: I'm going to stop being mysterious and just use the word Lagrangian. The equation for your fields is called a lagrangian.

      Basically the way you build a lagrangian for your matter fields is you decide which symmetries that you want your theory to have and you write down ALL possible terms which obey the symmetries. In the example above, I could have included + c|phi|^4 +d|phi|^6 + .... This sounds nuts, but it can actually be managed because there can be quite a few constraints from symmetry.

      Although, in general there are still an infinite number of terms. Through a process called renormalization, the higher order terms get suppressed. They are still there, but the coefficients in front of them are very small and so they can be ignored. If fact, even if you don't put the higher order interactions into your lagrangian initially, they will be "dynamically generated" through the renormalization procedure. I know this doesn't make sense, and I would need a dedicated post and a lot more thought to explain renormalization properly, but trust me it's really cool.

      One symmetry that I didn't mention above is Lorentz symmetry. This is a fancy name for "physics is the same in all reference frames" (remember Einstein?). A theory which is invariant under change of reference frame is called Lorentz invariant, which all good relativistic theories should be. This is one big thing that constrains the form of your Lagrangian. We categorize different Lorentz invariant objects by their spin (intrinsic magnetic moment). All the matter fields we know about have spin 1/2, and all the force fields have spin 1. this is another way of differentiating between what is a force and what is matter. That was all we knew existed until we discovered the higgs boson, which has spin 0. If you're wondering "what about spin 53 or 243/2?" We don't know; maybe the big physicist in the sky didn't want to do the algebra. But for some reason the simplest Lagrangians tend to be the ones that are realized the most in fundamental physics.
