    • wraithcube

      You're going to very quickly run into urban vs rural divides. Let's look at Illinois as an example. Most of the population is centered around chicago in the upper right. Here is what current districts look like.

      The population of Illinois as a whole is 12.9 million. The population of the chicago metropolitan area is 9.5 million.

      If you do it with similar size grids of say 10 districts you can end up with the 9.5 million all in 1 district and the remaining 3.4 million have control of the remaining 9 districts.

      If you do it by population the chicago area will control 7 districts and the entire rest of the state will only have 3 allowing the city of chicago super majority control of the state.

      Any computer algorithm runs into similar problems because you have to tell the program how to divide the population.

    • kouhoutek

      The problem is that the population is concentrated into cities.

      If the grids are big enough to prevent gerrymandering, too many people will be in the downtown LA grid and not enough in the rural Kern county grid.

      If they are small enough to allow a proper balance, they are small enough to allow gerrymandering.

      Also, grids have a hard time following federal court orders.
