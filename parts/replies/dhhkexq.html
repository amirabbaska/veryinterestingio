There are a lot of adaptive feedback mechanisms available to help mitigate these problems, but application is dependent on the inputs, and if you build a sloppy fixture or stamping sends you crappy parts, the robot is just going to go where it's programmed to go, and if it's off 1mm relative to the part, you get squeeze-out/expulsion, and possible corrosion issues.

    On the other hand, human labor on mass production is incredibly expensive, and so if you have 1 TGW per 10,000 in mass production, warranty eats that and it's perfectly acceptable.

    If the parts are perfect, and the locator strategy is perfect, a robot will win 100% of the time. Except, nothing is ever perfect, and humans have better feedback mechanisms. It comes down to what's important (money). I don't say that cynically, either. I'm happy to pay $40,000 for a Mustang versus $250,000 for a "perfect" Rolls.
