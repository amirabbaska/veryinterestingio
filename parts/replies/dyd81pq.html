    This is a good answer, and it is the only way flat (meaning straight, not counting roughness) surfaces could be achieved before interferometry.

    And even now the same principle is used, and on the highest metrological level flatness is realized using 3 reference plates, which are measured with an interferometer and rotated to compensate for errors with a symmetry. From these measurements you get a height-error map for each surface.

    Once you have a flat reference surface, you can build machines using it as a reference, and derive basically everything else.

    Amateur astronomers have been hand grinding their own telescope mirrors to better than 1/4 to 1/20 of the wavelength of light since the 1920's. This is a level of accuracy of better than 30 nm / one-millionth of an inch.

    Basically you start with a flat piece of glass for your mirror and a second one for grinding or you can make a grinding tool with plaster and tile. By sliding one over the other with successively finer abrasive grits you'll naturally get a concave, spherical surface.

    Eventually you switch to polishing techniques and then you figure the mirror to change the spherical surface to a parabola. Finally you have the finished surface coated and you're done.

    Hand made mirrors are often much better quality than ones made with machines. While it was common for people to make their own mirrors a few decades ago, it's far less common today.
