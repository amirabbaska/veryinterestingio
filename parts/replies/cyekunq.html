      Whilst the term "soccer" is indeed a British invention, there was never a national consensus within Britain of calling association football "soccer". That term was used initially and mostly within public schools and universities as a shorthand to distinguish "rugby football" from "association football". Of course association football became the predominant code, hence common usage eventually just became "rugby" and "football". Soccer remained popular in countries where the predominant football code wasn't association football.

    • obey-the-fist

      No, the soccer/football thing is different.

      Americans play a variant of Rugby called Gridiron. American universities got together in the late 1800's and standardised a set of rules for Rugby which they would use to play amongst themselves. This caught on pretty well domestically.

      The international offshoots of Rugby are still played quite passionately globally under a series of different codes (league, union, etc), and the competitions involve multiple nations.

      Australians play a totally different code which is an unusual hybrid of Rugby and Football, which they refer to as "AFL" (and well worth checking out as the gameplay is very energetic). Believe it or not, the Australian rules are older than the American College rules (by a decade or so).

      Rugby itself is a variation of the more traditional game of football, which was played in various villages and tribes around the world in various forms for centuries. The mainstream game of football, "Association football" is popular worldwide and derives from the formal English/European rules for the game. It's called "Soccer" in countries that have more prominent football codes to avoid confusion.
