    The hot Jupiters that you are asking about likely did not "move" closer to their sun but accreted near during solar formation.

    I'm not sure why you make the claim this is "likely" - these are two competing theories, and in situ formation is generally the less favorable of the two since it requires exceptionally high inner disk densities to operate. In situ formed hot Jupiters would also be frequently accompanied by nearby non-resonant planets, which so far have not been seen in observations in such numbers.

    In effect, the solar nebula attempted to condense TWO stars

    It sounds like you're suggesting a gas instability scenario here for in situ formation, which is very implausible given disk conditions - there's just not enough gas density that close to compensate for the fast rotation period. See section 2.1.1 of the paper that /u/nhammen provided.
