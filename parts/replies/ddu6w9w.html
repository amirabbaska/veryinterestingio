  • da_chicken

    The papers, stations, sites, whoever, prefer to not "burn the bridge" with both individual politicians and larger organizations like state and national parties.

    And, it's important to point out: they do not want to burn bridges with advertisers.

    Historically the media got the lion's share of their funding from newspaper subscriptions. Yes, advertising revenue was important, but the key was that the readers were the core customer base. Therefore, newspapers had a vested interest in producing the highest quality news because that sold more copies of the newspaper, which also had the added benefit of increasing the advertiser interest in the paper since more people would read it.

    Now, however, that's not the case. Most people don't pay for news stories. The reader has stopped being a customer, and has become a consumer. Instead, the newspaper's customers are the advertisers, and the reader is the produce they sell. Well, what do advertisers want? More readers regardless of the reason. They don't care why someone is reading the newspaper, they just want the eyeballs on their ads. So, news quality is suddenly less important. It's more important to have stories that get people reading repeatedly, regardless of the quality -- or, indeed, the truth -- of the news stories published. We see a shift from the primary focus of a newspaper from providing accurate news to the reader, to providing the best vehicle for ads to the advertisers. It was journalistic integrity and marketplace competition that kept the news accurate and informative. Today? Well, you're marketing to advertisers, and journalistic integrity is too expensive to bother with, especially when it just upsets your customers!

    You might ask, "What about television news? Weren't they always reliant on advertising?" And, yes, that's true. However, before the mid 80s the FCC required that all broadcast services employ the Fairness Doctrine, which meant equal time for opposing viewpoints. Under this system, even if advertisers don't like it you were required to fairly show both sides of the issues. The system was abandoned in the 80s mostly because the original reason for the Fairness Doctrine was because there were so few broadcasters and the barrier to entry was so high compared to a newspaper (many locations only had one affiliate station for ABC, CBS, and NBC, plus public broadcasting). Once cable TV took off, the argument was that broadcasting was much more available since most people had access to dozens of stations. Unfortunately, it meant that advertising and corporate interests could freely spend money to control the content of a news station.

    So, we have two changes that have undermined the news media, whose intended interests should be creating a public informed about the truth. Instead, we have a news industry whose actual interest is in creating a public informed about the products and opinions of others.
