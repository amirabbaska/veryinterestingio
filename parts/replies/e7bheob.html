    • Mr_Ivan_Karamazov

      Thanks for asking! That seems intuitive, right? We know there are these huge, multi-millennia effects that gradually shift our climate--so why isn't that just happening right now? We need to consider a couple different things to address this, so bear with me.

      ​

      First, we know absolutely that we're pumping CO2 into the atmosphere at an extremely high rate. Nobody is doubting this. It's just a known fact of what happens when you burn fossil fuels. We also know, without any doubt, how CO2 interacts with light; this can be observed in the environment, and perfectly replicated in the lab--CO2 allows visible light to pass, but absorbs infrared radiation and turns it into heat. That's what the "greenhouse effect" is, and, again, there's absolutely no question about that science. So it's known absolutely that greenhouse gases are increasing, and we know absolutely what the effect of that is.

      ​

      Second --But, as I mentioned before, climate is incredibly complicated, and how do we know the current heating is due to increased greenhouse gases? Well, even though it's hard to accurately predict the magnitude that the other causes of climate change have, we do know which ones contribute, and which ones detract--and it's relatively trivial to track volcanic activity, sunspots, and the tilt of the earth. And if you just look at these factors, the earth should be cooling slightly right now, as discussed in this article.

      ​

      Third, We can look at the rate of temperature change that we're experiencing, and the only climate change factor it corresponds with is the increase in greenhouse gases since the industrial revolution. This relationship is elegant;y shown here. Note that that the illustration at that link is not from a peer-reviewed article, but the author is a former scientist and engineer for NASA, and the information is very easy to fact-check, if you're so inclined.
