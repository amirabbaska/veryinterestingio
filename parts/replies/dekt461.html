      Nope, the observable fact is that heavy inbreeding often doesn't obliterate populations all by itself. It reduces fertility and increases susceptibility to disease, but if conditions are otherwise favorable that wouldn't necessarily result in population failure by itself.

      Examples of this are numerous. All the golden hamsters you see in captivity are descended from the offspring of a single female that was collected in the 30's. Many mice strains used in labs are similarly inbred. Most terrestrial animals living on oceanic islands are descended from similarly small starter populations that happened to be swept there (sometimes a single pregnant female). Of course, what all these situations have in common is that the ecological conditions are very favorable...you don't have to worry about disease very much when in captivity or when 1000 miles over water from the nearest sick individual. You don't have to worry about food much when you get it delivered in pellet form or when you are the first on an empty island where no competitors live. You don't have to worry about predators in either case. Sure, maybe half your offspring die of being inbred, but 75% aren't dying of starvation or predation, so you can still come out ahead.
