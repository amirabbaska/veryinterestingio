      Is it like a P vs. NP problem though?

      Yes. Probably.

      It's not known whether BQP=NP (as in, whether a quantum system can efficiently act as an oracle for all NP complete problems) and it is suspected not to be the case. However, an efficient quantum emulator could solve at least one problem that is in NP in polynomial time (factoring). That implies that either factoring is really in P or that a quantum simulator is NOT in P.

      All our current algorithms for classical quantum simulation run in exponential time.

    • 6thReplacementMonkey

      The only principles I know of that limit scaling are in some sense practical. First, you have precision issues whenever you deal with large numbers of interacting particles interacting through energy fields. At 64 bit precision, you get divergence in trajectories after just a couple of dozen femto seconds in a classical simulation. That's without accounting for error in the field calculations themselves due to limited precision. Then you have physical limitations - it takes a certain minimum number of atoms to build a transistor, and certain minimum numbers of those to represent the coordinates of a given system. Those numbers combined are always much larger than the number of physical objects that are represented in the system. So any machine you would use to simulate macro-scale systems would have to be much larger (physically, in terms of number of atoms) than the system itself. Finally, there are time constraints. It takes a certain minimum number of operations to compute gradients in fields and to move the system along them. Macro systems simulated down to the fundamental particle level would take such a large number that even with billion or trillion fold improvements in computational speed, it would still take much too long for the simulation itself to be worth anything.

      So I guess one way to describe it is that there is nothing that theoretically says you can't write a set of equations that would describe the evolution of a macroscopic system in terms of its fundamental particles as a function of time. However, if you want to try to solve those equations, or even represent that system in some sort of simulation, you quickly run into physical and theoretical limits.
