    • akanosora

      Because Japanese borrowed Chinese numbering system entirely for numbers (called 漢数字) but still use its original 和文 for counting people. Originally, there were 和文 numbers as well but not used in modern day Japanese anymore. For example, number 2 as a number is に which is a borrowed word of 二 in Chinese (In southern China, 二 is still pronounced as "ni" similar to Japanese). But two people is ふたり in which ふた is "two" in 和文. When you say twenty-two in Japanese, it is に じゅう に which is actually Chinese. The 和文 twenty-two would be はたち (twenty) あまり (the rest) ふたつ (two). Some of the 和文 forms of counting numbers are so archaic that there is no way to actually prove how they were actually counted (especially those larger numbers that were rarely used in the ancient time).

  • tiff1307

    Using the wrong measure word is actually a fairly common mistake that children or new speakers make, because specific nouns require specific measure words (sort of like how we say 'a gaggle of geese' versus a 'herd of cows'). For instance, to build on Mingly's examples, when referring to people you'd say 几个人, but if referring to cars, you wouldn't use 个 as the measure word. You'd use "liang" or 辆 which is a vehicle-specific measure word.
