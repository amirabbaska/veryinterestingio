    One distinction between the US highway system and the interstate system is that the primary north-south US routes terminate in 1 (e.g., 1, 11, 21, 31, ..., 91, 101). The three-digit US routes generally connect to their parent route (e.g., US 50 intersects to US 150 in Vincennes, Indiana).

    Many US routes were truncated upon the creation of the Eisenhower Interstate system. US 80 was transcontinental between Tybee Island, Georgia and San Diego; the western part was later truncated to Dallas with the construction of I-20, I-10 and I-8.

    Most interstates have an accompanying US route that was most likely the precursor. I-81, I-40, I-75 and I-59 have US-11; I-77 has US-21 and so on. In the western US, the trend was more towards decomissioning the routes. California used to have many more US routes than it currently does.
