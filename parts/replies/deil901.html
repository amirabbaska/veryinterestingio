• luke_in_the_sky

    Exactly. When painting looking to a 3D object, even if it's not moving and you are in a controlled environment where nothing changes, it's not simple to define shapes, color gradients, reflections, refraction, etc.

    But when copying from a photography, it's already in 2D. The color shades are already well defined, the reflections and refraction is already calculated and your POV will not change.

    So is much easier to notice (and even measure) the exact angle of a finger, the roundness of a ear, the refraction of a teardrop, the reflection of the subject's eyes, etc.

    • oblication

      This is coming from fuzzy memory of an 8am art history class over a decade ago but I remember there was a period where mirrors and mock projectors were being used. I say "mock" because they weren't really what we think of as projectors today. They were some kind of lenses and mirrors or something that could be positioned to project a view from one direction, to a wall to be painted. For a time their use was shunned in the art world because it was considered "cheating". But you'll start to notice certain things appearing in paintings like intricately folded table cloths with patterns morphed and folded over on themselves painted to exact proportion. I can't remember if purposeful perspective alterations began after that or before it, but I believe that period influenced our understanding of representing perspective on a 2d plane, simply because it became easier to see exactly how a long corridor appears, when projected on a flat surface for example.
