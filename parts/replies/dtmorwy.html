    • X7123M3-256

      It's because of induced drag.

      The purpose of a wing is to create lift. In order to do this, the air pressure below the wing is higher than the pressure above the wing. Near the wingtip, the high pressure air from below the wing will flow sideways towards the lower pressure on the upper surface. This creates wingtip vortices.

      These wingtip vortices are a significant contribution to the drag on the wing - all the kinetic energy that goes into creating them must come from the wing. If you take a wing and replace it by four wings of 1/4 the size, you now have four times as many wingtips for the same lifting surface, and hence produce more induced drag for the same lift.

      For this reason, longer wings are more efficient than shorter ones - which is why gliders (for which the lift/drag ratio is very important), are designed with very long, thin wings.

    This is a bit misleading. Humans CANNOT fly quadrotors. They must be stabilized by computer control. The human then asks the computer to pitch forward, backward or sideways, or to yaw (turn around the vertical axis). These inputs are easy to understand. The computer turns these requests into power output to each rotor many times per second - which a human could not do.

    Helicopters CAN be flown directly by humans, though the controls are complex (joystick, rudder, power and blade pitch). It takes practice to be able to do this.
