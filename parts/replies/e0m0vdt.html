    It’s a rule-of-thumb that takes the place of painstaking calculations that are only important in high-stakes cases. 30 is about where, for systems that aren’t terribly skewed, the sample mean of the observations is close enough to a normal distribution to treat it as such. With a normally-distributed sample mean, the idea of standard deviation and chi-square test statistic become meaningful.

    You can work with fewer data points, but then it’s better to use exact methods that depend on the process you’re investigating.

    As an example, if you have a loaded coin with heads probability p, you’d flip the coin n times, note x heads, and use x/n as your estimate for p.

    The distribution of x/n is important if you want to put a confidence interval on p. You can show that, for n = 2, 3, or 4 that clearly x/n is not normally distributed, but binomially distributed. And it still is for any value n. But around 30 or so, the binomial distribution is so similar to a normal distribution of the same mean and variance, that you can call it normal (with all of the mercifully simpler math that normal distributions allow) and assume a minimum of error.
