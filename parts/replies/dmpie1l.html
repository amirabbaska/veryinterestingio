    • bweaver94

      No, there are not levels of strength to an event horizon. This is because the definition of the event horizon is where the escape velocity becomes the speed of light. It is important to note that if you were to physically go to the event horizon, you wouldn't see anything special. It is not a physical thing, just a location that corresponds to certain physical properties. At this point, the amount of "bend" in space is so great that any light ray that strays inside will inevitably spiral into the singularity. As you move closer to the center, the "bend" becomes greater and greater, and presumably reaches infinity (so that's how much larger it can go) at a point infinitesimally close to the singularity. This corresponds to a quicker rate of a light ray spiraling into the core.

      To get into the math a little bit, the definition of the Schwarzschild radius is R = 2GM/(c^2) or more simply, as everything there but the mass is a constant, R = xM. This means that as mass is radiated outwards, the radius decrease linearly with it. That said, the surface area of the black hole is what causes the hawking radiation, and that is defined by the surface area formula, 4pir^2. With the radius decreasing linearly, the surface area with decrease as 1/r^2. Meaning that as the black hole gets smaller and smaller, the radius will not continue to decrease to compensate as well. This means that at least proportionately, the black hole with hawking radiate away more mass the smaller it gets. The actual rate will continue to decrease assuming a uniform generation of virtual particles throughout all space, but the proportional release will increase, and thus they will seem to disappear more rapidly as they get smaller. In short, larger black holes release more energy in general because they have more surface area, but will get smaller at a slower rate compared to a smaller black hole.

      To answer your final question, no the black hole will not transform into a neutron star, as it is fundamentally a different kind of thing. A neutron star still has a finite density, while a black hole contains all of its mass in an infinitely dense point directly at its center. As the black hole shrinks, all it loses is influence over the surrounding space. The singularity remains infinitely dense. At no point can the horizon become so small that it is inside the infinitely small point that contains all the mass.
