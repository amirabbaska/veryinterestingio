      So there are a couple of points I'd like to touch on that make this question interesting to think about. Your deglaciation timescale will indeed be in the thousands of years. It takes a long time to melt an ice cap. However, magma is generated throughout this period of time, as decompression is occurring with each little bit of ice removed from the surface. This magma will begin to move towards the surface as soon as it can because it is less dense than the surrounding mantle rock. Once this magma reaches the boundary between the crust and the mantle (a boundary called the Mojo [mo-ho]) it does one of 2 things. It either pools to form a magma chamber that will then feed volcanos or it will simply keep moving to the surface and erupt. The timescale of this magmatism is somewhat shorter. As far as I know it's more on the order of hundreds of years. Volcanoes can erupt while still capped be a glacier! There's a special type of igneous rock that is generated by this lava-glacier interaction. They're called hyaloclastite.
