    • ScienceGuyJim

      Reefs are all pretty much the same stuff world wide (obviously I know there are different species but they all work the same basic way). A big part of it requires something for the coral to attach to to start with. That is why artificial reefs are so successful. Artificial reefs work anywhere that the water temp/ph/nutrients/turbidity are within the range that coral like.
      Ceder Key is far to the east of the Mississippi River Delta and does not appear to have any other major rivers dropping a large sediment load nearby. So the water probably meets the requirements. As long as the coral had something to attach to they would do fine. Looking it up it seems artificial reefs are common in the area. I imagine it has something to do with what the bottom is made of in the area but I do not have that info readily available to confirm my hypothesis.

      As for different types, yes there are a number of types usually having to do with the shape and general location of the reef relative or absent of land forms.
