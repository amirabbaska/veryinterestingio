    • Gramious

      There is actually a difference in the densities and qualities of skin throughout the body. The approach I take to study this stuff is to use something called Optical Coherence Tomography which looks into the skin by firing a laser and measuring the reflectivity under the skin. If for instance, you look at the skin layer structure on the arm (somewhere without a print) you'll find that the density characteristics of the separate layers are less distinct, whereas on the fingerprint they're very different.

      Like I said, this is just a leading theory - I myself am not fully convinced how fingerprints are actually formed.

    • Gramious

      I've found it varies. It should be +- 0.3mm into the skin, but I've seen anything from half to triple that in the data I've played with.

      That said, I actually developed Eczema on my thumbs (ironically GREAT data for me as a researcher!) and was able to analyse before, during, and after (well, after it healed up a bit). There was almost no Epidermis layer - the papillary junction was right by the surface.

      I've actually recently presented a paper with these results, if you'd ever like to see it :)

      Edit: BASICALLY, if you bleed then there is a good chance the changes are more permanent. This is because the blood vessels are under the papillary junction.
