  • shleppenwolf

    I'll throw in one point for clarity: the mention of radiators can be confusing, because in ordinary speech, that name is attached to the heat exchanger in the front of a car -- which is a serious misnomer. Only a small fraction of its heat rejection comes from genuine radiation -- it really should be called a "convector".

    By contrast, a spacecraft radiator, sitting out in vacuum, operates entirely by radiation.

    What safety measures are in place at the IFHX to prevent accidental contamination of either of the systems?I know that there is wall between them, but it must be highly conductive in terms of heat exchange so... what is it made of how thick is it?Are there any active systems like ammonia sensing circuits in the ITCS?

    • Rkeus

      The walls are VERY thin (thicker than foil, but thin) and it is the one place we are seriously concerned about an ammonia leak. We regularly do ITCS sampling for ammonia concentration and there are multiple ammonia sensors on the interior.

      That said, any serious ammonia leak through the IFHX is basically the only emergency that would just end the space station - aside from hitting a giant asteroid or something of course.
