      • CyberJerryJurgensen

        It's rare. An example from where I work is that we create a record for people that enter our sales funnel but don't follow through to purchase. The idea is that if they decide to come back then we can pick up where they left off in the sales process vs having them start over from the start. Even then we purge this data after a few weeks as there's no real value in keeping it any longer. Presumably the customer has gone elsewhere or isn't coming back.

        If the customer were to complete a purchase then we do create a persistent record specific to them to track their account info but there is still a lot of data we collect during our interactions with them that isn't stored long term or is stored long term but isn't associated to their account. An example might be when we track their interaction with our page to create a heatmap op cursor loitering, clicks, etc for our UX people to use. The data collected is an aggregate of all of the users, not any one in particular.

        For the slicing and dicing part....without getting into data modeling and database design it's difficult to explain how we chop the data up so that it can be easily stored and accessed for different business functions in a way that's ELI5. But as others in this thread have noted, it's more common for the business to come to us to ask "how many people from OH, age 25-34 started a purchase between Jun and Jul but didn't complete?" vs "tell me everything Joe User did last month." Because of that we usually store the data in a way that readily supports answering the first question. We probably could pull data to answer the second question but since no one ever asks us that we don't put time and effort into storing data in a way that makes it easy.
