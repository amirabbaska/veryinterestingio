      • GuruLakshmir

        This MUST be because extensor digitorum is forcibly stretched and therefore unable to contract.

        I really don't know about that definitely being the answer. It sounds like it might be a decent hypothesis, but I don't know that it is 100% the answer.

        Intuitively, I don't know why stretching a muscle slightly beyond what it normally is would cause it to be unable to contract. It doesn't really make sense to me. Think about people that are able to "hyperextend" their joints (see pic). They have no problem contracting their muscles to return their joint to normal. If the joint ever gets "stuck" in that position, it isn't because the muscle is physically unable to contract, but because tendons/ligaments/whatever catch on the bone or some other structure.

        Personally, after some casual research, it seems the answer to this question may lie in a structure referred to as the juncturae tendinum.

        The following quote is taken from this article:

        https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC2408985/

        The tendons [on the back of the hand] are linked to each other by a highly variable collection of fibrous bands known as juncturae tendinum. The bands are probably important in controlling the spacing of the extensor tendons, channelling forces between them and co-ordinating the extension of different fingers [see von Schroeder & Botte (2001) for further details and references]. However, the existence of the juncturae makes it difficult for different fingers to be extended independently.
