      • Nyrin

        Absolutely. But a BMI of 23 is not categorically overweight by any stretch and it'd be a really counterproductive public health message to try to go in that direction. First focus absolutely needs to be on the 35+ and 30-35 groups, and telling a sedentary 250lb person at 5'10" that they'd "still be fat" at 160lbs will only hurt the big picture.

        Also, BMI itself is a terrible individual diagnostic measure. It's useful across a large population with known averages on the other variables, but a BMI of, say, 23, can easily range from an outrageously lean and healthy athlete to a very inactive person with alarmingly low lean body mass and alarmingly high lipids.

        Totally agree with the basic concept of a healthy body composition being important, though, and it's probably the biggest problem the developed world is facing now in health. It's just critical that we approach the issue in a careful way in an environment where anything over a couple hundred characters may as well be a PhD dissertation as far as the public is concerned.
