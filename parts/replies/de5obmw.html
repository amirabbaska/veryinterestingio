  • FarghamPoe

    Your first paragraph isn't correct.

    Banks need to keep a certain percent of "cash" available on hand that they cannot use to put into risky things like investing, but they can still give out loans based on their "total cash".

    Say they are required to keep a 20% reserve of all deposits. You put 20k into your bank account, and now the bank can loan 16k to someone else based on the 20k you have in your account.

    If all depositors at the bank put in a grand total of 100k, the bank can only give out 80k in loans. If there is 100 million in the bank, they can give out 80 million in loans.

    And loans have a massive impact on the economy because those loans are almost immediately all spent on a variety of things, directly boosting the economy.

    So to say that putting money in a savings account doesn't impact the economy is completely wrong. Your 20k probably isn't a lot, but with millions of people putting savings into banks, thats millions of people able to get loans to buy cars, houses, and start businesses.

    Edit: to note, in the US, the required reserve amount isn't a lot. Only 10% for major firms. Additionally, there is a distinction between the checking account (quick access to money) and a savings account. The savings account actually requires no reserve if it is owned by an individual.
