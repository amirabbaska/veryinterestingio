      Aging is definitely a part of it. Some of the 'harsh' flavors are removed via this process, (and un-harsh flavors added) and age doesn't directly correlate with price. For example, Elijah Craig until last year or so had a $30, 12-year bottle, where a lot of bourbons at $30 are only aged for 3-4 years. So within a given price range, you would see a significant variation in bourbon smoothness, some of which would be due to aging.

      As u/knockoutcpfreshsmart said, a lot is down to the distillation.

      There is also generally a filtration process that removes other organic compounds that lead to an off-putting or harsh taste/feel. You can test this yourself with the college student's favorite project, filtering bad vokda through a brita filter to make semi-decent vodka. The activated charcoal pulls out some organic compounds (I don't know which exactly, but this is how activated charcoal works on all liquids), thus improving the flavor and "smoothness".

      You also have the huge factor of personal taste / experience. As someone who has made a point of drinking neat whiskey for years, it takes some pretty powerful stuff for me to think something is "harsh". Over time, you supposedly kill off the nerve endings that perceive "alcohol harshness" in your mouth / throat in the same way that spice fans kill off the nerve endings that detect capsaicin.

      So, to sum up, as far as I know, harshness is a subjective factor that's influenced by:

      1. Presence of undesirable alcohols and fusel oils
      2. Presence (or lack thereof) of desirable flavor compounds
      3. Overall ABV
      4. Individual variations among the drinkers themselves
      5. Presence (or lack thereof) of undesirable flavor compounds
