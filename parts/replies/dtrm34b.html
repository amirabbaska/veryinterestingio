      His Footnote 4 drives me nuts, though:

      "If we let the energy blast the electrons apart at near the speed of light, we'd see that energy actually take the form of mass, as the electrons gained mass relativistically. That is, until something stringy happened."

      For one thing, he's using the outdated concept of "relativistic mass" here, though that in itself is no crime.

      The real problem is the implication that there's something special about a situation in which energy can "take the form of [relativistic] mass." In fact, relativistic mass is just total energy measured in different units, so total energy E is always equivalent to a corresponding relativistic mass of E/c^(2), in much the same way that xº Celsius is always equivalent to (1.8x + 32)° Fahrenheit. They're just different ways of expressing the same quantity.

      The wording he uses ("take the form of") obscures that simple truth, and misleadingly calls to mind the conversion of kinetic energy to rest energy (mc^(2)), when it's really the opposite: rest energy (mass) of the "electron Moon" (electric potential energy, to be precise) is converted to kinetic energy of the electrons.

This means that there's no monopole field, and only leaves the dipole moments as the most far reaching electrical fields, which fall of much quicker than gravity.
