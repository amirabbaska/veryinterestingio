    The cool thing about being a PhD scientist at a place like the State Dept. is that you are often the “token scientist” in the room (i.e. you are supposed to know all the science). I’m not here because I know a lot about cancer leukemia genetics, but because I have skills that all PhD scientists have: we’re all trained to think critically about data and results, value evidence-based decision making, know how to gather accurate information quickly, and can process large amounts of complex information. I don’t have any foreign policy training, but we’ve got really smart people in our office who do, and I rely on them to understand the international relations aspect of our work just like they rely on me to understand the biological aspects.

    Contrary to what most people think, State works on a huge range of issues that have some or a lot of bearing on science and technology: global health, WMDs, climate change, trade policy, oceans and fisheries, arctic policy, species conservation, space and nanotechnology, cloud computing and cyber security, etc. As such, there is a great need to have in-house scientific and technical expertise to make sure we, as the U.S. government, are making smart decisions on these issues.

    One of my favorite stories of the Iran nuclear deal negotiations is how then Sec. of Energy Moniz would challenge his DOE nuclear scientists to make sure all their calculations were correct. As a world-renowned nuclear physicist, his input was invaluable in making the highly complex scientific aspects of that deal so strong. And next to him was a career diplomat and foreign policy expert, Sec. of State Kerry, who could make the sensitive diplomatic aspects of the deal achievable. That melding of deep scientific and foreign policy expertise is “science diplomacy” at its finest, and it’s why State has worked hard over the past decade or so to recruit more scientists, engineers, and other technical experts into its ranks.
