    Genetics plays very little role, with a few exceptions where genetic screening is possible. Lifestyle factors are hugely contributory towards alzheimer's development. Diabetics get it at a much greater rate than the general population, telling us high blood sugar is a likely causative factor. In fact, alzheimer's is often referred to as type 3 diabetes. It's likely a consequence of brain insulin resistance; the result of widespread glycation due to high blood sugar levels.

    In fact, it's becoming increasingly clear, most diseases of aging, like heart disease, alzheimer's, cataracts, glaucoma, osteoporosis, and even skin aging has a great deal to do with AGEs, which primarily form when our average blood sugar is elevated above the level our bodies can routinely deal with, likely around 4.5mmol/l

      I actually disagree with you on the genetics front. A large percentage of those below the age of 90 that develop AD have the ApoE4 risk allele.
