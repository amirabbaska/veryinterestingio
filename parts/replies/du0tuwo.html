    The oxidation explanation is incomplete, as it just pushes the question further: why don't the oxide layers fuse together then? Or for instance, I have a block of a pure crystalized oxide, I can still break it apart and it won't recombine if I touch the parts again.

    So the main reasons for it to not recombine is the topography of the break, the roughness of the two pieces against each other, and the specific atoms that get exposed. This roughness captures non-binding molecules like gases from the atmosphere between them. The oxidation layer isn't uniform, so there's plenty of roughness.

    Even in a vacuum, this roughness would only make a few points fuse together (and only if the right atoms get close enough and energy is provided for them to bind), which wouldn't stick strongly enough for you to notice.

    Ionic compounds like table salt, for instance, don't fuse even though they are highly crystalline, are not oxides and have the right atoms exposed to bind to one another. So contact roughness has to be the main reason.

    If you have a cleanly cut surface down to the molecular level, you could do all of this.. Metallic bonds are just very easy to form compared to the others.
