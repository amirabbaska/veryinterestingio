    • Coequalizer

      No, curvature is an intrinsic property of a space (technically what we call a smooth manifold with affine connection) and curvature does not in any way require embedding into a higher dimensional space. Curvature measures the extent to which transporting data around a tiny loop in the space fails to bring it back to its original state. See the illustrations in the wiki article on holonomy for a nice example of what I mean in the case of a sphere.

      Geodesics are just the straight lines in the space, and represent the trajectories of particles without any forces acting on them (free-falling particles in physics lingo).

      For example, on a 2-sphere with its usual metric (distance measurement) the geodesics are the great circles and these live on the sphere, not in some higher dimensional space.
