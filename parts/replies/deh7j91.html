    Yes but these are all the current factors keeping it poor (though it should include diseases such as malaria and AIDS/HIV).

    For the historic factors (why it has lagged behind Europe and other continents), you'll want to start by reading "Guns, germs and steel" by Jared Diamond. The whole premise of the book is to explore why some countries ended up richer than others.

    Diamond outlines a couple of factors making Sub-Saharan Africa quite unique in geographic terms.

    1. Agriculture (needed to build larger civilisations) was established way later than in other places. Because:

    A. Africa's North-South axis made it harder to spread plants across the continent (South America suffered from the same problem, while Europe and Asia did not to the same degree).

    B. There were fewer good indigenous animals and plants available to use for farming.

    1. The lack of good rivers for transport encouraging trade and exchange of ideas

    One might also add:

    A lot of the soil is quite low in nutrients since it's source material is old, naturally this differs a lot, but the classic red soil is an example of this

    European techniques of farming proved much better suited to e.g. Latin America and Australia than Africa (with the exception of South Africa and Zimbabwe)

    Also: the Europeans came along and colonised the continent, including selling slaves that diminished population densities needed for more intense agriculture.

    All in all if you want to have a big civilisation with plenty of resources to invent, build armies, explore and invade with - you have to start with a good food source (plants or animals).

    Africa was given a shitty hand for this to happen, although it did to some degree before the Europeans came along and messed stuff up. By the time of decolonisation Asia and Africa were equally poor, but Asia could use its farming to build a stable foundation (look up the Green Revolution) while Africa couldn't.
