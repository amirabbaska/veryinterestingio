  • Ponches

    This is true, but a key difference between the sun and a fusion reactor is that the sun fuses ordinary hydrogen via the proton-proton chain, which has an extremely slow reaction rate. That's why the sun sips its fuel and lasts for billions of years. A reactor would use deuterium + tritium which reacts millions of times faster.

    Most of the time two hydrogen nuclei (protons) fuse in the sun, they just pop back apart. Once in awhile, the diproton spits out a positron, one of the protons becomes a neutron, making it deuterium, which almost immediately fuses with another proton into helium. So in reactors we cut out the middle man and just start with the deuterium.

    • CanadaPlus101

      The fusion we're trying to achieve on earth is different from that in the sun. We want to fuse ready-made heavy isotopes of hydrogen such as deuterium and tritium, whereas in the sun protons actually have to get converted into neutrons to make those heavier isotopes, and then the heavier isotopes have to find each other in a sea of light hydrogen. In addition, ITER, for example, is expected to reach temperatures something like 10x that of the solar core.
