    • Shadowkatana

      Technically, in the purest legal sense there is no verbatim script that has to be read. The charter designates that one must be made aware of the reason for their arrest, and must be made aware of their right to consult with counsel and be given the opportunity to do so immediately (read: as soon as practicable) should they desire to exercise said right. Each police service, depending on its province and/or municipality will have its own policy mandated script that an officer is expected to read verbatim. Though discretion can be exercised in a situation where a person is having difficulty understanding due to language barrier or level of education, etc. and you can put it in simpler terms for them. This is something that an officer would be expected to articulate in their notebook.

  • burgerthrow1

    To expand a bit on the duty counsel system (in Ontario, at least), it's a central number that will connect you with one of the x number of lawyers on call at that time. Advice is pretty much limited to "don't say anything" though, especially given the number of lawyers gun-shy about being subpoenaed (and that a lot are just there for their $30/hr).

    Important to note as well that, unlike in the US, Canadian police aren't obliged to stop questioning after a detainee contacts a lawyer.
