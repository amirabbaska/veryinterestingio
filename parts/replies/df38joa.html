  • Homeless_Gandhi

    My favorite example of this is the giraffe (basically long horses). They have a recurrent laryngeal nerve. Basically, this nerve was looped around the aorta when the neck was short. Once the giraffe started evolving the longer neck to reach higher food sources, the nerve didn't reroute of course. Consequently, this nerve, which really should only have to travel a few inches to its destination within the body of the giraffe, has to travel all the way down to the giraffe's heart, loop around, and travel all the way back up the neck to the brain. It would be a very poor design in a closed system.

    • Forkrul

      Yup, that nerve follows the same path in pretty much all mammals, independent of neck size. It's a pretty good argument in favour of evolution rather than intelligent design (as if we needed more).

    • fifrein

      Humans have a recurrent laryngeal nerve on both sides as well. Embryologically, both the right and left recurrents loop under the distal sixth aortic arch. On the left, it develops into the ductus arteriosus (connects aorta and pulmonary trunk in utero) and hence the left recurrent loops under the aorta. On the right, the distal sixth aortic arch atrophies away. On both sides the fifth aortic arch atrophied away. Hence, on the right, the recurrent laryngeal nerve loops under the fourth aortic arch, which develops into the subclavian artery
