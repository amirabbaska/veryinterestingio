    I think the question though is asking specifically why this happens with white shirts.... as opposed to darker colors.

    I'm not an expert on pigment but not all white shirts are going to be see-through. It really has to do with how sheer the shirt is, but even then, I think (and someone correct me if this is wrong) that bleached cotton is going to be more translucent than dyed cotton, and undershirts tend to be more sheer than t shirts, unless you're sourcing t shirts from a manufacturer who uses very sheer ringspun cotton.... I have some shirts that even in dark colors are see through when dry if there's enough light exposure.

    You can see that happen with celebrities, for example, when paparazzi snap photos with a very bright flash... they're wearing extremely sheer cotton.       If it's like paint, the white doesn't come from the inherent color of the material, but from gaps in the material yielding a scattering and averaging of the ambient light, just like snow. Fill the gaps with water and the effect is dampened reducing the scattered light. Soak a white t-shirt with resin and let it dry and it will probably be just as translucent as with water.
