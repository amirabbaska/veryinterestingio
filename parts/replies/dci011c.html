    Came looking for this, so relieved I found it.

    There's a huge difference between infant-directed speech (higher tones, simplified word forms, slower pace with greater space between words, etc.) and "baby talk".

    My twins were preemie so we had a speech and language pathologist (SLP) help with early speech development work. The model we were given was to use infant-directed speech, but not to model incoherent speech.

    For example, we'd say "Would you like the cup?", and she might respond with "bup!". Many parents' natural tendency would be to say "yes, 'bup'! Would you like the bup?", but the SLP explained that there's solid research that responding with "yes, a cup!" (accentuating the 'c' sound) was more effective.

    Anecdotally, I'd say it seems to have worked well, since despite some developmental issues at play, they speak significantly more clearly than their school peers.

      To add on: There is also literature suggesting that children's overall communication skills (beyond, but including, articulation) benefit the most from echoing the utterance and then replacing it with the emphasized standard speech. The CCC-SLP that works with my center suggests this approach. The comparison between sounds is beneficial for contrast similarly to benefit of those pipe phones children use to hear themselves talk--echoing helps clarify the sounds they make. Additionally, echoing helps children develop serve and return interactions (helpful in developing executive function skills and future empathy skills) and can help gain their attention for the corrected speech (try echoing with an infant or toddler and you'll often get a reaction seeming to say, "Whoa! They just made the same sound I did! I wonder what sound they'll make next?").
