    • Asshole_Salad

      The way you've described 4WD, it sounds like it lies somewhere on a continuum between AWD and locked 4WD. Is that correct?

      Yep, 4WD is almost exactly right between the two, and there are different types of differential lockers as well. Lockers or Limited Slip Differentials both limit or prevent the differential action in one or both axle differentials.

      It sounds like you had selectable lockers like the ones that came on the Jeep Rubicon-edition Wranglers (TJs) or he had installed aftermarket air-activated like the ARB systems or others. In those cases with the lockers turned off you can spin one tire per axle at will while the other one (with the traction) just sits, exactly as you describe. With the lockers turned on they are essentially "spools", meaning both wheels are tied directly to the driveshaft and must spin at the exact same speed as each other and at the exact ratio that the driveshaft spins. (similar to many ATVs) It's great because you only need one out of four wheels to have traction and you won't get stuck, but it causes all kinds of handling issues and wear and tear if you go on dry pavement and do any kind of turning.

      A Limited-Slip Differential is a compromise design that will transfer some minimum amount of rotation to the non-spinning wheel from the traction-free wheel. Sometimes this is a clutch-pack that allows some movement but also provides some friction, other times it's gear-driven. The point is that if one wheel is spinning freely, the other will rotate at some ratio, say 10%, hopefully enough to get the vehicle moving. The higher the ratio, the more it behaves like a full locker.

      I used a "lunchbox" locker in mine, it was a sort of ratcheting device that replaced the spider gears and allowed either wheel to turn faster than the driveshaft, but neither wheel to turn slower. It was a compromise design, not quite as good on pavement as no locker and not quite as good offroad as a full locker, but only cost $250 and installed in about an hour or less with practice.

      If your dad had put the Jeep in 4wd on dry pavement with the lockers off it would have behaved as I described in my original post - you'd feel some serious drivetrain binding while making sharp turns and your U-joints and driveshafts (or worse) would be in danger. If he'd done it with the lockers turned on, you'd have had a real adventure!

    • Philip_De_Bowl

      In a 4wd, either full time or selectable, you have the option to lock the center differential (with full time 4wd) or activate 4wd (that has no center differential) and the front and rear wheels get equal power sent to their differentials. You'll also get the option of a "low range" that AWD doesn't offer. Low range gives you about a 2:1 gear reduction, giving you a shit ton of torque with excellent engine braking (cause your manually shifting your automatic too at this point) and precision throttle control.

      At that point, you'll often have some kind of limited slip diff in the rear and an open diff up front, with aftermarket options to lock things up automatically or manually, giving you true 4wd instead of 1, 2, or, 3 wheel drive you get with open differentials or a combo of open/locking differentials.
