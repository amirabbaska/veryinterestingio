    • ARandomGuyOnTheWeb

      So, I get what you're saying, but the statement 'everyone should pay something' does not stop the existence of a negative tax rate.

      For example. Is a homeless person paying at a soup kitchen in your ideal world? Let's make them pay something, a penny. This is still negative tax rate -- the homeless person gets more than a penny out of the system, and that system took money from somewhere else. Wealth distribution.

      As long as I can get more money out of government than I put in, I would argue that I have a negative tax rate. I don't think that's a bad thing. I think it is natural (most insurance policies works this way too).

      Otherwise, every government service would have to be priced at some sort of zero profit margin for all citizens/consumers. And that's when homeless people can't afford soup kitchens anymore. And the average person goes bankrupt when they need chemo. And corn farmers go out of business from market shocks.

      And sure, you can say "well, health insurance and farming subsidies are there for temporary setbacks." So then you're for voucher systems, and longer timelines, but the issue is still the same. Eventually, the homeless person is less productive than the cost of the government services they depend on to live. Do you let them starve now, or N years from now when their vouchers run out? Otherwise, we are redistributing something in order to keep them alive, and the amount we redistribute is just as important as the tax rate. Talking about one without the other is a meaningless argument.
