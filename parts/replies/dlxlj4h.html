    Coral "bleaching" is the algae having left the "coral". That is, the coral itself is literally the skeleton, so being "bleached" is literally to be dead.

    Just a fact correction, coral bleaching does not mean that the coral is dead, it means that the polyps of the coral have expelled their symbiotic internal algae. While this is correlated with the eventual death of the coral polyps, coral can recover from bleaching.

    • redroguetech

      Doesn't increased temperature cause the increase in acidification and nitrogen?

      Carbon dioxide acidifies water. Warmer water has lower saturation points, so the exact opposite.

      http://pubs.acs.org/doi/abs/10.1021/ja01861a033?journalCode=jacsat

      However, the difference in the amount of CO^2 the oceans will hold due to increased temperatures is very minor, since a few degrees of temperature has fairly minor change to saturation, but radical changes to the ecology. It's also important to note that it's surface waters that are crucial, but the same applies to both temperature and CO^2 content/acidification. CO^2 that reaches substantial depths can largely be considered "sequestered". Mind you, I'm not sure if a lower saturation point changes the acidity level, either. It may be that proportion of saturation is what counts...?

      From spot research, it doesn't seem nitrogen directly affects acidity. It leads to acidification by either chemical reactions resulting in acid rain, or by creating an unsustainable biological "bloom" that depletes the oxygen. However, read the sources for more information - my knowledge is, at best, surface deep, and at worst, flawed (I skimmed them).

      https://blogs.scientificamerican.com/observations/ocean-acidification-may-disrupt-the-marine-nitrogen-cycle/

      It is my understanding that atmospheric nitrogen isn't rising, rather it's being injected into the soil and run-off waters. The top article will also clarify that not all nitrogen is equal. Ordinary nitrogen (N^2 ) is, by itself, mostly harmless. Mankind is producing "reactive nitrogen".

    • RegularNormalHuman

      In general warmer water is not able to absorb more gas; the relationship between gas solubility and temperature is generally in the opposite direction, although it does reach a maximum and a minimum solubility at some temperature.

      This is a table showing the dependence of CO2 solubility on temperature. http://sites.chem.colostate.edu/diverdi/all_courses/CRC%20reference%20data/solubility%20of%20carbon%20dioxide%20in%20water.pdf

      And this article talks about the temperature dependence of gas solubility in general. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry%27s_law#Temperature_dependence
