    (the prevalence of dental caries in Native American populations really sticks out in my brain)

    Can you explain this a little bit?

    • bigpoppasmurff

      Sure. When Native American groups started making the switch from hunting/gathering to agriculture, the did so by domesticating maize. Maize is pretty gritty has a lot of sugar in it. This led to a decline in overall dental health and an increase in the amount of tooth decay and the prevalence of caries (aka cavities) in the skeletal record. Some of these caries were absolutely huge and would often led to exposure of the nerve and the near total destruction of the tooth. It had to be painful as all hell.

      The indication is that there was a sort of trade off: increased population at the cost of individual health.
