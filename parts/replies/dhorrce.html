    • pilleum

      Well, for one, predictions like that should not be blown off so lightly. It's a ridiculously nontrivial thing to do. It's so hard, in fact, that no other theory can even come close to doing it. Interestingly, before the mid '90s or so, there were competitors to string theory. They all seemed to be very different generalizations of relativity and of quantum field theory. They were all independently discovered by different people following different lines of thought. It turned out that they were all parts of string theory.

      So, being able to reproduce not only gravity, but ordinary quantum field theories, is an extremely difficult thing to do.

      Aside from that, the predictions are difficult to explain in a nontechnical way. Most of modern physics isn't really about what non-physicists think a "prediction" is. It's not about predicting a particular number that's measured in an experiment, it's all about very sophisticated structures that underpin our models of the universe.

      Asking "what predictions does string theory make about particle physics" is kind of like asking "what predictions does chemistry make about evolution." The answer is a lot, but the two topics are separated by many levels of very sophisticated abstraction that makes it hard for non-experts to understand the significance of the connections between them.

      That said, the killer example, and one most amicable to hand-wavy pop-science feel-good explanations, is AdS/CFT, which is an equivalence between a particular kind of quantum field theory (which are known to be correct) and a particular class of models in string theory.
