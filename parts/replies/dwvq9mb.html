  • Sharlinator

    I am not sure if luminosity has a logarithmic unit, though.

    In photography we talk of "stops", or EVs (exposure values) which are base-2 logarithmic. The three sides of the "exposure triangle"—shutter speed, aperture size, and ISO gain/sensitivity—are all defined in terms of EVs so changing any of them by one "step" results in halving or doubling the amount of light.

    In astronomy the luminosity of celestial objects is usually discussed in terms of apparent and absolute magnitudes, which for historical reasons are defined on a roughly base-2.5 log scale.

      • Sharlinator

        I quoted him saying literally this:

        I am not sure if luminosity has a logarithmic unit, though.

        And I replied that yes, there are logarithmic units of luminosity used in some fields exactly because the response of the eye is logarithmic and luminosity differences found in nature span several orders of magnitude (eg. the difference of light levels between starlit night and bright daylight is roughly 20 stops, or a factor of one million). In photography it's really intuitive to think in terms of stops. In astronomy, the magnitude scale dates back to a one-to-seven scale of star brightnesses defined in the antiquity. Much later the scale was found to roughly correspond to log luminosity and was redefined in terms of logarithms (thus the weird base of ~ 2.516).
