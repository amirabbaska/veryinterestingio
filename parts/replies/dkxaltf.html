  • Sammyscrap

    I would argue it's actually due to stress crystallization of the polymer. The stress forces the molecules into alignment which changes their refractive index relative to the amorphous phase. The mismatched indexes cause scattering. The reason I support this explanation rather than the air pocket one is that reversing the stress usually reverts the polymer to mostly transparent, except in a few spots where it's been permanently crystallized.

    A further test would be to anneal the sample at it's glass transition temperature and see if the cloudiness disappears. You might have to go hotter to reach the melting point of the crystalline phase, but it should relax back to its lowest energy state. In something like polystyrene where this phenomenon is commonly observed, annealing should cause a return to amorphous material which can even be seen on a DSC trace.
