    • zeidrich

      Planck distance isn't a fundamental limit like you're saying, it's not a "max resolution", it's just a relationship between the speed of light the gravitational constant, and the constant we use to calculate the energy of a photon from its wavelength, and it has little actual physical significance.

      Light waves don't only become discrete particles upon closer inspection either. Photons propagate in a wave but interacts in a more discrete area. It's the interaction and the persistence of the information from that interaction that makes it discrete, not "inspection".

      On a macro level, there are constant interactions by nearby particles, so the particles stay more particle-y.

      Light waves aren't "approximations to reduce computation". If I'm writing a program, it's orders of magnitude simpler to calculate the ray of a single particle than it is to calculate a probability map of all potential interactions in a waveform. It's far simpler for me to simulate a photon as a particle than it would be to simulate the actual behavior of a photon.

      The simulated universe hypothesis is still interesting, but I don't think these things point to evidence to support it. We don't have anything in our universe, or barely even imaginable that could simulate our universe, because anything that simulated our universe would need to be bigger than our universe to do so, as it would contain our universe, plus itself at a minimum. But for pretty much the same reason, a simulated universe could be possible because it would require things that could do things that would be impossible in our universe. But more importantly, it is possible to imagine that within our universe could simulate a less complex universe of our own, that could not create a simulation of itself, similar to the way that we could not create a simulation of our universe.

      Determinism is not the opposite of chaos. Chaos just means that the output of the system depends very sensitively on the input. For a quantum system this would mean changing a small subsystem to an orthogonal state and getting a very different final state for the whole system, which you can analyze without mentioning measurement.

    • RobusEtCeleritas

      Unitary time evolution in quantum mechanics is linear and deterministic, but that doesn't mean that the "motion" of a quantum-mechanical particle cannot be chaotic. For example in strongly interacting many-body systems like atomic nuclei, you'll find chaos. There is a whole field of study called quantum chaos, which plays a role in mesoscopic quantum systems.
