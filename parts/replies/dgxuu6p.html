      One cable normally has multiple fibers inside. Each fiber, which is roughly the size of a human hair, is capable of transmitting massive amounts of data.

      How many fibers are in a cable? It all depends - a company called Prysmian recently came out with a cable that is 23mm in diameter (0.9 inches) that has 1728 fibers inside. Each fiber is 200um in diameter (0.0078 inches).

      Cost and need is always figured in when running fiber - so a cable may have 8, 16, 64 or whatever number a company costed out and was comfortable spending to meet their need.

    This is the anti-explanation. Unlike analog telecommunications from 50 years ago, a single wire or optical cable would be grossly underused if it was only carrying one conversation or one user's data (which is what your explanation implies). The main answer to OPs question is packetization of data, and transfer of a lot of packets from different users very quickly through the same cable.
