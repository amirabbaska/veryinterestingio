    To piggy back off this comment: the power grid has to produce the amount of power that is required at that exact moment in time. So there's extra generation capacity that's not used all the, but instead, only when demand requires.

    There's no full scale method to store excess power, so the right amount of power has to be generated moment to moment.

    They'll all turn at the same speed as they are all "locked" to the frequency of the electrical grid (60 or 50Hz). An analogy for this is that they're all pulling on a weight on the same rope, or even pulling on the same team in a tug of war. One team member can't pull faster than the rest of the team.

      It's actually really interesting, but the grid inherintly has a little bit of self regulation in it. Since transmission is in AC, there's an actual frequency running through the wiring that all generators have to sync with before they increase the energy output. If too much energy is present in the grid, it can increase the resistance in the generators and have them produce slightly less energy. Too much resistance or demand can cause problems, though, because equipment starts shutting down to prevent damages
