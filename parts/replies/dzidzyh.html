    Unless a company is planning a major purchase or is required to keep a set account of cash on hand I think most businesses try and keep their money working for them. Cash in a bank account can't earn you anything near as much as it can elsewhere.

    Companies (and to a certain extent wealthy individuals) treat money as an asset to be used efficiently.

    For example:

    • according to my dad you should pay extra on your loans to pay then off earlier. My dad believes it's better to have money in your savings account.

    • according to Warren Buffett you should make the minimum payments and invest the rest into something that makes a better interest rate than the rate in your loan. Warren Buffett believes you need to use money so it can bring in more money.
    • HermitCrabTuesday

      Those pay off loan vs invest calculations get a little more complicated when you bring in compounding interest and loan amortization schedules. Meaning that if you pay extra now on top of your payment, you'll be paying down strictly the principle. Which then means less interest expense down the road. I had this excel class, and we did a 30 year loan repayment schedule in excel. With the scenario we had set up, we paid an extra $20 on top of the payment one time at the start of the mortgage, and at the end of the term would have saved hundreds of dollars.

      This is broadly true in a lot of cases personally, not just business its just people don't always see it and or/understand percentages.

      So For example, here in the UK, mortgage interest rates are filthy low at the moment but most of my friends over pay in their mortgages 'because it saves them interest'
