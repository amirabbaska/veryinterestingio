    Neuroscientist here. I don't think your answer really addresses the question very well, and I also think it has a few major factual errors in it. In particular, the idea that living a fast paced life (or anything else at all, really) "floods your brain with dopamine."

    Neurotransmitters are not hormones. They do not "flood your brain." They get released in many different discrete & independent pathways within the brain. There is no such thing as a "dopamine rush". Dopamine release is not pleasurable, nor does it generally signal rewarding / pleasurable events.

      • Optrode

        There is nothing inherently pleasurable about dopamine. It's used by circuits that help modulate motion initiation (see Parkinson's disease), but nobody goes around saying "dopamine is the motion neurotransmitter." Dopamine is also used in circuits that help control lactation (see tuberoinfundibular pathway), but nobody goes around calling dopamine the lactation neurotransmitter.

        Yes, there is a circuit in the brain that is involved in motivation, which includes some dopaminergic neurons. Those dopaminergic neurons do sometimes fire when an unexpected positive event occurs, but ALSO when an unexpected negative event occurs.

        Should we then say that dopamine is rewarding, even though many non-reward related circuits use dopamine, and even though the motivation-related circuits you're thinking of use a half dozen transmitters besides dopamine, AND even though that circuit doesn't appear to actually encode pleasure / reward itself?
