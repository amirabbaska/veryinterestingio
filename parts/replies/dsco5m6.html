    • RoboticElfJedi

      I'm glad you asked that question, as my own work is around gravitational lensing, which is exactly as you described! This bending of light by mass can have a few effects: It can make a source appear to be in a different position than it really is; it can distort the shape of the object; it can magnify and create multiple images. This can indeed complicate matters. However, where this phenomenon occurs it will be very local to a particular patch of the sky where the large, massive object is - the one that is causing the spacetime curvature.

      Your question was about the sun, which is indeed a source of contamination as I mentioned about (for instance, if there is a full moon, reflected sunlight can contaminate your measurements), but that's generally easy to account for. Real day-to-day astronomy often involves working with images and spectra of very faint and far away objects as these are the ones where new, cutting-edge research can be done. This means looking at lots of faint, noisy blobs. Sometimes, knowing whether a source is contaminated by a nearby object in the foreground, or if there is some gravitational lensing going on that you can't detect directly, is very difficult. Even telling the difference between a star and a faraway galaxy is not entirely straight forward in some cases.

      So perhaps the real answer to your question is it can be complicated and even professional astronomers have to do a lot of work to disentangle all the glowing things in the cosmos
