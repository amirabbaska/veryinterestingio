    • Anytimeisteatime

      The main difference that I understand is that running and other primarily cardiovascular forms of exercise are "isotonic"- the pressure in the muscle is kept constant by changing the length of the muscle, they're dynamic, i.e. you move! Weight-lifting is primarily about "isometric" work- the length of the muscle stays the same while the strain it is under increases, in other words they're mostly static. Edit: wrong about this, see comment below

      Dynamic exercise is associated with the changes I outlined above. Static exercise increases both systolic and diastolic pressure. As a result, there is evidence that this increases the chance of causing the heart to "bulk up" to try to pump harder against that raised blood pressure. This bulking, or hypertrophy, of the heart is unfortunately not as beneficial as hypertrophy of regular skeletal muscle and can lead to obstruction of the outlet it needs to pump out through or lead to electrical rhythm problems.

      Most static exercise also doesn't increase the heart rate as much as dynamic exercise, and that heart rate increase is responsible for some of the benefits.

      I'm not totally up to date on this, though, and I know there are many studies showing other benefits of static exercises like weight-lifting, including improved bone health, improved oxygen delivery to muscles, improved fat:lean mass ratio which has knock-on hormonal benefits, etc.

    • Anytimeisteatime

      I also wonder the same thing. Mainly because I'm cheap and my house is always cold, so I like to think I'm doing myself a favour, 'cause this totally counts as exercise, right? And that means I earned biscuits with my tea.

      Mostly the answer is no, sadly. Being in a cold environment in and of itself does increase your calorie expenditure, so you're OK eating a bit more (but not much), but it doesn't have the extensive hormonal and autonomic (sympathetic/parasympathetic) effects of cardiovascular exercise. The higher perceived effort at very cold temperatures is probably to do with very low humidity, so your airways are drying out, and I'm not sure what else. In general, cardiovascular responses under heat stress are what is studied, so I'm a bit clueless there I'm afraid. There is a distinct physiologic response to sudden exposure to a very cold environment, particularly cold water: generally heart rate goes down and (more variable) breathing rate goes up. That's the diving reflex and is a whole other kettle of fish that I won't get into right now as I'm about to get to bed.
