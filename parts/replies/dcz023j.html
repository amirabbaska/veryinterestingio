  • maladat

    This is a good post, but it ignores things like AT&T Uverse where your TV signal comes over the same phone line as your internet service, and is basically just coming from the internet. Uverse set top boxes just connect to your home network (even if they're connected to coax cable - the Uverse router has an IP-over-coax thing in it).

    In this case, there are a couple of things that happen.

    Say you're paying AT&T for 18 mbps internet. AT&T maintains a connection to your router that's, say, 90 mbps, and they can use the full bandwidth for Uverse TV signals, but they limit you to the 18 mbps you pay for when you're doing general internet stuff.

    Also, on the home network side, Uverse routers and the switches they install when the routers don't have enough ports have a system in place that gives Uverse TV signals higher priority through your home network than your general internet traffic (it's called IGMPv3). I wouldn't be surprised if they do something similar on the infrastructure level as well.

    • XsNR

      Connecting with as few hops as possible (ethernet cable straight to your router/model) removes as much potential for a problem, but likely won't change the bandwidth unless any of the boxes between the cables, or the cables themselves are of low quality (and they'd have to be really low quality these days)

      The reason certain channels are worse than others are, is either because each of the channels has its own distributed supply server network, or because one of the channels that has been deemed less worth the bandwidth for its viewership (similar to NetNeutrality, within a company scale), likely because it doesn't pay as much per viewer.
      It can be neither of those though, as you're talking a very complicated system to supply thousands of people with a channel individually tailored to them (even watching live they're sending several different versions to account for different qualities and delays), so it can just be bad luck or bad code, such that a refresh of the browser page, clearing of the cookies or a restart of the streaming device can fix it.

      A wired connection is always going to be better. If it's possible to wire something directly to your router/gateway, I would recommend it. That said, nothing beats the convenience of WiFi, and you can't get around having many devices on your WiFi network. In my own (admittedly anecdotal) experience as a professional and a user, making sure you've got as many things on Wireless AC as possible will help.
