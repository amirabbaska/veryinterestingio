    • Y-27632

      It's a very oversimplified answer.

      Some neuronal cells of the brain actually do undergo limited cell division after embryonic development, but that's not the reason why the brain changes in size.

      While you're born with pretty much all the cortical neurons you're ever going to have, all the synaptic connections between them haven't been established yet - the cells might not divide anymore, but they can still physically grow, axons can get thicker, longer, branch, etc. The fine details of the "wiring" connecting the various processing centers are mostly established after birth.

      You also have a LOT of other cells in the brain that support the neuronal circuitry in various ways - oligodendrocytes, astrocytes, microglia - and they are very much capable of division, even in adulthood. (not all types of them, and not all the time, but broadly speaking...) Myelination - the wrapping of "insulation" around axons by oligodenrocytes, which significantly increases the size of the various white matter tracts - is not 100% finished in humans until early adulthood.

        Yes, I kept my answer simple, this is a another whole topic :) Basically, if you lose a piece of your brain, it will (almost) never grow back, but the brain can make alternative pathways which may preserve the function of the lost tissue. The neurons themselves are capable of some degree of elongation, but cannot replicate themselves, they do not poses the tools for mitosis (cell replication). BUT much of what we know about the brain is being rethought every year and we are making many new discoveries. The cells that support the nerves (neuroglia) are capable of mitosis, but they don't contain and "information" and do not transmit signals, just like the pericardial fat (fat surrounding the heart) can increase in size (hypertrophy) and number of cells (hyperplasia)
