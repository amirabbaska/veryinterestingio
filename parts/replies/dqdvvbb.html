      • bazmonkey

        Not even then. At some point the thickness of the layers of paper at the fold would be too much thicker than the thickness of the paper itself. The sheet would either tear, or you’re no longer bending it in half, but rather mashing it together. The next fold after that is really just mashing stuff and not folding at all.

        This is a property of the thickness of the paper, not the area of it. It’s where you get the “you can’t fold a sheet of paper more than X times in half” stuff. How many times depends on the material, but regardless of material, even an atom-thick sheet would not be “folding” at the end of 103 folds.

        Or think of it like this: the final fold stretches the outer layer across the entire universe and the inner layer also has to be mushed in between a universe-thick number of layers at the same time, and they are the same material. No substance of any sort real or theoretical can stretch that much without tearing. If you could stretch—separate individual atoms—of a material that much, you’ve described something that can’t be broken: I can split it as far as theoretically possible and it will never be “broken”.
