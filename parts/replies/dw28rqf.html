    To add to this, water has a relatively high specific heat, so it takes a fair amount of heat to increase it's temperature by a degree. Since the rate of heat flow is determined by the difference in the temperatures of the two bodies in contact, water draws heat out of you relatively quickly. This is also why a conductor of a given temperature will feel colder than an insulator at the same temperature (assuming both are below your temperature).

    Two fun related experiments:

    -Fill one bowl with hot, one with room temp, and one with cold water. Put one hand into hot and one into cold. Wait a few secs, then put both into room temp.

    -Touch a metal object and a wooden object that are both at room temp.

    • Coomb

      You've conflated specific heat and thermal conductivity to a certain extent in this post. A very thermally conductive material with any substantial amount of thermal mass will feel cold or hot when you touch it depending on its temperature relative to your body. A very good thermal insulator will not feel cold or hot regardless of its specific heat. However, there are examples of configurations of thermally conductive material that don't feel like they're any temperature. Aluminum foil is a good example. You can touch it directly out of an oven because it's thermal conductivity is so high that it almost immediately cools down to air temperature, and even if it didn't, there so little mass that there's not enough heat to burn you.
