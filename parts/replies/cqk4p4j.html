    This is famously the case with Kakapos in New Zealand. Kakapos are lek-mating (males do not provide any parental care or resources to the female and so females choose males based on their courtship displays & such) and like other lek-mating species the males have highly variable reproductive success.

    They're also critically endangered. Early conservation efforts used supplementary unlimited feeding. Because of this, the females of the population were in good condition and the sex ratio of the new kakapos was heavily skewed towards males as a result because each mother was "trying"* to have the next generation's top males.

    *It's not a conscious choice, hence the quotes
