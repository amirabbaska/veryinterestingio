    • bad_at_hearthstone

      It's all turbines.

      Coal power? Nope, that's heating water using coal and driving a turbine with the steam. Natural gas? Same story. Wind power is just sticking a big turbine outside on a windy hill, and hydroelectric means sticking the turbines below the waterline of a dam. And, yeah, nuclear power is... functionally 90% identical to your basic coal plant, with a reactor in place of your coal fire.

      About the only different sort of power generation we have are photoelectric cells, which we use to turn light from solar radiation into electricity; and thermocouples, which turn heat directly into electricity using the Seebeck effect. Those aren't terribly efficient, which is why we only use them in situations where there isn't space for a steam turbine.

    • myself248

      Yup! Nuclear reactors, coal-fired boilers, gas-fired boilers, oil-fired boilers, and solar concentrators, all produce their energy in the form of heat. The most practical way to turn heat into electricity is to use the heat to boil water and spin a turbine that turns a generator, regardless of the heat source.

      Pretty much all nuclear power is used to make steam, with the exception of radioisotope thermal generators (which use the Peltier-Seebeck effect) and betavoltaic batteries (which are basically solar panels absorbing a different sort of 'light'), and those form an utterly miniscule fraction of nuclear energy use.
