    To put a bit more simply:

    Remember that for these purposes energy and mass are interchangeable (E=mc^2 and all that). And it is ultimately the presence of mass/energy that creates gravity. So, for instance, a compressed spring will actually have slightly higher mass than an uncompressed one. Whenever you have energy in a system, for the purposes of gravity, you can consider it an equivalent amount of mass.

    • Iamlord7

      A magnetar has a magnetic field of about 10^8 to 10^11 Tesla. These are objects with the strongest known magnetic fields, and they have a corresponding energy density of around 4 x 10^25 J/m^(3). m = E/c^(2), so the mass density corresponding to this field is 10000 times that of lead. As a rough estimate, we could multiply that density by the volume of a neutron star of radius 15 km to obtain a "mass"(mass+density+of+lead)(10%5E4)) of about 10^21 kg. A neutron star has mass of order 10^30 kg, so even though 10^21 is a big number, even a magnetar's magnetic field has an associated mass density that is negligible when compared to its actual mass.

      I don't know what you are asking.

      Gravity is modeled in general relativity as a phenomenon due to the curvature of spacetime. The gravitational field of a mass distribution is a term that is well-defined in Newtonian gravity, but we still the term in general relativity. In general, "gravitational field" in this context just means the gravity due to a mass distribution. Specifically, the term may refer to the (derivatives of the) metric tensor, which is a perfectly well-defined object.
