    Yeah, banking marijuana dispensaries is extremely complicated. It's still illegal under federal law so technically processing transactions using proceeds from the sale of marijuana is considered money laundering. It wasn't until 2014 that FinCEN issued guidance on how banks could provide financial services to dispensaries, which essentially boiled down to requiring 24/7 monitoring of the dispensary. The problem is that the guidance didn't make banking dispensaries legal, it just told banks that FinCEN wouldn't prioritize enforcing those laws against banks that comply with the guidance. Complying with the guidance is absurdly expensive and time consuming (not to mention a bit invasive), and failing to comply opens the bank up to both criminal and civil prosecutions so, naturally, almost every Financial Institution in the US refuses to bank dispensaries.

    It's a pretty ridiculous situation, but banks don't really have much of a choice in the matter. The issue is 100% because marijuana is a schedule I drug. If it were schedule II, it wouldn't be a federal crime to sell medical marijuana, so banking them wouldn't be considered money laundering. Of all the things that rescheduling marijuana would do, allowing banks to safely bank marijuana dispensaries is probably the most significant.
