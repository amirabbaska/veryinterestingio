    • djangofoxx

      Med student, so my understanding of this is still relatively incomplete, but hopefully this makes sense. The reason for the differentiation depends on when the duplication of chromosomal material happens and what the future risks and consequences are respectfully. In your situation there is nondisjunction of the chromatids during meiosis thus, the ovum (or sperm) has 46 instead of 23 chromosomes, when fertilization occurs the morula/blastula/embryo has 69 chromosomes from the get go. With moles, you have two variants both stemming from the same pathology. A partial mole occurs when after viable fertilization occurs 23+23=46, there is an aberrant duplication of either the male or female sets of chromosomes leading to a 23+ (23 x 2)= 69 (XXX, XXY, XYY)(Another scenario that causes this is when the ovum does not properly seal out additional sperm from entering and two sperm fertilize the same ovum). In a partial mole fetal parts can be seen because you have all the working machinery its just disordered. A complete mole most frequently occurs when a sperm enters and empty egg and duplicates, or two sperm enter an empty egg. This results in a 46 XX chromosomal genotype and in this case no fetal parts are observed (since mom's machinery isn't there) and the moles are classically described as a cluster of grapes. The important aspects here are that Triploidy (your case) carries a low recurrence rate and shows no increased risk of cancer. Moles on the other hand, specifically complete moles carry an increased risk for the development of Choriocarcinoma. Thus, often times with moles, women's HCG levels are followed as there are dramatically increased when the mole is present and eventually renormalize after removal. A consistently increased HCG level could indicate cancer, or the persistence of some part of the mole.

      This was super long winded and probably unclear at parts, but I hope that helps.
