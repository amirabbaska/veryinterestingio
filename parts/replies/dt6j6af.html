      Velocity alone is not enough to define an orbit. Also energy alone cannot fully define it, but it determines the semi-major axis of your ellipse (or radius if it is a circle). https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_orbital_energy

      You appear to be confused about the prograde component. The burn itself does not provide prograde velocity, but you already had some prograde velocity before burning (otherwise you wouldn't have achieved orbit) and the outwards burn is adding a perpendicular component to it.

      See this diagram (don't take it too literally because it is hand drawn without caring much about the mathematical details). Before burning you were in a circular orbit (the smaller circle a bit to the right) with velocity v1. The delta-v was perpendicular to it, and after burning your velocity becomes v2. You are faster because the magnitude of v1 + deltav is greater than the magnitude of v1. So I drew an ellipse that is a bit bigger than the circle.

      In any case, your new orbit must be tangential to your new velocity at that point.
