    There would be a unique answer for every discrete 'yardstick' length you used, I'd imagine some of them are more practical than others, they should trend the same, provided they are sufficiently small relative to the minimum lake feature size.

    For practical purposes a length of around 10m is probably pretty decent if you care about only lakes larger than a small pond (100m²). This Paper shows different yardstick lengths, while all lakes show increased shoreline length with decreasing yardstick length, smaller lakes are dis-proportionately effected until the yardstick length is about the square root of the area in m² (fig5).

    tl;dr: the shoreline as a function of elevation should behave similarly for sufficiently small yardstick lengths, and give you an elevation of maximum area perimeter (though that area perimeter will grow arbitrarily large as you decrease the yardstick length).

    I'd say another factor that obfuscates results, are we talking about just water that can access elevations at the sea level from the ocean, or all area below some elevation?

    It's a significantly different problem, since one includes inland lakes and the other doesn't
