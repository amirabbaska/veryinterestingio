      • wfaulk

        What I mean is that an incandescent bulb that is "on" may have the electricity cycling that fast, but its temperature remains constant. (Thousands of degrees for the filament; hundreds of degrees for the bulb.)

        If you switch a bulb on and off over the course of seconds, you're having the components have thermal variations of hundreds (the bulb) to thousands (the filament) of degrees. Those temperature variations are likely to have effects on the components of the bulb. For example, thermal expansion ratios of glass and metals differ, which means that the joint between the glass bulb and the sleeve will move. If that movement happens a lot, if you're switching it on and off, as opposed to once, if you turn it on and leave it on, it stands to reason that that's more wear-and-tear on the bulb.
