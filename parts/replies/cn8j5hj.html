    • Lycopodium

      As others have pointed out, too much water sitting around the roots is bad. Roots "breathe" and need oxygen. If there's too much water, then the roots can drown. Once dead or weakend, they can rot.

      However, some plants can grow in water vases indoors. Likley there is enough dissolved oxygen in the water to support the roots--whereas the roots would have to compete with more microorganisms in soil for that oxygen.

      As for the rest of the plant, water uptake is a passive process. Water enters a plant until there are no more cellulose fibers it can stick to (think of a paper towel soaking up water) and the cells fill up with water and push against their cell walls. At this point, a plant is at its maximum capacity. Water may even drip out of leaf margins (water drops on leaf edges in the morning).

      Water can then be lost as it evaporates from the leaves. A little bit is used by the plant for biochemistry, but most evaporates. This evaportion opens up room for more water to come up. Also, since water likes to stick to itself, water molecules that move up help pull other water molecules too. Minerals that were absorbed can join the ride up to the leaves too.
