    I have a question - how are the study conclusions written? They sometimes seem very reflective of the study contents, and sometimes not at all.

    For instance, this conclusion:

    "Maternal pertussis vaccination is effective in preventing pertussis infection in infants aged <8 weeks and may be considered in other countries experiencing high levels of pertussis notifications."

    This was from a study performed on a sample size of ~100. Considering the tiny sample and vastly different demographics between Wales and other geographies, the conclusions seems quite broad. I do deployments of data science projects to business, and we would reprimand a data scientist for making conclusions like this as they are not supported by the work.

    • shorbs

      There really isn't a standard for what goes into the abstract. Most people aren't going to read the whole study that you publish, just the abstract. Naturally the authors are going to their best to grab the attention of the reader for more more publication/citations etc. Just like reading a book summary, a lot of context and buildup (the intro/methods) is naturally going to be left out because you only have 2-3 sentences to work with. I do agree with you that these conclusions don't seem generalizable based on the methods. But really to get the "big picture" idea, you have to read the paper.

      For this particular study, the authors said in the discussion: "The findings of this study should be generalizable to other high-income countries, where there have been increases in notified pertussis cases despite established vaccination programs." I'm reading this as the results are only contextual in similar countries to those in the UK. They make the generalization that the vaccine should be useful in those with higher prevalence of pertussis, but really should have elaborated more.
