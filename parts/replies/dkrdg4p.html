    It's less about how you wake up and more about when you wake up. You cycle through different sleep stages all night. If your alarm goes off while you're in NREM 1 or NREM 2, you'll likely feel okay. If your alarm goes off while you're in NREM 3 or REM sleep, you'll feel groggy and a bit out of it and probably want hit snooze a few times.

    If you're used to going to bed and waking up and roughly the same time every morning (even weekends/days off) and have generally health sleep, you should naturally start waking up around the time your alarm goes off, provided you're getting enough sleep most nights and aren't using lots of sleep medications.

    In terms of actual alarms, there isn't much in the way of evidence for or against a particular general approach. I know there was a popular study a while back that said alarm clocks were bad for your heart because they can obviously startle you, but for most people that isn't actually a health risk. There is also research that suggests snooze is the devil incarnate and a really unhelpful habit, which makes more sense--you are essentially starting a new sleep cycle and then waking yourself up in the middle of a cycle again.

    The real issue with alarm clocks is that they may mean the sleep-wake cycle you have to keep is out of sync with the sleep-wake cycle your body wants to keep. This is called "social jet lag." On the other hand, alarm clocks can be useful for people with circadian or sleep disorders or sleep dysfunction due to things like depression. So there's no one answer for all people.
