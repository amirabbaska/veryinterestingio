    I would argue that one of the things that free trade agreements seemingly ignore is worker conditions. In the US, there are a lot of laws that guarantee workers' rights, from OSHA regulations to labor relations regulations. Many workers in less affluent countries do not have the same guarantees. So in addition to those workers being cheaper because they're willing to work for less, the company also has fewer costs because they're not required to provide health insurance or lunch hours or safe work environments.

    I would personally argue that this is the biggest problem with free trade agreements; when purchasing labor, you're not purchasing the same thing, and, in my mind, that makes the trade agreement asymmetric.

    That said, I'm not an economist and I'm willing to have my view changed.

    Edit: The TPP's Chapter 19 is specifically about labor rights. To me, it feels very weak. It requires that the signatory countries not have child labor or slave labor, and that labor unions and collective bargaining be respected. None of the parts of this feel worded strongly enough to me. For example, it doesn't define what a child is, or what constitutes compulsory labor.

    • no_real_username

      Yep, that's another point.

      Decades ago, trade deals usually dealt with tariffs and quotas, with simply allowing goods to be sold in other markets at all. today, those play a much smaller role, and so trade negotiators have instead begun focusing on creating uniform standards and regulation, forcing the trading countries to place the same requirements on goods.

      Sometimes that requires one country to raise their standards and impose stricter quality checks (for example, China has been forced to make better stuff in order to be allowed to export to America and Europe). Other times, it requires a country to drop their standards and allow goods that were not previously allowed or considered safe.
