    • isparavanje

      Every orbit is an ellipse with the center of mass of earth at one of the foci, and the ellipse must intersect where you started the orbit (because orbits are closed under newtonian physics). If the orbit starts on the ground, the ellipse must intersect that point in the ground, which means it enters the ground somewhere else and shoots back out from the original point where it started. That won't happen because the orbit obviously ends when it crashes into the ground.

      Note that the "original point" I described above won't really be the same point on the world map as the Earth rotates. It will be a point on the earth's surface though.

    • shleppenwolf

      In one sentence, because F=ma. The trajectory of any projectile outside the atmosphere, with nothing but gravity acting on it, is a solution of that differential equation -- and there are no solutions that fit your description, no matter what the initial conditions (launch velocity and direction) are.

      If the projectile isn't going fast enough to escape Earth entirely, its path will be repetitive, which means it will pass through the launch point after once around -- except that Earth is in the way, which is what I meant by "splat". In order to get a successful orbit, you have to apply some thrust after it reaches a certain height.

      If you'd like to pursue it in some detail, at an undergraduate level, read Fundamentals of Astrodynamics by Bate, Mueller & White, universally known as "the BMW text".
