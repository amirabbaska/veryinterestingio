      • ILL_BE_WATCHING_YOU

        I don't know if there is any relevant mathematical definition of "pattern"

        A good definition comes from information theory. A pattern can be thought of as a sequence of information. If a sequence has no information, it isn't a pattern. A random sequence with a uniform distribution of symbols is impossible to predict without resorting to guessing, and is as "patternless" as you can get.

        In your example, say that pi's digits are random, and every digit appears just as frequently as every other. In this case, pi can be said to have no pattern. If you know the digits of pi to a certain number of digits, you will have no information about the next digit, no matter what. In this case, an optimal algorithm would guess the next digit correctly 10% of the time, no matter what algorithm was used. In the same way, if you try to guess coinflips, you will be right 50% of the time no matter what algorithm you use; whether you alternate between guessing heads and tails, guess tails all the time, or only guess tails every fifth flip, it won't affect your average accuracy. There is no information in the sequence.

        However, if you remove all the 9s, then a pattern emerges; namely, the lack of nines. Now, I have a small bit of info, so if you asked me to guess the next digit, I would make sure not to guess nine. Even though the technical number of possible states are the same(it's still a base 10 number), the observed number of possible states are less, and that constitutes a pattern.

        Suffice it to say, anywhere where you observe an informational "foothold", that's a pattern. Even things never happening, or things always happening, are patterns, they're just patterns that are so predictable, you take them for granted and put them out of your mind. Whereas with rolling a dice, the ONLY thing you know for certain is that you won't roll a 7.

      Being able to predict the next digit means that the number is computable. Most irrational numbers - Pi, e, sqrt(2), whatever - are computable.

      However, "repeating patterns" in this context refers to the concept that if the number has a finite number of repeating patterns then it can be reduced to a rational number, but the example above can not.
