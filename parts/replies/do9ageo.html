      I'm pretty sure we're talking about shutter speed, which is related to (but not the same as) frame rate.

      In a film camera, the film strip has to be physically advanced between exposures of the frames. While the film is moving, a device called the shutter blocks light from falling on the film to prevent the whole frame becoming a gigantic motion blur.

      If a movie is shot at 24fps, each frame lasts for about 0.04 seconds. The shutter and film-advance mechanisms can't operate instantaneously, so photography only takes place for a fraction of that 0.04 seconds. The shutter can be adjusted to allow the exposure to be longer or shorter. Digital movie camera sensors have a similar principle wherein they alternate between gathering light and offloading data.

      Since the exposure time is not instantaneous, moving objects in the camera's view will become motion-blurred. The longer the shutter is open, the more apparent the blur becomes. While this makes motion appear smoother to our eyes when played back, it has the obvious downside of reducing visible detail.

      In fast-paced action scenes, the shutter speed is often set to a short interval which keeps each individual frame relatively crisp and helps to keep the on-screen action legible. This is turned "up to eleven" for stylistic effect in the church fight, in part to represent the effects of the mind-control chips.
