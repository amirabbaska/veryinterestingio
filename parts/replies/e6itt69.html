  • Svankensen

    If the genes are separate, then I could be white and have black palms and soles?

    • AdultEnuretic

      No.

      The genes for skin color operate on at least 3 loci. The effect send to be additive. The more genes you inherit for color, the darker you are. You can inherit 0-6 individual genes for color. If two parents are each medium skin toned, with 1 color gene at each locus, they could combine to produce any possible color combination in their children, with the 3 copy combination being most likely, but 0 and 6 copy combination being possible as well. That why you occasionally get things like twins, where one is apparently black, and one is apparently white. For example: https://us-east-1.tchyn.io/snopes-production/uploads/images/photos/people/graphics/twins2.jpg

      The gene for for palm color seems to operate within the domain of overall pigmentation for that individual. It controls what fraction of potential skin color is expressed on the palms. You can have up to as much pigment in the palms as the rest of the body, but not more. Patches of increased volar pigmentation are often a sign of melanoma.

    • AdultEnuretic

      It's not due to friction. It's a specific adaptation for the skin we use for grasping and traction. It's also the only totally hairless region, and the only places with finger prints. It's the party of your skin you interface with the world with.

      The terms thick and thin refer specifically to the number of layers present in that type of skin. This skin has 4 strata, basale, granulosum, spinosum, and corneum. Thick skin has basale, lucidum, granulosum, spinosum, and corneum. The thick skin does callus a lot more than the thin skin though.
