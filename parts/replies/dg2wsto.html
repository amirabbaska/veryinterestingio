    Very informative answer. What would be the cause of a "snowball earth"?

    • Gargatua13013

      The jury is still out for a final and definitive answer, but I suggest you look up the paper I linked as a "snowball Earth" reference to get an idea of the kinds of changes we're talking about. Essentially, Earth's atmosphere enormously changed in those times, passing from a methane-rich greenhouse to an oxygen-rich coldhouse. So we're looking for a mechanism which can account for that kind of wholesale change. Kopp et al posit that the main culprit was the spread of photosynthetic activity.

      Kopp, R. E., Kirschvink, J. L., Hilburn, I. A., & Nash, C. Z. (2005). The Paleoproterozoic snowball Earth: a climate disaster triggered by the evolution of oxygenic photosynthesis. Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences of the United States of America, 102(32), 11131-11136.

    • rocketsocks

      The main mechanism is uncontroversial, there's a positive feedback loop between low temperatures and ice/snow cover. This is because ice and snow have a high albedo and reflect a lot of the sun's warmth back into space, cooling the ground. When there's a lot of land area in the equatorial region it can make the snowball Earth scenario easier. There's a point at which permanent glaciation below a certain latitude level becomes a runaway effect that tips toward whole Earth glaciation.

      Exactly how (or if) the Earth gets that far in glaciation, and the exact details of global glaciation, is still up for debate however.

    • Gargatua13013

      From what we understand, it's through the volcanic emission of CO2. Since photosynthesis has now ground to a virtual halt, volcanic CO2 stops being removed from the atmosphere and slowly builds up over time untill it produces a greenhouse crisis and ends the glaciation abruptly.

      In the geological record, the most typical expression of Snowball Earth is equatorially deposited glacial deposits (tillites and stuff like that), abruptly topped by a layer of warm water carbonates.
