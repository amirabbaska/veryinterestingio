    Boeing actually deployed this technique on a bunch of their communications satellites and it came back to bite them in the rear. Basically they designed the solar wings such that they had semi-cylindrical lenses running over them that would concentrate the sunlight onto the junctions, thus increasing the efficiency of the array.

    Initially it worked quite well, but they ran into two problems. The first, as you mentioned, was the increased temperature of the semiconductor. It caused premature wear on the cells, causing them to degrade more quickly than anticipated. The second issue was that the lenses themselves were not totally UV stable, and began to fog up in orbit, further reducing the power output.

    The overall result was a significant reduction in the power budget for the satellites, thus forcing their operators to shut down parts of the payload to stay within the power budget.

    The three satellites I know of that suffered from this was Anik F1, and the initial pair of satellites for XM Radio. In the case of Anik F1, Telesat was forced to build and launch a replacement (Anik F1R). F1R took over duties for North America, and the original F1 was reduced to just serving South/Central America (With both birds in the same slot). For XM Radio, they changed the solar arrays on their ground-spare satellite (Named Rhythm), and launched it. The two original birds (Rock and Roll) were then moved into a single slot, with each taking half the load.

    One strategy I've seen is to use a big (and fairly inexpensive per area) lens or mirror to focus a lot of sunlight onto a tiny (and expensive per area) section of ultra-high-efficiency multi-junction solar cell. It's mandatory in any discussion of solar cells that someone pulls out this chart, and you can see that the absolute highest efficiencies are all coming from concentrator cells. However, this is still more in the realm of "crazy laboratory tricks" than anything commercialized.

    They also generally have pretty serious cooling systems, because they really, really need them. Probably part of the reason why they're not so popular outside of the lab yet.
