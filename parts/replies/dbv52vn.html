    • akruschwitz

      That all depends on whether or not there's air stuck within your ears at the moment you're listening! Most people when they go underwater will still have their ear canal filled with air, and if there were an underwater speaker then theres a change in medium from the water where the sound originates to the air in your ear where you hear it.

      If there were a waterproof microphone however, who was completely submerged, as long as there's no significant change in the waters temperature or composition, and neither of them are moving relative to each other, then the microphone should pick up the same pitch the speaker is producing

      Edit: I forgot about one last thing! The thing I said about the air in your ear canal is half true, because that's part of how sound resonates in your ear (That's why humans are so sensitive to 4kHz frequencies). However, you would also hear sounds coming from the outside of your skull, in which there isn't air between that and your hearing. It would be quite a bit more quiet, but depending on how much the frequency changes you could potentially pick up on it.

      Most likely it would just blend with the sound you're hearing from your ear canal and create a comb filter, which changes timbre and not pitch.

      Only if the change in frequency is greater than a certain frequency (which I can't find in my notes anywhere, it has something to do with the Haas effect but I can't remember for the life of me what it's called) will we perceive the two sounds as two distinguishable notes.
