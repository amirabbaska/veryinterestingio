    • WikiWantsYourPics

      It's not a simple matter of whether the water is usable afterwards. Farming needs a certain amount of "clean" water: not salty, not full of bacteria or chemical contaminants.

      The supply of clean water is limited by how fast the water cycle can turn it around: how much rains on the catchment area and flows in through surface water (rivers etc.) and underground water.

      Irrigation and other farming uses of water also have an environmental impact. The water carries salts and fertilisers into parts of the soil where they don't help, or where they make it difficult to grow plants, and carries fertiliser into water bodies. Water plants then grow there, and when they rot, this uses up all the oxygen in the water, killing fish and causing other problems.

    • somahaiken

      Good question. In some cases, water used in agriculture is drawn from from aquifers through deep wells. Much of the water will drain off in some way, but it will take a long time to make it back to the aquifer. Thus we sometimes see aquifer depletion because withdraws are happening faster than they are naturally replenished.

      When thinking about water usage, it's good to keep in mind a few things.

      • Where did the water come from, and how will it return there?
      • How much energy was used to prepare the water for its purpose? For example by pumping, heating, purification, etc.
      • Will the process introduce waste into the water that makes it more difficult or impossible to use in the future?

      For example, municipal water conservation efforts in wet areas are usually based around the cost to purify water and deliver it through pipes, whereas conservation efforts in drought areas are more concerned with the depletion of reservoirs and aquifers.
