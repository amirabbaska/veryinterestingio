    Extra detail for your correct response

    In conductive material, any acquired charge is spread out over the surface of the mass, with more charge density around sharp corners and some other geometry. More surface area means more room for charge to occupy, and more sharp corners, of which some are designed for this purpose on aircraft and spacecraft, will dissipate charge faster.

    The frame of a vehicle like this ( or even a regular car for that matter ) provides a good proxy for the ground since their physical properties can allow enough static charge to bleed off safely so as to not damage the on-board systems.

    • ARSnowe

      If you ever look at the trailing edges of aircraft wings, especially the ailerons (which roll the plane), you’ll see ~6 inch protrusions that look like antennae. They’re called static wicks and use this principle to dissipate static charge. In some planes they’re made of a hard material. In smaller ones they actually end in tiny hairs. Bigger airplanes may have many of these all over the plane.
