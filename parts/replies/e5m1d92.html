    As the above post states - the main considerations are in taking a scarce resource (lungs available for transplant that are a suitable match for a patient in need) and distributing them fairly. In general, patients should have lung disease without good medical options, high expected chance of death if they don't receive a transplant, as low as possible mortality if they do receive a transplant (which means lack of other diseases such as advanced disease heart or kidney disease or ongoing behaviors that will cause the transplant to fail such as smoking or drug use), and a social support network that can help them get through a very difficult transplant process (large surgery, difficult recovery, many appointments, immunosuppressive post-transplant medications with severe side effects).

    Thus, patient with COPD do receive transplants inasmuch as they fit the above criteria - but given that patients with COPD due to smoking often have other medical problems, older age, or ongoing smoking - they frequently aren't as good of candidates as some other lung diseases (such as Cystic Fibrosis, COPD due to A1AT, PAH, etc)
