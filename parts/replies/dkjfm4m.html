    • NilacTheGrim

      I want to add to this that there were some experiments done on the subject. In one experiment, subjects were put in a sealed room with no information from the outside world about time (not even a clock). They got to control the lighting and could go to sleep or wake up whenever they wished.

      Subjects varied in how long their waking cycles took -- with some having a 24 hour cycle and others ending up with a longer or shorter cycle (the longest was 30 hours). The subjects were fine with whatever cycle they ended up settling on and didn't feel tired or jetlagged in the cases where their cycle wasn't 24 hours.

      Here is the study: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC1330995/

      Based on this research I think humans could easily tolerate a slightly longer day/night cycle on Mars, if they were living on Mars.

      Really the thing that resets your rhythm is light exposure. The NASA scientists that had trouble maintaining a Martian day cycle were probably feeling jet lagged because they still were on Earth, experiencing the Sun from Earth, which threw a huge monkey-wrench into the works for them.
