      • Hypothesis_Null

        It really doesn't. You're confused because you're right, but a lot of imprecise language people write on the subject will make you think otherwise,

        If you bombard something with radiation, you may do damage to it, but (ignoring a few edge cases) you will not activate the target.

        Activation means turning the target itself radioactive. To do that, you need to bombard it with neutrons, which will then either be absorbed by the atom, or split the atom, making it or its fission products radioactive.

        Irradiating food is actually a great, and underutilized, sterilization method. If you irradiate something like an apple, it will last for months, or even years, so long as you don't break the skin and let any new bacteria in.

        Your intuition is right.

  • ThatPlasmaGuy

    The food does not become a radiation source when exposed to X-ray radiation. The X-ray source is a component of the machine. Once the food has left the machine it is radiation free, as the X-rays have long since been absorbed or dispersed.

    This isn't true for all forms of radiation. Neutrons, electrons, and alpha radiation can actually activate the material (food) they are irradiating, causing the material to become a radiation source itself. Once activated, the material can then re-radiate at a later time, say when it is in your stomach.
