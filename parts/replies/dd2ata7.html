    To add to this: this question is similar to "Why does 60°F water feel colder than 60°F air?"

    It's because our bodies are constantly creating heat, and how our skin "feels" temperature is in no way indicative of what the actual temperature is.

    When you "feel" the temperature, you're actually sensing how quickly the heat is either leaving your body or not leaving.

    In air, the heat does not travel from/into your body as easy as it does in water.

    Thus, in 60° water, the heat your body creates is quickly distributed away, meaning your body retains hardly any of that heat.

    In 60° air, there is less "stuff" (particles) for the heat to propagate through so the heat leaves much slowly if not none at all.

    So, your body in air is feeling "Huh, the heat isn't leaving as fast" and in water is going "wow we're losing heat quickly", even though they are at the same temperature.

    100° air and water is a bad example. The water would actually be more uncomfortable, I'm not sure if you've been in 100° heat but if it's dry and you're not in direct sunlight, it's quite bearable.

    A better example is 130°. This would be very uncomfortable and even dangerous as dry air. As water? Much much more dangerous and you would be probably yelling from pain.
