    • Delusionn

      This is a noble idea, but in reporting news, there are often less "objective" facts than you think, particularly when you're discussing human-caused tragedies and violence. The very choice of words you use is often subjective.

      In a war situation, if you describe something as a "war", you legitimize the grievances of both sides. If you call a group "rebels", you're legitimizing the authority of the other side. If you call a gunman a "shooter" or "an accused shooter", that changes the context subtly. For a concrete example, think about Israel and the Palestinian situation - the very words you use to describe various groups helps shape readers' opinions as to who the legitimate powers are, who has legitimate grievances, and what the legitimate venues for disagreement itself are.

      How you write a story - even by organizations respected as the AP and Reuters are - helps shape that story. This idea that the media colours stories consistently one way is usually a fantasy of partisan politics.
