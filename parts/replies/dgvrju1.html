      OP asked why the war on drugs is considered a failure - these deaths are exactly why I'd consider it to be one. You're right that the mere fact that it's illegal will keep a lot of people from trying it in the first place; but that can also be accomplished by using the money that's currently used for enforcement ($30-50k/yr per person in prison), and using it on education/rehab instead.

      As to your fourth point, it's really arbitrary whether they're breaking the law or law-abiding - they're doing the exact same action; the only difference is whether the government says it's illegal or not. People are going to experiment whether it's legal or not - and I know, thanks to my education on the effects of these drugs (referring specifically to opiates), that I'd never touch them, even if they were legal, even though I'm a law-abiding citizen (as would most people).

      I'm not saying legalization would be a panacea, or that people would stop dying - it will unfortunately happen no matter what you try to do. Also not saying "legalization" should be to the extent of alcohol, having it on every corner - maybe "decriminalization" is a more accurate word. If we've got limited resources, I think we should be using them on treatment and education/prevention, as opposed to stricter punishments (the war on drugs). We have some of the strictest drug laws in the world in the US; and also some of the highest rates of drug use/addiction/death. We need to make sure we're allocating our resources intelligently.
