    Radon is ridiculously unstable, and you need fairly pure gas to make a gas discharge light.

    Even if you could somehow obtain enough radon to fill a tube, and you surrounded it with massive amounts of cooling so that it didn't melt itself, within minutes there would be a lot of radon decay products floating around in the tube. They'd very likely interfere with the function of the light, probably by creating a short circuit so that the electrons pass through the decay products and not through the radon gas.

    Of course, if you somehow got such a vast quantity of radon, it would probably emit plenty of light on its own. I mean, in a neon light you're energizing gas with electrons accelerated to a thousand volts or so... radon is constantly emitting beta-minus electrons with energy in the millions of electron-volts.
