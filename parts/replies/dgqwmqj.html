      Both your conclusions are suspect. Hierarchical societies did not emerge in humans until much more recently than increased cranial volume, most hunter-gatherer societies are egalitarian and the assertion that all social animal species are hierarchical is wrong. Social insects are not actually hierarchical, each individual is simply specialised to a different role. There is no class of animals 'in charge' in these species. In pack animals hierarchies may develop but this says nothing about the social behaviours of humans. Even among higher apes there is huge variation in social behaviours amongst species which are almost genetically identical, like bonobos and chimpanzees.
