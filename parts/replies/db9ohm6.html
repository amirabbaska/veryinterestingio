      e^i∞ is defined whereas cos(∞) and sin(∞)

      Mathematically, none of these are defined.

      But the process of integrating e^it as adding together a bunch of tiny e^it dt contributions can be visually understood as walking around a circle. So we know the integral is bounded by that circle, the same way integrating cos or sin is going to be bounded by [-1,1].

      What Feynman is saying is that any justification for the terms becoming sufficiently smaller in the limit (either by decaying slowly enough or a having a higher frequency) will mean they tend to cancel out towards some value near zero. This is true for cos or sin as well. The idea is simply that the ups and downs of sin and cos, or the rotation of that complex number, will tend to cancel themselves out in the end, so they can be neglected if you have a good reason to assume that value is going to be zero.

      Isn't the electric field an harmonic oscilator in this case? As it varies by cos x?

      This question is a bit weird. I would say in this context we should just say the charges on the plate are moving like harmonic oscillators. The field is just responding to that.
