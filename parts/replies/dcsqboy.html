    Just to clarify. When we talk about electricity, we have to remember that electricity is, by and large, an electromagnetic wave. When electricity moves through a wire it's actually electric potential energy that is transferring down the length with electrons as its medium (just like a sound wave travels through air as its medium). The air particles/electron particles stay in roughly the same spot as the wave transits through the medium.

    What you are thinking of is drift velocity, the average movement of electrons (and possibly holes) in a particular direction. In any given cross section of the wire electrons move randomly in every direction, but on average electrons are moving in a particular direction in a wire that has electricity flowing. The most shocking fact is that while electricity moves close to the speed of light, drift velocity of electrons is only on the order of a few metres per minute (aka very slow).

    • eigenvectorseven

      Car batteries are by convention negatively grounded, meaning the (-) terminal is at the same potential as the car body. Meaning if you make a connection with the (-) terminal, nothing happens, there's no potential difference (i.e. voltage). So it's actually the (+) terminal that is "lifted" away from the ground potential when the battery is charged. If you make contact with this terminal (as you found out) you will get quite a shock.

      Here's the confusing bit. In solid metal, electrons are the charge carriers, and they're negatively charged. Meaning they're actually moving in the opposite direction to the conventionally defined electrical current. Confusing as this is, it technically doesn't really matter what carries the actual charge, because you don't need some "source" of electrons (the battery, the wires, and the car are completely full of them, and they simply all move around the circuit together), what you need is a source of potentia differencel, which is the (+) terminal, lifted from the ground potential by the chemical energy stored in the battery.
