    The script on old documents is similar because those writers were trained and hired to conform to a standard form for official purposes. The same writers' casual handwriting would have displayed individual style, other than a minority who might decide to use their formal "business hand" for everything.

    I was taught a few scripts in school. We drilled and practiced and were graded on conformity from first grade on, even printing (which was no longer allowed after 2nd grade). Everyone in my class still had unique personal handwriting, but on tests and papers, our work was nearly indistinguishable. You could tell that some were more skilled and uniform than others, but you'd be unable to match a written page to its author in the class.

    The second paragraph is correct, and almost all the other answers in this thread are bad guesses. Certain scripts were the standard for certain periods. E.g. spencerian script in the U.S. between 1850 and 1925. Then Palmer then Zaner-Bloser then D'Nealian.

    So why the wide variety? Firstly there was a movement away cursive to print to allow children to write at an earlier age. There was also a movement towards speed over legibility to allow writing to compete with the typewriter. The script of Palmer onwards focused on using different muscles when writing than that of the Spencerian age. There was also less of a focus on standardisation. Now combine these all together, and as long as the writing is legible, it is deemed good enough, then you get a wide variety of styles of handwriting.
