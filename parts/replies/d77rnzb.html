      • Dim_Innuendo

        Because a scale is actually not defined as "Note 1 Note 2 Note 3 Note Note 4, etc." it's "Note Interval Interval Interval Interval."

        When you play on just the white keys and start on C, it's "Note, Whole Step, Whole Step, Half Step, Whole Step, Whole Step, Whole Step, Half Step." Which your ears hear as major, because that third not is a whole step above the second.

        When you start on A, it's "Note, Whole Step, HALF Step, Whole Step, etc." That third note up being a half step is part of what we define as a minor key.

        Every different starting note makes a different scale. These are called "Modes." Playing on just the white keys, C to C (major scale) is the Ionian mode (Some people call this Hypolydian, but they are wrong). A to A (relative minor) is the Aeolian Mode. (Some people call this Hypodorian, but I hate them SO much.)

        Every mode has a different sound. Some sound major-ish, some sound minor-ish, some sound different. And this works in any key; if you play three flats, Ab to Ab is Ionian (major), while C to C is Aeolian (relative minor).

      • ZapActions-dower

        Play them yourself (or look up a recording) and you'll know why. They sound different and focus on different root notes and intervals.

        If you remove the focus on major or minor and just look at the set of natural notes and the orders you can put them in, you get the seven modes, each beginning on a different note of the scale. The notes themselves are less important than the relationships between them and which ones are played together (i.e. the chords used)
