    League of Legends reduces the impact of wild RNG as well by increasing your crit % when you consecutively don't crit, and reduces it immediately after. The likelihood of getting 3 in a row or zero in a long fight are reduced, which results in a lower variance bell curve for number of critical hits per x attacks. I'm a big fan of this because nobody likes losing because the enemy got wildly favorable RNG.

      • Voltrondemort

        Yes, "gambler's fallacy dice" are a popular tweak to an rng. In a boardgame, you can implement them with a deck of cards - once a possibility comes up, it won't come up again until the deck is recycled.

        The infuriating nature of the dice in Settlers of Catan led to the introduction of an expansion pack "traders and barbarians", which includes (among other things) a deck of numbers to replace the dice.

      • NICKisICE

        Over the course of a game, let's say you attack 1,000 times. You absolutely will crit at roughly 20% of the time with a 20% chance to happen.

        The problem arises when your sample size of a fight is, say, 6. Your expected number of crits is 1.2 (so 1). However there is a rather high (about 26%) chance that there will be zero crits, which will frustrate the user. While the likelihood for crit 3 times isn't particularly likely (about 8%) it will happen about one out of 12 instances. Someone winds up unhappy.

        The impact here is it makes the instance of zero crits and the instance of 3 crits less likely which is usually a good thing.

  • Fiascopia

    In order to balance games I have made I also use the concept of a 'grab bag' to select numbers from. As a player, if you have 10% chance to crit and you hit 10/15/20/30 times and don't crit it feels unfair. As a developer it is hard to balance fights when a player might crit 4 times in a row. I made a function which would (pseudo) randomly select a number, say between 1 and 4. If '2' came up then it was removed from the pool of numbers. Next time a selection would be made from 1, 3 and 4. If '4' came up then it would select from 1 and 3. After it used them all up it would reset. Even this could be a bit intuitive feeling, when the numbers were reset as you could get two critical hits back to back. I have found that a person's perception of what random should feel like and what random is actually like are two very different things. I imagine the algorithms in games could be quite sophisticated to get the right 'feel'.
