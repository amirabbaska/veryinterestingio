    • Wobbles42

      It is all empirical in the end... just with layers of theory sometimes abstracting away previous empirical observations.

      Using 16th or 17th century technology, relativistic effects are mostly invisible. In a way this made Newton's job easier. He couldn't see relativistic effects, so he never had a reason to guess they were there.

      Maxwell's equations did imply that the speed of light is the same for all observers, but this was tested empirically (and accidentally) by the Michelsonâ€“Morley experiments less than 50 years before Einstein. Prior to that (and for some time after), people thought that there was an implied frame of reference (the "ether") hidden in Maxwell's equations.

      Einstein's initial insight came out of the struggle to reconcile the apparent contradiction between the notion of Galilean relativity, and the constant speed of light. He had to throw out our whole notion of time and just accept both as true. Then he made predictions and we verified his ideas empirically.

      Interestingly, it was another set of clashes between electromagnetics and classical physics that got us quantum physics (black body radiation and the " ultraviolet catastrophe"). Quantum and relativity actually contradict each other though... that is probably where our next physics revolution comes from.

      Really, you need both. Empirical observations, and some set of ideas to explain them. Discoveries come when contradictions emerge.

      The question Greg Egan's book asks is: "What if Newton's job had been a lot harder?". What if the theory of relativity came from the same direct observations that led to the theory of gravity, rather than from an initial development of classical physics leading to an apparent contradiction with electromagnetics? It really does lead to a more intuitive development, though, because you get to watch the theory emerge using observations of everyday objects with primitive tools.
