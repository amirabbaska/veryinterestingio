      Thank you. I'd like to add one more point.

      A lot of arguments that people casually have in philosophy come down to people arguing over the definition of a word. This is called a semantic dispute. Many people don't consider this to be a "real" question, because there's no "right" or "wrong" definition of a word. For example, we could define "swimming" to include submarines, or we could define "swimming" to not include submarines. But, really, it wouldn't matter -- it wouldn't affect what we say about the world. We could just pick one and then consistently use that. Submarines would sail instead of swim, and it wouldn't change anything. We'd just use a different word everywhere.

      This often comes up because philosophy makes people think very rigorously about the meaning of words. And a lot of time, the definition of English words is incomplete -- a dictionary won't tell you exactly whether a word should be used to describe something or not. That's because we don't need a precise definition -- we only make our definitions precise enough to handle the cases that we normally care about on a day-to-day basis.

      So, for example -- we don't normally define exactly what a "heap" is. If you put a grain of rice down, that's not a heap. But if you put a million down, that could be called a heap of rice. Somewhere between the two, the pile of rice became a heap. But there's no "right" or "wrong" place to draw a line, because we've simply never bothered to do so -- we've always worked with a fuzzy definition of "heap". And it's good enough for what we use "heap" for in daily English usage.

      So the right answer to someone being asked whether it's 1001 or 1002 grains of rice that makes something a heap is "That isn't defined".

      An example of a problem in philosophy that raises a semantic dispute is the Ship of Theseus.

      Anyway, while these questions can help you understand how we think about words or highlight the fact that our English definitions are vague, sometimes people start arguing about which definition is right. That's a semantic dispute. And what I'd call the appropriate response to this sort of thing is really "this just isn't an interesting question". It's not defined, and it doesn't matter that much what you choose, as long as you use the definition that you choose consistently.

      There are also a few complications. In law or in ethics, we use English words to define things whether or not something is legal or whether or not it is morally-right. For example, it's not legal to "murder", so the question of "whether or not killing a fetus is murder" isn't really just a semantic dispute, even though it looks like one, because we've already stated that murder is not okay. If it were a semantic dispute and we decided that killing a fetus is murder, we'd need to be willing to say "it's murder but that's potentially okay in this case". That's not the case -- instead, asking whether or not killing a fetus is murder is asking "what should our legal rule on this be?"
