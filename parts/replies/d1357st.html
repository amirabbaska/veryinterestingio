    So, in many learning and memory studies researchers teach mice to associate fear with a specific box (lighting, flooring, odor, etc.). Similarly, in our experiments the fear is learned without any colored light or lasers (I am assuming you meant light stimulation of brain cells). In rodents, the memory of a fearful experience is definitely among the strongest and most salient because it becomes about survival. In our study, we not only restored fear memories in early AD mice but also restored spatial/environmental memories (in this case the memory was for an object in a specific location within their environment). Based on these findings, we believe that our memory restoration experiment can be applied to fear and happy/neutral memories.

    I'd say that the light shock is a procedural memory (having happened many times) while the birthday one is episodic (linked to a single event)...not OP, but these do seem to be encoded and retrieved in different ways. Procedural memory, for example, can be a much stronger process. So much so that cognitive therapists use it to teach new routines (or re-teach old ones) to people with dementia with spaced retrieval techniques (repeating a process again and again over a period of time until it sticks).
