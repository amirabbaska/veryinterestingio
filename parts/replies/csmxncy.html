    • HungryLikeTheWolf99

      This is useful, but these historical differences don't take into account things which came up after the language was pretty much fully formed, as it were. For example, in German, despite English being "Germanic", they use an entirely different preposition for something being "on" TV - they use "an", which is otherwise usually used I describe something being up against something else, such as a picture "an" the wall; but not a bowl sitting "auf" a table.

      Also note that names for modern inventions (rather than the prepositions that accompany their use) are typically much more similar across languages. "Telephone" is probably a good demonstration of this effect - you'll find almost that exact work used in most languages to refer to a telephone. It was obviously concocted as an English word from Greek roots, but took hold most everywhere.
