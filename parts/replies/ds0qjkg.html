    Dry air contains 20.95% oxygen and you can simply multiply the percentage by 10,000 to get the ppm. So the concentration of oxygen in dry air is 209,500 ppm. Based on that, ±15 ppm doesn't seem like a big deal.

    Edit: There's a really handy graph right below the section of the article you copied. The ±15 ppm is based on seasons, and it's barely any change. To better answer /u/MAD_OR_NAW's question, seasons basically don't change the oxygen enough to really matter. What does matter is elevation. The ground has more oxygen than the higher levels of the atmosphere (and these levels are more evenly mixed), and if you go even higher, then space radiation just breaks it from O2 into just O.

      • JasontheFuzz

        You're correct; water does dilute oxygen out of the air. When the dew point is 0 degrees C, water vapor takes up 6020 ppm by volume. This effectively takes up 0.602% of the atmosphere, diluting the concentration of oxygen from 20.95% to 20.82%. Unnoticeable for things that like to breathe, but considerably more than the ±15 ppm we're talking about.

        However, we would also have to consider that we're not displacing air so much as adding water vapor (since mountaintops also have 20.95% oxygen but there's just less air overall).
