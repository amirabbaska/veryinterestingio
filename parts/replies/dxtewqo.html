    This requires an understanding of the muscles that control eye movements. To simplify it, there are 6 muscles that move the eyeball and 3 nerves (per eye). The nerves are called CN III, IV, and VI. I’m gonna leave out IV for this discussion and just talk about III & VI.

    CN III control the muscles that move the eye towards the nose, as well as up and down (which we don’t care about for this discussion). It also has other functions but, again, not important for this discussion. CN VI then, as you may have guessed, moves the eye away from the nose, or toward the side of the head. Both nerves have things called nuclei within the brainstem, which is where they originate and where the “control” is.

    If the nucleus of CN III of the right eye becomes more active, the muscles cause the right eye to turn towards the nose (towards the left).

    If the nucleus of CN VI of the left eye becomes more active, the muscles cause the left eye to turn towards the temple (towards the left).

    There is a pathway called the MLF that connects the right nucleus of CN III with the left nucleus of CN VI, and vice versa. This way, when one becomes active, so does the other. This way, when you want to look left, both eyes do.

    There is also a pathway in the brain that activates the right and the left nuclei of CN III. This causes both eyes to look towards the nose.

    To finally answer your question, there is simply no pathway in the brain that activates both the right and left nuclei of CN VI.
