    • Bend_Over_Please

      This is a quick and dirty version:

      One of the predictions of Einstein’s General Relativity is that light will be “bent” in a strong gravitational field. To test this, we’ll first need a strong gravitational field. Conveniently, we have an object in our solar system that can provide just that: The Sun.

      We also know the celestial positions of stars in the sky from repeated astronomical observations. So all we have to do is wait until the Sun is in between us and those distant stars, then see if the light from those distant stars still reaches us via bending around the Sun. Easy? One problem: The Sun is too damn bright, and our lenses will just be overwhelmed by the Sun’s luminosity.

      So how did Eddington do it? He blocked out the Sun with the Moon (total solar eclipse), so that you can now resolve the light coming from the distant stars. And he measured that the positions of the stars were indeed shifted, which meant that there was gravitational lensing taking place, which was a prediction of GR, which helped cement the theory.

  • zbufferz

    An important nuance of this is that the light does not bend because the gravity-causing object is pulling that object to it the way baseballs are pulled to the ground by earth gravity. Light has no mass. The light is traveling straight through space, it's just that space is warped by gravity, like the straight lines on a bedsheet are still straight even when the sheet is rumpled.
