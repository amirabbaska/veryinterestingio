        The gas isn't glowing due to how hot it is, although it is also quite hot; rather, it is ionized/excited, and what you're seeing is spectral line radiation from that. With your eye, mostly the OIII doublet, which is blue-green in color. The absolute main component of light from emission nebulae like Orion, though, is the red hydrogen alpha line. The rods in our eyes are not very sensitive to red light, hence why we see the weaker oxygen line better.

        Collisional excitation isn't very significant until the density climbs to about 10^3-10^4 particles per cubic centimeter, as I recall. Rather, the gas gets very hot because it's very difficult for it to cool down (nothing to exchange that energy with!), and because young, massive stars are very hot and put out enormous amounts of radiation. Much of the interstellar medium hovers at around 10,000 K, the temperature at which hydrogen ionizes, for exactly this reason: it gets heated up and ionized in a variety of ways (radiation, turbulence, etc.), recombines, and then has no way to cool further.
