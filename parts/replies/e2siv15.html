    • Junto26

      Yeppers. A capacitor is the clearest example. It charges up and the discharges “slowly” over time, much like a battery.

      Imagine putting an AC voltage across a capacitor, flipping the voltage over and over. Because it takes time for the capacitor to charge and discharge, the current coming out won’t be timed exactly with the voltage. Does that make sense?

      Resistive loads don’t do this because theres no charging or discharging over time stuff involved in resistive loads, by definition. You could think of a resistive load as just a longer, or narrower, pipe the electrons need to go through, that’s all.

      You said “especially inductive.” Inductive works similarly but I’d need to brush up on the details to explain it decently, sorry!

      Your second question: it’s talking real power (Watts), not imaginary power (Vars). You mentioned amperage - there’s no real or imaginary current, current is just current. Remember, voltage and current are the cause, power is the effect, and the timing of them determines how much real power there is and how much power is lost/wasted due to mid-timing (which we call imaginary power).

    • 0onimusha2

      The reason capacitive and inductive loads have lag but resistance doesnt is because of the electric and magnetic fields created when current or voltage goes through an inductor or capacitor.

      As current goes through an inductor, a magnetic field is created by the very initial current going through it, meaning we see current coming out of the inductor slightly later than expected by a couple nanoseconds or microseconds depending on the inductors value. As the current stops flowing (we turn off the power supply) the magnetic field begins to reduce in magnitude, and a changing magnetic field creates current in the inductor, so the current created by the magnetic field happens after the initial current was pushed through the inductor, and that current at the end, the lagging current, was the energy that initially created the magnetic field in the inductor. The same logic applies to a voltage put on a capacitor and the resulting electric field.
