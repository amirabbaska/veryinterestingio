    • equationsofmotion

      Hey, great questions!

      I get that the universe is expanding at an ever-increasing rate - but how do we know that the star is moving with the expansion?

      Well, the way we figured out that the universe is expanding is by looking at distant stars. On average, distant stars are moving away from us. And the further away from us the star is moving, the faster away from us it's moving. There are two explanations:

      1. The Earth occupies a special place in the universe. Everything is moving away from us and far away things are moving faster.

      2. Everything is moving away from everything else at a (roughly) constant rate. Imagine a bunch of dots on the surface of a balloon. As the balloon inflates, nearby dots move away from each other a little. More distant dots move away from each other more quickly. For a three-dimensional analogy, imagine raisins embedded in bread that's rising. As the bread rises, it expands and the raisins move away from each other.

      Number 2 is the one people tend to believe because it doesn't assume Earth is special (which would be strange). I say roughly constant rate because we now know that the universe is expanding more rapidly today than it was in the past. The source of that acceleration is dark energy.

      And is the expansion only at the edges or is all of space expanding, even here on earth?

      All of space is expanding. However, Earth and our galaxy resist the expansion because of their gravitational attraction. So basically the result of cosmic expansion is that galaxies move away from each other, but the planets in our solar system are not moving away from each other.

      And if that star IS moving, why can't it be moving towards us

      Yeah, so some distant stars and galaxies ARE moving towards us, just because they were given some velocity kick. But on average distant galaxies are moving away from us (and each other).

      assuming space expands only at the edges

      There are no edges that we know of. ;)
