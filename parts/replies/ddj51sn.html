    Legibility explains a lot of conventions, including some that have fallen out of use as digital data formats make it less of a concern.

    Older electrical schematics for example are notated as: "3k2" for 3.2k (3200) or "1v5" for 1.5v (1.5 volts). And if you've seen older handwritten schematics, not just the text/symbols but the low quality they were stored/copied/distributed with, you'll be happy you weren't searching for a decimal point in there.

    Even our modern typefaces with (for example) lowercase a's that look nothing like a handwritten a are a symptom of the same problem. In the early days of typing you couldn't rely on the letter being fully/correctly printed/displayed, so the handwritten a would be mistaken for an o. With modern computers and printers this isn't much of a concern and with the available equipment, we could read the handwritten style a just fine. But we already do it the other way
