    This is X linked traits, which is kinda different than codominance, which is why traits from both parents show up. The fact that calico cats' colour is X linked isn't relevant to the mixing of the colors, because there are two forms of the X gene where both are dominant. For example X^B and X^R where X^B is for black fur and X^R for red fur. When a cat's genotype is X^B X^R it results in a calico cat. If it has a Y chromosome, making it a male, it cannot have this combination. But the fact that it is an X linked trait only leads to the fact that the cat's always female, not the fact that it shows both parent's traits. Its because X^B and X^R are both dominant (shown by capital B and R) that they both appear in the offspring.

      The X-linkage is key to the patchiness. In some cell lines, the female's maternal X becomes inactivated, in other cell lines, the female's paternal X becomes inactivated. This leads to the distinct patches of different colour. It is only the X's that are inactivated in this way, not the autosomes, because it is a specific dosage compensation adaptation. (Interestingly, in fruit flies, the same dosage problem is instead solved by the male's one X being transcribed at twice the normal rate.)
