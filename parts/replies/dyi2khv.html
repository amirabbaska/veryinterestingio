    I recently had a gum graft and they gave me antibiotics and a rinse to use for a couple weeks instead of my usual mouthwash. Would you happen to know why they had me use that rinse instead?

    • TheMythof_Feminism

      Well I would need a bit more details to respond with confidence but I can give you a general answer very easily.

      The mouthwash used before/after periodontal treatment tends to be in response to specific periodontal issues like periodontitis (Swelling of the support tissue of the tooth [Gums, alveolar bone, periodontal ligament, cementum]), this type of mouthwash is very heavily oriented towards avoiding irritation of these tissues, decreasing swelling (Inflammation) and eliminating bacteria that exists in the periphery of teeth ("Pockets" that have anaerobic and facultative bacteria which heavily populate these pockets in comparison to the general oral cavity).

      This is VERY different from regular mouthwash which , for the most part, is a mixture of simple components designed to eliminate the most aggressive forms of micro-orgamnisms (Ex: Streptococcus sanguis) which colonize very rapidly or have rapid onset of effects and commercial effects like "clean breath". Simple clorhexidine, alcohol and other basic components , not targeted and not difficult to produce.

      I would suspect the mouthwash you were given to be wildly more expensive than your regular mouthwash, probably to the tune of about x10 or even x20 more expensive on average.... as it is meant to facilitate the assimilation of grafted tissue as much as possible while reducing the risk of infection as much as possible, it stands to reason the mixture is far more delicate , precise and therefore, expensive.

      A lot of patients ask what you ask with the implication that they're just giving you an expensive product to make more money, and that is sometimes true, but in your case I highly doubt it.
