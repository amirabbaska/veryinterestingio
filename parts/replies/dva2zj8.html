  • dave_890

    The chemical makeup of the water, the salinity, the current flow, pollution level, all of those can have a bearing on how much anything that sinks deteriorates.

    The planes are mostly aluminum. I don't know of many organisms that digest aluminum, unlike the bacteria eating up Titanic and forming the "rusticles" with its waste. Also, Navy ships are designed to combat the effects of salt water, so layers of rust preventative and anti-fouling paint keeps the rust and organisms away. Not as great a need for such measures on Titanic, and there's a 30-year difference between the 2 ships. Paint formulas likely greatly improved in that time.

    Would be interesting to compare water conditions at Lexington with those at Titanic. The "Atlantic Conveyor Belt", and ocean current from the Caribbean, up the east coast of America, and across to Iceland, Ireland and England, almost certainly has more organic matter in the flow. Palm seeds have made the trip and grow naturally into mature trees in Ireland (but it's too cold for those trees to produce seeds).
