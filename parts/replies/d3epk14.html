      • bazmonkey

        Now it's getting into what "intuition" actually means here.

        In this scope, knowing how to solve parts of the cube by intuition means not that there was nothing to memorize, but rather you understand how the steps work. You can see the pieces move and why the algorithm works to the point that you can piece it back together by simply knowing what "needs" to happen. E.g. a person that says they solve the bottom two layers "intuitively" still internally has short sets of turns (a.k.a. algorithms) committed to memory, but understands it to the point of being able to modify it on demand (such as performing the reflection of the algorithm on the opposite side, or adding in a step to deal with a different initial position of the pieces in question).

        That said, there is a method to solve the cube that involves two algorithms only, by solving all of the edges, and then all of the corners. One moves three edges and flips one, another moves three corners and rotates them. By first transforming the cube so that the corners/edges you need to move are in the right spot, doing this algorithm, and then backtracking the original transformation, the entire cube can be solved. Moreover, the algorithms are short and sweet (4 and 8 moves for the edge and corner algorithm respectively), and one could easily take note of the corners and see how the algorithm works.

        If you understand how those two algorithms work, and understand how to manipulate the cube in order to leverage only those two algorithms, I would consider that being able to solve the cube intuitively.

        This is the method of which I speak.
