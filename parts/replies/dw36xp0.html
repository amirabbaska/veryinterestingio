  • Sinai

    Well, plain old Newtonian motion is also responsible for a lot of the expansion of space, especially in the beginning.

    Metric expansion is supposed to have been very important during the first incredibly small fraction of a second of the Big Bang, or the inflationary period, but after that, plain old inertia would have completely dominated the expansion of space until we started seeing distances large enough for the cosmological constant to matter.

    The effect of this is that the expansion of the universe was slowing down for the majority of the universe's lifetime, and only began accelerating ~5 billion years ago.

    • danby

      The surface of the balloon has a down (in toward the interior of the balloon) and a up (out and away from the surface of the balloon). As best we can tell no such equivalent orthogonal directions exist for our universe.

      The surface of the balloon is an approximately 2D surface. The reason it has and up and down is that a balloon as we know it is a continuous, spherical surface embedded in the 3D space we are familiar with. For the universe to have equivalent orhtogonal 'up' and 'downs' as the balloon has would imply that our universe is embedded in a space with (at least) 4 spatial dimensions. Again, as best as we can tell there is no such 4D space so the balloon analogy doesn't hold.

      The balloon analogy gives people the wrong intuition about the universe because it reinforces the notion that the expansion can't happen without an interior getting larger and some exterior space getting that much smaller.
