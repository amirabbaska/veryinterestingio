    • geezorious

      I would be careful using phrases like "exists only on the surface". The holographic principle only says the information contained in the sphere's volume is equivalent to the information contained at its surface. It makes no prediction about where the information truly exists. This is the same type of equivalency principle Einstein uses to explain velocity, and that being stationary with an object flying past you at speed X is equivalent to flying at speed X past a stationary object. Einstein doesn't say who is stationary and who is moving, and his equivalence principle says they're the same thing. So your question of whether information is at the surface or in its volume is not valid, they're the same thing.

      That said, the holographic principle isn't an interpretation or philosophy, because it does make predictions and is falsifiable. The non-holographic principle predicts that maximal information density of a volume is its Planck cubed volume, while the holographic principle predicts maximal information density of a volume is its Planck squared surface area. These are vastly different. Imagine a 10x10x10 meter cube. That's 1,000 cubic meters. If the Planck length was 1 meter, you'd be able to store 1000 bits of information. Or so you think. The holographic principle says the information of that cube is equivalent to the information at its surface, which is only 600 meters squared, or a maximum of 600 bits of information. If you can show a cube exceeding that information density, you've proven the holographic principle false.

    • geezorious

      We don't know much about the physics inside the Schwarzschild radius, but the holographic principle is agnostic to that. A sphere can carry information prior to collapsing into a black hole, and assuming that information is not destroyed, the new sphere, defined by its Schwarzschild radius, carries the same information. The surface area of this new sphere has to contain the information of the volume of the old sphere. Both definitions are well defined since neither looks inside the event horizon.
