      I'm at the end of a long day, so I'll see if I can dust off the cobwebs...

      Centripetal force = ( mass * velocity^2 ) / radius

      So the force is inversely proportional to the radius. But you want to know about angular velocity. Well, say you have a 1kg mass at 1m/s at a radius of 1m.

      Fc = (1kg * (1m/s)^2 ) / 1m = 1N

      To keep the same force at a shorter radius, make Fc a constant and look at velocity.

      1N = (1kg * ( V ) ^2 ) / 0.5 m V^2 = 0.5 (m/s)^2 V = sqrt(0.5) = .7m/s roughly.

      In the first case, you were travelling 1 meter of arc per second, and now 0.7. Going slower, right?

      ArcLength = r * theta, so theta = ArcLength / r.

      In the first case, you needed to rotate by 1 radian per second to achieve the 1N force.

      In the second case:

      theta = 0.7 / 0.5 = 1.4

      By decreasing the radius, the angular velocity increases to maintain the same force.

      TL;DR: Yes, a smaller ring turns at a faster angular rate to achieve the same force.

      • tripyra

        Or, much more simply, we could substitute angular velocity * radius for tangential velocity. This yields

        F = m * w^2 * r

        where F is centripetal force, m is mass, w is angular velocity, and r is radius

        From this we can see that in order to maintain a constant force, we must increase the angular velocity if we decrease the radius.
