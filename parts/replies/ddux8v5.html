  • DaSaw

    Worse, Time Warner (or Comcast) could, for example, throttle bandwidth on the basis of political content. For instance, you could have a Democratic Party primary pitting a corporatist flunkie against a socialist reformer... and for some reason the Internet loads super slow any time you're trying to load the reformer's videos. Well, they can do that now, but under a Net Neutrality regime, it would be illegal.

    Though I prefer a more radical solution to this problem: the separation of content and infrastructure. (The parallel to the phrase "separation of church and state" deliberate; the concepts are actually very similar.) Time Warner simply shouldn't be permitted to own both the content provider and the delivery platform. It creates a conflict of interest that is in opposition to the public good. Companies should have to choose whether they're in the production business or the delivery business.

    I also think municipalities should be allowed to be in the "last mile" business. I don't think they should be legally allowed to exclude other companies by legal fiat (as the currently do in favor of the cable companies), but I do think they should be allowed to buy, sell, and lease infrastructure with the same rights as any corporation.
