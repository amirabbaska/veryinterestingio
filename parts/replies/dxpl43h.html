    What is it like learning to kill test subjects? Do biologists use any tricks to avoid looking the victim in the eye?

    • amihappyornot

      Had to do this a lot during my PhD. The first few times are a little difficult, but it gets easier. One main point is that killing the test subjects is not just kill-and-be-done-with-it, you have to maintain a lot of precision during the process to ensure that the brains/organs are dissected and preserved correctly, to make sure you don't waste reagents and tissues, and to minimize pain and stress for the animal being sacrificed. So, there is very little room for thought during the process and you are so focused on getting things right in the moment that the act of killing takes up much less mindspace.

    • Getmesomelube

      I worked as a scientist for a drug discovery company and did what is known as "pre-clinical" research- before any promising compound is trialled in humans it is tested in mice and rat models. For depression, learned helplessness is a valid test. There are committees that perform ethical review to reduce and re-use animals but scientists do need to look at the brains for depression research.
