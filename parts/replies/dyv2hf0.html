    What you are saying is true, but all the examples you mentioned where π is used can be explained by a circle cropping up somewhere.

    In Coulomb's law it's because a sphere's surface area is given by 4πr^2.

    In pendulum dynamics, the position of the pendulum as time goes on is cyclical. The motion is modeled by trigonometric functions, which are similarly cyclical. The values of trig functions are related to angles and those are essentially parts of a circle's circumference.

    In complex analysis it's because complex numbers can be thought of as magnitude and angle, which loops back to the trigonometric functions argument. There is always a circle involved with π.

      I see it similarly, but slightly differently. Point A is zero dimensions. Connect that Point A to another point a fixed distance away, Point B, and you are in 1-dimensional space. Log all the points on a plane the same fixed distance between Point A and Point B and you have a circle in 2-dimensional space. Log all the points in space the same fixed distance between Point A and Point B and you have a sphere in 3-dimensional space. Etc. The idea I end up with is that the radius, that distance between Point A and Point B, is the fundamental unit. And ratios involving the radius are far more fundamental than the diameter or other measurements.
