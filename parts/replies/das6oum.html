    I think this is the basis of the true "correct answer", at least according to all the psych and sociology classes I took in uni. They really drove this point home a lot to explain many of the things Americans find weird about the Japanese, reasons why the Chinese think Americans are so weird, etc.

    But if OP was looking for an explanation as to why America happens to be such an individualist culture, I think the current top comment hit on it: we suddenly had a massive amount of land to "conquer" compared to what we were used to in Europe, and homesteading/"heading west" was a big freaking deal for a large portion of our early history as a new country.

    The government would basically just give you free land for a song and a dance as long as you made use of it by farming or raising cattle or whatever. They just wanted to get it tamed and settled by, well, white people. Or at least non-natives. Americans. Whatever you want to call it. Once you have a population living across it, it's a lot easier to retain control and prove you own it, rather than just pointing and saying, "yep, everything to the left of here, all the way to ocean; that's all ours."

    A lot of people made as much use of the free land policy as they could, by doing things like a couple getting adjoining lots of land each under their own names so they could had double-sized farms. And adult children would want to get their own land as soon as possible too, which entails moving off the family double-wide farm.

    I don't have any sources to quote, I just remember this from various history specials I've watched over the years. I'm fairly certain this had a lot to do with us getting the idea in our heads that the kids needed to get out of the nest ASAP; there's land fucking everywhere- go get you some!
