    • amstan

      Possibly. It's not that it's a low escape velocity. It's that the ISS is already going pretty fast.

      ISS orbits at 7660m/s. Escape speed has been mentioned in this thread at over 10000ish m/s at ground elevation, possibly less at 300km up? If the escaping air goes prograde (direction of the orbit), the numbers(7660 and air speed) will add up and it might go over the escape speed. If the escaping air goes retrograde (backwards) it will very likely deorbit (you only need a couple of 100 meters per second that way to intersect the atmosphere).

      Accelerating up or down while in orbit is counter intuitive, it's not going to result you actually going that way in the long run. You want prograde or retrograde.

  • piousflea84

    I don't think "falling back down" would take all that long. The ISS orbits quite low and there's nonzero atmospheric drag.

    An isolated air molecule (N2, O2, or whatever) would travel a distance approximately equal to its mean free path before colliding with an atmospheric particle. I don't have the data for mean free path in Low Earth Orbit, but I'm pretty sure it is much, much smaller than the circumference of the Earth.

    Once the first collision happened, that air molecule would lose a large % of its kinetic energy and would no longer be moving fast enough to stay in orbit. So it would fall down and reach equilibrium with the rest of the Earth's atmosphere. The process would be quite rapid.
