      • markhallyo

        Short answer: Wiki says 18 H proteins and 11 N proteins currently known for influenza A. Some combinations have been labeled as extinct, but not necessarily because the combination is incompatible, but maybe because the populations that harbored each subtype never encountered each other to create new combinations.

        Long answer: A big problem is even within the same flu subtype there is some variety. I don't know if the old strains have been completely sequenced, but the Spanish flu subtype from the 1918 pandemic was H1N1. This is the "same" subtype as the H1N1 we saw spring up in the news a decade or so ago. However, the health impact of the more recent H1N1 was as you may know, much much different, I imagine even after you account for changes in access to healthcare and quality of care. This is because of the further variety that exists within the subtypes, which isn't necessarily conveyed by our current colloquial naming system. On top of genetic shift (significant swapping of genes between subtypes and species - how we have H5N1 and H1N1, etc.), genetic drift exists. Drift is the term used to describe the phenomenon of small mutations over time or in different regions that can create significant variation even within a subtype. Think same virus, just with a different haircut that changes its personality a little bit. H1 gene/protein from 1918 may have been 40% avian 60% porcine, whereas the recent H1 may have been 40% avian, 55% porcine, 5% novel sequence. This change due to drift could result in significant variations in virulence and susceptibility to our developed antibodies, even if they both end up being named H1. This example is oversimplified and not a perfectly accurate representation of how the virus evolves, but it does demonstrate the concept of genetic drift. Drift is present in all viruses and creates some problems, but can be outsmarted by vaccine makers. However, when you combine drift and shift, your pool of possible combinations opens up significantly, and makes the problem way more difficult to solve.

        To wrap this up, shift is probably the biggest obstacle in making a catch all vaccine. When you've got a virus that has multiple hosts, you decrease the likelihood of ever eradicating that virus. As with smallpox, even though it was very deadly, it was able to be eradicated thanks in part to the lack of other animal hosts. There was no real chance for it to undergo genetic shift, which is part of what makes a virus like influenza such a well-studied but still very problematic virus. Even if you managed to prevent humans from being infected from the current flu subtypes, eventually a subtype that has been evolving in the ways mentioned above in an uncontrollable host, such as birds, might make its way back into human populations with the potential to cause significant disease. I hope this makes sense.

        Tl;dr there may be too much potential variation to adequately answer that question, and even if it had an answer, the flu has properties that make it significantly more difficult to completely and indefinitely vaccinate against.

      • Zhentar

        It's effectively infinite; even if we could figure out all the configurations of the surface proteins that need to be there (which is pretty well beyond us currently and would still be an enormously large number), the virus can still always add extra surface proteins that don't do anything but confuse your immune system.

        That said, there are a couple proteins that don't change very much between influenza strains (because they're critical to making a viable virus). If we can teach the immune system to specifically target those proteins that are almost always the same, we could make a more or less universal flu vaccine. There's been a lot of research in this area and there is at least one attempt starting clinical trials.
