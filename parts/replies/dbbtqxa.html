    I'd like to piggy back on this by highlighting the inherent difficulty of getting 'solid' (i.e. double-blind) clinical research done on acupuncture.

    in ideal situations, no one directly interacting with the patient, or the patient his/herself, knows if they are receiving 'real' medication or placebo.

    in the case of acupuncture, however, this becomes exceedingly difficult. unlike administering, say, a pill, the content of which can be easily modified, acupuncture is based on physical phenomena -- the act of placing needles into the skin at certain points.

    if the drug is poking needles into your body, what would the placebo be? the most obvious one would be "not having needles poked into your body," but then all parties involved with the study would know who was in the placebo group and who wasn't.

    the alternative is to have someone place needles into the 'incorrect' part of the body, but then you have two problems: 1) the person administering the 'incorrect' acupuncture knows they're administering it incorrectly (assuming the person administering the acupuncture is licensed), and 2) you're still poking needles into someone, which could very well have an effect (possibly via local endorphin release, etc.).

    tl;dr: it's a tricky thing to design a study for.
