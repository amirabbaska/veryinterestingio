    • SoCalDoc

      That's a very tricky question to answer succinctly. The short answer is: it depends. Usually when you are at the point of having to use vasogenic drugs to assist in hemodynamic management you have gone from compensated shock to uncompensated shock. At that point regional perfusion to the various organ vascular beds could be compromised in a way that the body is no longer shunting blood to the 'vital' areas. This frequently happens in distributive or 'warm' shock in the setting of overwhelming sepsis. What is happening is that the vascular beds are no longer as responsive to endogenous or exogenous vasoactive substances and the capillaries relax and become engorged with blood. As blood is no longer being shunted to the large central vessels, blood pressure may drop significantly and you now have a situation where even the 'vital' organs (brain, heart, and adrenals) are undergoing hypoperfusion. This can be exacerbated by incorrect vasopressor management. For example, administering too much vasopressor in the setting of heart failure can cause the heart to fail even more leading to a quickly downward spiraling clinical status. Sorry this answer is so vague. It really does depend on the clinical situation. In most scenarios, I would still say that perfusion is preferentially preserved in the brain, heart, and adrenal glands.
