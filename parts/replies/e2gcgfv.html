    That's just because those languages didn't have a word to specifically describe blue. Lots of color words came about relatively late in history. The English language lacked a word to describe orange (although they did use "yellow-red") until the 14-1500s when the fruit physically made its way from southeast Asia to Europe and people began referring to the color using the name of the newly arrived fruit.

      • CuriousZap

        Well, it is actually very similar to most languages. The Japanese word for blue, ao, didn't originally refer to just blue (and it still doesn't), but rather referred to blue/green/violet/gray/etc. Similarly most languages had a generic color term to refer to blue and green and similar cool colors together.

        To this day the Japanese still use ao to describe these other colors because of the way you can use the adjective directly. For instance, green apples are often called ao-ringo (blue apple). If you wanted to describe an apple as green then you couldn't say midori-ringo, but rather you would have to say midori no ringo and that's just too many syllables.
