  • 67726e

    Unfortunately in a self-organizing group without an official leader, unofficial leaders will organically appear along with some semblance of social structure. I've worked jobs with true, proper management and even then certain people with more dominating personalities would easily win out in any meeting. In a setting where there is no manager to consider business needs over personal, one can easily imagine the domineering to win out over the quiet, contemplative.

    Having a strong hiring process, and not being afraid to take action on a bad hire certainly help, but having an organization with great freedom can result in some unpleasant side-effects. Oftentimes no one wants to do the dirty work, the maintenance and hard bug fixes. Products rot and die, other products do not get built. In the case of Valve, I've read many an allegation that Half Life 3 has fallen victim to folks not wanting to be a part of the project. Google, organizationally, does bear some similar structure and there's a reason why so many of their projects are sidelined, unsupported, and get killed off.

    I firmly believe, Valve, as any other company is a victim to unintended consequences. Incentive "innovation" and folks will flock to the new shiny thing for glory and honor. Prioritize "flawless" software, don't be surprised your product is stuck years behind. It's no surprise Valve is still able to hold such a policy, they're practically printing money with Steam. However for every open and innovative Google, there are companies that failed or realized they needed real, explicit management and structure to function.
