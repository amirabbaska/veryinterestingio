    • hamsterdave

      I've got some military aviation background, so I'll take a stab at answering this part, but the real answer is "It really depends".

      Once it was established that the aircraft was unresponsive to attempts to contact it via radio, or that it was somewhere it shouldn't be or was headed that direction, the closest fighter squadron would launch an intercept, probably at least 2 aircraft. There are bases around the country that have a couple of pilots sitting on "alert" at any given time for just such a purpose. Currently the most likely candidates if the aircraft is well away from the coast are the F-15 or F-16 (both of which are USAF primarily). Other possibilities depending on exact location include the F-18 (Navy or Marines, most common around the coast but there are inland Naval Aviation Stations) and F-22 (USAF but not many of them CONUS AFAIK). I don't know if there are combat ready F-35 squadrons filling the air defense mission yet, but if there are, there aren't many of them.

      The aircraft in question would be intercepted by the fighters and an attempt to ascertain its intent would be made. They would first make sure that the pilot knew that they were there, likely with a close approach well within the pilot's field of view, then they would likely take up a position at a safer distance but still keeping the aircraft in view, probably with one in a location where they could fire if necessary. Radio contact would be attempted on all common channels (UNICOM, local CTAF, ATC frequencies, etc), and failing that, the fighters may even attempt to get close enough to establish visual communications. Maybe the aircraft is in trouble and has no electrical power, maybe the pilot is disabled and the aircraft is on auto-pilot (this has happened several times over the years due to rapid depressurization and/or hypoxia), they might even be able to determine if there are people in the cockpit who shouldn't be (hijackers, perhaps).

      They would then attempt to escort the aircraft to a nearby airfield. If it is obvious that there is someone in control of the aircraft, and some form of communication weren't established, or it was but the pilot was defiant, the situation gets sticky.

      If the aircraft weren't within easy reach of an obvious target, perhaps it were just cruising over the central plains, or it was on a bearing that didn't carry it close to anything that would make sense for a suicide run, it would be very likely that the incident commander (whoever that might be, more on that in a second) would adopt a wait-and-see approach. There's little point to shooting now if there's not much damage it could do in the next few hours, and the situation might resolve in a positive way given some time and continued efforts.

      An order to fire on the aircraft would have to come from very high up. I'm not exactly sure how high, but by that point it's very likely that POTUS is in the loop (if he decides to show up to the briefing). I think it's improbable that they would fire on the aircraft unless it were heading toward an obvious target like Washington D.C. but I suppose any major metro area might present a high enough risk of mass casualties on the ground to warrant shooting it down.

      The goal would probably be to time the shot such that the aircraft was likely to come down in a very sparsely populated area, or the least densely populated area between where it is currently, anyway, and what they think the target might be. If the aircraft is fairly low, a shot is likely to bring it down in pretty short order and provide some degree of control over where it lands. If it's at cruising altitude, a lot can happen in the ~10 miles before it hits the ground, and it's a bigger risk to shoot it down.

      What they used to shoot it down would depend on the fighters that were on scene. Certainly not the cannon, there's too much risk of collateral damage from stray bullets, and it could leave the aircraft mostly flyable even after multiple hits. The most likely option would probably be the AIM-9 sidewinder, as the fighters will be in close proximity and the AIM-9 is a visual range missile commonly carried by US fighters. They might also use something like the AIM-120 AMRAAM, but I'm not sure how those are/could be employed in close proximity. If the decision to bring the aircraft down were made I think it is probable, especially with a relatively large aircraft, that they would fire more than one to ensure it comes down quickly, in the area they want it to, but that's purely conjecture on my part.

      What wouldn't happen, with virtual certainty, would be the employment of surface based missiles to shoot down the aircraft. Besides the more limited ability to engage in a very precise time and place, surface to air missiles present bigger risk of collateral damage in other ways (are you sure that target is the correct aircraft in a crowded airspace?). They also are just not widely maintained in an active combat-ready status when they aren't forward deployed, with the possible exception of areas like D.C. that have very sensitive targets. Even then, I don't believe SAMs are considered to be anything remotely close to a first-line defense.
