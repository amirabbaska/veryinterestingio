• FrankDrakman

    Where to get your best return on marketing dollars changes over time. At the beginning, mass advertising and promotion to build brand awareness are key, but as you develop a brand image, targeted sampling can be more effective. If you are in a niche market, it's different again.

    I was data analyst for a promotional products firm. We mailed 1,000's of samples each day of pens, key chains, and pocket calendars, and followed up with telemarketers. If they closed 2% of their accounts each day, they were a star. So we'd easily spend $200 for each new customer in samples cost alone, but our data showed our customer retention rates were great, and that our referral rate was too, so that each new customer represented multiple sales. The model is still working, AFAIK.

    Yes to all of this, but I'd like to piggyback one extra point. Companies will also be using a loss-leader pricing strategy, where they take losses on one product to promote sales of another. It's like when Old Navy has their $1 shirt sale. They'll heavily discount products to the point of giving stuff away so that customers come into their store and get their almost free shirt and maybe browse the store. If they can get about a third of customers who buy the $1 shirt to also add a couple pricier shirts to their cart ON is all of a sudden experiencing a jump in sales, and maybe even a spike in profits.
