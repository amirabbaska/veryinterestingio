    • Optrode

      Dopamine and serotonin are emphatically not hormones. They're neurotransmitters, which are by definition very different. A hormone is like a broadcast signal. It's usually meant to spread out and reach the whole body, even if not all of the body responds. A neurotransmitter, in contrast, is by definition released in much more tiny amounts and on a cell-by-cell basis, and is also usually mostly broken down or sucked back up within a few milliseconds or less. So neurotransmitter release is not a broadcast signal. It's more like a message from one neuron to one or more other neurons.

      As for your original question:

      Addiction is not that simple. The brain is not just a stew of chemicals with fluctuating levels, and emotions / feelings / states of mind are not produced by chemicals. Emotions, thoughts, and actions are complex phenomena, produced by complex patterns of activity in neuronal circuits. Exactly which neurotransmitters those circuits use to function is not really that important. It's about as important as "what kind of wires are used to connect two components" in a computer. So saying that someone has an addiction (including a behavioral addiction) because of dopamine levels is like saying that you're seeing pop-up ads on your computer screen because you have too much / too little monitor cable.

    • FjordTheNord

      Budding neuroscientist here! I might be able to point you in the right direction. Addiction has been found to be related to a circuit involving the nucleas accumbens, orbitofrontal cortext, and ventral tegmental area which has a lot of dopamine activity with inputs from the substantia nigra (which produces dopamine). These areas/circuits are active during enjoyable and addicting events (gambling, drugs, sex, etc.) The more they act, the better they get at their job (known as plasticity).

      If your brain experiences a lot of activity in a certain area, it will start to change. With certain drugs (as an easy example), like cocaine (which blocks the re-uptake of dopamine), more dopamine is around to activate the neurons. Eventually the brain responds to this by doing things such as down-regulating the receptors for dopamine. So now that amount of dopamine cannot result in the same level of activity (or pleasure/"high"/whatever). So you need more and more. Which as you can imagine can produce quite a problem!

      This is all super general, and if there's real neuroscientist out there reading this, please correct me (would love to keep learning!)
