      • wanna_be_doc

        Your blood pressure actually doesn’t even go up initially. First, because if you experienced an amputation, you’ve probably lost blood and so you’re short on blood volume. The heart will be pumping faster to keep your blood pressure elevated.

        However, even in the absence of blood loss, a healthy heart can rapidly change the speed at which it beats to maintain your blood pressure (much faster than the kidneys which will take several hours). Suppose you place a tight tourniquet on your arm right now and block the arteries in your arm. This will immediately cause the arteries proximal to the tourniquet to back up with blood all the way back to the aortic arch, carotid arteries, and the heart. You have baroreceptors in the aorta and carotid arteries- which are stretch receptors- and they’ll notice the increased pressure-stretch and send a signal to the brain which will send a parasympathetic signal to the heart telling it to beat more slowly (thus maintaining nearly the same blood pressure as before).

        Without a baroreceptor reflex and the heart’s ability to rapidly self-regulate blood pressure, modest changes to position could potentially cause severe problems elsewhere in the body. Imagine tourniqueting your leg and now having most of that blood shunted to elsewhere in your body. If the heart beat at the same rate, the pressures in your brain would suddenly become so large that you’d be at risk of a stroke. This obviously doesn’t happen in healthy people, because the body has multiple mechanisms to self-regulate.

        Now obviously, there are other reasons you may have slightly elevated blood pressure during an amputation...physical stress/trauma causing an increased sympathetic response, psychosocial stress, fluid shifts, etc...but as a whole, blood pressure is more or less maintained.
