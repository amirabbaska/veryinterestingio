      Easiest way for small circuits is to put a moveable shunt (called a delay line) somewhere and then just dial the shunt back and forth until everything is in sync. Don't know if that's what's used for high power systems though.

      Edit: also if you're trying to phase match two different generators you simply run one of them at a slightly lower frequency as the other until you're in phase, then speed it up again. Playing around with delay lines is only needed if you're combining two signals from the same source or from sources with a fixed frequency.

  • whitcwa

    To be clear, the rigid coax shown in the picture is not waveguide. The highest frequency used for TV broadcast in the US is around 700 MHz. Some station do use waveguide, but it is rectangular.

    There are expansion joints so distance isn't quite as critical as 1/10 mm. The thermal expansion of copper is much greater than that. Phase is only an issue if you are combining two transmitters which are running in parallel, and there is a knob to adjust the phase. The biggest problems I've run into with the rigid coax are the insulators arcing over, the dehydrator breaking, the hangers breaking, couplers (called bullets) overheating and real bullets being fired at the tower.
