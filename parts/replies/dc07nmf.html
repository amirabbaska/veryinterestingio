    • Icemasta

      Anecdotally, sleeping in a new place actually makes me sleep better; by which I mean I feel more rested after less sleep.

      I just want to point out that there are numerous studies on the "First-night effect", one such here, a more recent one here http://www.cell.com/current-biology/abstract/S0960-9822(16)30174-9. It's kind of a bad example considering that statistically, at least 90% of the people are affected by it, and it's a very studied part of sleep research, it's always taken into account for the first 2 days when analyzing the data, and has been for at least 50 years.

      While sleep and its effect is a topic that will be studied for at least another century, certain variables have been clearly identified as well. Stress and anxiety are two other well studied topics, and while everyone has a different threshold, the effects are generally fairly similar. There are other known factors, for instance, and this is generally critical in teenagers, people who do everything in their room. They eat in their room, do work/homework in their room, they play in their room, they watch movies in their room. The room become associated with stimuli, it does not become a place of relaxation, which leads to lower sleep quality; a fairly studied and understood effect.

      What I am trying to say is that there is indeed a lot of variables in play and not everyone is the same, but we also have a fairly good understanding of what works and what doesn't. Some people can go their entire life with no sleep trouble, but that is extremely rare. Most will inevitably get sleep quality issues, sometimes it's very simple, sometime there is a deeper issue, but ultimately, it comes down to the addition of various factors.
