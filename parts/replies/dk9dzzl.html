        And yes absolutely. Well... okay. A lot of things are polarization selective in optics. The first BBO is polarization selective. It only downconverts pump photons whose polarization is along its optical axis. But look... superpositions are only destroyed in the presence of an environment. In other words, if you make a bell-pair and let one photon get perfectly absorbed by a block of some other crystalline material, you now have a photon entangled with a phonon (lattice vibration) in the crystal. You observe decoherence when you measure a system while ignoring all the other things that the system is entangled with, the very definition of an open quantum system. These processes that lead to decoherence are very likely because an excitation in a quantum system will wanna go to everywhere there's a coupling, and in general it's hard to isolate a system completely. A traveling photon might get absorbed by a dust particle or something (maybe? idk. Again used to thinking of superconducting circuits) and now there's decoherence, because that dust particle is gonna float away.

        Okay so what sort of polarizer "destroys" coherence and what kind doesn't? One for which you can keep track of everything! So an absorptive polarizer is unacceptable... because you can't track the information of the photon once it's absorbed. A reflective (lossless) polarizer is fine because now one of the polarization modes is just moved into a different spatial mode and you can keep track it, do gates on it, measure it, whatever.
