    • tmntman

      I'm not an engineer. But what I do know about cars is that yes, much of the car is designed to "fail" and break before the limits of the human body. This is why modern cars seem to fair so much worse in accidents which older cars could have survived unscathed. It's not that those parts failed. They are engineered to break before you do so that they have an opportunity to absorb as much of the impact as possible.

      From what you describe, your accident is a perfect example of where being relaxed could make all the difference in the world. If you saw the car coming and had tensed up, when you struck the windshield all of that force would have been imparted onto whatever part of your body struck the windshield first. The windshield would have broken and absorbed some of the energy, but what it couldn't would have been applied to that one area of you body until the muscles, bones and other bodily components reached their breaking point. Then the remaining energy would continue on into your body until it hit more resistance. By being limp, that energy could travel through your body without having to break the first area to impact. And as more of your body hit the car, the force is spread out over a larger surface area and defused enough to allow you absorb all of that energy without actually breaking any major part of your body.

      The same principle is at work when you see athletes such as wrestlers who can take major impacts and still get up and walk away or even continue to perform. You just did on accisent what they have to train to accomplish.
