    • SpacemanSpears

      The entire notion that the collapse of Rome was the collapse of European culture is asinine. Part of a stagnant Mediterranean empire collapsed. It's impact was negligible to most of Europe. If anything, though, it liberated the former Roman vassal states to develop their own unique cultures. There are 51 distinct European nations. Compare that to East Asia which has been dominated by Chinese empires for 3000 years, you have 6 nations. It's clear evidence that there was massive cultural development in the period following the collapse of Rome.

      It's also worth noting the intellectual developments of the period. Hell, the Carolingian Renaissance predates the actual Renaissance by 600 years. On top of that, we also saw the introduction into Europe of crop rotation, the horse collar, the highback saddle, and the iron plow. They might not be exciting to modern museum-goers but their importance to agriculture, warfare, and political structure cannot be overstated. And we can't ignore the contributions the Vikings made to shipbuilding and commerce either.

      Without all those developments in the Early Middle Ages, Europe would have never been able to go on to develop/obtain gunpowder and the printing press which were necessary for the development of chemistry, the heat engine, and the Industrial Revolution.
