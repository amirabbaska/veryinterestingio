    • theSharkness

      Physically, 2 main ways I know of radiation hardening by process are Silicon-On-Insulator (SOI) and building an insulator guard band around each device on your die. SOI isn't as expensive, because some manufacturers are doing it anyways to shrink their process (AMD), but it isn't a perfect solution. Guard Bands are really expensive, because the guard band is wasted space.

      Logically, extensive use of error-correction (CRC, Hamming, etc) and TMR is used. TMR is very simple to visualize and implement, and adding complexity onto a system you're shooting into space on a rocket pulling 20Gs isn't a risk many people are willing to take.

  • monkeybreath

    I would like to add that another limitation is the communications channel. This is limited by the power available, the size of the antennas, the frequency used, and the error correction coding.

    Increasing the frequency used makes the antenna seem larger, so those are related. But higher frequencies don't make it through the atmosphere very well and would require orbiting satellites to receive the signals, so those antennas would be limited, whereas our current ground-based antennas are huge. So really, a frequency increase is not realistic. You also cannot make the antenna too big (i.e. the beam width too small) because it then becomes more difficult to point the beam at Earth.

    Error correction coding has improved considerably since the 70s, however the Voyagers were reconfigurable and used fairly sophisticated codes. At most they would get an increase of 3 dB or a doubling of data rate before hitting the Shannon limit of how much data a certain bandwidth can pass.

    The current data rate is 160 bps. Between the power, antenna size and error correction, I doubt you could get more than 3 orders of magnitude improvement, so about 160 kbps. While this is a lot in comparison, and there is a lot of time to send the data, it would still be easily swamped by the amount of data the sensors are capable of now.
