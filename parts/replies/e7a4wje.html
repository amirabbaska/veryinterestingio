        A good thing to understand is these switches, while we say they are binary, are not in teal life, it's more like a knob. One side is labeled ON and the other side is labeled labeled OFF. It is supposed to have two states, but it can be in the middle, in fact it's rarely ever exactly in the right spot. We look at it and just judge what side it's closer to. But like really switches you can turn it to ON, but it doesn't necessarily go to ON, sometimes it's springy or jammed up, sometimes you set it to ON over 20 minutes it changes to OFF. In fact this is common, to improve storage density we never use the knob that always works, we use the tiniest knob we can build, right at the end of our ability to use it. They are so bad that we design the system with maybe 10% extra knobs, and then write algorithms to deal with a few broken knobs and identify areas of bad knobs that just shouldn't be used.

        The other major source of error is honestly crashes. You're writing data out and maybe you are going through a file and changing all the 5s to 2s. In this format you can never had 5s and 2s, it's just not allowed and it is considered corrupted. But all 5s and all 2s are ok. To change between states you must temporarily create a corrupted file. What happens when the power goes out half way through? What happens when the program has bugs and crashes while making the update? We are pretty good at dealing with this, and most filesystem and databases write data such that you never crash in the middle state. But not all programs do that.
