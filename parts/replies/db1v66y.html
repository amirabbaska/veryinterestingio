    • notthemessiah

      Instead of trying to understand an electron in an atom, I think it helps to first look at how it behaves unbounded to an atom. Due to wave-particle duality, all matter exhibits wave-like properties (interference, propagation, etc). The lighter the particle, the more we can see observe wave-like behavior. The behavior of this wave is fully described by the Schrödinger wave equation

      Under the classical picture of an atom, you have a ball orbiting a nucleus. It is orbiting as if it were confined by the electric potential field (the nucleus is positively-charged, the electron negatively charged, so there's attraction). Just as a satellite orbiting the earth is negligable in mass compared to the earth, the electron is super-light compared to the nucleus, so we can simplify the force of the nucleus with a "potential field". You could imagine this potential field as if were a valley, and the electron rolling around within it.

      At this point, we bring back the wave nature of matter. Under the Schrödinger wave equation, the particle can be described by a "wavefunction", which is a function on space which happens to be a square root of the probability of finding it at that point? Why square root? Because the wavefunction itself is complex-valued (which has an imaginary and real component). One classical approximation of a point-like particle would be a Gaussian wavefunction (bell curve), with its velocity encoded by the complex value. This might be easier to visualize than it is to describe, so I recommend loading the "Harmonic oscillator" setup in this applet to see for yourself (requires Java): http://www.falstad.com/qm1d/ . Another thing to understand is that the reason it's "quantum" mechanics, is that the energies of stable configurations, "solutions" to the wave equation only exist at discrete energy levels. This results in the orbital configurations of the atom when you solve for the potential field of a nucleus in 3 dimensions. Another applet can help visualize these orbital configurations: http://www.falstad.com/qmatom/

      This Wikipedia article can also serve as a good intro to QM: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Introduction_to_quantum_mechanics
