      The human eye does not really take snapshots of our environment

      The central vision certainly does, and you can check it yourself. Such snapshots are integrated into a bigger picture, but not only are there snapshots, but their time of acquisition is very well defined and if you track the eye with a fundus tracker you can determine when they happen via post processing of the fundus image sequence.

      it ignores the information while the eye is moving and only once focused again does it start to use the information

      First of all, you must distinguish between the peripheral and central vision, since they act differently. Peripheral vision is always on. Central vision takes snapshots: there is no filtering, and no turning on-and-off. The start of the saccade enables image acquisition: it's like if you pressed the "enable" button. The subsequent cessation of retinal slip triggers the acquisition: it's as if you pressed a camera shutter release. The shutter subsequently closes and essentially won't retrigger until a next saccade starts or a timeout elapses.

      When the image is "focused" again the brain doesn't "start" to use the information: it takes a snapshot and promptly reverts to ignoring the information.
