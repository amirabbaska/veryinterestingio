    The thing that makes a black hole a black hole is the singularity, the event horizon is just a feature of the singularity. A singularity has no volume, and so it has infinite density. The event horizon is not the defining part of a black hole, something I don't think you quite understand, it's just a feature of all singularities.

    That said, there are more types of theorized collapsed stars between neutron star and black hole. The biggest evidence of this is that the smallest black holes have around 5 solar masses, whereas the largest known neutron stars have a mass of around 2.3... so between those numbers, it's possible to overcome neutron degeneracy pressure while still leaving a star comprised of even more elementary particles, such as quarks, whose degeneracy pressure might be higher than that of a neutron, leaving you with a quark star of between 3-5 solar masses.
