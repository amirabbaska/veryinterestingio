    The point about engineering work becoming very difficult has affected me as well.

    When I was working in union plants (as an engineering student), almost all of my projects had some sort of barrier related to a union. One project was developing local parts storage throughout the plant, the goal being to eliminate walk time for workers all the way to the main stores crib. When they needed parts they could go to a smaller local crib instead.

    I was out labeling the new cabinets - not stocking them, or moving parts - just applying labels to the cabinets. I got chewed out by the union head and "written up" for a misconduct. The next day I had to go out there, pull an operations worker away from his duties, give him a label maker and give step by step instructions on what to print and where to stick each label. It was absolutely humiliating for both of us. Me looking incapable of doing any kind of hands-on work, and him taking orders from a 20 year old.

    Another time at a different job, we were testing a new plastic type in a double-screw extruder before running it to production scale. In order to run the test, the perfectly capable head engineer and I needed to pull a union worker off his regular task to operate the machine. At one point the operator needed to go offload a pallet, and in the 5 minutes he was gone another union worker came by, saw us running the machine without a union worker, and filed a complaint.

    I work at a non-union plant now, and if I need to test something, I let the operators and specialists know what I plan to do, what the risks are and what to expect, what the goals are etc. and I go get the work done.

    The best part is that operators will happily help me out anyways without being instructed to, because there's no union body enforcing a particular job description on them. Workers that get involved and interested in the improvement projects learn more and better themselves, and often end up scaling the ranks more quickly. The company is now known for high internal promotion tendencies, and I'm confident that it's partly attributed to a lack of union vs non-union barrier.

    Example: Teachers Unions. In 2013, New York City (where I live) paid 30 million (30 million!) in taxpayer dollars to teachers who were deemed unfit to serve in the classroom. It's almost impossible to fire them. Uniformly, they get removed from the classroom but still collect their salary and benefits. In 2012 it was only 22 million. I have no idea what it is today. This is a recurrent problem.

    American education sucks. It does. Really. While far from the only reason, teachers today come from the bottom of the barrel. Their scores, on say, college admissions testing, are far below those of other successful applicants. Yet efforts to improve results are frequently met with ardent resistance by teachers unions. Recent efforts to improve pay to recruit higher quality teachers were met with resistance. For example, one state's endeavors to provide higher salaries to teachers resulted in furthering entrenched union interests. If any teachers were to be fired, it had to almost exclusively come from newer recruits. The obvious result is that entrenched, underperforming teachers retained their jobs, with a pay raise, against more qualified candidates.

    The same is true when it comes to measuring outcomes. Teachers unions routinely resist any initiatives to introduce such measures.

    In Michigan a few years ago, when the state took action against public sector unions, schools would bus students to participate in political protest measures instead of doing their jobs.

    Unions are out there for themselves. Union leadership is an entrenched interest. The US pays far more for the education of each student than other countries do yet has inferior results. Much of the funding for increased education exposure goes not to improve the quality of education but to line teachers own pockets.

    Politics: Unions are a racket.

    Unions in many industries are a political tools. Unions advocate for laws requiring industry to hire only union workers. Then they advocate for laws which require all employees to pay union dues. Then they send this money to leftist politicians who advance union interests. Regardless of political affiliation, employees are obligate members of a union and fund, through a portion of their paycheck, lobbying efforts which promote the interests of the union, rather than their own interests.
