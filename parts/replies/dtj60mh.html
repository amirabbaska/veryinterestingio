    I'm a sheep rancher and sheep definitely show little sign of disease or injury, whether they intentionally "hide" it or not. Sheep and cattle diseases are quite closely studied as they directly affect profitability.

    Those with casual or hobby exposure to the sheep industry often claim that sheep die readily with little provocation. However those who keep sheep know that what actually happens is that a sheep can be in incredibly poor shape before it's apparent that it has a problem. Then it suddenly expires.

    A good example is clostridial disease in sheep, which a sheep will usually show no symptoms of until hours before death. Upon opening up the animal the affected organs will often be already rotting or liquefied, the animal having been sick for quite some time.

      • evranch

        Fortunately most diseases that cause large losses of sheep are preventable by vaccine these days. A common vaccine is the 8-way which covers most lethal sheep and cattle diseases together. These diseases only show up when the vaccine fails to protect an animal.

        The unfortunate thing about sheep is that an individual is only worth about $200, which is less than a vet visit. So flock health tends to be holistic and preventative rather than individual and reactive, i.e. vaccines, dewormers, vitamins and minerals, good quality feed.

        Metabolic ills that could be considered similar to a diabetes-type syndrome are usually detected when a sheep cannot get up. At this point diagnosis and treatment again must occur within the hour or the sheep is doomed. However most can be saved with a good inventory of SQ electrolytes. Blood tests would work for vitamin and mineral status but it's considered more effective and easier just to supplement them with excess mineral.
