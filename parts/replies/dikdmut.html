    • fourcornerview

      Not stupid at all. The vapor pressure of water at 25C is just over 3 kPa. However, some of the molecules will have enough energy to spontaneously undergo a phase transition from liquid to gas. This is evaporation, and will always favor the direction of water to atmosphere, until the humidity is 100%, at which point equilibrium will occur and the rate of transfer will be the same in both directions.

      Your second question is great. Boiling is not necessarily a temperature thing. It also has to do with pressure. If you put liquid water under a vacuum, it will "boil" at room temperature. If you increase pressure, you have to increase temperature needed to boil. When the vapor pressure equals atmospheric pressure, the water molecules are able to undergo phase transition more easily and rapidly because those pressures are the same. So the water molecules can go to the gaseous state, which has a much greater volume than the liquid state. So bubbles form and the liquid boils as vaporization occurs.
