      • Qxzkjp

        There is also a procedure called "stochastic integration" where you just generate a bunch of random points, and then check if they are under the curve or not (so if you are integrating y=f(x), you check if y<=f(x), which is computationally fairly simple) and then the fraction of points that are under the curve is the integral. This is very inefficient in most cases however, and is mainly used in theoretical physics where you get 10- or 11-dimensional integrals, for which stochastic integration can be as good, or even better (!) than actually trying to compute the integral.
