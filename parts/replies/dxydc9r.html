  • Jainarayan

    ...and Hinduism all use large amounts of physical representation

    Yes, and there is a reason. In the Bhagavad Gita Sri Krishna says "For those whose minds are attached to the unmanifested, impersonal feature of the Supreme, advancement is very troublesome. To make progress in that discipline is always difficult for those who are embodied."

    The reasoning is that as mortals in a physical form, we are sensory beings. It's difficult for us to connect with something that is intangible or unmanifest. Hence the use of icons.

    It's not impossible to connect with an unmanifested God, but it's difficult, given that everything else we interact with is with physical form.

    Very interesting. Growing up Filipino and briefly studying pre-colonial beliefs, paganism was mainly the belief back then. It's a very nature-based belief wherein anything that's inherently connected to the earth is praised or respected, as a way of also respecting the god (called Bathala.)

    A lot of pre-colonial Filipino cultures involved venerating gods, especially those who are thought to control weather conditions and calamities, and the success of farming and harvest. Everything was basically tied to one's way of life.

    Fast forward to colonialism and spread of Catholicism via the Spanish occupation of 300+ years, some of the pre-colonial nuances merged with the new religion.

    From venerating trees and other stuff from nature to now, idols - statues and physical representations of God and saints, etc.
