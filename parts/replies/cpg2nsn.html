    • simplequark

      Can't speak for Switzerland, by my understanding of the German system is this:

      • Everybody is required to have health care. If you are very poor, the government covers your premium, otherwise you pay it yourself.
      • There are several public (i.e government sponsored) health insurance companies whose prices are slightly different, but not by very much. They are always a certain percentage of your income. They don't have much wiggle room in the pricing, since there are strict regulations about it. Public health insurance normally cannot refuse to take you, unless you have changed into private health insurance before (see below).
      • There are also private health insurers who have a bit more freedom with their pricing. Generally, they are cheaper than the public ones when you're young, and more expensive when you're old.
      • You can only opt for a private insurer if you earn more than a certain amount of money per month, and once you've switched, there's no easy way back to public health care. The only way would be to show proof that you are so broke you basically need welfare, in which case (among other things) you get reassigned to the public system.
      • An official catalogue lists the prices for every insured medical procedure, ranging from a simple consultation to a complex surgery. Public health insurers will pay doctors the base price, while private insurers will pay the base price * some multiplier.
      • When you're in the public insurance system, you hardly ever see any medical bills. The main exceptions are dental work and glasses/contact lenses where you usually have to pay at least part o f the cost (unless you're on welfare, in which case the government steps in again). At pharmacies, you also pay some part (up to 10 Euros, I think) of a medication's price. Your premiums are not affected by how much or how little you need to get medical care. (The idea is that it averages out in the long run – everyone has times when they are healthy and times when they are sick. If the pool of insured patients is big and diverse enough, there should be enough money available at any given time.)
      • Private patients will often get better service (i.e. nicer hospital rooms, shorter waiting time for doctor's appointments), but they usually will have to foot their medical bills themselves, first, and then send them to the insurance to be reimbursed. As a private patient, your insurance premiums may rise, if you have the insurance pay for a lot of medical bills.

      I've skipped over a lot of details, but AFAIK that's the basic design of the German system. (Somebody correct me, please, if I made mistakes.) It's not perfect by any means – "fixing health care" has been on the agenda of every government since the 1980s – but from my perspective as a lowly public health care patient, it works comparatively well. At least I never have to worry about not being able to afford necessary medical care.
