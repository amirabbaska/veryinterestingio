  • emilyst

    It depends on the time period.

    Aristotelian-influenced thought posited that the planets were contained in celestial spheres, fixed within them. The spheres were crystalline "quintessence," a primordial fifth element on par with earth, fire, air, or water, from which the universe was made.

    Later, the observations of Tycho Brahe led to Copernicus' positing a heliocentric model of the solar system, and Galileo's subsequent direct observations of celestial bodies reified this model as fundamental reality—physical objects impelled by an unknown force through seeming empty space. This was powerfully challenging to authorities at the time, both religious and scientific, as it required "occult" (hidden) forces to move the heavens. It was believed (again, inspired by Aristotelian notions) that nothing could move unless impelled—pushed—by some force mechanically.

    Descartes posited that space contained a kind of fluid, with the planets trapped in fluid vortices which pushed them. He had no evidence of this, but the idea became influential for a time.

    Newton made a new leap. In telling the story of the apple falling (which did not fall on his head, and may not have happened at all), he describes that he observed nothing mediate the force which pulled the apple from the tree to the Earth—and then he merely extrapolated this invisible force out to the heavens. That the force was invisible to him, able to act over distances with nothing between to mechanically transmit it, troubled him, but he proceeded to describe the force mathematically without an explanation. (Famously, he said, "I feign no hypotheses," about the nature of gravity.)

    The power of his mathematics—a full derivation of Kepler's laws and a nearly complete explanation of the motions of the planets which served for hundreds of years—allowed scientists to become comfortable with the idea of empty space.

    For more information about what ancient astronomers in the Western tradition thought was between the stars, one source is Ptolemy's Almagest.

    For a more complete accounting for the evolution of the ideas between Aristotelian thought and modern science, try The Invention of Science by David Wootten.
