    Even if air resistance were not a factor, the equation for kinetic energy is

    K=(mv^2 )/2

    Meaning that as the velocity of a mass doubles, the kinetic energy actually quadruples, because it's v^2. It takes four times as much energy to go twice as fast, while moving a twice heavy mass only takes twice as much energy.

    When a weightlifter lifts an olympic weight, they aren't judged by how fast they can lift it. They simply have to lift a heavier weight the same distance above their head even if they take more time to so it, meaning power lifting is seeing how much m can be increased. Sprinting, the focus is seeing how much we can increase the v portion of this equation. Even if we had the Olympics in a vacuum, we'd still see a similar discrepancy between improvements in power lifting vs improvements in sprinting speeds, but it wouldn't be so drastic as it is when air resistance is thrown in.
