    Possibly, though it seems unlikely the animal was a pure scavenger. It would certainly be comfortable eating scavenged remains, and would not face much competition once it arrived at a corpse, but tyrannosaurs as pure scavengers doesn't make much sense.

    There is fossil evidence of tyrannosaurs suffering and recovering from wounds inflicted by large herbivores. Scavengers don't suffer from such wounds- only predators.

    Its immense jaw size and strength is unnecessarily specialised for a scavenger. A scavenger doesn't need such an arsenal.

    An adult couldn't run, but they could certainly walk fast enough to keep up with or surprise large prey (elephants can manage a 30 km/hr walk easily- and silently, too). Tyrannosaurs also have larger legs for their body size than other theropods.

    The growth rates of tyrannosaurs, and the skeletal structure of adolescents, is unusual for a scavenger. Adolescents had fairly long legs, smaller jaws, and could run well. There are theories proposing that tyrannosaurs did engage in pack tactics- using younger members as runners to herd and harass prey, and adults as ambush predators actually performing the kill.
