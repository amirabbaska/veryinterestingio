    You're looking at it one layer too shallow. A signature doesn't prove that I'm me at all. Anybody could sign a reasonable approximation of my signature.

    What it does prove is that someone who is claiming to be me is standing in front of the register at the store. If it turns out not to be me, now there's another element of fraud. Not only are they accessing money they don't have a right to access, they're pretending to be someone who does have access. That seems like a subtle difference, but it it legally significant. For one thing, it's hard to argue that it was all some kind of mistake when you commit that kind of intentional fraud.

    Interestingly, this is mostly the function of signatures on legal documents too. It doesn't prove that I agreed to anything, but it does prove that someone claiming to be me was present and agreed to whatever. It prevents me from coming back later and trying to say that I didn't agree to the terms I signed to, but doesn't prevent a claim that someone else pretended to be me.

    In a legal context, you can add notarization for another layer of verification. That's when a trusted third party with some kind of certification from the state, a notary public, verifies everyone's identity and adds their seal to the document saying everyone is who they say they are.

    On cards, if you actually want to verify identity and agreement, you can use a PIN. In theory, no one else should be able to enter your PIN even if they somehow steal your card. The US hasn't transitioned to PIN and chip cards, due to concerns about complexity partially, but they probably should.
