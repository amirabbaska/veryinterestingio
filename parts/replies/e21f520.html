    • ThoughtRenegade

      I mean, I don't have like a scientific study or anything if that's what you're after.

      Anecdotally, I preferred high dynamic range before realizing I even wanted to make movies, and all of my friends that I can think of that loved movies did too except for the one that lived with his parents still. I'm the only film student. The rest of those people are just regular people who love movies.

      Another thing, it's pretty easy to decrease dynamic range using software. It's harder to do the reverse. (you still can, but you lose some information)

      So, if we did it that way and shipped Blu-rays with low dynamic range audio, the people looking for a more pure cinematic experience still won't be happy.

      It's the same thing with video compression. A highly compressed video is smaller and easier for a lot of people, but you can't go backwards from there and raise the quality. I don't want to buy a highly compressed Blu-ray. I bought a Blu-ray to get the highest quality possible.

      I think a happy medium would be apply audio dynamic range compression to online streaming video, like iTunes or Google play and whatnot, and leave Blu-rays with high dynamic range because the whole point of Blu-rays is the quality.
