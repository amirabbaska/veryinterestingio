      I think a better way of thinking about significant digits is to consider what the number actually means, not what you would round to get there.

      When a measurement is 5, that means it is 5 plus or minus 1. When the measurement is 5.0, it is 5.0 plus or minus 0.1.

      If you are calculating 5 x 5 = 30, you are saying it is 30 plus or minus 10.

      If you are calculating 5.0 x 5.0, you are saying it is 25 plus or minus 1.

      Example: Lets say that you need to time someone in the mile run. All you have with you is a clock that shows the hour and minute.

      The run starts at 4:30 and ends at 4:38. You know then that the mile time is 8 minutes plus or minus one minute. It would be incorrect to say it is 8 minutes and 0 seconds since you don't technically know that.

      Your friend is using his iPhone's stop watch to time the mile run. This goes to the hundredth of a second. He times the mile at 7 minutes and 34.12 seconds

      Now lets say that you want to know how many seconds it took for the mile run.

      For your measurement, you multiply 8 minutes x 60 seconds in a minute = 480 seconds. but this has 2 significant figures, not 1. This implies that the mile time is between 470 and 490 seconds, which is NOT correct (as seen by the iPhone measurement of 454.12 seconds). You would need to reduce to 1 significant figure, which is 500 seconds. This means that the mile time is 500 seconds plus or minus 100 seconds, which IS correct.
