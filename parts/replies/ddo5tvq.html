      Fuzzy on exactly how long killer t-cells are stored in your body for, I think it' close to a decade, but it's been a few years since I've even thought about that so I could be wrong.

      As far as the second part goes, I believe the answer has to do with gene mutation, the thing about killer t-cells is that they are designed to kill specific strains of antigens, (bacteria, viruses, etc...) but they can't adapt to different strains of the same antigen.

      Take the flu for example.

      Every year a new vaccine for the flu comes out, that's because every year the flu virus mutates and is different from before. The flu is a great example for this because of how good at and often it mutates. Every year scientists spitball at what they think the virus will do and then spit out a vaccine for it. Hopefully it works, but it might not.

      Every year your body encounters the flu, fights it, builds an "immunity" to it, and moves on. Theoretically after this point you are immune to the flu meaning it won't kill you or make you sick anymore, but you can still be a carrier for it with contact.

      Thus, every year you might come down with a bad case of the flu, but it is because it is a different strain of the same virus, not because you are just weak to it compared to something else.

      TL;DR killer t-cells are stored for a really long time before they are destroyed, and your body can succumb to the same antigen multiple times if it's a different strain, e.g. the flu.

      • inoculation_theory

        The relative risk of developing the disease for vaccinated people compared to unvaccinated people shows how effective the vaccine could be given ideal conditions and 100% vaccine persuasion; vaccine effectiveness measures how well a vaccine persuades when it is used in routine circumstances in the community.

        If the criteria changed, such as if it was based on a larger population that wasn’t as restricted and in a more natural environment, that would be the vaccines effectiveness. Because testing that is based on people who are taking the vaccination and people who aren’t, there is a risk for disease, and optimal treatment is needed for those who become too infected; too much persuasion. In other words being over-fooled by a vaccination exists, but sample size required is driven up to achieve clinically useful statistical power.
