    • functor7

      There's lots of easily understandable open problems, but these are usually deceptively hard. Fermat's Last Theorem is a good example, simple to understand, basically need a PhD in math to understand the proof. One popular problem of this type is Collatz Conjecture. Many a naive new /r/math subscribers try to answer it, to no avail. Give it a shot yourself if you want, you most definitely won't solve it but it would be a really good learning experience and that's really the whole point of having unsolved problems.

      Aside from this, there are more computationally intensive problems that will generally always be open. Like finding the next largest prime. You can use the GIMPS software to contribute your computer to the search, and if the next largest prime is verified using your computer you win a couple thousand bucks. Good luck, the last 5-or-so have been won by the same guy who converts his university library to a huge prime-searching-bank during off-hours. Prime Grid does a similar thing, but for a variety of different problems. These would probably be the easiest way to "contribute" to math, but if you want to learn math and do problems, pick up a textbook in a subject that's always interested you and have at it! A CS degree doesn't really cover enough math to have much of a vocabulary about it, but reading textbooks and just being actively curious about it can fix that. Who knows, maybe you'll think of your own problem and solve it, it's not unheard of (some standard statistics stuff was created and solved by someone working at a Guinness brewery).
