    • Pedogenic

      You are asking about lithification, the process by which sediment turns to sedimentary rock. Because wind-blown (eolian) sand grains tend to be very well rounded, they do not compact a whole lot (compared to clays, for example). The main variable of interest here is the cementing agent--which depends on groundwater chemistry and the position of the water table. If the water table is relatively high, for example in intermontane basins with artesian springs, the water table could be quite shallow (a few meters below the surface) and if the chemistry is right, carbonates or iron oxides might start cementing the grains together. If the water table is super deep (hundreds of meters or more), then the sand might stick around for a very long time and remain unconsolidated.

      • Anhydrite

        And even if it gets cemented it can be cemented to varying degrees depending on the concentration of carbonates or iron oxides in the water and length of time spent saturated. Last summer I was hiking through some fairly weakly lithified sandstone of the Milk River Formation (85Ma) that would crumble into fine sand very easily. The softness of this rock helped it form into hoodoos very quickly after the retreat of the Laurentide Ice Sheet compared to hoodoos in the American southwest which are harder sandstones and much older.
