      • fishify

        Your answer here is technically correct (saying you have to look at the whole system), but off the mark. Decay of nucleons via baryon-violating processes, such as occur in grand unified theories, would be occurring on such short distance scales that the nucleus in which the proton or stable neutron sits would be essentially irrelevant to the process, and, in addition, nuclear binding energies would never overcome the energy released in these processes. For example, one of the standard proposed routes for proton decay is into a positron and a neutral pion; there isn't a nucleus you can think of where this wouldn't be allowed. Likewise, if you had a neutron decay into a neutrino and a neutral pion, or an electron and positive pion.
