        Your responses are so odd that I think you are trolling, but on the off chance you aren't:

        Birds have two lungs and nine air sacs. Bird lungs don't collapse. Mammalian lungs are suspended open by a low pressure pleural space and can collapse with minimal trauma, including spontaneously. The collapse of a single lung can often be enough of an impediment to be fatal, dying in minutes rather than the seconds of a bilateral collapse. The change in volume of mammallian tidal respiration leads to so many challenges, and consequently so many potential failure points. Alveoli can become damaged from opening and closing (atelectrauma). Mammals are more likely to develop stress failure and pulmonary edema since their lung has no rigid support. They are more likely to develop pulmonary hemorrhage when exercising (seen in race horses). Mammals cannot separate their ventilation and gas exchange systems, while bird lungs allow for separating these functions.

        At altitude, the only way a mammal can get more oxygen is to hyperventilate, and it doesn't work that well. A bird doesn't need to hyperventilate in order to oxygenate.

        From a bioengineering standpoint, the avian lung is the elegant solution to a challenge. There are more species of birds, and they have greater geographical diversity (in part because of their ability to occupy high altitudes) than mammals. One could argue that it is therefore the more successful approach, although it's a bit silly to try to apply this to an entire class of animals, and evolution doesn't really rank success in terms of anything other than alive or dead.
