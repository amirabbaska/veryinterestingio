    Boy, this field is so vast, one can hardly cover all nuances. Just a couple of annotations:

    You pretty much sumed it up in the end yourself - only those in the position to be submitted to punishment can and will be punished. This is also in part due to a perceived "just cause" argument of the winning party, where anything potentially scandalous will be interpreted as military necessity. A direct consequence is that whistleblowers of obvious war crimes are prosecuted as defeatistic criminals "aiding the enemy" as opposed to upstanding citizens just doing the right thing. (see Bradley/Chelsea Manning as one modern example)

    If potential scandals do come to light, they will usually be outright denied or downplayed as criminal acts of individuals - even if those acts were/are a more or less obvious and coherent part of a waring party's official policy.

    You can go back in history and look at the Armenian Genocide in the Ottoman Empire during WW I, or the mass raping of German civilians by Soviet soldier, or Chinese/Korean citizens by the Japanese at the end of WW II - incidents which even in present day textbooks of their respective countries are being outright denied.

    But you don't even have to go that far back - basically any more or less recent military conflict by the US has not been deemed a war (which requires a declaration by Congress, which never happened after WW II). As a result, you can subcontract engagements on enemy soil without direct supervision and responsibilty of their outcome (e.g. Blackwater in Iraq), and treat enemy individuals as mere combatents without the same rights of protection as offical enemy soldiers/POWs (Guantanamo, Abu Ghraib) .

    Similar to this: Russian soldiers fighting undercover in the Ukraine can by definition not commit any war crimes, because the official Russian standpoint is that there simply are no official Russian soldiers in the Ukraine. As a result they can kill Ukrainian POW without much consequence, other than the expectation that, if they were captured themselves, they can pretty much expect the same. Which is pretty much the reason for rules in war in the first place:

    DO NOT DO WHAT YOU DON'T WANT TO SEE TO BE DONE UNTO YOURSELF. But without fear for consequence - oh well...

    One last note: The dearmification of the GermanReich after WW I was NOT a direct result of the German military breaking statutes of the Hague Conventions (which was done on mass, by all parties involved), but simply an attempt by the French to weaken Germany sufficiently to restore pre-1870 central European balance. We all know how well that played out.
