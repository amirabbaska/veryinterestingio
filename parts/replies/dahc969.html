    • EfPeEs

      You'd think so, but it does not always work out that way in practice.

      I live on an income sharing commune where labor and resources are distributed according to the principal "from each according to ability, and to each according to need." I don't have to worry about the bills getting paid because our worker-owned business is reasonably successful, there is always more than enough food in the pantry, and sharing resources like cars and housing allows us to have a smaller ecological footprint than what is typical of our geographic region.

      Decisions are made by consensus.

      We've agreed to a 42 hours/week labor quota, and there are a lot of things that get counted as labor that most people don't get paid for - cooking community meals, washing dishes, childcare, mediated conflict resolution, visiting the doctor, learning new skills, exercising, and pretty much everything else that's arguably beneficial to the community in some way.

      The problem arises when creating more efficient systems that increase productivity and reduce labor requirements result in a reduction in quality of life rather than an improvement. People mostly work in areas that interest them - by innovating to reduce the amount of labor required in those areas, people are forced to find something less desirable to work on to make the 42 hour quota.
