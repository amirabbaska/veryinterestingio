    • rocketsocks

      Not necessarily. In broad strokes that's true but there are also ways for salts to be sequestered away from the oceans. For example, when a large shallow body of salt water with no outflow evaporates over time it leaves the salt behind in "evaporite" deposits. The Great Salt Lake is a related example of a body of water with no outflow for salt. And salt flats are examples of evaporite deposits that are still at the surface. Under many common geologic conditions evaporite deposits can be covered by rock and buried underground. This is how you get structures that can be mined and why a substantial amount of salt comes from underground salt mines instead of salt farms that use evaporation of sea water.
