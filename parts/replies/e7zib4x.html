    • angrymonkey

      The general answer is "computation". Computation is the process of turning patterns into other patterns. In the case of your mind, you can think of this as the process of turning the patterns of your sensory input-- what you see, hear, taste, smell, and so on, right now and at every point in your life up until now-- into the patterns of how you act.

      Your thoughts are intermediate patterns that connect these things. Ruminations on what's happened to you, what it means, what matters to you, what might happen next, and ultimately what you will do about it. New patterns being extracted from the patterns of your experience, which in turn guide the patterns that are your behavior.

      Now for neurons. What is a neuron? Basically it's a cell that can fire a little electrical pulse-- or not-- based on whether it's currently being stimulated by another pulse. This encompasses what a transistor can do-- basically pass or block a bit of information based on another bit of information. A tiny, microscopic "if/then" statement.

      It turns out that it's possible to do any computation-- any conversion of one pattern into another-- with networks of transistors plugged into each other. Even more powerfully, there are some networks that are mathematically equivalent to a kind of information-processing device called a Turing Machine. Turing Machines-- and the networks that implement them-- are single devices which are in principle capable of doing every possible computation, if given appropriate input. Your computer or phone are examples of Turing Machines made out of electrical transistors.

      Your brain is almost certainly another kind of Turing Machine, one made out of networks of neurons, which are at least as good as transistors at processing information-- turning patterns into other patterns. Your brain is doing some absurdly complicated pattern finding, developing complex and nuanced reactions to what's happening to you; those reactions and internal brain-patterns being your thoughts, emotions, and intuitions.

      Exactly what computations the brain is doing we don't yet know. But we are starting to untangle all the neural connections in the brain, and meanwhile we are using computers to simulate more and more advanced AIs that can extract more and more nuanced patterns on their own. Someday in the future we will no doubt develop a good model of precisely how the brain does what it does.
