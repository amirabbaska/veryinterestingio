      What he is referring to would actually be 2/2 or "cut time" where each written note would get half of the marked value. Regardless of whether the conductor actually individually enunciates each beat in a measure, the time signature of the music is unchanged. So 4/4 means there are 4 beats per measure (this is X) and the quarter note gets the beat (this is Y). In something like 6/8 there are 6 beats beats per measure ant the eighth note gets the beat. Similarly 2/4 means that there are two beats per measure and the quarter note gets the beat. Just because there are 4 quarter notes in a measure, that doesn't mean that every measure consists of 4 notes. A 4/4 measure can consist of any combination of rhythms you want as long as the total adds up to 4 quarter notes. An example would be a 4/4 measure can have 8 eight notes, or 16 sixteenth notes. So a combination of all those parts could be 4 sixteenth notes, followed by 2 eighth notes followed by 2 quarter notes: 4/16 + 2/8 + 2/4 = 1 or one measure.
