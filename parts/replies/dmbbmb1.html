  • VoiceOfRealson

    It depends a lot on context.

    "Medical history" is mostly relevant in order to estimate your risk of developing conditions that are known or suspected to have a hereditary element.

    In that context, medical history becomes irrelevant for a condition when the estimated risk you have for that condition based on the medical history of your ancestors is similar to the estimated risk for the general population.

    I the condition is linked to a dominant gene, then only your parents medical history should matter, but then maybe they didn't live long enough for the condition to develop or maybe the gene is not always expressed even though it is supposedly dominant.

    For recessive genes, where you supposedly need the gene from both sides, you would generally assume that any ancestor having the condition would have given it to all of his/her children.

    Going from the closest direct ancestor who had the condition, your estimated probability of carrying the gene from that ancestor would then be halved for each generation. Once that number is similar to the probability of carrying the gene in the general population, your medical history on that condition becomes irrelevant.

    So for relatively common hereditary diseases, medical history becomes irrelevant after a few generations with no occurrences.

    For very rare recessive conditions it takes longer.
