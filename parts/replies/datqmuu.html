    It's interesting to look at it under that theory, but I think the theory doesn't really represent what's actually happening.

    It's less that people are aware of and following the rules you mention and more that there is a set of natural laws:

    1. Validating conflicts to an entity's claims will piss that entity off.
    2. If you do things that piss off an entity, that entity will be less likely to work with you.
    3. Your well being is aided by the size and number of entities that collaborate with you.

    What that translates to is that most countries will, no matter what, tend to agree with majority opinions or very powerful entities (e.g. the US) about who is a state. In some cases (e.g. the cold war) this means that it may be advantageous for these conflicting ideas to coexist because different entities are under the allegiance or influence of different factors. Neither is following some global law; instead they're following the lead of those they need to impress or not piss off which is necessary to their own well being. This is an important distinction because it more clearly recognizes how there isn't a right side and how the rules can rapidly and arbitrarily change. Changes in a large entity's stances (i.e. Nixon) will naturally cause smaller entities to follow that change in their own interest even if it doesn't make sense.

    While in 1970 it was pretty absurd to claim that Taiwan governed mainland China, it's equally absurd today to claim that mainland China governs Taiwan. By the rules you mention, Taiwan has long not been a part of mainland China's permanent population or its effective borders. Taiwan has proved a powerful, independent trade entity. Additionally, it has (sometimes formally, sometimes not) been recognized in a variety of ways. So, under the rationality of the rules you provided, it makes as much sense to recognize an independent Taiwan today as it did to recognize an independent mainland China in 1970. Mainland China's claims to Taiwan are largely invalid for that reason. At the same time, we know that's largely because, as polite as we chose our public words, the Taiwan Relations Act's existence shows in practice that we essentially recognize and defend the existence of Taiwan.

    This isn't the story of the US following some global rule about how to classify Taiwan, it's the story of the US trying to appease China by not actively working against its claims. In a way, the story of Taiwan explains both why the US needs to and why it doesn't need to follow these rules. Its unilateral redefinition of China way back when was possible because the current global norms were outweighed by the proposition of appeasing communist China. The fact that the US is such a global leader who has so much weight in determining recognition and engagement in trade by the states of the world allowed it to redefine back then just as it can today. The challenge to the US here isn't the backlash of the world itself based on some global code, it's that it ends the strategy of appeasement toward China and impacts Chinese-American relations in a way that stops diffusing a conflict in their military commitments.
