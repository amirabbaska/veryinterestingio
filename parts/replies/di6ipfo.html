    There should also be differences in the chemical composition.

    • kodack10

      It really depends on the intended use but you're correct comparing water stains to grease. Water stains are caused by mineral deposits and they can be easily cleaned with any weak acid like vinegar or citric acid powder. But for grease you want a strong base like bleach which reacts with the glycerides and fats in the grease and through saponification turns them into a soapy residue that washes easily.

      Bleach reacting with the oil in your skin and forming soap is why it feels slippery when you get bleach on your fingers.

      • try_harder_later

        Grease cleaners are alkaline to chemically break the grease down, but it does not work particularly well against water scale (carbonates).
        Water stain/scale is easily dissolved by acids (carbonate + acid -> water + carbon dioxide).
        Btw, if salts (the water stains) were really so easy to dissolve, they couldn't form in the first place!

    • kodack10

      The main thing to be concerned about is to be careful using bleach based products to clean a toilet, and especially not to mix bleach and ammonia together.

      Hydroxides react with ammonia and one of the substances created is gaseous chlorine gas which is deadly. The ammonia doesn't need to be a cleaning product like windex either. Sometimes there is enough ammonia from urine on a toilet to produce some. Ventilation is always suggested.

      Personally I clean hard water stains with vinegar or powdered citric acid scrubbed into a paste and left to sit a few hours. I also use alcohol cleaners like lysol. I only use bleach in the kitchen and then only in low concentration to get rid of grease.
