• GhostReddit

    High frequencies are damaging (ionizing) because individual photons have the ability to disrupt chemical bonds by energizing electrons. It has nothing to do with penetration.

    An electron requires a certain amount of energy in a single particle to raise its energy level. This threshold is what makes radiation ionizing. For example if you stand in front of an extremely bright red light you'll never get a sunburn because even though there's a lot of energy hitting you, the individual photons have very low energies. A dim UV light will do more damage.

    • Logic512

      To a certain degree, yes. Introductory physics course teach that when atoms transitions from one shell (S, P,D,F,...) to another shell it requires an exact amount (quanta) of energy to do so. Further along in physics education you learn than these transitions are blurred by all kinds of effects and so it’s not an exact quanta of energy.

      That said, it’s possible for an atom to momentarily absorb a higher energy photon than its transition requires and then allow the remaining energy to scatter as a lower energy photon (with a ‘phase shift’ but I digress). This means thinking about photons as discrete particles is inefficient for explaining why high and low frequencies pass through matter.
