    • Drachefly

      Your assumption is correct.

      But, the density of stars in the arms is not much higher than between them. What's different is the density of star formation. The brightest stars are much brighter than the others, and they do not last long, so star formation regions are much brighter than the others. So, we don't need to see every dippy little star to identify the shape of the galaxy - we need to just see and locate the brightest stars, and the nebulas.

      Our star has alternately been in arms and between arms several times since it was formed.

  • wolf_on_the_fold

    Imagine if you were in a house, stuck in one room. Now, you want to know what you're house looks like. How might you do that?

    You can look out the window and see other houses. They're all different, but can be categorized. Brick, Cape Cod, Ranch. It's a good bet that your house looks like one of these houses.

    You can look around the room. Plaster walls. All the ranch houses on your block have drywall, so your house probably isn't a ranch.

    Then one day somebody hands you some infrared goggles. Suddenly you can see all through the house. The other side of the house is a little fuzzy to make out, but you can see all sorts of things. 3 floors, radiator heating system, enclosed porch. There's even a brick chimney running up the center of the house.

    Aha. You live in a brick house. It probably looks like that other brick house down the street, except you know yours has a chimney running up the middle. You can show everyone else in the room a picture of the brick house down the street, and ours looks just like this except we have a chimney running up the middle.
