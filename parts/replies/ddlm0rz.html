      Adding on to what I said about typing Chinese, transcribing spoken Chinese for any dialect is tricky! For example, in Mandarin, the most popular way to romanize it is pinyin, but there are other systems like zhuyin that manage to do it with non-Latin letters. Since inputting Chinese characters is so dominating by pronunciation-based entry, you can often just type pinyin in a lot of Chinese search engines and apps and you will find them anyway.

      Another layer of complexity is what other threads have touched on: Chinese characters are used in a lot of different regions with different dialects as well as different countries. In a sense, the written Chinese language has resemblance to Norwegian Bokmål or Hochdeutsch, since many Germanic languages bear deep regional varieties and yet have a standardized written form. Like different Germanic dialects, Chinese dialects differ in possible consonants, tones, and a bunch of other phonological features. Certain dialects are so different that their mutual intelligibility is very low. For instance, I speak Mandarin, and can only understand 10% of Shanghainese if I strain really hard.

      Due to historical word-borrowing, many Chinese phrases have similar pronunciations between different dialects and languages. Take the phrase 簡單 ("simple"). It is composed of the two characters 簡 ("terse") and 單 ("single").

      • In Mandarin, it is pronounced jiǎndān (the accent marks denote tone)
      • In Cantonese, it is pronounced gaan2 daan1 (the numbers denote tone)
      • In Japanese, it is pronounced かんたん (kantan)
      • In Korean, it is pronounced 간단 (gandan)

      Sometimes the same character can be written in several different ways! The character I mentioned before, 單 ("single"), has a few other variants if you look at the database page it links to, under the variants section: 单 and 単. These variants either come from different historic styles of scripts, or a modern simplification process done by the PRC government. The first kind has some similarity to English words that were borrowed twice, e.g. jail and gaol are the same word, but the first one came through Old French jaiole and the second came through Anglo-Norman French gaole. The second kind is similar to American English spelling reforms: for example, thru is the simplified spelling of through, and both are accepted to mean the same thing.

    • TWK128

      Not "less." There is almost no correlation between the appearance and the pronunciation.

      In some cases, the appearance of a given element has something to do with the pronunciation, in other cases, the same element in the same position does not.

      The written and spoken languages are, effectively, two separate languages, with pinyin the only "bridge" between them, as it indicates the established standard pronunciation of the given written character.

      • kourge

        On a theoretical basis there is no correlation, but over 90% of Chinese characters are phono-semantic compounds. So I can infer that 清 is pronounced qīng and 晴 is pronounced qíng with some level of accuracy (the tone may be wrong) simply because the right-hand side of both of those characters are 青 (qīng). Sure, if you try to further decompose 青 itself, there is no information on the pronunciation, but knowing how to pronounce Chinese characters is not a process that involves memorizing flat lists.

        To extend your analogy, the written and spoken language may be two separate languages with pinyin being the only "bridge", but there is a similar bridge between simpler characters and more complex characters. Just as more complex characters are composed of simpler characters, most of the time a character's pronunciation can be easily guessed from one of its parts, hence the folk wisdom youbian dubian. This principle works in varying capacity in other languages that extensively borrowed Chinese vocabulary. For example, I was asked the pronunciation of the character 鏃 in Japanese. I didn't know off the top of my head, but I knew 家族 ("family") is pronounced かぞく (kazoku), so my guess of そく (soku) was very close.

      • ilaeriu

        It definitely is not a purely phonetic system, but most linguists agree that written Chinese has some degree of correlation between appearance and pronunciation. It is not a purely pictographic writing medium, which is what a system would be if there is no correlation between appearance and pronunciation. I think something like 80% of characters contain a phonetic component, so at the very least the pronunciation is hinted at in the character.
