      They tend to just know a small handful of non Japanese people casually, and know what those people have told them about themselves. This in an international, semi tourist environment, so the collection of people they know might be like--one African-American dude from New Orleans, a white guy from Mississippi, two Harvard grads, and a trucker from Michigan. All of these friendships blossoming through the veil of about ten percent functional English.

      So, when these Japanese people meet me ( I spoke Japanese well enough to go to college in Japan and get a normal job ), they're like, "Dude--what's up with your culture? I hear you eat crawfish, but the dude from Michigan said he'd never had them. What's a crawfish?" and so on.

      Honestly, I think Japan--and every other country--is the same way: lots of people, lots of variations in culture. But, in America at least, being able to say, "I'm half Puerto Rican, quarter Filipino--and quarter Irish," is an important part of explaining culture.
