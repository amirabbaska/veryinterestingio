  • Themagard

    Microbiologist here, and yup. What you said. Also bacteria, and as another commenter mentioned, parasites. A lot of pathogens actually have genes, proteins, toxins, etc that become "activated" by mosquitos, flea, etc saliva or through the temperature changes between living in an insect to living in a mammal. Fun fact: Yersinia pestis, i.e. the bacteria that killed most of Europe during the Middle Ages (The Great Plague, Black Death, etc) does this super well. It actually changes the structure of sugars on its cell surface when it hits human body temp (37C) to cause disease in humans, but not the flea. It also senses the temperature change to adapt its metabolic pathways to survive in different hosts. Fleas have diets rich in proteins, so the bacteria eats proteins when it's in the flea. Humans have diets rich in fats and carbs, so it eats fats and carbs when it's in the human. TL;DR some pathogens are really good at adapting to different hosts to survive multiple environments, be transmitted between hosts, and keep the disease cycle going; while other pathogens just like one host so they don't adapt to be carried by mosquito AND human
