    As I understand it, the engine angle is dictated by the need for the thrust to go through the centre of mass for spaceflight, otherwise there'd be a turning moment. The ideal angle for the intake is different for the reasons you discussed. Hence the bend. Although the cowling curves down, from the drawings I've seen the nozzles point straight backwards.

    This is rarely seen in normal aircraft because they don't require the thrust to be aligned with the CoM because the aerodynamic forces from the wings and tail can counteract any misaligned thrust.

      • dcw259

        Which is why rockets go straight up to get orbit, the atmosphere is a speed killer.

        To expand on this: They go straight up at first, because they start vertically. A vertical start needs less structural reeinforcement due to lower aerodynamical forces - way less lift and shear forces than a plane. The whole design of a vertical take-off launcher is a lot more mass efficient than a spaceplane that has to rely on atmospheric lift.

        Going horizontal as soon as possible is still the most effective way to get into orbit though every second you burn vertically, you lose 9.81m/s of delta-v, because of gravity (for 60° 8.5m/s, for 30° 5m/s and so on)
