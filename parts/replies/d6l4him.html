    • Sungolf

      Taking a "picture" involves bouncing a photon (or any other particle) off of the electron in question. The direction and energy with which the photon for bounced off (as detected) tells you where and how fast the electron (inside the atom) was going.

      Unfortunately if you want any precision with position, you need to use a photon with a high energy (therefore small wavelength that isn't spread out over a uselessly large space) which, on interaction, will give the electron enough energy that it will leave the atom.

      You can't have an Atom with an electron missing.... That's called an ion. Therefore you've "destroyed" the Atom.

    • sveinb

      It's a fundamental property of quantum mechanics. You can't know the position and momentum of the electron at the same time. Measuring the position (which is what it means to take a picture in this case) means that you will have to destroy any information that might exist concerning the electron's momentum. That means, among other things, that it can't remain in the same orbital, because if it did, you would know its momentum to a quite narrow range.
