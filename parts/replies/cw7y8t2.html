  • drunkenknight9

    It's all about reference frames. For an observer outside the event horizon it appears to take an infinite amount of time for things inside to reach the center. However, for an observer inside the event horizon it takes a negligible amount of time. Once something crosses the event horizon it simply accelerates toward the singularity presumably in the same way anything accelerates toward an object of greater mass. Since these masses tend to be colossal at the singularity of a black hole, the inward velocity would likely accelerate to a reasonable percentage of the speed of light (unless it is already light of course...). Of course, it doesn't help that we can't see information once it crosses the event horizon, either, making all of this speculation based on the models we can predict. The presence of black holes in general seems to violate much of what we know about physics so it is possible there are other effects we have never predicted or thought about that affect what really goes on inside the event horizon.

    As for the idea of "time-like lines becoming space-like" there is certainly something interesting about the idea and it isn't all that farfetched. Under the present model, time is expected to be linear in at least some local reference frame. There is an interesting theory about the arrow of time that suggests the expansion of the universe causes us to experience time in its current direction and that if the universe were to contract instead we would experience it in the opposite direction but even this construing is linear. If a black hole were treated as a microcosmic version (feels weird to suggest) of the universe expanding and contracting one could suggest the theory that time may run in reverse inside the contracting region of spacetime that a black hole is affecting.
