    • ANGLVD3TH

      It varies by person too. This is the heart of the dress debate. The actual pixels where very light blue and gold. So people that didn't automatically adjust for the washout from the camera flash thought there was some soft blue light tinting the white. Those that could adjust for the camera flash could see the true colors. In this case, seeing in more absolute colors actually resulted in the wrong outcome, it took context cues and comparison to get the right answer.

    In that sense, we all have "perfect pitch" vision

    Do we? In my mind perfect pitch for vision would be being able to give the RGB code of arbitrary colors, and I'm not sure how good humans are at that in general (even sticking to the relatively simple problem of colors on a screen - colors in the real world are more tricky). We can probably (almost) all reliably recognize pure red, white or black, but how about various shades of fuchsia or brown or grey?

    (edit) more discussion here: http://www.mathpages.com/home/kmath578/kmath578.htm

    Conclusion: yeah okay we probably do have something like perfect pitch for colors, and knowing the right rgb codes is not a very good analogy.
