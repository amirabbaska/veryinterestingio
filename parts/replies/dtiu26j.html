    • e-wing

      Cats can also contract a strain of the Coronavirus that often will give them flu-like symptoms. Runny nose, sneezing, respiratory infection, etc. Most cats get over it, but some, usually kittens under a year old, can't stop the spread of the infection, and will die from it. Cause of death is usually respiratory or heart failure from acute peritonitis when the infection spreads to their body wall linings. Basically, their body cavity fills with fluid which causes heart failure, liver failure, and eventually respiratory failure. It's called Feline Infectious Peritonitis, and it's fatal 100% of the time. I had a beautiful little kitten who died from it...he made it almost to his first birthday. It's completely devastating to watch...his health declined so sharply over a period of two or three weeks, I could hardly believe it. I took him in because I thought he had a bit of a respiratory infection, and they basically just very coldly and flatly told me he'd be dead in a month and I should just put him down now. So crazy that a tiny, usually harmless virus can cause such destruction.

      The answer to that question is still in doubt. From Wikipedia:

      "Researchers from the University of the Mediterranean in Marseille, France have found tenuous evidence that suggest a virus common to peppers, the Pepper Mild Mottle Virus (PMMoV) may have moved on to infect humans. This is a very rare and highly unlikely event as, to enter a cell and replicate, a virus must "bind to a receptor on its surface, and a plant virus would be highly unlikely to recognize a receptor on a human cell. One possibility is that the virus does not infect human cells directly. Instead, the naked viral RNA may alter the function of the cells through a mechanism similar to RNA interference, in which the presence of certain RNA sequences can turn genes on and off," according to Virologist Robert Garry from the Tulane University in New Orleans, Louisiana".
