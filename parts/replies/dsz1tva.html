    Sound will also curve away or towards the ground due to a temperature gradient since the speed of sound is dependent air density. This can cause sound shadows where the receiver cannot hear a sound despite being "close" to the source.

    Also happens in water. Sound will curve up toward less dense water, and will curve down down toward colder water; this is called a "divergent zone". A skilled sub crew can sneak up close to an enemy sub by staying in the gap between the two curves.

    If you're above a thermocline, sound will curve down, reflect off the interface, go back up, then arc back down, reflect, etc. It sounds like the source is nearby, but it can be several divergent zones away. Takes a skilled sonar operator to figure out the true range.

      I'm not quite sure how to answer that question in the context of the original question.

      Regarding the general question the answer is yes, through psychoacoustics it is incredibly common to have a sound appear to come from somewhere it isn't. Just listen to any well mixed stereo piece of music and notice how different instruments appear in specific places (we call this "imaging").

      In terms of temperature effects, I'm not sure how the perceived direction and distance of sound is perceived with a gradient, but the volume effects are well known. Say you have an acoustic barrier outside between your house and a highway, its efficacy is going to get worse if the sound can bend back down over the barrier.
