However, combined with a more acidic modern diet, it creates a growth advantage for acidophiles like strep mutans, which promotes dental caries?

    • curiouswastaken

      The key difference is that in a Petri dish this is true, but in vivo the bacteria produce a biofilm. A biofilm dramatically reduces the effectiveness of topical applications of an antibiotic. Some studies suggest you would need a mouthwash that was between 100x and 500x the concentration (which in many cases is impossible) to be effective. Mouthwash is largely ineffective without first physically removing the biofilm -- brushing and flossing-- to better expose the bacteria so the mouthwash can reach.

      As far as creating a more acidic environment, there is a huge variation of ph levels in different mouthwashes, but let's say for the sake of argument that this is the case. Let's take soda as an example. Coca-Cola is around pH 2-2.5. When you drink it, it only alters the pH in your mouth for a few minutes after a swig -- not even long enough for one division cycle of bacteria.

      Strep mutans makes its own acidic environment by metabolising carbohydrates over longer periods. Mouthwash as an acidic contribution would be negligible.
