    (From the perspective of a Psychiatrist) It's also worth noting that the diagnosis of "PTSD" is a DSM-5 construct, which our field built to have a sort of "common language" for research purposes, and for communicating our impressions of patients to other clinicians. If we say a patient "has PTSD," we mean essentially that they've suffered a life-threatening or life-altering trauma and have developed symptoms X, Y, and Z in response to it.

    It may not be as useful to say that an animal "has PTSD" or doesn't, because even humans can have maladaptive responses to stressors without being diagnosed with PTSD (Psychiatrists may call this "Adjustment Disorder" or in some cases, may say it doesn't meet criteria for any diagnosis whatsoever).

    Furthermore, it's difficult to translate that to non-human animals because a lot of symptoms of PTSD manifest - as best as current research indicates - because parts of the brain involved in fear and memory essentially "overreact" to the trauma, and non-human animals have both distinct neural anatomies as well as neural mechanisms for recording and recalling traumatic experiences. Humans may record traumatic experiences in exquisite detail, while non-human animals may more simply record traumatic experiences as "that was bad, don't do that in future."

    I can offer a couple thoughts, though these are merely personal conjectures and the methods to test them scientifically may present both methodological and ethical quandaries:

    a) I consider it fairly likely that even early humans likely experienced symptoms that today we would call PTSD. At the very least, I don't think civilization, society, and human evolution would have significantly altered the process by which we believe modern humans develop PTSD.

    b) I think there's at least some evidence that non-human animals experience symptoms potentially on the spectrum of PTSD or other stress-reactions. Consider for example abused dogs and cats, which in many cases become extremely distressed or agitated in the presence of humans; this may reflect what humans experience as either "flashbacks" or "hypervigilance."

    These are just some thoughts on the matter, and sadly I think that's about all I can offer at this point. I am very fascinated by non-human Psychiatry and I think the question on non-human PTSD is a fantastic one. But as accurately pointed out, it would be difficult to translate to non-human animals, and testing for such would both present its own difficulties as well as perhaps it's own ethical dilemmas.
