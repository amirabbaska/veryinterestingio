        Starts around 30:00 here.

        Again, to put it in context: if you are a weaponeer, you care about how high and how far the blast pressure from the bomb goes. It's how you judge whether you can destroy something and with what accuracy/yield you need to destroy it. There are standard ways to know how pressure waves scale as a result of yield and height of burst of the bomb (the aforementioned cube-root scaling). What they're saying here is, if there's a rainstorm over the target, it will dampen (absorb) some of the blast pressure, and your peak pressure will be reduced by 15%.

        So if we set off a 1 Mt bomb on the surface, under "ideal" conditions we'd expect that there would be about 20 pounds per square inch (psi) of blast pressure some 1.35 miles from ground zero. What they're saying here is, if there's a rainstorm, you're actually going to experience something like 17 psi at that range. It says nothing about how the rainstorm behaved other than as a dampener. Presumably absorbing that blast pressure had some effect on it, but they don't care about that (they only care about the reduction in blast damage).
