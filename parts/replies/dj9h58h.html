  • base736

    The problem is that these coordinate systems are difficult to define precisely. To define "coreward" as anything much more than "you know, generally over that way", you'll need a specific object that coreward is toward. If that object is nearby (say, a star near the center of the galaxy), then it's constantly moving. The math becomes trickier, the dynamics becomes non-Newtonian... If that object is far away (not in our galaxy), then it's not alway toward the core.

    Modern coordinate systems are generally defined either directly or indirectly based on the position of several objects well outside of our galaxy, so that even over relatively long times the coordinate system doesn't move much.

    As a side note, "coreward", "spinwise", and "north" define a cylindrical coordinate system, in which adding vectors (for example) is trickier. You can translate to and from a local cartesian coordinate system, but why not just start that way in the first place?
