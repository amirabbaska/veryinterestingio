    an atmospheric pO2 lower than 40mmHg, you would technically be losing oxygen as you attempted to breath

    Yes, but if the venous pO2 is 40mmHg, you wouldn't be losing oxygen. You would just be not replenishing it.

    I think the key is that, at a normal altitude, when you hold your breath your lungs have a reservoir of air that contains some oxygen that you can draw from for a while, extending how long you have enough oxygen in your blood to stay conscious.

    At a high altitude, your lungs get depleted of the high-pO2 air they had. So I guess, in theory, you may last longer by holding your breath even for altitudes of a pO2 around 40 mmHg.

      • Lung_doc

        There is a separate reason you do poorly at altitude. Technically you can get your mixed venous down to nearly 30% (PO2 20 or so, and possibly slightly lower if you are a little acidotic); people do this with exercise all the time - extraction goes way up, venous sats reach the lowest possible, lactate production starts etc.

        Sick people do this too: in heart failure we see venous sats of 30 to 40% sometimes in folks just lying still.

        At rest normally, however, given an O2 consumption of say 250 ml per min and O2 delivery of say 1000 ml per min (typical 70kg person), this should allow an arterial sat of only 50 to 60% to be survivable.

        However, its not. Partly because at sats < 90%, you get some pulmonary artery vasoconstriction, and this will get extreme at lower sats putting a huge strain on the heart and lowering cardiac output. Thus no more 1000 ml per minute O2 delivery due to BOTH hypoxia and a drop in cardiac output (DO2 = 13.9 x CO x hemoglobin x saturation )
