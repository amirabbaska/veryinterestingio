  • robb04

    A super charger will improve economy as much as a turbo. As long as you drove it economically. The biggest upside to a supercharger is that because it’s directly related to engine rpm you get low end boost much quicker than a turbo. However the supercharger will hit a limit and at high rpm does not deliver as much power as a turbo. Also the parasitic drag on the engine is greater than a turbo (there is still some drag for a turbo). A top fuel dragster supercharger takes about 800 horsepower just to turn if I remember correctly. There are other small differences, superchargers go right on top of the intake so on carbureted applications it’s compressing the fuel with the air, a turbo only compresses air. Also turbos are typically intercooled. Meaning there’s an air-air heat exchanger between the turbo and throttle body.

    • Dachor

      1. There is such thing as a centrifugal supercharger which has a compressor very similar to a turbo's cool side, which DOES NOT sit on top of the engine.

      2. Both superchargers AND turbos can go both ways when it comes to where the fuel is mixed. With fuel injection, fuel is almost always added post compression, but with carburetors you have two options; blow-through, and pull-through. Blow-through requires stronger seals and solid floats in the carb, whereas pull-through allows use of a standard carb but requires a compressor that can handle the corrosive properties of the fuel. AFAIK blow-throughs are the more common configuration.
