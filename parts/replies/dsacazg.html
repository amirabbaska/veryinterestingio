    • throwhooawayyfoe

      You’re on the right track- the benefit with countercurrent heat exchange is in reducing total heat loss to the environment by allowing the extremities to become colder. In general the rate of heat transfer is dependent on the surface area and the temperature difference. The extremities of warm blooded animals will always transfer a lot of heat because they have more surface area, but the rate of heat loss can be lowered by reducing the temperature difference with the environment.

      However, they still need blood flow to provide oxygen and nutrients, so the solution can’t just be to cut off blood flow to their extremities. Frostbite occurs in humans when blood vessels contract to prevent overall heat loss that can cause hypothermia, but in doing so it cuts off the tissue from oxygen as well.

      Animals that make use of countercurrent blood flow use it as a mechanism to allow their extremities to drop to a colder temperature (reduce total heat loss to environment) while still providing them oxygen/nutrients of blood flow.
