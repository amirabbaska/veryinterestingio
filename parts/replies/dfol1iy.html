    The German "von" and Dutch "van" may be the same preposition (of/from), but in names they have very different meanings and origins. In Dutch "van" in a name often indicates location, as you say. However, in German the "von" was a privilege of nobility until 1919 (with some exceptions of course, especially in northern Germany, i. e. close to the Netherlands). The "von" was what is called an "Adelspr√§dikat" (a sign of nobility) and indicated association with an important lineage, not necessarily a location. Take for example the poet Johann Wolfgang von Goethe. He was born a common citizen in Frankfurt in 1749 as Johann Wolfgang Goethe (no von). He was made a nobleman in 1782 by Kaiser Joseph II, which added the "von" to his name. Interestingly the nobility does have an additional way to indictate location in a name, namely with "zu" (to/at). So someone would be called "von [family name] zu [location]" or "von und zu [family name]" if the person is living in the family's traditional location.

    The virtually exclusive use of "von" for nobility also explains why names with "von" are much less common in German than names with "van" are in Dutch.
