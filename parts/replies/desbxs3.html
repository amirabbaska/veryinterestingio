• to_the_9s

    If it's digital, it will most likely already be shot at 23.976.

    If it's film (true 24), it will be converted to 23.976 by a third party/network, with cadence issues (frame blending and artifacts) being dealt with during a QC process. Most likely a frame will be pulled out here and there automatically, then moved if it causes a jump.

    If any conversion is needed for broadcast, it will happen at the network level. They will need to reformat for time and commercial, as well as other things. Not reason to convert beforehand

    For a DVD/Blu Ray release, the manufacturing studio will handle the conversion as well. Always best to work with the native raw video when mastering.

    However, you are thinking correctly when it comes to PAL (Europe+), 25fps. This is converted as 'a frame is a frame,' meaning, the first second consists of 24 single frames plus the first frame of the next second. The result is slightly sped up.

    The difference is 0.1%. Over a three hour movie it's eleven seconds of time. If the soundtrack pitch is changed by the same amount that's 2 cents, which is similar to instrument tuning error. Consider that for PAL viewers, showing the 24 fps film at 25 fps for TV, a speed increase of 4%, has generally been considered acceptable (although complained more about for the audio, which is sometimes separately pitch-corrected).

    It's nothing. The 3:2 pulldown to convert ~24 fps video to ~30fps TV is rather more noticeable in terms of impacting the quality.
