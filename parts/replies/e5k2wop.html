    It is my understanding that the older C band satellites which required 10 foot diameter dishes were 2 Watts per channel while the newer 60 cm diameter dishes required 75 Watts per channel.

    No, definitely not.

    In both cases, the transponder power on the satellite is about the same. A typical 36MHz transponder (which was how much bandwidth was needed to transmit a single analogue tv channel back in the day) had a tx power of roughly 70W or so.

    The antenna size difference comes in for a couple of other reasons. First, antenna gain is a function of frequency and antenna diameter. The lower the frequency, the larger the antenna that you need for the same gain. As such, a 1.8m C-Band antenna (the smallest you can go and legally transmit) has roughly the same performance as a (good) 1m antenna at Ku-Band.

    The second issue is the 3dB beam width. The satellites in orbit are typically slotted every two degrees along the equator. A 2.4m C-Band antenna will have a beam width of about 1.5 degrees or so, vastly reducing the amount of adjacent satellite interference it picks up.

    Lastly, between ye olde BUDs (Big Ugly Dishes) and the little 60cm TV dish, there were dramatic improvements in Forward Error Correction. The Polynomial Codes used in DVB-S2 are within a fraction of a dB of the Shanon Limit. Back in the days of the BUDs, there was no error correction, so they needed dramatically better antenna performance.

    It is an interesting math exercise to determine how much power reaches a 60 cm diameter satellite dish considering that the area of the U.S. is about 3,000,000 square miles or 7,650,000 square kilometers.

    Looking back at one of my link budgets, one of the networks I Engineered had a flux density of approximately -112dBW/m^2. That's in the billionths of a watt per square meter or more.
