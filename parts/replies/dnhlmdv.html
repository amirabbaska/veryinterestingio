    The short answer is nothing directly, because quantum computers will, for any even remotely foreseeable future, be specialized research devices. They will not be in your computers.

    Even with the people I've talked at my lab who work on QCs they've said unless there is a massive breakthrough quantum processors would likely be similar to how graphics cards are now where a normal CPU would do most of the logical calculations and then submit the sections that are suited for Q-CPUs off to the quantum computer. So if it was inside of your computer it would likely be on a separate card just like your GPU.

    Although the first major challenge with Q-CPUs is just to get them to a scale large enough to even be competitive with a normal CPU on the specialized operations. It doesn't matter if you can do in 10 moves what takes 10000 on a traditional CPU if your Q-CPU can only perform 200 operations a minute while a traditional CPU can do billions.

  • mfukar

    I think it's a little short-sighted to claim the development of quantum programming languages won't affect the rest. Vizzotto et al. (2006) already noted how type classes can be used to model computation, separating it into pure and effect-ful parts, which, trivially, will allow us to 1. use existing idioms/styles, 2. use existing static analysis algorithms & tools, 3. keep the internal details of QP semantics confined in the compiler, instead of exposing application programmers to it. Quipper already builds upon this result (without a citation, I might add).

    If anything, results like this show there's no clear incentive to isolate/separate research of quantum from 'classical' programming languages. It is not a stretch to suggest research into one might be applicable to the other.
