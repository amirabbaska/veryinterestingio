    • RobusEtCeleritas

      It's an integral counting the phase space volume of the system. That's how you quantify the "number of states" for a system with a continuous spectrum.

      In classical mechanics, h is an arbitrary constant so that (d^(3)q d^(3)p)/h^(3) is dimensionless. In quantum mechanics, Planck's constant is the natural constant to use.

      You can't actually "count" the uncountable infinite number of states, but you can loosely think of this phase space integral as being "proportional to" the absolute number of states. If you double the phase space volume, you "double" te number of states the system can exist in.

      As for the question about electrons in a box, if you imagine an infinite or finite rectangular well, the degeneracies of the wells don't depend on their physical size. You can fit the same amount of particles into each energy level regardless of how big the box is. If it's a finite well, the number of bound states will depend on the well depth, but it's not true that having a spatially larger box will allow you to put more electrons in each energy level.

      What happens when you squeeze the box depends on how quickly you squeeze it. In the adiabatic limit, the particles will stay in the lowest-energy configuration, even though the basis states are changing. If you compress the box, the energy of each orbital will increase, and that's where your degeneracy pressure comes from.

      Each orbital does take up a certain amount of space, but it doesn't have a well-defined size; it's a wavefunction.

        The anti symmetric aspect of the wavefunction is understood to mean that if spacial and spin components of the wavefunction are inverted the transformation has odd symmetry (i.e. f (-x)= -f (x) in one dimension) . Recall that the wavefunction can have positive or negative amplitude depending on spacial coordinates. For example the p orbital consists a positive and a negative lobe, corresponding to the amplitude of the wavefunction. This has real consequences in spectroscopic transitions and the theory of molecular bonding because amplitudes can be added and interference constructively or destructively representing bonding or antibonding states respectively.

      What's more important than the amount of space is the uncertainty of that space. The uncertainty principle requires that dqdp > h, so in a sense, planks constant is a resolution which specifies how close to states can be and still be separate states. You cant have points in phase space, not even in principle. A state occupies a volume of at least h^3
