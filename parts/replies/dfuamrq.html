    I don't have an in depth knowledge, but have read about it. And think about it logically. If you have a pot of water at 0° and a pot at 50° and heat them both, eventually the water that was 0° will rise to 50°. So how is that different than using water starting at 50°?

    Weirdly, there's some evidence to suggest that this doesn't work going in the opposite direction. It's called the Mpemba Effect -- the idea that, in certain situations and depending on your definition of 'freezing', hot water might freeze faster than cold. It's a bit of a contentious topic, and has been for a while now; in 2016, a paper proclaimed that 'there is no evidence to support meaningful observations of the Mpemba effect' (Burridge and Linden), but this year another paper (Tao et al.) gave a reason why it might happen on a molecular basis, so research is obviously still ongoing. Either way, it's been an attested phenomenon since the time of Aristotle.

    Some real counterintuitive stuff, there.

      The biggest factor behind the Mpemba effect is evaporation, which results in a loss of mass. The Q= mL heat loss is far greater than the Q = mC deltaT heat loss. It doesn't matter much if the ice cube starts off boiling if it is releasing steam when you put it in a freezing environment.

      As far as boiling water, it's mostly hokum. Sure, lots of water tanks are dirty and have solutes which trivially raise the temperature of water. But, for most recipes you're supposed to add salt anyway.
