    • usernumber36

      because electrons are both negatively charged, they will repel eachother to some extent. This makes a paired set of electrons slightly more unstable than an unpaired set of electrons, and is one good reason why Hund's rule happens - i.e. fill boxes one at a time before pairing).

      Now that rule holds true most of the time because the pairing energy (more unstable = higher energy state) tends to be quite small relative to the big gaps in energy between the different orbitals. But for the 4s and the 3d in chromium, it turns out the orbital energies are really close, and the gap between them is LESS than the pairing energy. So if the electron is looking for the lowest energy state to be in, it's better NOT to pair them up and instead continue on as if the 3d and 4s are all just the same energy, and apply hund's rule.

      someone noted before that this seems o only work for chromium and not all the others in the d block before and after it - fair comment. This happens because the energy level of each orbital is HIGHLY dependent on the number of electrons already present in the atom. The energy levels of the orbitals are different for every atom and heck, different for every single number of electrons you might put in that atom. The energies change and the order of the orbital s changes too.

    while it is energetically unfavorable to pair two electrons into one orbital, this does not explain what you just wrote. by that logic, scandium would have to be [Ar]3d2 4s1, which it isn't.

    the full explanation is some kind of energy gain from the half filled d orbitals in Cr and Mo, or completely filled d orbitals in Cu, Ag, Au, Rg. this gain in energy seems to be higher than the difference between d and s plus the spin pairing energy. there isn't much you can find about that on the internet though. everyone just states that filling half of all orbitals of one kind is favourable. someone in this thread pointed out that there is an entropic gain from this.
