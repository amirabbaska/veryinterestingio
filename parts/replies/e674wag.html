    • tankthestank

      I never thought about it like that, but I believe you can. The smaller bubbles form at the heat source when the heat source is above the boiling temperature. If the heat source is slightly below the boiling point bubbles won't form. If you heat water at the lower temperature slowly, then raise the temperature of the heat source you could form bubbles that can make it to the surface before they cool enough to collapse. It would be a cool experiment to try.

      The smaller bubbles form because we typically want to boil water as fast as possible so our heat source is much higher than the boiling point of water causing the water to boil at the bottom and collapse as it rises through the cooler water. They also promote promote convection and mixing which also speeds up the boiling process.

      • tankthestank

        Also something related, it is possible to superheat water so that the temperature is above the boiling point but it isn't bubbling. This has something to do with the molecules still being stable and there not being any nucleation sites to promote bubble formation. In this state, a small disturbance can cause the water to start boiling spontaneously.
