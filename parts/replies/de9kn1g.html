      I feel reasonably confident I did an experiment not very different from this (I forget the exact configuration of the rotating mirror and slits) but the only other mirror we used was only like 100m away.

      IIRC, we had a small mirror rotating quickly, and a laser would bounce off of that mirror, to the large mirror far away, and then back to the small mirror (which would rotated some small fraction of a degree), and you could measure that change in angle depending on where the laser spot was showing up elsewhere. So using the known distance between mirrors and the known speed of rotation you could calculate the time it took for the light to travel 200m. Something like that, anyway.

      • Celebration2000

        Yeah we did the same experiment in undergrad. However we had the advantage of using precision scientific equipment (stable, controllable roto-mirror from Leybold and a photodiode array for displacement measurement). We were able to set it up in the class room. The frequencies of the roto-mirror weren't extremely high either, ranging from 100-600 Hz, but they were very stable. In this setup we observed displacements of the laser spot on the order of tenths of a millimeter. We measured c within 1% of the definition.

        This general setup was invented by Foucault. Here's the wiki page for those interested.
