    • JohnShaft

      No, it is pretty true. For example, patients are typically awake while deep brain electrodes are lowered into their subthalamic nucleus for Parkinson's treatment. There are pain receptors in the dura mater and in blood vessels, but they are in less density than those in other parts of the body. The dura is sensate, but no where near as sensitive as, say, skin, which itself is much less sensitive than the dermal layer underneath.

      Migraines are still undetermined with respect to their pain source. In some cases, they are extremely sensitive to the components in the blood (plasma), and react quite quickly to changes in plasma. So, it may be a biochemical reaction of brain blood vessels to components in the bloodstream - at least for some types of migraines. Still much to be learned. I have heard from monitors of the upcoming CGRP antibody treatments - and they tell me that the early results are very large in magnitude and very promising...
