    • escherbach

      Sorry replying late, just got on reddit after a long day. Irreversibility in classical statistical mechanics is not exact - even the Second Law of Thermodynamics is false on long enough timescales due to the Poincare recurrence theorem, but for practical processes (within the lifetime of the universe) the 2nd Law is true.
      Theoretically speaking, all the zillions of chemical reactions that take place when the library is burnt down could be reversed (assuming all the products of the burning, the ashes, the fumes etc have been captured). Of course it is a practical impossibility.

      In Quantum Mechanics reversibility of physical processes is guaranteed by unitarity - conservation of probability (or information) essentially. (There is the problem of "wave-function" collapse which is non-unitary (and, in general, non-reversible) - but I assume that is not relevant here). But just like in the classical case, there is not a practical way to exactly reverse most macroscopic processes, even small ones, never mind Black-hole evaporation!
