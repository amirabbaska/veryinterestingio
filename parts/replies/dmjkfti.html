    • purple_pixie

      I imagine the other answers have covered this, but there's an important distinction between heat and temperature

      If something is very hot, it has a high temperature, but that doesn't mean it has a lot of heat. To have a lot of heat you need a lot of mass at high temperature.

      Temperature is sort of like density, it's like a measure of how much heat something has per unit mass. (While heat is more like weight, it's a function of both the density of the object, and how much of the object there is)

      So something with barely any mass at all (like a spark from a welder) can be thousands of degrees in temperature, but you can't even feel it when it lands on your skin because the amount of actual heat inside it is tiny, and you can't even notice the change in temperature to your skin.

      Imagine dropping an ice cube into a lake, it doesn't matter if that ice cube is 0C or -200C it is not going to make any difference to the temperature of the lake because it's tiny.

      That's why it's possible to have something at a super super high temperature without melting the Earth.
