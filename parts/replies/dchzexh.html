    • PostMortemBoredom

      Industrial radiographer here, I X-ray airplane parts and have also done some gamma work with iridium and cobalt isotopes. We use primarily lead because you can get away with thinner sheets when working with x and gamma radiation. We also use concrete, leaded glass, beryllium glass, other dense metals, etc. half and tenth value layers are how you would calculate the reduction in dose vs thickness of shielding. Oftentimes tungsten is used to collimate the radiation beams to control and aim the radiation as isotopic sources emit gamma rays 360 degrees

  • Hiddencamper

    Nuclear engineer here.

    There are standards for radiation from sources to workers and the general public. The manufacturers of these detectors have to keep that in mind while designing a system to ensure 10cfr20 limits or other applicable limits are met. This means shielding around the X ray scanners along with occasional maintenance which includes a check of how much radiation is escaping the device.

    In the nuclear power industry, we have much stricter controls for radiation exposure. At my plant we actually have radiation badges attached to the side of the X ray machines which get checked regularly to ensure there is no unnecessary exposure outside of the machine. We have security continuously manning the X-rays just like at an airport. The X-ray scanners are just well built.
