    • HugodeGroot

      Isn't the same sunlight just arriving at a lower angle, so it's spread over a larger area?

      Yes, for the most part. What you are describing is called the projection effect. This effect basically says that the irradiation of a beam decreases as the angle to the surface becomes shallower, since its projected area becomes larger as shown in this diagram. This is the main effect that reduces the insolation at the poles. On top of that, the effective optical path of the light is longer and so more of it is lost to atmospheric scattering and absorption. Both of these effects serve to reduce how much energy you can collect with say a 1m x 1m solar panel. Now of course you could compensate for this reduction by building more solar panels, but the same argument works everywhere on Earth. For economic reasons, it seems more practical to place such large installations in deserts closer to populated regions.

    • globular_custard

      Now if we just built long distance transmission lines from both poles

      There's your problem. It's brutally expensive to maintain infrastructure in the high Arctic. For one thing, there's rarely land access via road or rail, so all maintenance is done via helicopter fly-ins. And this isn't even considering the incredible engineering and logistical challenges that would have to be overcome during construction.

      Second, even if you got over the insane costs of maintaining a transmission line from the pole, you'd have to have some pretty sophisticated technology to transmit the electricity over thousands of kilometres. Ordinary high-voltage AC transmission lines that you commonly see here in the south are only designed to run tens of kilometres, or perhaps hundreds at the extremes. High voltage DC is a possibility, but even then it's not really designed for more than a thousand kilometres.

      Far from being cheap energy, it would end up being one of the most absurdly expensive sources you could come up with.
