    This is true for circular orbits, but in the context of the OP, increasing orbital velocity does increase altitude. He said:

    If I increase the speed (by burning prograde)

    Assume you are in a circular orbit. Burning prograde will increase your current velocity, and make the orbit elliptic, with the old orbital radius as periapsis and a higher apoapsis. Near the apoapsis, the velocity will be smaller, as you said. But near the periapsis, we have a higher velocity than before making the orbit elliptical. Only after circularising the orbit by burning prograde at the apoapsis will the orbital velocity be smaller than before raising the orbit.

    • EvanDaniel

      A purely radial small impulsive burn changes the eccentricity of the orbit, but does not change the semimajor axis. The new orbit will (as always) cross the old orbit at the point of the burn (and another point). If you started with a circular orbit, you'll end up with an elliptical orbit. (Large burns, however, will add to the total energy and therefor change the semimajor axis.)

      The question is a little complicated for an elliptical orbit: note that normally, in an elliptical orbit, a "radial" burn has a pro- or retro-grade component to it except at periapsis or apoapsis. You'll also likely shift the argument of periapsis of the orbit.
