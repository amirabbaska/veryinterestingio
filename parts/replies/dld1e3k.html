    • boobodst

      That's not how telomeres work. Long telomeres are important because they provide a lot of opportunities for mutations to hit the telomeres instead of the usable genome. Mutations in the telomeres are unlikely to ever be read so they would more likely than not be "silent". The question of mutation is inevitable. Cells are always under a barrage of attack on their genomes from errors in replication to high energy particles running through like a wrecking ball. They cell is normally pretty good at repairing these issues by sewing the genome back together in the correct order or proofreading its replications. However, sometimes this slips though.

      If you have a bunch of junk you don't care about and it can get harmless, non-cancerous mutations you'd like for there to be more of that around just to increase the probability it will be hit and not the vital parts of your genome. Telomeres are more like if you imagine London in World War II several times larger in size with all of the extra space totally and unused, unoccupied, and inaccessible to the occupied parts of the city. Air raids meant bombings were a near certainty somewhere in the city, but our telomere London is probabilistically much less likely to have those bombs hit anywhere useful.
