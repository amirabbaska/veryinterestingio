    It's a matter of context.

    Becquerels is a literal unit of activity. It's easy to measure because individual emissions are taken as equivalent. Doesn't matter if they're alpha or beta. Basically a rate of clicks on a Geiger counter for a standardized volume of sample.

    Becqurel is a useful measurement when you can make assumptions of the source. Say you're monitoring negligent leaks of tritium water from seeping out of a power plant heat exchanger. Since you're assuming a tritium source, you assume only alpha emission so a becqurel can be contextualized in terms of health impact because there is only one type of emission assumed. Also, the monitoring is mostly done to keep an eye on a certain kind of reactor problem.

    A becqurel isn't useful for adding much context to a nuclear disaster beyond "this water sample is freaking hot". It's not very reasonable to make accurate estimates for safe exposure time because it lacks context for the profile of emissions and therefore consideration of the health impact.

    Sievert becomes useful if you can assess the profile of the different emissions to assess health impact. Sievert is a combination of intensity and distribution of emissions with weighting upon the damage potential of the different emissions which makes it a much more complex measurement that is expensive for routine monitoring of low health impact sources and for the most part uninformative for the context. If you got a very high becqurel output from a reactor outlet, one might put that sample in for a more comprehensive analysis though.
