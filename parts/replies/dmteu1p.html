      • piggypigpigs

        A variety of factors are involved. If you've ever felt a penguin's foot, especially from a cold climate, you'll feel very thick skin. Their feet are primarily skin, bone and tendons which makes dealing with the cold easier for them - think of the parts of your face that hurt the most during freezing weather: it's always the fleshy parts like the cheeks and lips rather than parts like the ears, which don't have much actual flesh hanging around, just skin and cartilage and some thin blood vessels.

        Some of the warmth from the heat exchange I mentioned before does actually go to the feet, maintaining a safe temperature. It's a very small amount but the blood always enters the foot warmer than it exits, so the feet will never have an opportunity to actually freeze. Imagine filling an ice cube tray, putting it in the freezer and then every few seconds replacing the water with slightly warmer water and giving it a mix... it would just stay as cold water.

        Also, the dual-layer of feathers and thick blubber extend all the way down to the bottom of the legs for some cold-climate species, classically the Emperor. The important muscles allowing movement of the feet and legs are actually located higher up than other animals, meaning these muscles are protected by even more blubber and feathers as they're closer to the warm core of the body. Penguins probably never experience that 'oh shit it's -20 and my legs hurt so bad' feeling that we humans get because they have their muscles kept in what is basically ski pants all the time.

        Finally, during the extreme cold of Antarica, Penguins are almost always moving their feet about. When they huddle up in large groups they spend a lot of time shuffling around between the centre and the outer layer - when they're on the outer layer they tend to rest on their heels and their tails (protected by feathers and blubber) which allows their toes (the most sensitive part) to have a break from the ice. I'm pretty sure Emperors can actually tuck their toes into their feathery/blubbery parts whilst doing this too.

        So yeah, that's basically how they stop frostbite. May be a few bits I've missed but I'm super tired.
