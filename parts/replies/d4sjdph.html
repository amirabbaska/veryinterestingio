    • flamingtrashcan

      In a hollow shell, we wouldn't feel any gravity anywhere inside the shell

      Gravity is an inverse-square force, which means that the force of gravity is inversely proportional (gets weaker) to the distance squared.

      Now, consider a close rod and a far rod that look the same length. If the far rod is double the distance to the close rod, then the far rod must be twice as long.

      Now, consider a close circle and a far circle that look the same size. If the far circle is double the distance to the close circle, then its width must be double the width of the close circle. Thus, the large circle's area is quadruple the area of the close circle.

      In other words, given an object's apparent area, the object's actual area is proportional to the distance squared.

      Now, imagine yourself anywhere in the hollow space inside a hollow shell. Look at a patch of earth. Now, turn around. As long as the shell is completely closed, there will always be a patch of shell on the other side of the same apparent area.

      Both these patches exert gravity on you. Remember that the actual area of the patch is proportional to the square of the distance. Furthermore, remember that gravitational force is inversely proportional to the square of the distance.

      Thus, the gravitational forces of the two patches are of equal magnitude, and cancel each other out. Therefore, you would feel no gravity in a hollow earth, regardless of your location.

      This line of thinking forms the basis of Gauss's law for gravity and electromagnetism (both inverse-square laws).
