    A couple of points add up to an answer. The moon has been there a really long time, about as long as Earth. There are lots of small objects around that are still big enough to make a crater on the moon and there were WAY more billions of years ago. It's true that it's much more likely for them to blow up in our atmosphere than blow up on the moon in the same way that it's more likely to lose the lottery than win, but if you try thousands or millions of times a year for billions of years, you're going to get a lot of lucky shots!

    Also, I'm not sure if it's important (not an expert) but the moon wasn't always locked to us. When baby pre-Earth smashed into big thing and made actual Earth and actual moon, we were both spinning fast. Earth and moon started slowing each other's spins (because reasons to do with gravity) ever since. Because Earth is big, we slowed moon's spin to zero compared to us pretty quickly, but it was still a fair amount of time for all sides of the moon to 'see' the rest of the sky and take some hits

    Most of the dark spots on the moon facing us are called Maria (singular: mare). These are volcanic in nature.

    There are some craters we can see by eye. From the Moon's perspective the Earth blocks around 2% of the sky. To see this: extend your fist all the way out, with your thumb up. Your thumbnail blocks 2%. Look around, see how much your thumbnail doesn't block.
