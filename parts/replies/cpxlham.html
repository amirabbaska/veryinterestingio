    • MugsayBoges

      You're thinking on the right path.

      "...the act of measuring or looking at the wave somehow interferes with it, causing its collapse? For example, looking at it sends out some sort of energy that we aren't able to perceive? Meaning, something sent out from the recording device interferes with the natural state of the wave function."

      We know exactly what energy is interfering. In order for us to observe something then we need to shine "light" (photons) on it. For an electron to become detectable, a photon must first interact with it, and this interaction will inevitably change the path of that electron. It is also possible for other, less direct means of measurement to affect the electron.
