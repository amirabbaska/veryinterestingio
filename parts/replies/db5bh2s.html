    Tacking on to this, in the US for example, if GPA was that important it should lead to grade inflation(schools giving higher and higher grade averages) so that graduates of a school would have an easier time getting a job, and would thus be more highly ranked, along with all of the prestige and financial benefits for the school(excuse to charge more) and previous graduates that accompany the improved ranking.

    I personally would argue that a similar sort of grade inflation is the primary factor in improving high school graduation rates in the US in the last couple decades. The education curriculums have shifted to make it harder for people to fail, because failing students mean: you have to deal with parents complaining or worse; the kid has to repeat the year which can be disruptive for other students; your school and teachers look shittier since graduation rate ends up directly and indirectly affecting performance perceptions; it costs the various levels of governement that contribute funding more in the 2-5 year term (though ostensibly loses them money via tax dollars in the long run); it makes everyone warm and fuzzy inside to see rising pass/graduation rates.
