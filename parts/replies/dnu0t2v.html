  • bill___brasky

    Arabic is a great example. There is Modern Standard Arabic (MSA) which is a version of Arabic based on classical Arabic but modernized and simplified for the current day. MSA is used in most media and formal speech in the Arabic world. Its also what is taught to westerners learning arabic. But just about every region has their own dialect. Countries like Egypt and Saudi Arabia speak variants of MSA that are fairly easy for people who aren't native to the region to understand. Then you have countries like Morrocco which has some bastardized French/Arabic mashup that is completely bonkers and throws native Arabic speakers for a loop. And then you have the Arabic dialects spoken in SE Asia and Europe which also borrow words/phrases/pronunciation/grammar from a multitude of different languages.

    This is the case with Zulu(from South Africa) and my native tongue Ndebele(Zimbabwe). There are differences in cadence, emphasis, pre- and suffixes and a few amusing word substitutions (the Ndebele word for pumpkin, "thanga", for example, is the same as the Zulu word for thigh, and the word for "burn" in Ndebele , "bhebha", means "orgasm". A friend of mine tells a story of one time she forgot she was on the wrong side of the border and during a church discussion intended to say "you must burn with Christ's fire", but used the Ndebele word).

    The closeness of the languages isn't surprising considering the Ndebele rebelled against Shaka in the 1830s, less than two centuries ago.

    What makes things confusing is that there is another closely related but very different linguistic/ethnic group called the Ndebele that resides in South Africa. They're a bit more well-known for their geometric art and the tribal practice of women elongating their necks with brass rings.
