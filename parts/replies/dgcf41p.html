    Why do pairings cancel out the magnetic field? All electrons have a magnetic moment (this is what gives rise to the magnetic field), it's orientation is dependent upon the particles' spin (either up or down). Due to the PEP, electrons in a filled shell will always pair up (one up and one down) and their magnetic moments will cancel.

    It is unfortunately one of those seemingly simple questions that are tremendously difficult to answer. Perhaps one has to give up on accuracy for the sake of simplicity. But I thought the pure chromium is in fact a spin density wave (SDW) metal rather than an insulating antiferromagnet. In this case, I suspect that the origin of SDW in chromium has more to do with the Fermi surface geometry (or broadly, electronic structure) of chromium in its paramagnetic state than the local exchange interactions.

      What's the deal with bismuth?

      • uberhobo

        Bismuth has the opposite property that gives rise to ferromagnetism, called diamagnetism. Solid bismuth has no unpaired electrons in its electronic structure, so it repels magnetic fields instead of being attracted to them like iron would be. This means that if you had a large disc of bismuth, a strong magnet would float on top of it through the repulsion it experiences.

    • swapwaspspaw

      Fe2+ can be both. In the presence of a cubic crystal field, it may be in the so-called "high spin state", which carries spin quantum number S=2; or "low spin state", which carries no spin and hence no magnetism. Which of the two cases happens depends on lots of details. Sometimes, as you heat up a material, you will find a transition from a "low spin state" to "high spin state"; in other words, the material becomes magnetic as you heat it up! Similar phenomenon happens to Co3+, too (and more well known, actually).

      The most dramatic display of this phenomenon is in a certain molecular magnet with Fe2+. In its ground state, the molecule is non magnetic magnetic. But you can shine a laser on it and excite the molecule and turn the molecule magnetic non magnetic. The magnetic non-magnetic state of the molecule may survive up to a week.
