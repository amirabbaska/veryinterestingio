    Energy + fat storage + waste leaves out a large part of the equation - body repair and maintenance.

    If you eat, say, a steak, your body won't use most of it for energy or fat storage, and it won't waste it. It will largely use it to repair broken cells, make new cells, and so on. As most cells consist of protein and fat in about the same ratio as found in steak, this is the most efficient use of those nutrients, so it's what the body does. It's different if, for example, you drink a can of cola. There's nothing much in there that can be used to maintain the body, and so it will all be used as energy or stored as fat for later.

    • Nyrin

      Yes, but the bound and the diminishing returns are both high and slow enough that it shouldn't affect your decisions.

      As we can see from endurance athletes, it's very possible to take in close to 10,000 kCal per diem on a regular basis; this would translate to more than two pounds of weight gain per day for a sedentary individual at or below 2000 kCal maintenance.

      A number of the very calorically dense nutrients are enzymatically limited, to be sure; you can find stories (even videos; thanks, Internet) of people drinking entire liters (1000ml ~= 8500 fat kCal) of olive oil. Lipases don't keep up with this and the experimenters are made eminently aware of this fact once elimination comes around.

      Actually, in general, elimination gives you a pretty reasonable picture of how dysfunctional your absorption is.

      But in the end, the fourth helping still "counts." It's OK to gain weight some days, though, so don't necessarily let that stop you; just don't gain weight most days and you're fine.
