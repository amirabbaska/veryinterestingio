  • Funkit

    Any specific reason they use potassium perchlorate over a standard thermite reaction wth iron oxide?

    • zebediah49

      More oxidization potential per unit volume and weight. Plus, you don't really want to produce a whole bunch of liquid iron as a byproduct in this case.

    • EvanDaniel

      Thermite burns too slowly, with no gas output. You need a fast burn, and just a little gas output to get a large radiant cloud. Too little gas, you get a hot bright blob that stays hot for a while while giving off only a little light (because it's small). Radiated light is proportional to surface area and T^(4). So a flash grenade, that wants maximum total light in a short time, needs to get a large radiating area.

      The flash powders chosen mostly have non-gaseous products (aluminum + potassium perchlorate nominally gives aluminum oxide and potassium chloride), but it's hot enough to vaporize some of them and the combustion isn't perfectly complete, so you get a little gas. There may also be small amounts of stabilizers that also produce a little gas as they burn.

      The material also needs to be easy enough to light when needed by the fuse, yet also stable enough for manufacturing, storage, and normal handling.

      You could probably get a flash grenade or similar to work with a Copper II thermite, since that burns very quickly and has some gas output (it boils the resultant copper). But normal thermite just burns too slowly, I think.
