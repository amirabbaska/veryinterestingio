  • MrAlpha0mega

    New Zealander here. We have a similar blackout, though it is only on the day of the election until 7pm, so it can be reported on that night.

    Last time, a sports celebrity got in trouble for tweeting who he voted for on the day of the election. I don't think he was actually charged, just given a warning or something, but I think the point was that 'an ordinary member of the public' depends on how high profile the person is and who is going to see their comment. Sporting personalities obviously don't qualify as ordinary citizens in this context, but 'ordinary' people who happen to have a lot of followers ought to be carefull too.

    The law applies to everyone from what I understand, but there is discretion in who is charged. They obviously wouldn't chase down random people no-one cares about for posting on facebook.

    I think the only reason he wasn't charged was because this was a unusual situation that hadn't occured before and people weren't generally aware that this kind of conduct was illegal.
