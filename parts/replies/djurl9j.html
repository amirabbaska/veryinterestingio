      A key part that was left out in this explanation is the role of the keel. Yes, as mentioned, the sail acts like a wing and generates lift. However a large portion of this force acts horizontally across the beam. The keel of a sailboat uses the force of the water to push horizontally in the other direction, leaving the remaining force in the forward line. Think of squishing an apple seed, with one finger being the sail force, and the other being the keel force: the sideways forces will balance and the remaining force will cause the seed to shoot forwards!

      • audigex

        I find this is actually easier to describe with a kite and bike than with a sail and keel: it's easier to picture a bike's wheels digging into sand, than to picture the keel of a boat in water.

        Imagine you're on a beach, on a bicycle and you have a big kite, and it's pulling you downwind. If you point the bike towards the kite, the kite pulls you along. Intuitive enough

        Now imagine you turn the bike 45 degrees. Because of the way your bike's tyres digs into the sand, the bike resists being pulled directly down wind, so instead you're pulled along at 45 degrees from the wind direction, even though the kite is still directly down wind. A boat's keel works in exactly the same way.

        But wait, that kite doesn't have to be exactly down wind does it? A kite can fly nearly 90 degrees from the wind. Let's say we put it 80 degrees from the wind and point the bike directly at it... we'll travel at 80 degrees, right? Straight at the kite, because it can still only pull us directly towards it.

        And then as the final step, we just combine these two things - the kite is at 80 degrees from the wind, and the bike is an extra 45 degrees from the kite... suddenly we're moving at 125 degrees from the wind direction: we're actually riding towards the wind, because of the way we've redirect the force from the wind
