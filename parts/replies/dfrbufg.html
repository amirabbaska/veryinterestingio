      "Fully discharged" is just whatever the lower voltage cutoff of the cell is. With the right equipment, you can even discharge Li-ion cells into the negative potentials (I've done around -1.0V). You will permanently damage the cell, and it is unsafe, but it is possible.

      What I'm saying here, is that discharging a cell to 3.5V is going to result in longer cycle life than 3.2V, the manufacturer chose 3.2V as the lower voltage cutoff as it maximizes capacity, while having acceptable cycle life. By limiting how much you discharge the cell in your phone, you will extend the useful life of your phone.

      This is indeed something that has been bugging me about the new "quick charge" fad, while this capability is nice if I need a quick top up, for the most part I just charge my phone when I sleep, as long as it charges at least at 0.2C then I am happy.       Energy cells are cells with high specific energy (Wh/kg), while power cells give up some specific energy for higher specific power (J/kg). The cells are classified by the cathode material used. The most common are:

      Energy Cell: Lithium Cobalt Oxide (LCO), Lithium Nickel Manganese (NMC)
      Power Cell: Lithium Manganese Oxide (Spinel), Lithium Iron Phosphate (LFP)

      Phones use energy cells, as the currents required during use are quite low. Whereas electric cars and power tools are two common uses for power cells.
