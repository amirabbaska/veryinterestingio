      If they have same coeff of linear expansion, then there was/is a gap between the two materials originally, like space between threads. If they all expand evenly, the gap distance will also expand and break the corrosion. If the inner material expands more, it will crush the softer corrosion and also deform into outer material, when it cools there will be more gap.

      But mostly the problem is corrosion "welding" materials together (usually galvanic, or wet rust vs powdered iron oxide). You can sometimes help the situation by hammering head of bolt and vibration will break things up, and tightening then loosening can help fir same reason. Heating may do same thing, in addition to expansion of metal.

    Bimetallic strips are a good way to demonstrate this.

    A bimetallic strip is a strip of metal made from two different strips of metal layered on top of each other. When heated they expand at different rates, which causes the strip to bend.
