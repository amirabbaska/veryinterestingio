  • FatComputerGuy

    Varying the frequency would not be practical, as the slice of the frequency band that would be considered ‘microwaves’ is actually really narrow.

    It is true that varying the frequency would not be practical, but not for the reasons stated. "Microwaves" are defined as radio frequencies between 300 MHz (a wave length of 100 cm) and 300 GHz (0.1 cm). The usual frequency for microwave ovens is 2.45 GHz which is a wavelength of around 12cm or 4.8 inches - pretty close to the estimate above. And the part about the relative size of the waves, compared to the gaps in the shielding is also reasonable, but would apply equally to any radio frequency below a certain limit.

    The design of the magnetron itself is the primary reason for the fixed frequency. The physical shape and size of the device determine the frequency it generates, so varying the frequency would require a much more complex and expensive means of generating the high-powered radio waves.

    This frequency was also chosen because it was available for uses such as this in all parts of the world. (See my other comment in this thread for more about that.)
