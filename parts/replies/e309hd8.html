    Some alkaloids may not even serve a particular purpose directly. Some also may simply be a metabolic byproduct that prevents build up of an unfavorable metabolite. Natural selection can come with coincidences, a simple waste product could be extremely toxic to animals by chance.

    A quick look at some curare toxins reveals them to be EXTREMELY complicated heterocycles. Usually alkaloids are products of amino acid metabolism as this could be as well. I might check some journals later in the week and see if anyone has done some studies. I’m sure they have.

    • Somnif

      Often, the "factor" of an alkaloid that leads to it being selected for is something as simple as being very bitter/unpalatable. Simply put, they taste nasty.

      The molecule could be a ridiculously complicated heterocyclic mess resulting from a mass of gene duplications, pathway byproduct shunting, and oxidative stress reactants, but if it makes critters less likely to get past a first nibble, it will probably manage to make it a few generations down the line.

      (This is largely circumstantial, given how tricky it can be to tell precisely what effect a compound has in every organism that may encounter it, but I've heard it thrown around at conferences to general nodding of heads so I'm willing to run with it for now)
