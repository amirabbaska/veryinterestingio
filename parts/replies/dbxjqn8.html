    We still don't know what makes the Great Red Spot 'exist'.

    We have some very good theories, though. Both cascade (feeding from the jets) and inverse cascade (feeding from smaller vortices) processes help to maintain the Spot. Unlike Earth hurricanes which have a low at depth and a high aloft, the Great Red Spot seems to be an isolated high, much like isolated meddies in the Atlantic which can last for decades. (The wind shear is far too great for the Great Red Spot to have much vertical structure.)

    Combine those processes with its location - it's at low latitudes, where the Coriolis force isn't very strong, so the deformation radius is quite large - and it's not at all surprising to find the largest vortex there.

    Anticyclonic storms, or storms which 'spin' in the wrong direction (like Great Red Spot), are not impacted by the Coriolis effect because they are so 'small' compared to the planet's size.

    This is definitely not true for the Great Red Spot. The Coriolis force keeps the system together. In general, this is also not true for any large anti-cyclonic system. On Earth, they aren't storms, but rather large blocking highs (Rex or Omega blocks) and bring fair weather.

    To be honest, even calling the Great Red Spot a "storm" isn't accurate - it's a vortex, but the interior is very quiescent.
