    • Sermoln

      I'm not an expert in noise induced hearing loss but basically: If it hurts, you're definitely damaging your ears. If it doesn't hurt, it still might be doing damage to your hearing.

      There's this rule I read about that says you should only listen to 60% volume for up to 60 minutes a day with earbuds (this is kinda silly, because not all volume ranges are the same, and some music is louder than others)

      But if you're having trouble hearing with headphones at lower volumes it's worth investing for better fitting earbuds/headphones. If they can fit into your ears, it'll block out other sounds so that you can hear the music easier.

      I also recommend earplugs for loud events, like concerts or monster truck riding.

    Good answer! Except the damage is the opposite... Yes, high pitches are more painful, but lower bass tones are actually a lot more destructive to your ears. The bass tones carry more energy, and are like someone banging on your door with a battering ram, as opposed to the high tones which are more like someone rapping incessantly on your door with a cane. The later is more annoying, but the former is more destructive.

    Also, your ear has an internal mechanism that can sort of turn off your ear, if it needs to, to protect itself from dangerous it distracting noises, in an emergency. (Source: "On Combat: the psychology and physiology of deadly combat in war and peace" by Grossman and Christensen)... This is why a lot of people who, for example, accidentally shoot themselves in the leg only hear the shoot as a bit of a pop, and don't have ringing ears after, because they had (if only subconsciously/instinctually) a slight warning that the sound was coming.
