      • DillingerRadio

        Hi there, actually in micro right now as part of pre-med stuff.

        Bottom line: soap doesn't really kill any bacteria. Soap is a surfactant (in industrial - not medical - terms). Basically it helps break down the barrier between lipids/oils and water, making it easier for water to carry off microbes. In addition to that, it holds the oils and any additional particles - e.g. dirt - in suspension (wrapping it up in soap molecules like a rubber ducky germ suit.) This all makes it easier for the water to wash the microbes off your hands. You're furthering this removal mechanically when you wipe your hands on a paper-towel or whatever (actually why a paper towel is better for your 'cleanliness' than an air-dryer).

        In fact, if you culture someone's hands immediately following a hand washing you'll find a large and lovely assortment of microbiota growing on your plate the next day. This is not a bad thing, the normal microbiota are pretty standard across most humans and generally aren't anything to be worried about. To further assuage your concerns about soap, think of it as a washing off what doesn't belong (and keeping what does). If you truly want to destroy the life on your hands use ~70% or higher concentration alcohol-based hand sanitizer. I personally recommend you don't - unless you're actually in the medical field - because really the bacteria on your hands don't matter (edit: and washing your hands is more than sufficient). But you do you.

        As far as confirming/denying /u/Loki_123 's statement. Yes G- bacteria have a very thin PG layer sandwiched by an inner and outer membrane; whereas G+ have a thick PG layer and singular inner membrane. The gram negative/positive notation comes from the fact that the thick PG layer in G+ strains is what actually holds the gram stain in place (it collapses in on itself during staining, trapping the violet stain while the thin membrane of the G- permits the violet stain to be drawn out by alcohol during the process).

        Edit: /u/celo753 that's a big ol' negative. Bacteria do not have protein coats, peptidoglycan is a fancy polysaccharide (sugar). Viruses - on the other hand - do have a protein based coat that's known as a capsid.
