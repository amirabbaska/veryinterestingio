    • mfb-

      Galileo didn't have a chance. Without satellites for direct observations, Newton's law of gravity (~1680) is the only way to approach this, and it was formulated after Galileo's death.

      The easiest approach is probably to determine the orbits of Earth and Venus as precise as possible, to about 1 part in a million (with the precise number depending on what exactly you measure). In 1860 measurements were precise enough to measure the perihelion precession of Mercury with an uncertainty of just 10% of the effect of Earth, at that point a second Earth would have been clearly notable. While the perihelion shift didn't match predictions with or without a counter-earth (later resolved by General relativity) other calculations would have easily spotted it.

      That leaves a 200 year time frame.
