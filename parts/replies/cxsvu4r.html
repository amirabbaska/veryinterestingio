        Such a thing won't stop an attacker or anyone who is determined to index your site specifically, but will often make the unattended spam-scanners go away.

    • loljetfuel

      Not only possible, it's actually less work than making a crawler respect it.

      All the reputable search engines respect the robots protocol because it's mutually beneficial (e.g. I often include robots directives to keep the search engine from indexing failover copies of data; they wouldn't want to link that anyway, and it helps me by reducing the amount of bandwidth the crawlers consume).

    • jpsi314

      Yeah, that's how it works. Basically the crawler visits all the urls on a list and it adds to that list any links it discovers as it is crawling. If a site isn't on the initial list and isn't discovered then it remains invisible to the crawler. There is no reason your company's internal URL would be on the initial list at say, Google, but if someone publishes it somewhere public (like Twitter, but not Facebook if it is somewhere you'd have to be logged in to view) then it's likely to make it into a crawler's list.

    • collinsl02

      Yes, and yes.

      Crawlers can't find anything that isn't linked to, and if it gets linked to they will find it eventually, depending on the permissions of the social networking site - most allow crawlers to index comment pages.

      In fact, this is one of the ways search engines rank where pages appear in their list - the number of links found to each page is one of the factors used in how high up the list the page goes.
