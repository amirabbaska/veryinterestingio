      • forams_galorams

        I think those requirements are a little muddled in terms of what happens to the trees and I'm not sure where the decomposer narrative is coming from. Peat formation relies on the preservation or at least incomplete breakdown of carbon compounds in the organic material before burial.

        Aside from the huge swathes of forest across Carboniferous landmasses, the other factor in creating such significant coal deposits was the absence of wood eating bacteria, specifically anything that could break down lignin or suberin. Bacteria with the relevant enzymes did not appear until right at the end of the Carboniferous period, so there was a good 60 million years of great initial conditions for coal formation.

        Coal deposits have formed before and since the Carboniferous, although it has never been such a global phenomenon. Despite this, significant coal deposits were formed in the Jurassic and especially Cretaceous, the latter creating what is now the Northern Alaska-Slope Coal Province with billions of tonnes of low-ash, low-sulfur coal.

        This represents an extremely effective carbon sequestering system despite the presence of decomposers, due chiefly to the fact that the Cretaceous saw extensive forests in polar regions. Virtually wholesale deciduousness or winter die-back of vegetation, coupled with winter temperatures low enough to limit decay (no sunlight for weeks on end), high precipitation and delta sediment compaction all led to preservation of large amounts of terrestrial organic matter.

        I know I've gone on a bit, I'm just trying to stress the importance of preservation rather than decay in the coal forming process.

    • lythronax-argestes

      There were already various decomposers which were adapted to break down the simply constructed "early" plants. Lignin, however, is an extremely complicated polymer that is extensively cross-linked, so for quite a while decomposers just couldn't break it down. Compare lignin with the more linear cellulose, which was more dominant before then. Very few lineages of fungi managed to evolve the complex enzymatic machinery needed to tear apart lignin; once they appeared, we see a rather sensible decrease in coal production [1].
