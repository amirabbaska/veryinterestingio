      Sometimes, book lungs can be absent, and gas exchange is performed by the thin walls inside the cavity instead, with their surface area increased by branching into the body as thin tubes called tracheae. The tracheae possibly have evolved directly from the book lungs, because in some spiders, the tracheae have a small number of greatly elongated chambers.

      Are the lungs modified versions of some homologous structure in the lungless spiders?

      Well, I think it's rather likely that the lungless spiders Caponiidae secondarily lost their book lungs, but they still breathe through a pair of tracheal tubes on the abdomen. Almost every other spider family has book lungs. Ticks, similar to the lungless spiders, breathe through a pair of spiracular plates. Not sure what kind of consensus can be made from this.

      But there's certainly chance that the common ancestor of all spiders did not have book lungs, since ticks and a few other arachnids don't.
