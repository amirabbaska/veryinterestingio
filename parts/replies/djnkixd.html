    I was surprised that the heads can be positioned so accurately on a track without position feedback. I think it's a little more complicated than that. Wikipedia says:

    Feedback of the drive electronics is accomplished by means of special segments of the disk dedicated to servo feedback. These are either complete concentric circles (in the case of dedicated servo technology), or segments interspersed with real data (in the case of embedded servo technology). The servo feedback optimizes the signal to noise ratio of the GMR sensors by adjusting the voice-coil of the actuated arm.

    I guess that the received signal strength can indicate how good the tracking of the heads is, and if needed, the disk can go read so dedicated calibration patterns to zero out any error it has. It still seems pretty amazing that they can whip the heads around so fast and have them stop on the right track.
