      • Icestar1186

        We can make a grain of sand to have same mass as a bowling ball, we just need to accelerate it to astronomical speeds. So expelling one grain of sands worth of material with high enough velocity the effect is same as expelling a bowling ball that has less velocity.

        This is technically correct, but misleading. What actually matters is the momentum (mass times velocity). You don't have to hit relativistic velocity for your sand to be as effective as a slower bowling ball. Based on a couple of google searches, a bowling ball has a mass of about 5 kg, and a grain of sand has a mass of about 0.000666 kg. The bowling ball has 7500 times as much mass, so the sand needs to go about 7500 times faster to have the same momentum. A good cutoff for "relativistic velocity" is one tenth the speed of light. Dividing that amount by 7500, we find that if you are launching your bowling balls slower than around 4000 m/s, a comparable grain of sand will still be going slower than relativistic speeds and the effect on its actual mass will be negligible.

    • mikk0384

      If you consider the combustion chamber in the engine, it is basically just a tube that is open in one end. When you ignite the fuel on the inside, the pressure inside increases, and it pushes on all the walls of the chamber equally - only, at one end, the wall isn't as big - it has the opening. This means that the fuel that would have pushed the rocket downwards instead escapes the engine, leaving only the parts of the fuel that pushes upwards and outwards, giving the thrust.
