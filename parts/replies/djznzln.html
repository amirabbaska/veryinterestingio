    • Lagrangsch

      Maybe I was a bit unclear.

      First let's talk about the particle thing: On-shell particle means, that the excitation of the field satisfies the classical equations of motion, has a certain energy, momentum, spin, charge, ... . For scattering experiments, we take these particles to be asymptotic states, i.e. at times far away from the scattering (plus and minus infinity), they are plane-waves with given energy-momentum. But you can also take them to be distributions, e.g. gaussian type (so a localised particle). These are the particles we can observe.

      The scattering experiments I considered above, like electron-positron scattering, are elastic, that means the energy is conserved in this process. What we observe is incoming and outgoing particles, of which we can measure energy, momentum, the scattering angle, ...

      What happens inbetween these two states, in and out, is what we describe mathematically using perturbation theory. In this framework, we say that there are intermediate, "virtual" states of the system, e.g. exchange of virtual photons, loop corrections and so on. However, this is just our mathematical description. There are no actual photons exchanged between the two particles.

      So in short: Electron field and electromagnetic field interact with each other, to describe this interaction, we use a perturbative framework. In this framework, the notion of "virtual particles" arises.

      So it's best to not think of these disturbances as particles (in fact, it's probably best if we don't think of particles at all, but rather of fields :) )
