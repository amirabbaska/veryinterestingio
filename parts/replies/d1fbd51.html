    The relativistic Doppler effect takes into account the time dilation of the moving object and is governed by sqrt((1+beta)(1-beta)) where beta=v/c. This is always greater than 1 for an object moving towards the observer, so no, there's no speed at which the observer would receive them at a slower rate.

    • widdma

      Don't forget beta is signed! It's negative when the source and observer are moving towards each other. The relativistic Doppler factor can be anywhere in (0,inf).

      This is why you have both blue and red shifting --- speeding up and slowing down of the received rate of a ticking clock.

    Why would the (non-relativistic) Doppler effect influence when a pulse starts and stops rather than the frequency ("pitch") of a pulse?

    • ThereOnceWasAMan

      Instead of pulses, imagine the (stationary )craft is shooting out individual photons at the observer. From the craft's perspective, these photons are sent out at a rate of one per second. The observer receives these photons at a rate of one per second.

      Now say the craft starts moving towards the observer, still emitting one photon per second. At t=0 seconds, the craft emits a photon. Between t=0 and t=1 seconds, the craft is waiting to emit its second photon. But while it is waiting it is also "chasing" the first photon. When the craft finally does emit the second photon at t=1 second, it's emitting that photon from a position that is closer to the observer than the starting position of the first photon. So at that instantaneous moment, the distance between photon 1 and photon 2 is not (1 second)*(speed of light), as it was in the stationary case. It is (1 second)*(speed of light) - (1 second)*(speed of craft) (which can be rewritten as (1 second)*(speed of light - speed of craft)). This means that when the photons are received by the observer, time between their reception is not (1 second)*(speed of light)/(speed of light) = 1 second, as would be true in the stationary craft case, but rather (1 second)*(speed of light - speed of craft)/(speed of light) which is, of course, less than 1 second.
