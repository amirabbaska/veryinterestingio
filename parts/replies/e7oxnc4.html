      That's part of it. But if we assume it's actually capable of not bursting at all, forget that it's water for a minute. A really big factor is the basic physics of forces.

      Start with a drop moving at a given velocity.

      If the drop hits a surface head-on, it hits at that velocity. End of story.

      But if the same drop hits the surface at a more glancing angle, part of its movement is across the surface. That means its closing velocity is less. It hits the surface slower, so it doesn't hit as hard; it's as simple as that. The more glancing the angle, the lower the closing speed, and the gentler the collision.

      After that, yes, then it's down to how liquids behave - surface tension and so forth - as to whether or not it actually bursts.

      (At heart it's at least partly the difference between a racing car scraping along a wall at speed and the same car hitting a barrier head-on at the same speed. In the first case, if the car's remotely well constructed, it will probably lose little more than a few lumps of bodywork. Whereas in the second, if the barrier's not designed to cushion one heck of a collision, you really wouldn't want to be in that car.)
