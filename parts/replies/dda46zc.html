    Specifically, the Reticular Activating System: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reticular_activating_system

    I'm no expert in this (but am married to someone who is!), so I've heard the ELI5 on it and can relay. The RAS monitors your senses when you sleep - not just external stimuli, but temperature and other sensations (like having to go to the bathroom). When any of those stimuli start to suggest problems, the RAS kicks off a cascade of other brain-stem activity that wakes you up. How quickly you wake depends on how far out of normal those input stimuli are: a sudden loud noise represents an emergency, and your brain pulls out all the stops to get you up and moving in a hurry.

    This system depends on other parts of your brain to learn which stimuli are worth waking for. The absence of a stimuli can trigger it (sudden silence in a usually noisy environment), and common loud noises (horns, sirens, and garbage trucks in the city) will eventually get ignored. You can also learn to be far more sensitive to certain things: parents of young children usually wake very quickly to unusual noises or changes in the environment.
