    • Unique_username1

      Yes. The energy has to go somewhere and as a rule of thumb, it usually becomes heat.

      Don't quote me on this but my understanding is the dielectric has polar molecules which align with the electric field. Thus, while a capacitor is electrostatic rather than "chemical" energy storage, the chemicals in it physically respond to the field and their positioning is responsible for its performance compared to empty space, for example.

      This is true for inductors, and "saturation" is when all the dipoles are aligned and this effect cannot continue to improve performance (beyond that of empty space). Disclaimer: I may be mistakenly applying this concept to capacitors.

      So the losses are essentially friction as the electric field is causing motion (alignment) of the molecules to the polarity of the field.
