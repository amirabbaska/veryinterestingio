    • SurprisedPotato

      John von Neumann "proved" that hidden variables theories are impossible, in 1932.

      Grete Hermann found a flaw in his proof in 1935, but the physics community didn't notice until fifty years later, and as you can see from the other comments here, the general public still hasn't caught up.

      Von Neumann's theorem rules out certain types of hidden variables theories, but not others. The 'pilot wave' theory is an example of a hidden variable interpretation of QM that isn't excluded by Bell's inequalities or with von Neumann's article.

    • GaugeSym

      The basic idea is describing a system in a way with hidden variables and in a quantum mechanical way and showing that these descriptions are not compatible.

      Description1:

      Lets say you have two objects O^1 and O^2 that have three properties A,B,C (e.g.: size, colour, weight) that take one of two values (big/small, red/green, light/heavy) that can be described by either 0 or 1. These are our hidden variables. Now we demand that both objects are identical in all three properties (statement I) and put them in two different boxes. From this, one can derive in a straight forward way the following inequality for what you will find by opening the boxes (measuring):

      P(A^1 =B^2 )+P(A^1 =C^2 )+P(B^1 =C^2 )>1 or =1

      where P(X^1 =Y^2 ) is the probability that property X of O^1 and Y of O^2 have the same value (both 0 or both 1).

      Description 2:

      Now we do the same thing but with the difference that our properties A,B,C are defined in a quantum mechanical way. Namely, they are projectors living on a 2 dimensional hilbert space H=span{|0>,|1>}. The whole system (both boxes) can be described as the product of two such hilbert spaces. If you define the three projectors A,B,C in the right way, then you can find a state living on this combined hilbert space that fulfills statement I from above but violates the inequality from above when you calculate the probabilities in the usual quantum mechanical way.

      Therefore description 1 (hidden variables) and 2 (quantum mechanics) are not compatible.

      You can prepare photons in an entangled state that resembles this whole analogy. By conducting the experiment one finds that the predictions by description 2 but not 1 are compatible with the measurements.
