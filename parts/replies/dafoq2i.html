    Well hold on a minute here...

    There is no naturalistic boundary between what I normally conceptualize as 'me' and what I conceptualize as 'others'-- I am a collection of atoms working together as a system, there are more interactions within this system than there are between this system and things outside of this system, however this is a difference of degrees, rather than absolutes. Such a conceptualization indeed warrants a fuzzy boundary of self.

    However my perspective is discrete and bounded. I am aware of my subjective perspective, you are aware of your subjective perspective, and we are not mutually aware of each others' subjective experiences, despite there being interactions between us. The same applies to any two people. We may share information about the world, but the subjective perspectives we each possess are singular, individualized, and discrete (Unless you're a philosophical zombie, in which case you have none), rather than gradient. If my subjective experience was wholly derivative of interactions in the above sense, I would not have a bounded subjectivity-- my sense of subjectivity would be isomorphic with the interactions which comprise it, and it would be gradient.

When you are in the latter state: you still perceive, but it is no longer tied to your construed persona, there is just an 'it' observing that is no longer a 'you'. Without a sense of self, everything feels objective, instead of subjective. There is no longer a 'you' separate from the external world, the boundaries between the external and internal have blurred to an extent that the duality is no longer there: everything is one, everything is interconnected.
