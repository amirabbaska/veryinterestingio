      • ryancp89

        Sorry about the confusion. I'm going to have to get a little a little deeper into terminology for this one. When setting up the sequence of numbers we've been referencing, this is called the Key Bitting Aray (KBA). Typically this is what your master keying is based on, but I will dive into that on another comment I saw asking about master keying.

        Lets choose a KBA: 274985

        If you notice, the numbers alternate odd and even so that there is less of a risk to duplicate numbers next to one another. The reason you don't want to do this is because it will create a large flat spot in your key, which is not a good idea. Once you have your KBA, you expand your key system using a method called progression. This is very similar to how an odometer operates. Example of a 2 step progression:

        Step 1: * 274987 274989 274981 274983

        Step 2:

        • * 276987 276989 276981 276983...and so on

        Each progression is called a block.There are 4 sequences in a block, 4 blocks in a Vertical Group, and 4 vertical groups per page. This can be extended to 4 pages per KBA, 16 pages per KBA, and 64 pages per KBA(max for a 6-pin system).

        I think this might be a question so I want to clarify. The KBA is what locksmiths use as a guide to create a master key, so the KBA is really just used as a base. It is only used when pinning a cylinder that is only operated by the master key and nothing else.

        Hopefully this helps to clarify...I had to open my keying manual for the terms, but sometimes it's just easier to use them.

    Just to add-on to this: Many locks in the same box/shipment are configured with the same pin settings, making it easier for customers to just have one key to open multiple locks without going through the trouble of changing the pins. If you go to a store and look at locks, they will often have a 5-6 digit number on the package which indicates the pin settings. Sometimes this number is even on your keys!
