    • raycluster

      An electron's spin is always either parallel or antiparallel (opposite direction) with respect to all directions. This is independent of any magnetic fields, that may or may not be present. The spin is never at a 90° angle (or 30° or 60°) to anything, but it can be in a superposition of parallel + antiparallel, with some complex amplitudes.

      If the spin is parallel in the X direction, it is in a superposition of parallel+antiparallel in the Y direction. If it's parallel in the Y direction, there will be a superposition in the X direction. That's what's known as uncertainty principle: not all observables are determined at the same time by the same state.

      So when the electron enters the magnetic (gradient) field, the dipole moment of the spin will result in a force on the electron, sensitive to the spin state along to the field lines. In general, this will be a superposition, so the parallel-spin component of the electron experiences a different force than the antiparallel-spin component. The electron will be in a superposition of two different trajectories. The spin is not "rotated" though, the spin state of the electron stays the same when entering the field. Only after a measurement the state of the electron spin will be purely parallel or antiparallel to the magnetic field, and the "true" trajectory of the particular electron is determined.

      A great demonstration of these effects is the Stern-Gerlach experiment. It is usually implemented with an uncharged particle though, to make the deflection less complicated.
