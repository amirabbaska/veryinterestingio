    I’d be careful not to confuse the issues here, though. The plastic changes in the CNS occur because those circuits are, by function, supposed to be plastic and adaptive. The mesolimbic circuit is there to encode incentive salience to stimuli perceived as rewarding, so that the subject learns to pursue that kind of reward in the future. Peripheral and enteric circuits don’t function that way. They operate either as relay points or to maintain homeostasis.

    It’s well known that people starting SSRIs tend to have gastrointestinal side effects for some amount of time. But the number of people for whom this is a serious and long-term complaint seems fairly low. So the enteric nervous system probably just reaches a new homeostatic set point at the elevated serotonin level and maintains that set point, just as it does the “normal” one in “normal” people.

    The serotonin pathways in the gut just aren’t meant to rewire themselves based on life experience like the ones in the CNS. They regulate things to reach a stable equilibrium.

    I would be interested to know if this isn’t entirely the case, though.
