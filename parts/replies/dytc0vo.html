    In a similar vein, you can use a "cooler" to keep food hot, or a "blanket" to keep things cold.

    I recently had some groceries I needed to keep cool in the car, so I put a couple blankets over them. The people I were with thought this would "heat them up faster" since blankets are associated with keeping you warm. This is when I realized that most people think of "hot" and "cold" as discrete physical properties, not just relative values (my pizza oven is quite cold--when compared to the sun).

    Blankets and coolers are both just thermal insulators, and a thermal insulator is just something good at preventing the temperature of things on one side be affected by the temperature of things on the other side.

    OP's question is based on the same misunderstanding, but in reality, liquid water is just good at making other things the same temp as the water, whether it's hotter or colder.
