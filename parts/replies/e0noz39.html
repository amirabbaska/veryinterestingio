    • MS3FGX

      Communication satellites going to geosynchronous orbit are still monsters. But miniaturization has certainly given rise to very tiny spacecraft, such as Cubesats.

      So yes and no. You still need a powerful rocket to loft a major communication relay, but we're also seeing an unprecedented number of small satellites that can be launched for a fraction of the cost.

    • libmaint

      A commercial communications satellite has to make a profit, the more the better. They must strike a balance between how many satellites they need in orbit at one time, the number they need over time, the cost of the satellite and the cost of the launches. There is a lot more, but those are the big requirements constraints. The net effect is usually as big a satellite as the available launchers can handle, with as much communications capability as possible, with as much lifetime as possible. Among other things, circuits getting smaller means they can launch more capacity using the same launcher, or include more fuel in the satellite for a longer lifetime, or a combination of the two.

      The economics of commercial satellite design is complex. If this kind of thing interests you, try to find a library with a subscription to Aviation Week & Technology and read it regularly (subscriptions are expensive).

    • whitcwa

      For the most part, the chips used in satellites are not digital. The transponders are analog amplifiers. They just happen to be carrying a lot of digital signals. The signals are modulated using analog techniques. Some newer satellites do demodulate digital data and re-modulate it, but that does not use much power.

      There are many small satellites, but they can't reach small earth antennas.
