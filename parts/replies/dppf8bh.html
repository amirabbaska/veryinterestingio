    • DramShopLaw

      The experiment measured the charge of an electron. This was done by admitting a fine mist of oil into a chamber built with two plates at the top and bottom. Mist droplets were allowed to fall through a hole at the top and a voltage was applied to the plates. Electrons were then beamed across the mist flow.

      By measuring the rate at which the oil blobs fell with the voltage on and off, they could determine that the charge that had accumulated on the drops during descent was always a whole number multiple of a certain value. That value must be the charge of a single electron.

    • Nemo309

      You're welcome! The follow up answers, as best I can offer:

      1) I don't know anything about Faraday's actual technique. I can only say that he was able to very closely calculate the number of elections in a gas. Perhaps your description is correct.

      2) Before the charge was ever calculated, the mole was already established. Avogadro didn't know about electrons, and he never came up with 6.02x10^23 . Essentially, he needed to conduct calculations on a given "lump" if mass. He worked I believe mostly with gases, and gases are tricky beasts because their volumes change all. In order to make a standard "lump" of hydrogen, etc, he used the a.u. and just made the mole that many grams. So a mole of hydrogen (a.u. 1.01) could be understood across Europe as 1.01 grams.

      Exactly how many particles we're in that lump wouldn't be discovered until long after Avogadro had died. The eponymous constant is honorary.

      There's of course more to the story than I know. For example, someone else but me could tell you why Avogadro needed the constant to be a different amount of mass for each gas - I think it relates to how gases take up volume - instead of just doing calculations with 10g if each gas.
