    Lastly, and perhaps most importantly, limiting the number of people who know the really big secrets helps a lot. Compartmentalization, or the "need to know policy," basically says that you limit the number of people who know something to the bare minimum who need to know it. The more people who know a secret, the harder it is to keep — every person you let "in" to it is another person who might later leak or spy or screw up.

    The latter point is really important. The secrets that have really had a chance of being held for a long time tend to be pretty small groups of people. (The Indian nuclear program surprised the CIA in part because it was so small — every scientist was doing two or three jobs, essentially. This made it very hard to detect.)

    Which is precisely why I don't buy into any of those vast conspiracies that, somehow, thousands of whackos buy into. The Earth is flat? So you convinced every scientist on the planet to just sit on that little nugget? Alternatively, you pay thousands scientists to do science when you know their entire premise is bunk? Cmon. Same for conspiracies about chemtrails, vaccines, GMOs, 9/11, and on and on and on and on.
