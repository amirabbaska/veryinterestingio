    • holomntn

      It is somewhat debatable. There were some concepts from Calculus around, but they had not been put together. They were also far from complete.

      A couple of examples. I remember a thought experiment from the Greeks. How do you measure the outline of a human hand? It turns out that if you have infinitely many infinitely small particles that you can measure it, but the Greeks had never found a way to achieve this. It wasn't until Calculus that a reliable solution was achieved.

      I don't remember who originally observed this one. The double pendulum. https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_pendulum gives an explanation. But again while the device is ancient, and there was some effort at a solution, the solution wasn't complete. The double pendulum is shockingly difficult to solve and the solution is relatively recent.

      So there were some glimmers of Calculus before, but nothing that we could genuinely say was Calculus as Newton discovered it.

      Edit: spelling and

      I almost forgot the Antikythera Mechanism. Basically an ancient Greek sky calculator. Getting it right requires Calculus. The Greeks tried and using their methods got shockingly close, but they still missed. The difference wouldn't be noticeable for quite some time. Newton's Calculus allowed for vastly higher precision.
