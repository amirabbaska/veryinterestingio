    Why is it that the image of the supernova takes a discrete set of paths around the galaxy in between, rather than kind of showing up in a continuous ring?

    • xea123123

      Your intuition about what you ought to see is correct, for gravitational lenses which are perfectly uniform and perfectly in the way of the object you're seeing an image of. A black hole directly in the path between you and a very bright object would result in you seeing a continuous ring of light.

      Most of the gravitational lenses astronomers have been able to collect images from are galaxies, and galaxies come in many shapes which aren't spherical. The lenses we're seeing images through aren't uniform, so the images they reflect aren't uniform.

      Here is an image which shows what you get when sunlight passes through a lens onto a surface and isn't focused in just a certain way: There's one very bright spot and one slightly less bright spot, because the light happens to be deflected that way by that shape of lens in that orientation. (That example doesn't have much in common with the gravitational lenses we've seen, aside from being nonuniform and creating a nonuniform image)

      Galaxies, being shaped like galaxies instead of like magnifying glasses, tend to produce other numbers of images, like the Einstein Cross pattern.

      Well, let's start at the beginning. The supernova occurs, releasing light in all directions. Because we're so far away, the light has been gravitationally accelerated/decelerated(relative to us, through bending at different angles) many times.

      I would say it's conceivable that after being bent so many times, gaps in arrival times occur.

      The other possibility is that Hubble only needs to observe the supernova for a certain period to get a clear image, and so a ring would never appear in an image produced by Hubble.
