    The ozone hole isn't really an area that 'ran out' of ozone because the ozone layer isn't a permanent collection of ozone molecules.

    Ozone is continuously generated when O2 molecules high in the atmosphere absorb a UV photon (specifically UVC, I think). Ozone is already pretty unstable, so it breaks down on its own. The relative production and decomposition rates determines the steady-state ozone concentration. CFCs (chloroflourocarbons) offer an additional decomposition pathway for ozone, which speeds up the overall decomposition rate, which results in a lower steady-state concentration.

    So if you took a crapload of ozone and sent it into the hole, the concentration would settle back towards the same levels. (If you are comfortable with some math, think of it in terms of an ODE: dC/dt = production - decomposition. No matter the initial conditions, the system will always settle to a situation where production = decomposition.)
