    • javier1287

      I hope you understand photography, because it's the only way I have to try to explain it.

      Imagine NMR is a picture you are taking of the protons for 3 seconds. If they stay still, you will get their picture clearly. When you have equilibrium, where A is converting to B and B to A, it is in equilibrium because both species are interconverting at the same speed, so the concentration doesn’t change (but both of them are actively changing). But the A specie can convert to B after a few milliseconds, return to A after a few more ms, and in a 3 second period can be A and B many times.

      So when you take a picture of a person physically changing position between 2 places many times during the picture, you don’t see a clear picture of the person in both position, but a blurred trail between those two positions. In NMR happens something similar (again, I’m talking about 1H-NMR, not MRI). If a proton of A appears at 3 ppm, and the same proton in B appear at 4 ppm, you won’t see both signals, but an average one between 3 and 4 ppm. The way to separate them is reducing the temperature, so the speed decreases and A spends most of those 3-4 seconds as A without changing into B, and vice versa. There is a specific temperature (coalescence temperature) where the average signal separates into both signals (A and B) and it can be used to get information about the equilibrium process.

    • Nowhere_Man_Forever

      Many chemicals are in a state called "chemical equilibrium" which is the state with the lowest free energy. This is often not actually a pure compound, but instead a mixture where some compounds are the "product" of a reaction and some are the "reactants." What the other commenter is saying is that it doesn't take long for a pure chemical to go into equilibrium, and so it's essentially impossible to get a pure NMR spectrum for some chemicals.
