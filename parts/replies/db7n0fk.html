    • martheen

      In CDMA and W-CDMA (3G) it's possible for adjacent towers to operate on the same frequency due to their nature. Handover, when you're leaving one tower to other can use 'soft handover' where for a period you're connected to the new tower without disconnecting from the previous tower. The phone doesn't send the signal twice, just encode the signal with two different code simultaneously, then send the 'mix'. Think about listening to an ambassador speaking in her native language overlaid with English translation. Only one audio channel, yet it can be understood by either language speaker just fine, and if they compare notes later, they might even be able to deduce unclear words. Indeed in soft handover phase the loss from interference can be reduced (clearer voice, or maybe higher effective connection speed). This is also why adjacent towers can use the same frequency, you can talk just fine with your friends in crowded party if everyone else are talking in foreign languages.

      1G, 2G & 4G meanwhile use different frequencies on adjacent towers. This mean no soft handover (although in 4G the switch to the next tower is near instant enough to not drop your call or download).

    • thekeffa

      Normally, just one. It will be the one that returns the strongest return signal and therefore polls as being closest. It's not unusual for a phone to be within range of several towers, particularly in Europe. The phone will use whichever one gives it the best return.

      Not so normally, new technology has been developed that allows a phone to use more than one tower if it's available to it. This has benefits for things like channel bonding thus increasing bandwidth which has all sorts of uses, though it's unlikely to be offered to people down at consumer level (Probably emergency services and focused requirement users) given it would likely stretch capacity and the specific hardware requirements of the handsets.
