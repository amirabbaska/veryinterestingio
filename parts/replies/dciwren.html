  • z3r0sand0n3s

    Part of the problem with globalization is that certain areas tend to get hit extremely hard. In the U.S.'s case, one big example is manufacturing (because it's cheaper labor).

    This argument is a pet peeve of mine, because it's (partially/mostly) wrong. Or at least incomplete.

    Factory production is actually way up in the US - since 1979, "American factory production, minus raw materials and some other costs, more than doubled over the same span to $1.91 trillion last year" (emphasis mine).

    The problem isn't that our manufacturing is down. As a whole, it's not (yes, some sectors suck more than others). The problem is that, due to robotics and automation, we need WAY less warm bodies to generate that level of production.

    Yes, trade does make a difference. Companies should try to keep as many Americans employed as possible, the economy doesn't benefit from people with no disposable income. But it's a smaller part of the problem than just simple technological advancement.
