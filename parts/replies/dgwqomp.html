    Extreme nitpick, but nothing can reach 0 K from either direction. There is no highest temperature, just like there is no lowest temperature; you can always make something hotter and you will never reach 0 K from below, just like you can always make something colder and you will never reach 0 K from above.

    To add on to your explanation: this weird behavior makes more sense if you flip the definition of temperature upside down. “Thermodynamic beta” is defined as β = dS/dU. Higher thermodynamic beta means colder, and lower means warmer. Absolute +0 on the temperature scale corresponds to +∞ on the beta scale, and -0 temperature corresponds to -∞. Zero beta would correspond to ±∞ temperature, but zero beta would mean zero change in entropy over time, which is prohibited by the second law of thermodynamics.

    Temperature is simply the reciprocal of beta. This explains the strange behavior: if you look at the graph of f(x) = 1/x, you can see that f(x) always decreases when x increases, but f(x) is always positive when x > 0 and negative when x < 0. This is only possible because f(0) is not defined. This is exactly how temperature works: an increase in temperature is always hotter, but negative temperatures are hotter than positive. This is only possible because 0 K is undefined.
