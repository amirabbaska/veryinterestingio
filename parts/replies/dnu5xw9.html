    A lot of dialects carry that stigma. I talked about this in another comment, but I was pretty amazed to see the difference in how dialect is treated differently in Switzerland and Germany. In Germany, if you speak Standard German you're educated and accomplished, and if you speak dialect you're a backwoods hillbilly (exaggerated and varies by location, but that's more or less how its seen). National TV features very little spoken dialect, and if it does, it's mostly sanitized so that everyone can understand it (i.e. someone from Hamburg can still understand the Bavarian farmer).

    In Switzerland, everyone speaks dialect. Doesn't matter if you're a farmer from the Alpine village with a population of 10 or a banker from Zurich, everyone speaks their regional dialect. Now, most Swiss dialects are mutually intelligible (except for the Valais, nobody understands them), but they're still markedly different. And on Swiss TV, everyone speaks their own variant. But the most striking thing is that regardless of education level, everyone speaks in dialect. There's no "uneducated" stigma associated with dialect in Switzerland, which was a stark contrast to the German attitude.

      Talking about stigma, I think that some UK accents have stigma to others within the UK. (Warning: I'm getting on my hobby horse...)

      For example, National TV news tends to be over-represented by people who speak in RP (Received Pronunciation, a sort of standardised form of the middle class south eastern accent). RP was originally chosen by the BBC as their standard accent, as they believed it sounded sober and authoritative - and more 'educated'.

      There's a kind of unspoken hierarchy of accents that are deemed acceptable for 'responsible' TV and radio jobs like news readers, or for national politicians. RP is the most common, followed by other accents from the home counties, and middle class Edinburgh accents are also quite common in national news, presumably for similar reasons.

      There have been some interesting surveys ( 1, 2 ) about the British public's opinions of various well-known regional accents, in general terms, and people with south eastern accents (or Yorkshire accents only more recently) are percieved as more intelligent, and people with eastern Scottish, Welsh and Irish (Republic) accents were percieved as more trustworthy. And people with Birmingham, Liverpool or Manchester accents are generally percieved badly on every measure. Interestingly, people who speak in RP are likely to be percieved as more intelligent, but less trustworthy.

      There are more people who speak in regional accents coming through, especially on regional news, and gradually on national news. However, no dialect or slang words are used.

      There are also more politicians being elected who have the accent of the area they represent, but it's still not unusual to see situations like my home town where a London-born, privately educated person, who speaks in RP, represents a constituency with mostly working class people, who speak in their own local accent which is very different.

      Some people in the UK argue in favour of teaching kids to speak 'proper' English, or RP, in order to ensure that we're mutually intelligible to each other across the different regions. But I'm not sure that there is an issue of mutual intelligibility because a big exception seems to be comedy.

      Lots of stand up comedians have quite strong regional accents. John Bishop is a broad Scouser, Rhod Gilbert has a strong south Wales accent, Johnny Vegas is very noticeably from Lancashire, Billy Connolly is a strong Glaswegian, and there are lots more comedians with regional accents who've achieved the top level of success on a national level. If they were unintelligible to people outside their region, their jokes wouldn't land, and they wouldn't be popular - but that's far from the case.

      My conclusion is that there's a hell of a lot of snobbery in the UK towards people with certain accents, and not for any rational reasons.
