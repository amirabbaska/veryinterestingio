    I went through the data from the PHMSA and this is what I found:

    • Between 2010 to present PHMSA has reported 2730 flagged incidents for liquid hydrocarbon spills. Flagged incidents are defined here: https://hip.phmsa.dot.gov/Hip_Help/pdmpublic_incident_page_allrpt.pdf

    • The PHMSA uses 5 different types of spilled commodities:
      • REFINED AND/OR PETROLEUM PRODUCT (NON-HVL) WHICH IS A LIQUID AT AMBIENT CONDITIONS
      • CO2 (CARBON DIOXIDE)
      • HVL OR OTHER FLAMMABLE OR TOXIC FLUID WHICH IS A GAS AT AMBIENT CONDITIONS
      • CRUDE OIL
      • BIOFUEL / ALTERNATIVE FUEL(INCLUDING ETHANOL BLENDS)

    For the purposes of this post, I looked only at liquid spills, so that's Refined product, Crude, and Biofuel.

    • I looked only at On-Shore incidents

    • I did not look at incidents in Totally contained, operator owned facilities (i.e spills in a spill containment which did not migrate out of the containment).

    At this point there were 574 incidents

    • I looked at all incidents above 5 bbl net spilled (Unintended spill + Intended spill - Recovered)

    207 Left

    • I looked at all incidents over 100 bbls spilled

    81 left

    • All incidents over 1000 bbls spilled

    22 left

    I should note here that only 18 of the total number of incidents were offshore. I should also note that the data for the deepwater horizon spill isn't in this report, so this report is for pipelines only.

    What I'm getting at here is that while there were over 2700 incidents in the last six years, the vast majority of those have been either C02, airborne (which isn't to say harmless) chemicals, or very small spills (like leaks in valves, etc). It's a relatively safe venture, when properly maintained. I do recognize that there are many aging pipelines, and that is worrisome. But on the other hand, if we don't build new ones, the old ones will continue to be used (here, not saying that they will replace the old ones with the new, but it limits our options if we don't build new, improved pipelines).

    The data can be found here: http://www.phmsa.dot.gov/pipeline/library/data-stats/flagged-data-files

    Edit: I suppose I should also add the disclaimer that this is all flagged incidents. Most operations are encouraged to report any and all incidents. This is primarily because small leaks do happen, and are usually repaired easily. If a site isn't reporting any issues, they can bet that the regulating body and inspectors will have them on the short list of sites to visit and inspect. And despite what people think, not reporting a large spill is a huge deal. Maybe not for the company, but for the guy(s) on the ground, it could mean loss of their job, personal fines, etc.

    Edit 2: I feel I should note that for liquid hydrocarbon pipelines, the number of fatalities since 2010 is 8 in this report. I don't know where the 80+ number comes from as the gaseous natural gas, plus LNG, plus crude is 19 fatalities in the reports I see. Perhaps I'm missing something...
