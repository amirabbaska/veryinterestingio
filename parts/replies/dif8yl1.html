      I'm not the poster you replied to, but, hopefully I can give a brief answer.

      You brought up compound interest, which basically means the interest for an entire year is "divided" and then applied at certain points over the course of the entire year. You could compound twice a year, or four times a year, or even every single day, or even every minute of every day.

      Assume you have a fixed interest rate, and you compound at different rates. As it turns out, the more often you compound during a year, the more money you'll end up with at the end. For example, suppose you have a 100% interest rate (not realistic obviously, but it makes the math easier) and you invest $1 at the beginning of the year.

      If you compound once over the whole year, you'll end up with $2.

      If you compound twice over that year, then you'll apply 50% interest ($100/2) twice, so you'll end up with $1(1.50)(1.50) or $2.25.

      If you compound n times over that year, you'll end up with (1+1/n)^n. As n goes up, this value goes up.

      But, the value doesn't go up forever, it levels off. Try plugging really big values of n into this equation, and you'll see it won't go higher than $2.71...This value is exactly $e. In fact, one way to define e is in terms of this limit, as n approaches infinity. So, if you compound continuously, which is like compounding every single second throughout the entire year, the exponential function pops out.
