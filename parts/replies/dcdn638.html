    Bohr's model was actually designed to answer the problem you just mentioned. When scientists (Planck) realized that atoms only emitted specific quantities of radiating energy after being energized, Bohr changed Rutherford's model to show why that might be. That's where we got the idea that an electron can be at, say, N1 and N2, but not N1.01, N1.02, etc. Bohr's model wasn't perfect, but it did present a solution to the stability issue that was found in Rutherford's model.
