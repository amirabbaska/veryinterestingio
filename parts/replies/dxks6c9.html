    Yeah, while we often talk about education funding in national politics, much of a schools funding is raised through local taxes. So money collected from a district stays in that district.

    That policy sounds pretty fair (you're paying directly for the schools in your own community), but it unfortunately exacerbates the larger issues of racial and economic inequality in America. America's housing is incredibly segregated along the lines of wealth and race, especially for a country that purports to have laws against such things.

    So this general rule of localities shouldering the burden of funding for their own schools results in schools in already rich (disproportionately white) districts getting more funding while schools in poorer (disproportionately non-white) districts struggle to make ends meet. Schools with fewer resources provide less opportunity for advancement. They earn a reputation as a "bad school district." People who have the means to relocate switch school districts to wealthier areas in the best interests of their children, taking their potential tax revenue with them. The cycle continues. Inequality grows.

    It's a pretty pervasive problem across many sectors in America. Too many people see equality as something we've already achieved by nature of being American rather than something we will always continue to strive for. So, when the time comes to make new rules within our existing systems, we demand that those rules apply to everyone equally without taking into account the inherent inequality that already exists because of these flawed systems.

    A similar example is affirmative action, or college admissions policies that contain diversity quotas. You often see the argument that these policies are unfair because they factor demographics into a process that should judge people on merit. But, that argument never properly addresses the fact that a candidate's "merit" is inherently affected by existing inequalities across demographic lines.

    The demand for equal treatment is a completely understandable notion, but equal treatment across an unequal playing field often just exacerbates existing inequality.
