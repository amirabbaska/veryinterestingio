  • raltodd

    Man, even the wikipedia page you cite ends with saying that the theory doesn't hold when you do proper stats (correcting for confounds).

    Heartbeat needs to be faster for smaller animals to ensure adequate blood flow. If you don't take that into account, what looked like a fascinating scaling law just becomes the trivial observations that large animals live longer (by the time an elephant is fully matured, you can grow 50 generations of mice).

      • thundersaurus_sex

        I mean, "theory" doesn't mean "hypothesis." This is a statistically confirmed trend. Human age isn't that off. Primates tend to buck the trend a little, but not too much. And while some birds provide exceptions, many others fit the bill just fine. Birds also tend to have longer lifespans in general, which would indicate something unique occuring in their evolutionary history. What's interesting is that the majority of animals do follow this trend and when exceptions do occur, they are typically found in specific clades (meaning a group of species that can be traced to a single common ancestor), something that indicates unique biology.

        The main thing is this isn't considered an absolute, perfectly predictive law. It's just a confirmed trend that is interesting and worth examining and probably applies to the original question.
