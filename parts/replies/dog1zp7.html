• tylerthehun

    You're missing the important caveat that that binding energy is mass. That's what E=mc^2 means. Sure none of the nucleons themselves are destroyed in the conversion, but a single atom of uranium has ever so slightly more mass than its constituent decay products due directly to that binding energy, and that excess mass is what is directly converted to energy which is utilized during fission.

    It sounds trivial to say binding energy is converted to other energy, but because energy and mass are one and the same, a particle with more binding energy does have more actual mass than one without, even if all the normal "massive" particles are conserved in the transition.
