    • snoos_antenna

      It has a massive loss rate if you aren't a bank. If you have paid the up-front cost of getting the hardware and software to plug in (like any major bank or brokerage has) then the cost of each trade is nothing.

      For individuals who pay by the trade, yes it is a great way to lose money. Also because of short-term capital gains/losses. US tax law states that that you can only claim $3,000 in net losses each year. You can carry this forward forever, but if you had a big loss it won't help much.

      Example: Suppose in year 1 you lose $100k. But in year 2 you gain $100k. What sucks is that in both years you only get to claim $3000 in losses. So your taxable capital loss in year 1 is -$3000, and you get to pay capital gains tax on $97k in year 2. You won't run out of that carryforward for 34 years.

      Between this and transaction costs, day trading is a terrible way to entertain yourself. If you're a business the costs are vastly lower and the tax treatment is different (it's just part of your business operations).

      I personally know someone who made lots of money in options for his first two years, paying short-term capital gains tax on them both times. Then he lost nearly all of it in year three. He can deduct the loss on his taxes but it'll take him forty years to do it at $3k per year. He is 72 today.
