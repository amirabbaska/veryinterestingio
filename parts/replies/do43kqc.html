    Essentially, this is because English spelling rules are a mess.

    I'd add that there's a distinction to be made here between two different ideas of "rules."

    There are the "rules" baked into the language. In English these are complicated because of how different languages (and languages from different families) are combined within English - caused by the waves of invaders over hundreds of years, who each brought their own language that got combined with everybody on the island - rather than a gradual ebb and flow of population through overland migration. People who come by boat can come more abruptly and come from farther away than people who walk over.

    And it's also because of how much the language of origin of words matters in English. We have more plurality of language of origin in our history than most other languages. And English continues to carries shades of meaning from this - people learn it without knowing they learn it.

    Then there are the "rules" of institutions - like national academies or printers' associations looking to conduct spelling reform.

    The job of these bodies is harder or easier depending on how much people would be asked to depart from what they are used to in order to adopt consistent rules. It's not just a simple matter of authority - it's about who listens.

    A big part of why the English attempt at a national language academy concurrent with the French and Italian language academies failed is that they did not understand the English language well enough to make rules for it that matched the information it was carrying out - and so people rejected and did not use a lot of their suggestions. And, missing the opportunity to get in on literacy on the ground floor, there's a lot of inertia to fight.

    If the contention is that no governing body has launched a major spelling reform in English since the onset of printing, that would be false.

    If the contention is that no governing body has sorted out a comprehensive spelling reform that English-speaking people at large have actually adopted, that would be more accurate.

    And it also follows that there is no single nationalistic central political entity in the English-speaking world with the authority to tell people how to spell things. That's why we are more dependent on coming up with changes that people adopt voluntarily. And so spelling improvements happen, but it is piecemeal and slow.

    This is not true of places like France or Italy which have longstanding language academies and more history with absolute, centralized forms of government.

      In English these are complicated because of how different languages (and languages from different families) are combined within English

      No, this is an oft-repeated myth, but the truth is a lot more subtle. English is not much more of a "mongrel tongue" than other European languages (even French, which likes to consider itself pure, is full of Celtic and Germanic influences).

      There are a few rare cases where spelling was tweaked to reflect its etymology -- an example is "debt", which was originally spelled "dette" until some well-meaning academics decided to add a "b" to reflect the Latin "debitum" -- but this isn't the norm.

      The influence of non-Germanic languages on English is surprisingly limited. Norman French in particular only really added some vocabulary relating to the things the Norman French were in charge of: jurisprudence, the military, government and royalty for the most part. And those words have been, for the most part, fully anglicised (e.g. Old French "jugier" became English "to judge") as far as both spelling and pronunciation go, because they were introduced into the language long before printing.

      The myth that English has "more plurality of language of origin", as you put it, than most other languages is usually supported by statistics that show that, for example, considerably more than 50% (some say as high as 75%) of our words are not Germanic in origin. This number is arrived at by simply counting the words in the dictionary, but this gives a false impression. Most of the non-Germanic words are recent borrowings from Latin or Greek (or even, in the case of Frankenstein words like "television", both) coined in modern times, especially technical jargon used in fields like medicine, chemistry, technology, etc. And those words exist in other European languages as well, for example:

      modern English modern German
      camera Kamera
      radio Radio
      video Video
      dialysis Dialyse
      abscess Abszess

      In fact, if you look at the way we talk normally, English is overwhelmingly Germanic. (I have italicised all the non-Germanic words in that sentence.)

      On the subject of a language academy, that's not what I meant at all. Bodies like the Académie Française actually have much less influence than they like to think, and actually they try to act as a brake on language evolution. This is one reason French spelling is also a horrible mess.

      I don't actually know of any body that made a serious attempt to regulate the language as such, but there are authorities that have had an influence. Noah Webster is one, which is why American spellings sometimes differ from British spellings.

      An example would be the German language. In this case, it's really just a style guide drawn up and occasionally revised on behalf of the state Culture Ministries (because they are in charge of school curricula) and used by central government. Media outlets and publishing houses then usually base their style guides heavily on that one, but they don't have to. In fact, the last spelling reform in 1996 has had to be revised a couple of times, in part because publishers refused to adopt some of the more obscure new rules.

      it also follows that there is no single nationalistic central political entity in the English-speaking world with the authority to tell people how to spell things

      It's simply too late for a reasonable spelling reform because English is so widespread and has so many different variants, it would be virtually impossible. For example, although most people rhyme "shone" with "gone", there are many who rhyme it with "bone".
