    • oranges7apples

      Aging it longer doesn't always make it better. It can, but you need the right proof, the right casks, the right flavours, and you always need to be checking on it and changing casks as needed. As well, a company that sells whiskey aged 8 years is usually associated with a quality around the 8 year mark. If Jack Daniels had a couple casks that were 40 years old, that's cool, but you don't buy Jack Daniels to be aged for 40 years so overall it probably wouldn't be worth it for them as it wouldn't appeal to either their core audience or the high end crowd. Storing it in steel vats would stop the aging process, allowing them to sell it as their normal type, whatever that may be.

  • PA2SK

    This is probably the best answer here. The short answer is they can't predict the future, so they do their best and then use various means to meet demand depending on how things turn out. One thing you need to to consider is that a lot of whiskey is only aged for a few years and isn't as subject to these fluctuations. Right now there is a bourbon shortage but it really only affects the ones that have been aged a lot (15 years, 23 years, etc.). Some bourbons are aged for as little as three months. So if there is a shortage of aged bourbon they can just raise the price of it and sell more of the cheap stuff. Or they can eliminate and combine certain categories, for example phase out their 12 year aged whisky which they don't have enough of and start selling a 10 year aged whisky instead.
