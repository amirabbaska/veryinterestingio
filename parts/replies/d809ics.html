      • jetpacksforall

        (And what do we need to do to harness such a star and bring it close to earth so we can get all those tasty metals? Or is there a flaw in my master plan? ;-) )

        The first flaw would be the colossal amounts of energy required to change the velocity of a supernova-capable star and move it in a direction you wanted it to go. Preferably over a time period in which the human race and the solar system would still be around to enjoy it. Then you'd have to alter the momentum of the star a second time in order to "park" it into a nearby orbit.

        Would you build enormous rockets? Would you build some kind of framework capable of applying force to a gigantic fusion reactor without melting? Would you use some kind of gravity sling? Build a gigantic rocket engine around the star capable of shaping its energy output into thrust? Any way you approach the problem of moving a star, you are likely to find the solution far more costly than the value of materials.

        Another flaw is that, when all was said and done, you would wind up with a star going supernova in relatively close proximity to our solar system. That could be bad.

        A third flaw would be gravitation perturbations and how they might mess up the orbits of the planets, Oort Cloud objects, etc. But that problem seems small and niggling compared to #2 above.
