    Far clearer and more in depth that the top comments. Thanks! Though I'm still confused about the shark/urea thing.

    • KvasirsBlod

      It's because of (lack of) osmosis. Osmosis in short means that (example) if a very salty solution and a less salty one are divided by a membrane, water will move from the less salty one to the other, to try to balance the concentration. If you put seawater and freshwater divided by a membrane, saltwater 'absorbs' the water from the other side until both sides are equally salty. In our bodies our cells have salt but not as concentrated as seawater, while more than fresh water. The cell walls are the membrane. An example which we now know might not be true, is when our fingers get wrinkly in the water. We thought that it was because water gets in and cells get bloated with water (they look like dry raisins but it's actually the opposite).

      In freshwater then, cells absorb water because the saltiness tries to be the same as the outside. Therefore, freshwater fish don't need to drink water. They need to pee diluted urine to get rid of the excess water. In saltwater, it's the opposite. Our cells get dehydrated, as water tries to dilute seawater to reach a balance (nice try). So, saltwater fish need to drink water constantly and pee very salty urine.

      Sharks are an exception because their blood has a high urea concentration (urea, uric acid and others make up urine). The saltiness with seawater is balanced so there's no osmosis.
