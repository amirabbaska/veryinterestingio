    • bulksalty

      Plastic is made up long, narrow repeating chain chemicals (called polymers). Some of these chains are aligned (crystalline) and others are jumbled together like a plate of cooked spaghetti (amorphous). When there are enough aligned chains, that the groups are the same size as the wavelength of visible light, they scatter light resulting in the plastic becoming opaque. When there are no aligned chains (all of them are jumbled together) the plastic is clear or transparent. When you bend plastic (or heat it to the right temperature) you force the chains to align which creates the opaque, white chains.

      Since you specifically asked about colored plastics. Colored plastics have a small amount of pigment mixed in with the plastic that reflect only a portion of the visible light (in their color). The same bending causes chains to align occurs, but in this case you create many new aligned chains that scatter all colors of light. All the new chains you create scatter far more light than the pigment is reflecting, so the color changes to white. Sort of like if you had a small colored flashlight in daylight, even though you have a colored light, it's so much less bright than the sun, it becomes very hard to see the small amount of reflected colored light.
