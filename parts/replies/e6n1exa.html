      • dsvii

        With most trees the appearance of the bark has more to do with how much total growth has happened and less to do with the speed that it grew. Different species will have different looking bark because their bark has different properties. It's one of the ways you can identify similar trees.

        Imagine if you halfway inflated a balloon and then painted it. If you pump more air in the balloon the paint will crack. Now for examples sake let's say you added another layer of paint underneath the first one and then pump up the balloon some more. You would likely get cracks all in the same places as the last cracks and maybe a few new ones. This is exactly how bark forms on a tree and you can sort of see how the speed you inflated the balloon has a very little effect on the way the cracks look

      • Bigbootyswag

        Not necessarily, that I've noticed. Lots of trees have unique features about their bark, like the mockernut hickory which looks like crossed ropes with large deep furrows, or the common persimmon which looks like blocky alligator skin almost. Both fairly cracked and relatively slow growing.

        Then something like the red maple is relatively fast growing, and doesn't have deeply furrowed bark.

        I think the biggest difference is just how quickly a certain species exfoliates bark.
