  • delventhalz

    Most of what you said here is erroneous. All that matters for this question is the strength of gravity. This is related to mass, but more importantly to the density.

    The extreme gravity of a blackhole comes not from it's mass so much (some are only a few times more massive than the Sun), but from the infinite density of the mass. With the mass compressed into an infinitely small area you can get very very close to the center of mass, and since gravity gets exponentially stronger as you get closer, it can increase to a point where gravity is strong enough to bend the path of light into a circle, creating an orbit.

    With any other object, say a star, as you add mass you are also adding volume, so while the extra mass is increasing the gravity, you are also necessarily moving further away from the center of mass, decreasing the gravity. Even if you try to get closer by diving inside the star, and could somehow survive the trip, the gravity will actually start to decrease once you get below the surface. This is because now some of that mass is above you and some of it is below you. At the center of a star you would actually experience zero gravity, because the mass on all sides of you is pulling you equally.

    And speed has nothing to do with any of it. Light is always traveling an the speed of light relative to you, even if you are traveling very very fast. At 1% c, light approaches you at c. At 99% c, light approaches you at c. It's relativity and it's weird, but light in a vacuum always travels at the speed of light no matter what you're doing.
