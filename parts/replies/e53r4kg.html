    • High_Seas_Pirate

      The upper and lower limits are defined by the designers and engineers, and aren't intrinsic to battery chemistry. There are tradeoffs between overall capacity and battery life.

      Eh, yes and no. The upper and lower limits are based on the charichteristics of the cell's chemistry. Obviously, yes an engineer has to pick the high and low limits, but if you graph the voltage vs SOC (as measured by the number of Amp Hours charged or discharged) you get charichteristic curves. At the low end the voltage tends to drop off a cliff and may not be sufficient to provide power to the device. The SOC your device reports usually comes from plugging the voltage into a function derived from the SOC curve. Set your lower limit too low and you'll get lithium plating on the cathode and anode any time you over discharge. This takes lithium permanently out of the places where it belongs and lowers your capacity. Do it enough times and the lithium buildup can stretch across from cathode to anode and short the cell, resulting in venting or fire. Over charging can also result in fire, though you only have to do that once to get the result. So knowing that, you have to pick starting and ending points that won't damage the cell, but still maximize the usable range of SOC.
