    On your comment regarding countershading: that is usually more important for either pelagic (open water) fish or for those that tend to swim above and around a reef.

    For instance, many different (not all) species of sharks that live around reefs use countershading as a means of camouflage - such as a Grey Reef Shark.

    Sailfish are another good example of a countershaded fish, and their niche is in open water, hunting smaller fish and squid.

    A Parrotfish isn't going to need countershading, as it typically hugs the reef and avoids open water. Additionally, it is a fin-swimmer, not a tail-swimmer, and eats algae and small crustaceans, so it isn't camouflaging itself from potential prey. More important is (as you mentioned) the distinction between male and female, or the distinction between species.
