    My last comment was poorly worded - but what you've described isn't tidal locking, it's a synchronous orbit.

    A synchronous orbit is where the orbital period of the satellite matches the rotational period of the body it is orbiting (like GPS satellites).

    Tidal locking is a consequence of synchronous orbits, where the satellite's rotational and orbital periods are equal. This means the satellite presents the same face to the body which it is orbiting (e.g. the Moon is tidally locked to the Earth). Our moon is tidally locked, but it isn't geosynchronous.

      Your definition is wrong. A synchronous orbit is where the orbital period of the satellite is a harmonic of the rotational period of the body it is orbiting. GPS satellites orbit at twice the rate of the Earth's rotational period: two orbits in one sidereal period.
