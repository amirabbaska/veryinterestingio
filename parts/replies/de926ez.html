    • mrjast

      I don't know what it's like for you. A large amount of the taste of food, given a normally functioning sense of smell, is actually the smell (going through the mouth and the upper airways) getting mixed into what the taste receptors report. It doesn't feel like smell, but it is, largely. Other perception (e.g. texture of the food) plays into this, too.

      Taste receptors can really only detect a few properties: sweetness, sourness, saltiness, bitterness and umami (the taste MSG adds to things, also creating by browning meat and some vegetables and roots). Everything else comes from other senses. So, sense of smell is basically taste minus the parts perceived directly in the mouth. For instance, I can't really smell sweetness but I can smell many things that are typically sweet, including caramel and chocolate, and I've developed very strong mental associations between smell and pure taste, so I can "almost taste it" if I smell it. I can smell salt a little but it's very different from the powerful taste sensation on the tongue. There isn't one singular smell for sour things, but vinegar, for example, has a very characteristic smell. And so on.

      I do have experience with tasting things without smell, from when I have a bad cold and the upper airways are all blocked. In direct comparison, almost no taste is left. This will almost certainly be different for you because you don't have the direct comparison. It's quite possible your sense of taste (and some of the other senses involved, like texture, temperature etc.) on its own is rather more refined than mine because you have to deal with a reduced or altogether missing olfactory component and your nervous system found ways to compensate.

    • mrjast

      Possibly. Smells are part of subjective experience, strictly speaking we don't even know all smells are the same to all of us humans. The same argument goes for colours.
