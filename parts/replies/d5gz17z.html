      • drusteeby

        What you're doing is actually called "unlearning", going from unconscious competence to conscious incompetence. This is how habits are broken. When we first learn a skill we usually go through all those steps.

        1. Can't do it and I don't even know how.
        2. Can't do it but I know what to do.
        3. Can do it and I know exactly how
        4. Can do it but couldn't tell you how.

        You're using your conscious mind to try and relearn the task, even though your unconscious knows how. The ability to switch between the stages is not easy, thus sporting games are very mentally challenging.

        Start with getting rid of the limiting belief "I'm always overthinking" and "I always end up doing terribly". If you keep believing these things they will be true, but know in your mind that you are capable of anything, including not overthinking.

      • wpgsae

        Well that's a boiled down version. Basically when someone learns a new skill, especially as a child, they will be bad and not really know it. Then as they get more exposure to the skill and see how it's supposed to be done they realize how bad they are. Then after practice they can perform the skill with some conscious effort. With enough practice the skill becomes second nature. An athlete who is competitive in his or her sport doesn't need to think about the basic skills (dribbling a basket ball, passing a soccer ball accurately etc) and can therefore focus more attention on the strategic and predictive elements (setting up plays, deeking, seeing a play develop before it happens). Unconscious competence is the end goal of any learned skill.

        What you're describing is something that affects everyone. A golfer who over thinks their swing won't be able to perform the way they want to. You're trying to take conscious control of a movement or skill that is better left to you subconscious which has been trained to do the skill without thought.
