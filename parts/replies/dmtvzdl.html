    • Minovskyy

      Coordinates on the surface of the Earth are labeled by latitude (north-south) and longitude (east-west). The standard units for these are degrees, (arc) minutes, and (arc) seconds. One degree of latitude is 1/90^th the distance from the equator to the north pole. One minute is 1/60^th of one degree, and one second is 1/60^th of one minute. A nautical mile is defined to be one minute of latitude. Basically, suppose you are handed a sheet of graph paper. It makes sense to choose your standard unit of length to be the same size as the length of a box edge. Same rational applies to the nautical mile. The inconsistency is that the Earth is not perfectly symmetric, so where you measure your minute changes the length of a nautical mile. The difference, however, is ~1m, so the error is about 0.0004% (the current international standard nautical mile is 1852m). It is unlikely that this error would have caused much of a problem considering, that over the course of even a few days, the error in distance would be less than that of the error in a sextant measurement.

      The problem with multiple values for various units isn't really the problem with The Imperial system having various values, but rather there being multiple Imperial systems. As long as you kept track of what system you were in, there was no redundancy. Of course, in this day and age with globalization and high precision measuring systems, it's best to have consistent definitions for weights and measures.
