    • thenebular

      There is nothing that restricts space from expanding faster than the speed of light. That's why inflation at the beginning of the universe is ok. However, space is expanding much slower than the speed of light locally and it's doing that everywhere. So in small distances it's barely noticable, but as you get to larger and larger distances those small expansions that are happening everywhere add up and you find that very distant galaxies are moving away from us at speeds much faster than the speed of light.

      In the case of the spots on a balloon, two spots next to each other won't move too fast from each other as it inflates, but spots on opposite sides will seem to be moving much faster as the space between them gets larger.

        No, this isn't right. Photons are massless for example, yet also can't travel beyond the speed of light.

        The real reason is that special relativity forbids two inertial frames from having a relative velocity larger than c, but general relativity puts no such restriction on the expansion of spacetime.
