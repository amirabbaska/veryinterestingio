    The Bitcoin paper solves an important problem in computer science called "the Byzantine generals problem", which aims to achieve trust and consensus among two or more parties by using untrustworthy communication channels.

    It was claimed in forum posts that "Bitcoin's basic concept" is capable of solving Byzantine Agreement. Later a paper by Miller & LaViola argued a probabilistic solution for computationally limited adversaries, with negligible probability in a security parameter k, where the number of messages exchanged is O(n^(2) k) and running time O(n^(2) k) - no description as to how additional messaging or computation leads to a solution of BA. A thorough analysis describing the relevant security properties has yet to appear.

    I think it serves no point for this claim to be misrepresented in this context. It is irrelevant to the properties that prevent forgery.

    You can read a more thorough analysis of Bitcoin-related claims in Garay, Kiayias, and Leonardos, "The Bitcoin Backbone Protocol: Analysis and Applications", EUROCRYPT 2015 , where the authors begin by describing a randomised BA protocol on top of solely proof-of-work assumption for bounded adversaries.

    • mfukar

      There are currently (June 2017) no known collisions for SHA-2. Currently, the best public attacks break preimage resistance for 52 rounds of SHA-256 or 57 rounds of SHA-512, and collision resistance for 46 rounds of SHA-256.

      See: Dmitry Khovratovich, Christian Rechberger & Alexandra Savelieva (2011). "Bicliques for Preimages: Attacks on Skein-512 and the SHA-2 family", IACR Cryptology ePrint Archive. 2011:286 and Mario Lamberger & Florian Mendel (2011). "Higher-Order Differential Attack on Reduced SHA-256", IACR Cryptology ePrint Archive. 2011:37
