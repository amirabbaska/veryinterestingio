    You are right: Pressure and temperature activates Rhodopsin within the rods and cones of our photoreceptors, but "Eigengrau" is something different.

    Eigengrau is the adaptation of every single photoreceptor within the eye to the presented wave spectrum (light), it happens quite fast (10 to 15s). Thats why photoreceptors can only see change, not constant pictures. If the "light" doesn't change the receptor fades to Eigengrau.

    To avoid this sensation, mammals e. g. have an eye movement (slow and fast, both of them not concious) to vary the input of the receptors to avoid Eigengrau.

    Birds like pidgeons move their complete head (like they are nodding) to avoid Eigengrau.

    Frogs probably see nothing when they don't move - just Eigengrau. But so they can see changes like moving flies around them easily.
