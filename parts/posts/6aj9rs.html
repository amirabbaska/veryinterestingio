• Physics The Lense-Thirring effect - does it affect the orbital inclinations of planets or moons?

    NEW
    Q:

    There's a guy on reddit who insists that the Lense-Thirring effect (L-T) somehow causes the orbital plane of a planet to align with the equatorial plane of the (rotating) star about which the planet orbits. Similarly, the orbital plane of a moon would align itself with the equatorial plane of its host planet, assuming the planet is spinning. He cites this paper to support his thesis.

    From what I've read about L-T, this is not the case: the orbital inclination, or the angle between the orbital plane and the equatorial plane, remains the same. However, the orbital plane precesses about the axis of rotation of the star. And there's also nodal precession, too, but that also doesn't affect the angle of the orbital plane.

    The one case where L-T does result in something aligning itself with the equatorial plane is in the accretion disks of Kerr black holes: if (for some reason) an accretion disk forms that is out of alignment with a fast-rotating black hole, L-T causes the the orbital planes at different distances to precess at different rates, which causes shearing in the viscous mass of the accretion disk, and as the disk relaxes into a lower energy state, it will end up in the same plane as the plane of rotation of the black hole. But this is due to internal friction within the disk, and that is not a significant factor in ordinary orbital mechanics involving stars, planets, moons, and even rings.

    Is he right, or am I? Or are we both wrong?

    I'm far from an expert, but I think the fact that the orbit of earth's moon is inclined by 5 degrees to the ecliptic and between 18.3 and 28.6 degrees to earth's equator disproves that guy's theory. Since the moon doesn't align with earth's equatorial plane, then the L-T effect doesn't require moons to orbit on the equatorial planes of their planets.

    Pit_Droid A:

    I'm currently doing a masters astrophysics course where we touched on the L-T effect during a black holes module. I am inclined to agree that you are correct (in fact, your explanation is a nice clear one).

    Though just to clarify: the L-T effect causes a precession of the plane of an inclined orbit, like a spinning coin on a table top. The inclination is affected by dissipation of energy in the disc (from different orbital radii precessing at different rates). In our coin analogy, though it is spinning, it only falls down because of friction dissipating energy.

    So, the L-T effect may have had some impact in the early solar system with regards to the protoplanetary dust disc. But without a source of dissipation, the L-T can do nothing more than precess an orbit, not change the inclination. Does it precess the moon? Yes, it probably does on some level. However the masses of the Earth and the Moon are so tiny that the L-T effect may as well not exist (especially when compared with the much more important tidal forces present in planetary systems).

    I've had a quick look through the first paper, and I don't really trust any of it at all. It doesn't seem to have been referenced by anyone and the whole thing seems off. The arguments they've used don't seem to add up either.

    Not on the theory side, but there are plenty of known planets with orbits completely misaligned to their host star's rotational axes, with some proceeding retrograde. These orbits are very close in (orbital periods of days), where any GR effects are far stronger than the Earth-Sun system! The planets and stars are also old enough that they've had several gigayears to realign their axes through whatever mechanism you choose, but haven't.
