    Torkmatic A:

    Like some other people have said, most Christian sects and related religions (like Judaism and Islam) are largely against religious art, since they see it as a slippery slope to worshiping idols. The Catholic Church is different because during the middle ages, Western Europe had terribly low literacy rates, so pictures were used as reference material for teaching history and belief to the common people. A depiction of a saint, or a scene from the bible, was full of information and would help viewers learn and remember that information. So while other churches were willing to destroy their icons, the Catholics would have been giving up a valuable teaching tool, and felt it would hurt their followers to get rid of them.

    martin4reddit A:

    Catholicism is actually comparable in their relatively liberal use of imagery/iconography in depicting religious subjects with many other sects/religions. Eastern Orthodox, Judaism, Buddhism, various Shamanistic religions and Hinduism all use large amounts of physical representation

    The perception that Catholicism is bigger on imagery in large part stems from the predominance in the West of Protestant Christianity which emphasizes austerity and simplicity in worship, eschewing the pomp and ceremony of Catholicism. Islam contributes to this contrast as it similarly emphasizes austerity and simplicity in places of worship.

    alphaomega4ever A:

    Catholic theology lecturer here.

    No one's mentioned it so far, but the deepest rationale for sacred iconography (images), in addition to a number of other sensible elements (e.g. incense, candles, sacred architecture, etc) is what might be called the "incarnational analogy" of faith. Because we believe that God, in an earth-shaking way, took on flesh and became human (which we refer to as the incarnation), God has decisively restored and reclaimed broken humanity, and creation as a whole, as good. In light of the incarnation, Catholics can't (or at least shouldn't) treat matter and the material world as something to be shunned or avoided as tainted/evil/weak simply speaking. The positive side of this is we love employing created things, if they work, in our liturgy - the service and worship of God.

    Another central aspect is the basic idea that God reveals Himself in a way and manner that humans can understand (in part - divine "mysteries" like the Incarnation by definition can't be exhausted by our intellectual investigations). God desires to be known, and even though He in Himself is spirit (not material), He nonetheless condescends to the limitations of human thought and language in order to be better known and loved. Art and imagery are essential to the human experience and our coming to fully know and appreciate that which is beautiful, including God. As such, the Church takes seriously the challenge of communicating divine truths across a number of mediums, acknowledging their various inherent limits.

    I didn't get into some of the doctrinal discrepancies we have vis-a-vis Protestants or other faiths, but I hope this was helpful!
