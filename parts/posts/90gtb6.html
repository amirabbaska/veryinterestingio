    Q:

    Why does the sun's magnetic field keep changing every 11 years?

    BluScr33n A:

    In short: We don't really know.

    A bit longer: We heavily suspect that the solar magnetic field is generated by some kind of a dynamo effect in the convective envelope of the sun. It was long believed that the cause of this was lying in the shear region at the tachoclyne, the region between the radiative and the convective envelope. However research has shown that this likely isn't the case. The dynamo therefore is probably located further outward in the convective envelope. Unfortunately the solar magnetic field is relatively weak and this weakness makes it difficult to observe magnetic fields of other solar type stars. So we can hardly compare the solar magnetic field to that of other solar type stars. It also appears to be related to the temperature of the corona, maybe the newly discovered structures in the corona can help solve this and the coronal heating problem.

    Venatrix26 A:

    I actually just spent the last few months researching magnetic cycles in sun-like stars!

    There's not too much more to add from what other people said (because as they've pointed out, theres a lot we don't know about why this happens). But there is an interesting point to bring up that hasn't been mentioned yet -- The length of a magnetic cycle seems to be correlated to the rotation rate of the star through something called the Rossby number. If you're interested, this paper is really interesting! https://arxiv.org/pdf/1507.03611.pdf

    edit: I forgot to add, it seems somewhat likely that the sun has another cycle for it's magnetic activity, right now we think it might be ~87 years long. Look up "Gleissberg cycle" if that interests you!
