    connaught_plac3 A:

    Moore's Law was about transistor's in a circuit becoming smaller so the processor would double in speed every two years (IRL 18 months). But it has also applied to storage and RAM and whatnot.

    Scientists are constantly striving to miniaturize and get more efficiency. So far this has been a linear process more or less, we could always make it a bit smaller and faster. There isn't a huge incentive to jump technologies, that's why your breakthroughs don't happen more often. The company always wants a few price tiers going, so it needs to constantly replace the top tier and let the rest move down a spot.

    But we always knew there was a physical limit to how small a transistor can be. The solution was adding more cores.

    With storage we went from optical lasers like DVD from magnetic tape drives, now the big breakthrough is SSD. Each time a technology hits a limit other technologies become more viable and cost efficient.

    colohan A:

    Moore's law is interesting.

    Basically it predicts that we'll keep getting better at making smaller and smaller features on computer chips.

    Hard drives have one really critical chip that is produced in a similar way to other chips--the read/write head. If you can make the features in this smaller then you can read and write smaller magnetic domains in the platters.

    So, amongst other advances, hard drives have benefited from Moore's law improving computer chips.

    Bigjoemonger A:

    Moore's law was actually defeated in 2004 with the creation of the single atom transistor. They made a transistor using a single silver atom embedded in silicon. https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single-atom_transistor

    But ultimately they found that with transistors this small, when you put a lot of them on a microchip their individual electrons start to interfere with each other. It's now part of the research into quantum computing.

    But as far as size, we've gone as small as we can go. Now it's all about the geometry. Rather than arranging them on a single plane you can arrange them in a lattice and take advantage of the larger surface area to include more in a small area.
