    Q:

    Do they just use radioactive material decays or do they have machines that concentrate/focus them in a way? I know that Cobalt 60 can produce up to 1.3~ MeV during its decay into nickel. I have also seen alpha bombardment of boron using polonium but that only gets up to 3~ MeV. I could not find any information on how higher values are achieved. How are values above let's say 7 MeV reached?

    RobusEtCeleritas A:

    There are some common laboratory gamma ray sources (cobalt-60, potassium-40, sodium-22, cesium-137, etc.), but this will generally produce gamma rays of a few hundred keV to a few MeV.

    To produce very high energy gamma rays (up to ~ 100 MeV), you can use inverse Compton scattering in a free electron laser. An example would be HIGS at TUNL.

    Ethan0l A:

    This is my time to shine! I am a medical physics master student working at a hospital. Here in our hospital we have medical linear accelerators, they work in the 2.5 - 16 MeV. Here we mostly use the 6 MV energy for most treatments but for any electron therapy we have machines commissioned for up to 12 MeV (16 MeV is not commissioned but can be used for research). Medical linacs work using an electron gun, just like any x-ray machine, and then an accelerating waveguide is used to accelerate those low energy electrons to higher energies. 1 After the accelerating waveguide there is a bending magnet that shapes redirects the beam towards a tungsten target and then it is collimated to reduce scatter.

    honger A:

    For hospital use there are two general methods to produce high energy photons: one is to use Cobalt-60 as you've already mentioned. The other is a machine called a linear accelerator (linac)--basically, electrons are accelerated to a high energy level towards a high Z target producing photons which are then filtered and/or shaped. In clinical use energies >= 20 MeV aren't used because of the increasing probability of pair production when higher energy photons interact with matter/tissue.

    http://radonc.wikidot.com/linear-accelerator

    edit: Depending on the size of what you're irradiating and the machine you're using, the beam can be fairly flat and symmetric. There's likely to be a radiation oncology practice somewhere near you if this is a one-off thing. Make sure you work with a physicist to get whatever you need done. Imprecision in your setup can lead to large discrepancies between planned and delivered dose.
