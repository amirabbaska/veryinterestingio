    Q:

    If water has mass and surface tension, what is the minimum mass for a drop of water?

    bencbartlett A:

    There is no minimum size, at least until the molecular level (at which point "drop" doesn't have much meaning). For a spherical drop, the total "inwards force" keeping a drop intact is proportional to the surface area of the drop, while the mass of the drop is proportional to the volume, so small drops can be very stable. For a 10nm drop, the surface tension integrated over the surface area exerts about 150 atmospheres of inward pressure keeping the bubble spherical, while a 10mm drop exerts only about 0.001 atm. [1]

    There is, however, an expected mass for a drop of water if you allow for air disturbances and a gravitational field; this is the reason that all rain drops are of similar size.

    cbrock3 A:

    Lets say a water droplet is forming on the bottom of a solid surface prior to detaching and falling. We want to determine the weight of the water droplet. Water temperature= 25 degrees Celsius (77 F).

    weight of droplet= force due to surface tension γ= 9781 N/M^3 (Specific Weight- Physical properties of water at 1 atm)

    σ= 0.072 N/M (Surface Tension at 25 Degrees C) (Varies with temperature)

    Use Formula: γV=σL *L=2πr (circumference)

    (9781)(2/3 πr^3) =(.072)(2πr)

    Solve for radius: r=.00469932 m

    Assuming the water droplet is spherical: V= 4/3 πr^3

    V=4.35x10^-7 m^3

    Converting cubic meters to grams: (4.35x10^-7 m^3) (100cm/1m)(100cm/1m)(100cm/1m)(1g/cm^3) =.435 grams

    Remember that this is strictly theoretical. Surfaces which are hydrophobic (Teflon, wax paper) or hydrophilic (glass) will give different results.
