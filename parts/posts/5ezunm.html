    Q:

    Do 15 year old dogs get cancer at rates similar to 15 year old humans, or to humans at a similar point on their lifespan?

    pulchritudinousest A:

    Definitely the latter . A 15 year old human is an adolescent. Cancer in adolescents (14-19) is pretty rare , accounting for less than 1% of all cancers. They also tend to be different types of cancer than what children or adults get. This would be more equivalent to a 1-2 year old dog. A 15 year old dog is beyond geriatric. That's beyond the normal lifespan of many breeds. A better example might be comparing to a middle aged or older dog (10-12) in many breeds. Their rates of cancer are going to be higher, comparable to people above 65. . Age is a big risk factor for cancer; certain cancer types in particular are linked to aging.

    Shooterdog A:

    An interesting observation is that the number of cells in an organism does not necessarily correlate with the risk of developing cancer. This is referred to as Peto's Paradox (wiki link if you'd like it: https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peto's_paradox )

    The longstanding theory of the development of cancer is that as you age, your cells acquire mutations that give them a survival advantage, allowing them to proliferate faster and acquire even more mutations, eventually leading to cancer.

    However, mutation is a stochastic process in replicating cells, and by the time you are 18, every single base pair in your entire genome has at one point been mutated. Now, the vast majority of these mutations are deleterious and are selected against, some are neutral, but you would expect that the so-called 'cancer mutations' like c-Myc or NRas/KRas or p53 would pop up and provide cells with a survival advantage. But that doesn't happen, as adolescent cancer is extremely rare.

    This brings up 2 concepts: Clonal Hematopoiesis of Indeterminate Potential (CHIP), and microenvironment fitness.

    CHIP refers to cells that harbor these classic cancer mutations but aren't actively proliferating. Which at first seems to make no sense, because if these cells have the mutations that boost replication, why aren't they constantly dividing? The microenvironment idea may hold a plausible answer. Think of a young person's healthy body and an elderly person's frail body - the younger person likely has healthier tissues and a stronger immune system than the elderly person and will be better at suppressing the growth of these pre-cancerous cells.

    Similarly, a lifelong smoker, in addition to causing additional DNA mutations due to tobacco smoke, has broken down their tissue microenvironment and has provided a scenario in which not only is the suppression of cancer cells reduced, but those pre-cancerous mutations now are in an environment in which positive selection may begin to act on them.

    These are new concepts that are actively being tested, and if you would like to read more I encourage you to read about CHIP from Dan Link's lab at Wash U in St Louis, and the concept of Peto's Paradox and the pre-cancerous microenvironment from James DeGregori's lab at the University of Colorado
