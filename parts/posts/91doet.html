    Q:

    ELI5: Will water freeze if it is unable to expand?

    PUSSYDESTROYER-9000 A:

    Yes. However, such a system would be under incredible amounts of pressure. For example, frozen water stuck in a pipe can crack the pipe. Also, under extreme pressures, new crystalline structures form, giving rise to different types of ice. For example, Titan is theorized to have Ice VII, which only forms at 100000 atm pressures.

    ShyElf A:

    This question is commonly asked here, and as almost always answered incorrectly. I'm assuming you talking about lowering the temperature of an extremely strong container which begins full of water near atmospheric pressure and 0C, without changing the volume of the container.

    The answer is that at a temperature slightly below freezing, some of the water freezes into ice. The constrained volume will be mostly water with a small amount of ice.

    If there are both liquid water and ice, this means we are following the water/ice I line on the phase diagram. As the temperature decreases, the ice fraction and pressure will increase.

    The density of the ice on the ice/water phase boundary remains below that of the starting water until the Ice III/Ice I/water triple point. This means that the water is never all Ice I.

    After this triple point, we abruptly change to a mix of Ice I and Ice III. Eventually this changes to a mix of Ice I and Ice II. What happens next is not definitely established, but is believed that we change to a mix of Ice XI at very low pressure and extremely dilute vapor.

    The total maximum pressure is quite high, equivalent to around 21km of water column. The vast majority of containers will simply leak at a higher temperature and a lower pressure.
