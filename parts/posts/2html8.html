    Mirzer0 A:

    You can do this with simple things... if you take two photos from about 4 inches apart, put the two images side by side, and look at it while coaxing each eye to look at the other image... you can get the stereoscopic effect. But the things in these pictures have to be relatively close to the camera.

    In essence, you have to have the cameras placed such that the two images are sufficiently different for your brain to perceive the parallax.

    For clouds it would probably work with a football field. Doing video instead of still images is a really neat idea.

    For stars - unlikely. Stars are far too far away for the distance of a football field to matter to human perception. However, we've used things like taking the images 6 months apart (effectively using the diameter of the orbit of Earth) to perceive parallax in the stars.

    I'm sure there's probably a relatively simple formula for figuring out roughly what kind of space you need between the cameras, based on distance-to-target and general feature size of the target. But I'm at work, and don't have time to try to figure it out.

    buttpiracyagent A:

    In theory, yes. The space between our eyes is what gives us 3D depth perception, so spreading your eyes further apart would give you more depth. The setup in the comic would definitely make clouds a lot more fun to look at and probably make them look closer and smaller. However, if you wanted to really get any difference in the appearance of the stars you would have to move the cameras much much further apart. Even using cameras mounted to satellites on opposite sides of the earth might not be enough to create any noticeable 3D effect.
