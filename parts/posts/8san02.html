    AlkaliActivated A:

    Just a ballpark calculation for the limits on man-made things:

    Engineering materials range in properties by orders of magnitude, but lets just consider a material like aluminum with a tensile strength of 300MPa, a density of 3000 kg/m^3 , and a poisson's ratio of 0.3. The peak tensile stress in a rotating disk is given by

    (3+v)ρ(rω)²/4

    Plugging in the numbers gives a maximum value for rω of around 350 meter-radians-per-second. So for a disc with a radius of 1 meter, that means a maximum speed of ~350 radians per second (~3300 RPM). While you can increase the angular velocity by going to smaller radius, a disk will still be limited in the velocity of the outward edge to this value around 350 meter-radians-per-second. You could probably push that by another factor of 3 using really high-end composite materials (since it's proportional to the square-root of specific strength). In principle you could push it arbitrarily high by using geometries other than a disc (for instance, a symmetrical concave cone) but I can't see why you would.
