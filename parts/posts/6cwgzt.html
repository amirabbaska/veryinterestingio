• Engineering How difficult is it to provide cell service on underground services such as subways?

    NEW
    Q:

    If we can have cell assisted Internet access on above ground services like trains why can we not do the same underground?

    TunnelFET A:

    Very good question. It depends. If you put your transmitter above ground, it is almost impossible to get the signals deep underground and it would be a waste of energy to try to. If you put transmitters inside a tunnel you can actually reach quite far with them because the signals would reflect a lot. So we could do it. We probably don't because it takes a lot of money and it isn't really worth anyone's time since you will be above ground soon anyway.

    pehrs A:

    Typical wavelengths used for cellular connections does not penetrate earth very well. They can manage a few walls in the house, but put a bit of solid of stone in between, and the signal will be soo weak that you don't get any connection. So we can't use normal antennas to radiate underground.

    Instead, we can use what we call radiating cable (AKA Leaky feeder). It is basically a very long antenna which you put in the tunnel, and connect a cellular basestation at the end. The antenna distributes the signal along the tunnel. This is used in many underground systems around the world, as well as in airplanes.

    Gusfoo A:

    Largely it is not difficult, however it is expensive.

    Specifically you use what is called a "Leaky Feeder" which extends the antenna along the length of the tunnel. The user is in the same cell site/sector along the entire length of the tunnel so no rapid hand-offs are needed. Other than the leaky feeder antenna (with it's boosters), the radio equipment is the same as a normal cell site. The cell site needs power, cooling and connectivity of course.
