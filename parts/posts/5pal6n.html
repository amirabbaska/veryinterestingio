    Q:

    When a photon redshifts, it's wavelength becomes larger, which corresponds to a lower photon-energy. What happens with this energy difference?

    aphysicist A:

    If you mean redshift due to a doppler effect, then energy is still conserved in an inertial reference frame. A doppler shift changes reference frame by moving at a different velocity when the shift is observed.

    If you mean in general relativity, then there is no conservation of energy in this case. (Energy is not in general conserved in general relativity.)

    yeast_problem A:

    I always think of it as lost kinetic energy. Gravitational redshift is caused by photons going "uphill" effectively.

    With redshift due to motion, the photon the right energy in the frame of reference of the source object, but less in the frame of reference of the observer.

    This is obviously a gross simplification, and needs relativistic corrections, but its a simple way to explain. Imagine a bullet fired backwards from a train that is travelling at near bullet speed. On the train it moves like a bullet. To a person on the ground , the bullet moves slowly with little energy.

    Photons aren't bullets, they appear to be moving at c to both observers, but the idea is the same.
