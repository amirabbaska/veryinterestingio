    kodack10 A:

    Blood coagulates, it doesn't just dry out. Platelets in the blood rush to the damaged area and help to plug the hole, while clot factor enzymes begin to clot up the blood around the damage.

    If it's in air it will eventually dry out forming a hard scab. If it's in a moist environment a scab is still formed, but it's more like a membrane over the injury instead of a hard scab. IE flexible and it can stretch.

    Sometimes this soft scab can come off during eating, and if it causes new bleeding, a new scab is formed, and if it's repaired enough not to bleed, then a full scab may not form, but there are other things in a scab than just blood, so it may still ooze and form a new cap.

    Think about the difference between what happens when you paint with watercolor, versus oil based paints. Water color paint sets when the water evaporates. But with oil paints, the oil doesn't evaporate. It stays wet, and would stay wet for a very long time, except that sunlight and heat cause the oil to polymerize which takes the short carbon chains in the oil, and starts linking them together, making longer, polymer chains that give it structure and harden the oil.

    Blood is like the oil paint, it hardens because of structural changes, rather than just drying out.
