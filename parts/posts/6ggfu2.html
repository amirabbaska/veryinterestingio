• Biology Why aren't dogs subjected to the "law of diminishing returns"? I feel like my 8 year old lab still gets the same amount of enjoyment playing fetch as he did when he was a puppy--even after playing it practically every single day of his life.

    NEW
    Q:

    And yet, nothing I do in my everyday life is as fun as it was the first time I did it. I feel like I am constantly in search of something new and exciting. What is it about dogs' brains that allows them to have practically endless joy?

    Edit: Thanks for all the replies, and a special shoutout to /u/kouhoutek -- that was a great answer!

    kouhoutek A:

    Humans tend to anthropomorphize their pets, and attribute human like emotions to their actions. That makes it easier to forget that they are animals and experience the world very differently than humans. What appears to be "joy" could be, and often is, something very different.

    In the case of fetch, dogs have a hunting instinct that makes they chase prey and bring it back to the pack. Throwing a stick triggers this instinct, and their brains rewards them for following through. This "joy" is similar to what a human would experience when following the instinct to drink something cold when they are hot and thirsty, and would explain both humans and dog continue to feel a sense of enjoyment even after doing it thousands of times year after year.

    z0mbieskin A:

    Not sure if someone has said this already, but during the domestication process, dogs suffered a process called [neoteny] (https://www.sciencedaily.com/terms/neoteny.htm) . This means that during the domestication of the gray wolf, individuals were selected for juvenile features, physical (larger snout, broader head, smaller teeth) and behavioral (puppy like behavior, like playing a lot, not very aggressive, etc) and the physical and behavioral traits are often connected.

    This basically means most modern dog breeds were selected to look and behave like a giant puppy. That's also why if you take a grey wolf or wild fox puppy and raise it to adulthood, it won't be a completely domestic animal, it will have strong instincts and often times will try to run away for mating etc.

    This is kind of my field of study (dog genetics) so if you have more questions feel free to ask
