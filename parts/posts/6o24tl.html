• why does saying "tock, tick, tock, tick" seems really weird, but saying "tick, tock, tick, tock" sounds more correct?

    NEW
    Q:

    ELI5: why does saying "tock, tick, tock, tick" seems really weird, but saying "tick, tock, tick, tock" sounds more correct?

    rewboss A:

    This is an example of what's called "ablaut reduplication". An "ablaut" is a specific type of change in a vowel sound; "reduplication" is repeating something exactly or almost exactly.

    In English, the vowels in an ablaut reduplication nearly always follow the same pattern: "i" comes before "a", which comes before "o". This is one of those unconscious rules of English pronunciation that we have: we have somehow learned that this is the correct order, and putting the vowels in any other order sounds "wrong".

    So we have "tick, tock", "tip-top", "chit-chat", "tittle-tattle", "bish, bash, bosh" and so on. If you're not British, you may not have ever heard of "bish, bash, bosh", and yet it still sounds right, while "bosh, bash, bish" sounds more awkward (or should do).

    In the phrase "big bad wolf", this ablaut reduplication actually overrides a grammar rule: normally, adjectives describing your opinion of something should come before adjectives describing that thing's size, so it should be "bad big wolf", in the same way that we say "boring little man" and not "little boring man". But the urge to preserve the order of ablaut reduplication is much greater, so we say "big bad wolf".

    Redditor /u/qdatk explained:

    i-a and i-o vowels are chosen because they are maximally different from each other and hence "interesting" to the ear. The i comes before the a/o because a/o are longer in duration, which makes them fit better with final syllables.

    ZenmasterRob A:

    The reason ablaut reduplication works is because of the physics of sound. Almost all sounds begin with high pitched information and end with low pitched information. The order of vowels in ablaut reduplication is the exact order of the brightness of the vowels. Say "Fe Fi Fo Fum". Every word gets darker/lower than the first. It sounds almost like a piece of gravel bouncing down a hill, sounding further away with each bounce.

    Sentences that are phonetically pleasing are sentences that closely match the sounds of nature.

    Zuzublue A:

    In linguistics there is something called collocation, which is where pairs of words are always used in the same order. Examples are in and out, cream and sugar, rain or shine, suit and tie. There's no rule about these pairs, but are used through habit.
