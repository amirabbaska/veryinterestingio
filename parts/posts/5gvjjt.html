• Astronomy Why doesn't the n-body problem make the rotation curves of disc galaxies unpredictable?

    NEW
    Q:

    The difference between the observed and expected rotation curves of disc galaxies is explained by the presence of dark matter.

    But how do we even have a predicted rotation curve for disc galaxies?

    Doesn't the n-body problem mean the radial velocities of the stars in a galaxy are completely chaotic and unpredictable?

    AgletsHowDoTheyWork A:

    The n-body problem doesn't mean that trajectories are completely chaotic and unpredictable. It just means that there is no known analytical solution for 3 or more bodies. We can still do numerical simulation to an arbitrary degree of precision -- limited only by our computational resources.

    The general technique is to calculate the two-body solution on every possible pair of bodies in the simulation, and sum the effects on each body for a given time-step. The number of calculations is proportional to n^2, so it becomes time-consuming very quickly. Various methods are used to approximate the result with less complexity.

    Astrokiwi A:

    I'm a postdoctoral researcher, largely concentrating on simulations of galaxies in N-body type simulations.

    Basically, once you get enough bodies, you can make good predictions about the general large-scale properties of a system. The path of an individual star might be chaotic, but if you have a system of two hundred billion stars, then this starts to all cancel out, and you can actually start to treat things like a type of fluid (a "collisionless" fluid). This is basically what we do with real fluids - we treat a system of a large number of molecules with some large-scale bulk values. So in these simulations you can't usually say "this is what will happen to the Sun". But you can say "this is how the stars will form a spiral arm", even if you can't predict exactly which stars will be part of a spiral arm at any point in time.

    There are so many stars (and so much gas and dark matter) that unless a pair of stars happen to basically bump into each other, each star actually feels a gravity force that only changes quite smoothly. So you can do these things with computer simulations and get pretty accurate answers.

    Overall, it's actually very similar to weather and climate models. There is a lot of chaos in there, but overall you can actually made decent predictions, especially if you aren't concerned about the tiny details.

    Xeno87 A:

    Doesn't the n-body problem mean the radial velocities of the stars in a galaxy are completely chaotic and unpredictable?

    Stars and solar systems in a galaxy aren't interacting much, they are way too far apart. Of course, this doesn't really apply to stars in the bulge (the "center" of the galaxy), but when it comes to the disk it is a valid assumption. So it effectively becomes a two-body-problem with a star and the galaxy center.

    LusciousVagDisaster A:

    They are long-term unpredictable. We can still predict them in the relatively short term because any perturbations from the true interactions of all n bodies are dominated by the behavior seen from the strongest and most recently relevant interactions. Over a long period of time these systems will behave chaotically and are unpredictable.
