    NEW
    Q:

    I know that studies have shown that sleeping in a colder room is better health wise and sleep wise and helps us sleep better, but why do people turn the air down colder in rooms during the day to help stay awake?

    alolio A:

    The brain actually needs to cool down in the evening to slow its activity and begin to rest.

    Source: David Avery, MD. He's my doctor, who has specialized in the study of the effect of sleep on psychiatric disorders for many years.

    Further detail on sleep. His recommendations to me have always been:

    Sleep in a cold, dark room (I forget the ideal temperature)

    No blue light an hour before bed (screens, daylight/full spectrum bulbs). Use lamps, not overheads in the evening if possible.

    Get to sleep within 30 minutes of the same time every night - same for waking. Hormone production throughout the day depends on the diurnal cycle.

    Wake gradually with a dawn simulator if you can.

    Don't disrupt your routine on the weekends (social jetlag he calls it).

    It's natural for your body clock to shift through the seasons, but as it shifts, maintain consistency in the amount of sleep you get and don't shift too far. It's tempting to sleep less in summer, but especially for people with bipolar disorder, lack of sleep is associated with the onset of mania.

    Edit: I don't mean to dispense medical advice. I'm really just repeating some things my doctor told me, all of which seems like pretty common sense advice. It's what amounts to clean livin' - sleep enough, eat well and be active. It's all the stuff we know we should be doing. Also, made a sentence clearer.

    esontew A:

    A cold room helps lower core body temperature, a signal for inducing sleep. Your circadian rhythm typically lowers body temperature at night, so this exacerbates the signal. But it typically is combined with high sleep pressure (accumulated levels of adenosine in the brain). During the day, your body temperature is closer to the peak regulated temperature and you have low sleep pressure. (There's also a midday dip, but I'm ignoring that.) So cold during the day isn't usually going to make you fall asleep. Probably people have the idea that jolting awake people with cold air will keep them alert.

    This is all off the top of my head, so there might be some inaccuracies.
