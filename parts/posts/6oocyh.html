    Q:

    Just like in supersonic motion, the object that creates the sound can move faster than the waves it produces, is it possible for the E-field or B-field that make up light to move faster than the light?

    Midtek A:

    The light (i.e., the electromagnetic wave) is the E- and B-fields. So the answer to your question is trivially "no"; something cannot travel faster than itself.

    But perhaps you mean to ask whether a source of light can travel faster than the speed of that light in that medium. So, for instance, can an underwater light source travel faster than the speed of light in water. Yes. That's perfectly fine. That particular question is commonly asked and a common example of this phenomenon (called Cherenkov radiation) is seen in the typical blue-ish glow of underwater nuclear reactors.

    DishPapuam A:

    The electromagnetic wave, like any other wave, has a phase velocity and a group velocity. Of course, EM waves only act on large (Classical) objects according their group velocity, which corresponds to a photon's velocity expectation value. While the group velocity of an EM wave has to propagate at the speed of light, the phase velocity is free to exceed that value. For example, X-rays dispersing through certain materials can indicate superluminal phase velocities, but the group velocity never exceeds the speed of light. This is called anomalous dispersion.
