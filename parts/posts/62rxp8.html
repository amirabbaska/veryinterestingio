    Q:

    As I understand, the only difference between the two is that a proton consists of two up quarks and a down quark, and a neutron consists of two down quarks and an up quark. I know their masses are almost exactly the same, but why aren't they EXACTLY exactly the same?

    RobusEtCeleritas A:

    If isospin were a perfect symmetry, they would have the same masses. But isospin is not a true symmetry of the standard model. It's broken by the electromagnetic force. Since the proton has charge and the neutron doesn't, they couple differently to the electromagnetic force.

    PedroV100 A:

    A slightly more elaborate view of hadrons: They're a quark/antiquark/gluon soup. The proton has 2 more up quarks than up antiquarks, 1 more down quark than down antiquarks. The neutron has 1 more up quark than up antiquarks and 2 more down quarks than down antiquarks. The kinetic energy + potential energy (energy stored as inteactions) between all this particles in the soup is what gives hadrons most of its mass. The remainder mass is gained by the interactions between all this particles and the 3 remainding fundamental forces of nature + higgs field. The interactions, for example, with the electromagnetic field are going to be diferent since the proton has charge and the neutron does not. This extra potential energy shows itself as mass acording to einsteins mass-energy equivelancy.
