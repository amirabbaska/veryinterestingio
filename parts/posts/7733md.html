    StardustSapien A:

    As pointed out by /u/ShoelessHodor, highly concentrated sunlight of great intensity can be achieved in applications such concentrated solar thermal power generation. In such processes, coherance isn't important as long as you can deliver energy to a focal point at a high enough density to heat whatever working fluid is being used to transform heat into electricity.

    On the flip side, there are collimating techniques you can use to produce beams of low divergence and low dispersion. But this is mostly accomplished by blocking a great amount of the original source such that you end up with a much weaker beam instead of a more concentrated one. For some applications such as college physics demonstrations like low precision interferometry, it exactly fits the bill, so no judgement there.

    Because sunlight is composed of a spectrum, what you really want is basically impossible. Magnetic fields do not affect photons in the way you think. From a purely theoretical perspective, yes, if your engineering is advanced enough to manipulate gravity, you can use a processes similar to gravitational lensing to bend light at the scale you imagine. But I can think of more exciting things to do with such power.

    nebulousmenace A:

    For the original question: No. The XKCD idea that the sun is not a point source is the main problem. (it is about half a degree across.) So you're going to get about 1 % "sunspread" (the beam becomes 1 meter wider for every 100 meters it travels) and there's pretty much nothing you can do about it.

    source: used to work in concentrating solar thermal. It felt like every time I turned around, I'd find some new way we lost efficiency. "Sunspread" was one I didn't see coming.

    Mysunsai A:
    1. Classically, external electric or magnetic fields do not act on light. A simple explanation is that electric and magnetic fields only act on charged particles, and photons have zero charge. There are much more complicated explanations of this when treating light as a wave (ones which I am not at all confident on myself), but they come out to the same thing.

    In quantum electrodynamics, there is some effect at high energy levels, delbruck scattering, which is related to the possibility that a photon will disintegrate into a electron-positron pair. I can't really explain it to you beyond that, but I suspect it wouldn't be able to do what you want.

    1. Gravitational lensing has many of the same properties as traditional optical lensing, and therefore should run into the same problems as the xkcd post you saw.

    2. I don't believe this will work the way you want it to, but I don't know enough about the subject to be sure.

    At a certain point, you may just be better off collecting solar power and using it to power a laser, if your goal is minimal spread/diffraction.
