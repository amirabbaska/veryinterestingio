    lishyguy A:

    You're quite allowed to have a superposition over more than two photons. The GHZ state that you mentioned at the end is exactly that - it's usually a certain type of entanglement over three Hilbert spaces (photons), but you can extend the concept to any number of photons.

    The GHZ state looks like (|000> + |111>)/sqrt(2) in its most common version, but the general idea is that you have "n" qubits (here we're using photons), and two terms in the superposition. For each qubit, it should have a |0> (or other single basis vector) in one of the terms, and a |1> in the other. For example, (|000> + |111>)/sqrt(2) is the usual one, but we often say that (e.g.) (|011> + |100>)/sqrt(2) is also a GHZ-type state, or (|0+-> + |1-+>)/sqrt(2), where two of the qubits are in the |+> and |-> basis instead. Also, we can go to higher orders, and say have a GHZ-type state that looks like (|00000> + |11111>)/sqrt(2), though it becomes more difficult to make these. These types of states have been made experimentally: this Nature paper is one example (you can find the pdf online if you search the name of the paper).

    Like your other answerers, I'm not in non-linear optics, so I'm a little hazy on the mechanics of SPDC and what would happen in it. I can say, though, that the chance of SPDC happening with any given photon is really really small - something like one-in-a-billion (10^(-9)), so it would be quite hard to realise this scheme experimentally (but not impossible, and my information on it may be a little outdated).

    Cera1th A:

    As I told you in another post you can in principle generate GHZ states by down-converting a photon that is already part of an entangled state again.

    Down conversion, however, is a very inefficient process. While down-conversion of already downconverted has been demonstrated, it comes with relatively low efficiencies.

    You don't get super-superpositions, but just superpositions in higher dimensions.

    I got slightly lost but I think you are asking what happens when we entangle an entangled system.

    As suggested you get another superposition but something really cool happen when you collapse the wavefunction: called entanglement transfer or teleportation. There was a recent experiment where they entangled two electrons that were separated by several km in this way.

    This was done entangling both elections with photons and then bringing the photons to the same point in space and entangling them. When you measure the photons the two electrons become entangled without ever having directly interacted.

    Here is the experiment: https://arxiv.org/abs/1508.05949
