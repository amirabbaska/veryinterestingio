    NEW
    Q:

    ELI5: Why did it take 3 years to convict James Holmes of a mass murder that he clearly commited?

    palcatraz A:

    Because things aren't that clear cut.

    Murder is a very specific charge (it requires killing that is premeditated, malicious and wilful). Even if you see someone kill another person, that doesn't mean that person is automatically guilty of murder. Could just end up as manslaughter. Or not guilty by reason of self defence. Or not guilty by reason of insanity. To prove whether it is one over the other, evidence needs to be gathered, witnesses need to be interviewed, psych profiles need to be made, examined, possibly redone. All of that takes a long time. And seeing as the US has double jeopardy laws - as in, you cannot be tried twice for the same crime - it is of the utmost important that this whole process is done carefully and that every little detail and fragment is looked at. Because if the prosecution fails and the guy walks away from this, they can never charge him for it ever again. Even if he admits to it later or other evidence comes out to prove it was him. Criminals have gone free in the past due to the prosecution bungling and rushing into a case with not enough or proper evidence. You really don't want to do that with a guy who is suspect of killing multiple people.

    Add in a good dash of how backed up the court system is, the time it takes to find a good jury in cases like this (a jury should be unbiased, but that is hard to find when cases like this have been so hugely reported on in the news) and it should be clear why this all took so long.

    Keep-reefer-illegal A:

    Short answer: he waived a lot of his rights

    Long answer: 99% of the time, it won't take 3 years to get to trial unless you want it to. You have the right to a speedy trial, this varies by state a lot but in all states it is a matter of months.

    Now on to the waiving or rights. The justice system is clogged as heck. Blame it on drugs, blame it on Nixon, blame it on the Cookie Monster, the fact is we have a lot more cases than we have time. If you want to say "I Waive my right to a speedy trial" most of the time we will accommodate. People do this for a lot of reasons, more time to prepare, more chance of evidence discovery, witnesses forget over time, or just because it is your right to waive a right.

    The fact is if James Holmes wanted a speedy trial it would have been done in 2012. He (or his defense team) waived the speedy trial right and decided to drag it out.

    Edit: there is also no "clear cut" case, in law you have to prove the evil act (the person arrested for th crime did it) and the evil mind (the person knew what they were doing was wrong)
