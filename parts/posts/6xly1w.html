    CrateDane A:

    People were already living in Beringia, where the Bering Sea now divides Asia from North America. The glaciation had lowered the sea levels and created a huge band of dry, cold steppes ranging from Europe to North America. Beringia, on the shores of the Pacific, would have been a little wetter, especially as the deglaciation occurred, but it was still fairly dry and not forested.

    It wouldn't have been an especially difficult place for people to live; consider how Inuits were still living at a stone age level along the Greenland ice cap until very recently. And people in Beringia would have had good access to both marine and land animals to hunt, unlike the Inuits.

    Once the glaciers blocking the route southeast into America melted, they could spread that way. No long journeys required.

    FigueroaYakYak A:

    Nothing drove them to suddenly decide to cross, populations just tend to spread to new locations if they can survive there, because they'll face less competition from other members of their species. That's what drove most human migration up to that point, and what led humans to settle in Siberia in the first place. What changed 13,000 years ago is that they were actually able to cross, because the sea level dropped due to so much water being trapped in glaciers, which exposed the land bridge of Beringia. Small groups probably followed prey animals across it, those animals were crossing because vegetation was being exposed which had never been grazed before.
