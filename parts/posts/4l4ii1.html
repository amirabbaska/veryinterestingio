    NEW
    Q:

    ELI5: How do guide dogs know where their owner wants to go?

    sonataconfuoco A:

    I am blind and on my second Seeing Eye dog. My current partner, a lovely 23-month-old German shepherd, doesn't know where to go, but she does know how to follow my commands. It's my responsibility in the parntership to translate all routes into a series of commands the dog understands -- right, left, forward. My partner follows these commands to the extent that doing so will not place me in danger. She may deviate from them to go around mobile or stationery obstacles, or refuse to obey them if the route becomes dangerous (car cuts me off in a crosswalk, I get disoriented and tell her to walk off the train platform). We have overlapping responsibilities to know where we're going and get there safely. I memorise the routes, or carry a GPS set to "walking" navigation mode. She knows the commands, recognises obstacles and threats, and points out familiar landmarks such as businesses we've entered before, an office or bathroom along the hallway that we've entered before, et cetera.

    vwlsmssng A:

    In my limited experience of knowing people with guide dogs, both the dog and the owner needs to be taught and practice the routes they use. They train together for 4 weeks.

    If they want to go to a new route, e.g. the local post office has closed and they need to go to another one, then someone has to come and help them learn the route.

    From the Guide Dogs for the Blind website

    Some of the skills Guide Dogs learn include:

    • Leading a person in a straight line from point A to point B
    • Stopping for all changes in elevation, including curbs and stairs
    • Stopping for overhead obstacles, such as tree limbs
    • Avoiding obstacles in their path

    Two things Guide Dogs can't do:

    • Determine the route to a new destination
    • Read traffic signals

    They can't check the traffic lights!

    It’s a popular misconception that a guide dog will know when to cross the road by waiting for the green light. In reality, it’s a team effort – and not an easy task. On a standard kerb-to-kerb crossing known to the owner, the guide dog is trained to stop at the edge, to indicate it has reached a crossing. The owner will listen for traffic, then, when he or she decides it’s safe, give the command ‘Forward!’ However, the guide dog’s training teaches them NOT to obey the command if a car’s coming.

    Source: Guide Dogs for the Blind

    n0esc A:

    Seeing eye dogs don't actually know where to go or how to get to somewhere. What they are taught is how to safely follow commands such as "left" "right" and "forward". If the handler says to go forward, the dog will only do so if it is safe. For example at a traffic light, a blind person may command forward based on the sound of traffic being stopped, or the newer talking "walk / don't walk" lights saying to go, but the dog will stop if he sees a car parked in the crosswalk, or senses another unsafe condition.

    Blind people generally know their community and are familiar with the areas they live and work, so giving direction to the dog is way for common destinations. For a new or unfamiliar area, they ask directions like anyone else would, and then issue the same commands to the dog.
