    Q:

    Would an hourglass measure the same amount of time on a planet/moon with a different gravitational pull?

    Midtek A:

    This is a relevant paper. The flow of granular objects is very different from the flow of a liquid in terms of underlying mechanics (e.g., pressure vs. friction). So determining the flow rate is not a trivial matter. What follows is a short paraphrasing of a relevant section of the paper.

    Dimensional analysis can get you a rough relation for the flow rate. For simplicity, suppose our "hourglass" is a right circular cylinder filled with sand, which flows out through a small hole punched in the bottom of the cylinder. There are ostensibly (at least) 5 parameters that enter the flow rate: the diameter of the cylinder (D), the height of the sand (H), the diameter of the hole (r), the diameter of each grain (a), and the local gravitational acceleration (g).

    It turns out that experimentally you can determine that if H is large compared to D and r is large compared to a, then the flow rate is independent of all our parameters except r and g. By dimensional analysis, the speed v of the grains as they exit the hole must be proportional to (gr)^(1/2). The flow rate is proportional to this speed times the cross-sectional area, hence proportional to vr^(2) ~ g^(1/2)r^(5/2).

    The time it takes for an hourglass to empty is inverse proportional to the flow rate, hence proportional to g^(-1/2)r^(-5/2). So, for instance, if a certain hourglass measures time T on Earth, it will measure 6^(1/2)T on the Moon (because the Moon's gravity is about one-sixth that of Earth). Hence a 1-minute timer on Earth becomes about a 2.5-minute timer on the Moon.

    Summary: Flow rate = Q ~ g^(1/2)r^(5/2), Total time = T ~ g^(-1/2)r^(-5/2).

    Non scientific but logical answer. Since nothing falls at zero gravity everything will fall slower at lower gravity, meaning then that the hourglass would have to be calibrated for a specific gravity. In turn then, an hourglass calibrated at sea level would be inaccurate at high altitudes. FWIW, Captain Morgan has helped me see the gravity of this situation.
