    Q:

    [physics] If the earth wasn't spinning would we feel the gravity more because of the lack of centrifugal forces?

    iorgfeflkd A:

    The centrifugal force, plus the reshaping of the Earth that it induces, amount to about a 0.7% variation between the equator and the poles. So yes, we'd feel slightly more (except at the poles where it would imperceptibly less).

    SidusObscurus A:

    You'd be under slightly greater net downward acceleration, but it'd be so small a change you wouldn't notice, and almost nothing directly would change due to gravitational acceleration.

    Other significant things would indirectly change, however, such as the oceanic currents, the seasons, the shape of the earth, and the rotation of the earth's core. I don't know enough to guess at those changes, but I think its safe to say it would be very serious.

    introvertedintooit A:

    F = mrw^2 = centripetal force

    m = mass [kg]

    r = radius of circle traced by human on earth [m]

    w = angular velocity [rad/sec], of human in computer chair. Equals 360 degrees per 24 hours

    F = 60*6371000*0.000073^2 ~= 2 [Newtons]

    Weight of human on computer chair = F_2 = ma = 60*9.8 = 588 [Newtons].

    Net force on human in computer chair = 586 Newtons

    Multiply w by 17 to roughly cause both weight and centripetal force to be equal, resulting in a feeling of weightlessness. 24 hours / 17 = 1.412 hours, roughly the orbital period of the ISS. Coincidence? I think not.
