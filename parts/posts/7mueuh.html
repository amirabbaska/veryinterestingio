    Q:

    What's the difference between antibiotic, antiseptic and anti-bacteria?

    Seldon_ A:

    'Antibiotic' refers to a class of drugs designed to kill and inhibit the growth of bacteria and/or other microorganisms within the body.

    'Antiseptic' refers to any substance which is administered topically on living tissue to do same. Its mechanism of action is generally limited to the area upon which you apply it. Think hand sanitizer or iodine.

    'Anti-bacteria' is anything designed to kill bacteria.

    frazwood A:

    Definitions vary according to context. The definitions that I use are:

    An 'antibiotic' is a naturally-produced compound that specifically kills microorganisms, usually bacteria. Examples would be penicillin, tetracycline, etc.

    An 'antibacterial' is a synthetic compound that specifically kills bacteria (an 'antimicrobial' would kill microorganisms). Examples would be ciprofloxacin, sulfamethoxazole, etc.

    An 'antiseptic' is a compound that kills all living cells. Examples would be bleach (hypochlorous acid), ammonia, alcohol, etc.

    That is, the specificity is important. Antiseptics kill more than just bacteria, so they often are as useful as a medical treatment. That is, you can take penicillin for an ear infection and it won't hurt your cells... but you can't drink a bottle of bleach for the same purpose.

    Other people use different definitions. I've heard people claim that an antibiotic is specifically a medicine. So, for example, triclosan (the most common 'antibacterial' in antibacterial soap) is not an antibiotic because it is not used as a medicine.
