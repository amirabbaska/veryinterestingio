    It's obvious most of the time, everybody knows what a ciave is. You walk into a hole in the rock and wow, you're in a cave. But when is a hole in the ground not only a hole but considered a cave by science?

    netiquett A:

    Caver here. The Tennessee cave survey defines a cave as a natural passage (can be explored by a person) that extends at least 40ft horizontal. If it is a pit straight down that isn't 40ft horizontal, it's not a cave.

    Hello_There_Austin A:

    There is no official way to define a cave.

    “‘A natural home in the ground, large enough for human entry’ is probably the most useful definition. This covers the enormous variety of caves that do occur but eliminates the many artificial tunnels and galleries incorrectly named caves. The size criterion is arbitrary and subjective, but practical, as it eliminates narrow openings irrelevant to explorers but very significant hydrologically, that may be better referred to as proto-caves, sub-conduits or fissures. A cave may be a single, short length of accessible passage, or an extensive and complex network of tunnels as long as the hundreds of kilometers in the Flint Mammoth Cave System. Most caves are formed by dissolution in limestone but sandstone caves, lava caves, glacier caves and tectonic caves also occur. Marginal candidates for use of the name cave include riverbank undercuts and rock shelters of various origins. In some countries a cave is regarded as being a horizontal opening, as opposed to a pothole, which is a vertical opening. This usage is common in England but is not ubiquitous[9]. 2. A natural opening formed in the rocks below the surface of the ground large enough for a man to enter. It may consist of a single connected opening or a series of small or large chambers connected by galleries[20].

    1. A similar artificial opening[10]. Related to cavern. Synonyms: (French.) grotte, caverne; (German.) Höhle, Grotte; (Greek.) speleon; (Italian.) caverna, grotta; (Russian.) pescera; (Spanish.) cueva; (Turkish.) maTMara; (Yugoslavian.) pec#ina. pec#, pes#tera, spilja, zijjalka, jama. See also active cave; bedding cave; cave system; grotto; sea cave.”

    See page 32 (https://innerspacecavern.com/schools_pages/docs/lexicon-of-cave-and-karst-terminology.pdf)

    A karst is a relatively fast growing cave in comparison to other geologic features. They form when a rock like limestone begins dissolving from groundwater. I suppose if the water was acidic enough and the flow strong enough that a human would be able to see a difference in the rock surface, but I don’t think an entire cave can form in a humans lifetime. I guess you can say that cave formed then, but then again not really because it would have been dissolving and eroding for tens or hundreds of thousands/million of years prior.

    Prof_Explodius A:

    I'll take a stab at your bonus question.

    Sudden rock collapses can create caves. A rockfall or rockslide from the base of a cliff could leave an overhang with space underneath. Likewise, there are sometimes caves under large boulders in talus fields, which could be deposited in a single rockslide.

    Your more classic limestone caves form slowly through dissolution of carbonate minerals as water flows through the formation and this is not really something that happens on a human timescale. There are other minerals that dissolve more quickly like salt or gypsum, so for example sudden flow of water through a salt formation could quickly form voids, but it's more likely these would collapse as quickly as they formed.
