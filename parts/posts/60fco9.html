    Q:

    Since the flow of electric current through a wire is same as that of water through a pipe, will the flow of electric current be restricted when we "squeeze" the wire?

    Rannasha A:

    Well, not literally, since squeezing a metal wire typically won't do anything (at best you'll compress the plastic insulation that surrounds it).

    But it is true that the current that can run through a wire, at a given voltage, depends on the thickness of the wire. Thin wires have a higher resistance and can therefore carry less current at a given voltage (or, stated differently: require a higher voltage to carry a given amount of current).

    This is why certain high current applications have requirements on wire thickness.

    chcampb A:

    In a system with fluid in a pipe, if you have a pressure difference in the pipe, the fluid will try to equalize the pressure, and will move from high pressure to low pressure to do so. The higher the pressure, the higher the flow rate, proportional to the resistance in the pipe.

    In a system with electrons in a wire, if you have a voltage difference in the wire, the electrons will try to equalize the voltage, and will move from low voltage to high voltage to do so. The higher the voltage, the higher the current, proportional to the resistance in the wire.

    In both cases, resistance is proportional to the cross sectional area of the pipe or wire. If you reduce that, you reduce the rate of current or water flow.
