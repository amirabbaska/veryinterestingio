    xolieo A:

    Xenon increases Erythropoietin (EPO) levels which promotes the formation of red blood cells by the bone marrow. There is some anecdotal evidence that some top tier athletes were using it to increase testosterone production as well.

    tcush89 A:

    Keep in mind xenon is not inert! It can experience a dipole due to weak electron attraction to the nucleus in the outermost electron shell, which not only allows it to form compounds (e.g. xenon tetrafluoride), but it can also interact with certain protein structures.

    For example, as another user mentioned, xenon is actually a brilliant anesthetic. There’s been quite a bit of work done with it, and it’s totally awesome: Blood solubility is low, so it goes in, does it’s job, and leaves your bloodstream super fast, allowing for quick induction and quick recovery. The only limiting factor is price. Xenon costs about $8 per liter in the current market. Say we have a mean flow of 2L/min for a given procedure that lasts 60 minutes. That’s $960 for the gas alone. Not to mention fees for other supplies, fees to pay the anesthesiologist, or the cost of the actual surgery.

    As others have mentioned, EPO production is why it’s banned in sports. Just wanted to point out though that xenon (and some other noble gases) are not biologically inert. If you inhale enough xenon, you will pass out.
