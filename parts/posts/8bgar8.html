    pxalt A:

    The court would ordinarily provide the defendant with a payment plan, potentially combined with other avenues such as wage garnishment or a writ of execution (ordering that some of the defendant’s assets be sold and the proceeds given to the plaintiff).

    However, the court can’t wring blood from a stone. If the money simply doesn’t exist, then it doesn’t exist. The term for a defendant who doesn’t have the resources to pay a legal judgment is judgment proof. Many lawyers working on contingency won’t agree to take a case where the only potential defendant is judgment proof. It’s a lot of work for nothing.

    Lothlann88 A:

    I am a solicitor in the UK and this is one of the main considerations we have when advising a client whether to undertake litigation or not.

    If the defendant does not have the funds to meet a judgment, then there is little point in litigating.

    Interesting note, a defendant may apply to the court for an interim order for security for costs. This is done early in the process (before trial) and is essentially a defendant convincing a judge that there is a risk that the claimant cannot meet the defendant's costs if the defendant wins.

    As the defendant usually has no choice but to defend a claim, the court may order the claimant to pay a sum into the court as security in the event that they are ordered to meet some/all of the defendants costs.

    Side note: you almost never recover all your costs in an English court. 2/3rds is the usual amount.
