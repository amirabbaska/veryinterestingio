    Q:

    During the Cold War, how were ICBMs guided? Without GPS how do you accurately hit a city in the middle of Siberia from across the world?

    Problem119V-0800 A:

    They used inertial navigation systems, much like jetliners still do to supplement GPS. With some gyroscopes to know the missile's orientation and some accelerometers to know its acceleration (or even just with a pre-programmed knowledge of the engine's thrust profile) you can get a pretty good idea of its current velocity and make corrections to stay on course. This works pretty well. The German V2 rockets used this in WWII, and there were even torpedos with steampunky brass gyroscopes and gears and clock-springs to hold course underwater. By the 1970s, though, physical spinning metal gyroscopes were being replaced by laser ring gyroscopes (e.g., e.g.) which are more accurate. The micromechanical gyros in your phone work on a vibration principle.

    Some missiles used ground guidance during the boost phase — that is, radars near the launch site would follow the missile's course and send info to it by radio — but I don't know if that technique was used much for ICBMs. If you're launching ICBMs, it means things have really gone to hell, and you might not want to rely on any particular other part of your country to still be in existence if you don't have to. But ABMs like the Sprint were effectively ground-guided.

    A ballistic missile is a bit like a bullet, in that (almost) all of the acceleration is at launch time. For most of the travel time, it's just coasting/falling — that's what "ballistic" means in "ballistic missile". There might be a midcourse correction (maybe based on star sights, aka "astro-inertial" navigation) and some terminal-phase corrections during reëntry, but for the most part, the guidance happens during boost and immediately afterwards.

    As a side note, one of the reasons that large nuclear weapons went out of favor after the '50s was improved missile guidance. A 50-MT bomb is useful if you can only get it approximately on target, but if you have a missile that can drop things within a mile or so of where you want, it makes much more sense to load it up with several independently-targeted 100-kT bombs instead of a single monster. Today, most of the US's warheads are below a megaton.
