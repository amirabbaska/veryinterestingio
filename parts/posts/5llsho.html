    zimmah A:

    It mostly boils down to cost.
    Natural fruit juice is expensive and usually does not have a long shelf life (longer shelf life is usually a good thing because it leads to less waste and more important less write-offs (a write-off meaning a product has reached the sell-by date and now either has to be 'sold' for free or thrown away, depending on laws and store policy).
    Even if they have high amounts of actual juice it's often concentrated apple juice because apple juice is relatively cheap (especially if it's from concentrated juice) and it also has a quite neutral taste so it doesn't overtake the other fruits in taste.
    This way they can legally claim "80% real fruit juice" even though 95% of that is apple juice and only 5% is the actual fruit they are marketing.

    RippingAallDay A:

    Beverage chemist here.

    It's marketing. All marketing. Some here have said cost but if it was done exclusively for cost, said juice would not even be in there. HFCS and sugar was way cheaper on a solids basis.

    As far as the other ingredients in there, some ingredients (ascorbic acid (vitamin C), sucralose, cyanocobalamin (vitamin B12) are effective at very low doses (think milligrams & micrograms worth).

    If you're drinking tea that you've purchased, there's a good chance that there is only a small amount of "spray dried" tea in there and caramel color added.

    Drink water. (not alkaline water... more marketing).

    ferrouswolf2 A:

    The other issue that hasn't been mentioned is seasonality. Sure, juice can be stored and packaged year round (orange juice is one of the most "processed" foods, not matter how much branding goes into making it seem like it was picked yesterday), but the fruit is only harvested in certain months of the year, and every harvest produces juice with different acid and sugar levels that have to be adjusted.
