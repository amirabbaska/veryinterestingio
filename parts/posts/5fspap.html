    goes-on-rants A:

    There are a couple things that influence shine on any object, including skin.

    Part of it is form factor. Shiny skin is really just the reflection of light, like an arrow rebounds from the light off the object and into the eye, and a dome shaped object like a head will always be positioned to reflect a light source into your eyes unless the light source is in between you, which is a small angle. The sun in the sky would almost always throw a reflection. The reflection is called specular light.

    The other part is the roughness of the surface itself, called the diffusion coefficient. If you study your fingers you would see that the individual ridges are very shiny, but they do not reflect light in a consistent direction so they don't add up to a strong specular reflection.

    I can't say exactly what causes your typical arm or leg to appear to have a low diffusion coefficient -- they don't seem shiny but also lack fingerprints. Maybe it's because the head is more oily.

    The skin of humans (and most mammals) contains sebaceous glands, which produce an oily substance that lubricates and waterproofs the skin and hair. In humans, these glands are concentrated more on the face and scalp. Also, some people polish their bald scalp to make it shine more.
