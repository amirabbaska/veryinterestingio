    DrColdReality A:

    All the answers here currently are wrong to one degree or another.

    A single long exposure absolutely would show blurring under these conditions. Some probes have camera tracking platforms on them, which keep the camera aimed at one spot for long exposures, but New Horizons doesn't, they have to turn the whole craft if they want to track.

    So instead, they stack images. They take a bunch of very short exposures--about 100 msec for Pluto at closest approach--which are too brief to show any significant blurring, then stack them together optically.

    ka-splam A:

    I saw somewhere in the NASA IAMA that they take many shorter exposure photographs and combine them, because it doesn't require the probe to be as stable. I can't find the exact link, it's likely in one of the 'load more comments' bits.

    It's moving at 14,000 metres/second. A decent consumer digital camera (e.g. a Nikon D7200) can take a 1/8000th of a second exposure. That would mean the probe moves 1.75 metres in one picture.

    NASA can likely afford faster, and more light-sensitive sensors than a consumer grade digital camera. The probe can turn, as well.
