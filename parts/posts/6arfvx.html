    Because flouride is good for our teeth.

    Very very good for them.

    Eating or drinking it (via water or table salt) helps to increase your teeths lifespan, and prevent cavities.

    Smearing it on your teeth (via toothpaste) also helps to fight off cavities, (it's a slightly different kind of flouride).

    Drugsnstuffs A:

    Fluoride has been found to prevent tooth decay since the early 1900's, but fluoridation only prevents tooth decay when it is frequently used (brushing your teeth everyday). Since many rural communities in the United States lacked dental care, fluoride was added to water supplies in the early 20th century as a way to provide a more holistic approach.The results shown through these communities were enough to warrant other cities and towns to use fluoride in their water supply and soon became a public health necessity for the United States.

    Liftingmanatees5 A:

    To build on some other comments - fluoride can actually help to harden the structure on your teeth. It is believed that the fluoride can replace some of the hydroxyapatite structure in enamel with fluroapatite which is a harder compound that is more difficult to erode.

    The level of fluoride in drinking water is quite low. To get the maximal benefit it would be better to use toothpaste or mouthwash which have much higher concentrations of fluoride (all of which are safely below concerning concentrations). These levels are highly regulated by the FDA in the US, as you will see most toothpastes will deliver similar amounts of total fluoride. In addition, fluoride uptake is not 100% efficient so the amount you see on the tube is likely not the amount your mouth will absorb.

    The fluoride delivery itself is typically through three common ingredients- sodium fluoride, sodium monofluorophosphate, and stannous fluoride. Changing the source of fluoride can potentially have additional benefits for your oral health (e.g. stannous fluoride is known to have some degree of sensitivity benefit). This can also influence the total fluoride uptake in your mouth.

    One final note is that fluoride is so important for oral health that it's very rare you will find a toothpaste without it that bears the symbol of the ADA. As a previous oral care researcher who worked with fluoride on a daily basis, I believe many of the concerns around fluoride are misguided due to the relatively low exposure levels we actually experience.
