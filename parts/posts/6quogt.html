    kouhoutek A:

    It is an artifact of how your eyes process color.

    A normal human eye has cone cells sensitive to red, green, and blue. However, the red cone is a little quirky and is also sensitive to a region in the blue spectrum. As the frequency of light increases, the red cone fires, then the green, then both blue and the red, which is why violet looks a little reddish. In fact, RGB screens can't produce monochromatic violet light, so they simulate it by combining red and blue in a manner the stimulates our eyes in about the same way violet would.

    stevemegson A:

    Colours aren't exactly just frequencies. They're the result of those frequencies being detected and interpreted by our eyes. Your TV can produce the colour yellow, but (usually) it can't produce light of a frequency that we'd call yellow. It produces red light and green light, and that combination looks just as yellow as "real" yellow light.

    If you want to transition smoothly from red to blue, there are two ways to do it. You could gradually change the frequency, moving through the rainbow until you get to blue. The "long way" around the colour wheel represents that. Alternatively, you could gradually make the red light dimmer and a blue light brighter until you have just blue light left. These mixtures of red and blue light give the "short way" around the wheel, through the magentas and purples. There's no single wavelength of light which corresponds to those colours, but they're still colours that you can see and name.
