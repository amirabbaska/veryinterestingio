    It_worked A:

    The short and skinny: IPA (isopropyl alcohol) effectively dissolves the lipid membrane of a bacteria. 100% IPA is great at dissolving fatty acids but some bacteria have more than just lipids in their membranes, there are a lot of proteins present as well which the IPA can't effectively penetrate. The water helps the IPA denature these proteins. Some bacteria, called spore formers will form a very protein rich shell which even 70% IPA can't denature so H2O2 is a great alternative (6% hydrogen peroxide). H2O2 is effective against most bacteria other than the species that are rather well suited to break down H2O2 (Catalase rich species) . Nature truly is metal and always finds a way to survive harsh conditions/environments.

    DieterVonCuntth A:

    Most cells have something called "Aquaporins" which are highly specialized transmembrane proteins that act as special Toll Road lanes for water to easily get into and out of the cell. These are extremely important, because a cell regulates it's water content by ion pumps, and let's the water freely flow via diffusion in response.

    Having ~70% allows the antiseptic alcohol to hitch a ride in the "toll lane" of water (via intermolecular hydrogen bonding) and act on the intracellular contents of the cell.

    Source: Medical Student
