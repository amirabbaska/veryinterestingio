    I was thinking about how when I read silently to myself, I "hear" the words I'm reading in my head. When I read the word "window" I "hear" it, know what it is, and continue on. I don't "see" in my mind's eye a picture of a window unless I need to (e.g. to solve a puzzle I might picture various kinds of windows).

    Then I wondered, for someone who has never heard the the pronunciation of the word or know what it sounds like, what happens when they read the word "window"? Do they picture it in their mind's eye or does something else entirely different take place?

    robustoutlier A:

    What is known from research on people with congenital blindness is that the cells in "visual cortex" (the back of the brain) adapt to other input. If I recall correctly, the visual cortex would process tactile information. This can be taken as evidence that cells in specific brain areas are not specialized and very specific (as perhaps Broadmann's research would imply). The argument is perhaps more difficult to make from what we today know about pluripotent stemcells (although that would be more elegant); these findings were simply based on information on the anatomical and cognitive level.

    It is possible that this principle applies to the brains of people with congenital deafness too. However, deafness from birth may be due to multiple causes. For example, deafness can be caused at any point of the auditory pathway (inner ear, cochlea, afferent fibres, brain stem, mid brain, thalamus), including damage to the brain itself (i.e. auditory cortex). If the brain remains intact, the "principle" of compensatory neuroplasticity suggests that all neurons will be used for something.

    I have only addressed the third-person perspective here. I am not sure "what it is like" for people with this condition, subjectively. Perhaps it varies depending on the nature of the condition.
