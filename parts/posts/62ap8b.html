• Who, whom, whoever, whomever, and whomsoever. How do these work?

    NEW
    Q:

    I always see these in arguments and I'm never sure what are the rules for using them.

    PaulsRedditUsername A:

    If the answer is "him," use whom.
    If the answer is "he," use who.

    "Who is going to catch that snake?" "He is."

    "Whom did you give the snake to?" "I gave it to "him."

    ERRORMONSTER A:

    Who - subject. "Who am I? I am me."

    Whom - object. "To whom are you giving that? I'm giving it to you."

    Whoever - pronoun for an unidentified person used as a subject. "Whoever did that is in trouble."

    Whomever - pronoun for an unidentified person used as an object. "I will speak with whomever I desire."

    Whomsoever - formal synonym for whomever. "I will write to whomsoever I need to."

    E: nitpicky sentences adjusted for pedants

    StupidLemonEater A:

    "Who" is a subject. It does things in sentences.

    "Whom" is an object. Things are done to it in sentences.

    E.g. "Whom did you see today?" vs. "Who saw you today?"

    It's the same as the difference between "I" and "me;" "I" is a subject and "me" is an object.

    As far as I know who/whomsoever means the exact same thing as who/whomever.

    OhCamembert A:

    Who = subject (person/thing doing the action) Whom = object (person/thing receiving the action)

    "I don't know who stole my applesauce."

    "I don't know whom you told to steal my applesauce."

    Notice in the second sentence, "you" does the action of telling someone to steal. The person being told to steal is "whom".

    We don't care much about this in English anymore, but in other languages (German for example), the difference is crucial.

    You'd be surprised at how many things we say incorrectly. We accept them even though they might be totally illogical. I only became aware of them from learning other languages.
