    zachalicious A:

    In most cases, no. You can read up on a quick summary by Peter Pressman of Cedars Sinai Medical Center and Roger Clemens of the University of Southern California School of Pharmacy here. The gist of it is that cravings are more a factor of sociocultural factors, stressful environments and hormonal fluctuation than nutritional deficiencies. You can read further on the topic here where Karen Ansel, a registered dietitian, discusses how cravings work, and what areas of the brain are activated. She asserts that most cravings are dictated by social tropes, visual cues, or childhood memories.

    The one caveat to this seems to be pica, where people eat non-food items such as soil, clay, stones, etc. From the little research that has been done on pica, the cause seems to point to a mineral deficiency, most likely iron.

    Sluisifer A:

    Clara Marie Davis did a really cool experiment in the 1930s looking at what choices children made given many (healthy) options. Generally they would eat balanced diets instead of favoring just e.g. meats or cheese or fruit. Some even displayed unusual choices that may have benefited particular conditions they had.

    http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC1626509/

    There's been some interest in this work recently, but I'm not sure if much followup has been done.

    anamorphism A:

    i don't know what this sub's policy is for answers of this type.

    the answer is that no, the vast majority of studies have concluded that food cravings don't seem to stem from nutritional deficiencies. i'm not really going to go and find links to a dozen abstracts for you, but here's a google scholar search for you to get you started: https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=food+cravings+nutrition&btnG=&hl=en&as_sdt=0%2C5

    cravings tend to relate to foods high in calories (also what most people tend to find tastes good). they also tend to stem from sociological and psychological factors (reward mechanisms, what you grew up eating, etc.).
