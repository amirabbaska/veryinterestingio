• Biology Why does music provoke deep emotions in some people yet does nothing for others?

    NEW
    Q:

    For example, I absolutely love music, I have playlists for every mood. Some music makes me just completely stop what I'm doing and listen, and music can change my mood in seconds. However, my friend doesn't enjoy listening to any genre of music. She doesn't mind it if its playing somewhere, but she is indifferent to listening to it, and despite listening to many songs me and my friends have recommended, she says she doesn't feel any emotions from it.

    cantcountnoaccount A:

    The human brain is infinite in its variation. Some people respond most emotionally to their auditory senses (like music), some to their visual senses (like art), some to olfactory senses (smell), some to written language (reading), some to kinesthetics (body movement). And this is mediated through their life experiences which may push them closer or further to interest in one particular area.

    Have you cried, or felt a chill, at a short story or painting ever? Why/why not? Have you ever become emotional because of a smell? Why/why not? Perhaps your friend has an emotional connection with another aspect of their senses that means nothing to you.

    DarwinZDF42 A:

    One reason is that there is a thing associated with how we learn called "long term potentiation," which basically means that when two things happen at the same time, like a sound and an emotion, for example, they become associated, so one can trigger the other.

     

    For example, there was a famous experiment where babies were exposed to a loud noise, which scared them, and bunnies, and for much of their lives were afraid of bunnies, because they associated the visual stimulus (bunnies) with the scary thing (a loud sound).

     

    So say you're an angsty teenage and you're feeling all angsty, and also listening to a particular album a lot. Your brain is going to associate those two things - that specific music and that specific emotion - for a long period of time afterwards. So a decade later, that music still evokes an extremely strong emotion. Because in your brain, the two things are tied together - one triggers the other.

     

    This also explains why these responses are hyper-specific to each person. It requires a specific set of conditions - repeated exposure to the song at the same time as experiencing a specific emotion. That's not something someone else is going to have, so having them listen to the same song won't get the same response. In your case, you listen to music all the time, so this has probably happened for lots of songs, albums, even genres - that's why it can change your mood in seconds. But none of that would happen for someone who just doesn't listen to music. The associations aren't there.

     

    Fun fact: you can use this as a hack to learn something effectively, like if you're studying for an exam. Listen to the same playlist or record while studying, then get it stuck in your head for the test. "Hearing" the music in your head will trigger memories of the material that you studied while that music was playing.
