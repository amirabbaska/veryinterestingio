    Skadoosh_it A:

    This is a very common phenomenon in rivers with varied sources. I've seen pictures of the west/east Nile river similar to that, as well as the Columbia/snake river and Missouri/Mississippi rivers. It boils down to a difference in the salinity and silt deposits in the river waters. You'll also see a similar occurrence when river water meets the ocean.

    Dr_Imp A:

    No one has really addressed what is causing the sharp boundary here, so I’ll comment.

    The muddy water on the right is fresh, and has a high sediment (clay) content. The little clay particles all have the same charge, so they repel each other, and the water stays cloudy for weeks or indefinitely.

    The water on the left has much higher salinity, and salts have their own charges.

    At the boundary the waters are mixing quite quickly and some of the salts are attaching to the clay particles. After that the clay particles don’t repel each other, and they drop to the sea floor (creating mud-banks).

    Source: have PhD and degree in related topics (remote sensing, soil science)
