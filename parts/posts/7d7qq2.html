• Physics [physics] Why doesn't Helium freeze at 0K?

    NEW
    Q:

    I know reaching 0k is actually impossible. But from my understanding, all predictions indicate that helium would remain a liquid at 0k

    HumanFtw A:

    The interactions among Helium atoms are too weak, at atmospheric pressure, to condense Helium to a solid even at absolute zero. Note that, at absolute zero, the atoms still possess a positive amount of kinetic energy. (I.e. they do not slow down to zero speed at zero Kelvin.) An increase in pressure is required to freeze Helium.

    edit: For more information see Zero Point Energy.

    somedave A:

    This is actually quite a complicated question which requires a strong knowledge of quantum mechanics to understand.

    Typically the answer of why anything hasn't frozen by a certain temperature, is the entropy drop in the system from becoming solid is not balanced by the entropy increase to the surroundings due to the enthalpy (energy from interactions between the atoms in the new configuration) of the transition.

    With helium it comes down to the energy cost of being localised to a position in quantum mechanics. In liquid helium near 0k the wavefunction of each helium atom are larger than the spacing between the atoms, giving a lower energy state. While being in a solid phase could slightly increase the interactions out would force more localisation and actually be a higher energy state. Eventually with enough pressure this stops being the case and freezing is possible.
