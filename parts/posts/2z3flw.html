• What makes it so your body can stay in a coma for years and just randomly wake up? Why are comas so unpredictable for how long you are asleep for?

    NEW
    Q:

    ELI5: What makes it so your body can stay in a coma for years and just randomly wake up? Why are comas so unpredictable for how long you are asleep for?

    GetOutOfBox A:

    Generally people do not stay in comas for years and just randomly wake up. And the few that do wake up with fairly deteriorated mental capabilities and usually do not make full recoveries.

    People that suddenly wake up from a coma after several years and with a relatively intact mental state are pretty much medical marvels, and it's usually because of very light and precise brain damage. Precise enough that very little could cause complete unconsciousness, but small enough that there is a decent chance that the brain could gradually recover.

    In this case, recovery is facilitated by the brain reforming the crucial broken connection. The parts of the brain that facilitate being the "on-switch"/"power supply" reside in the brain stem (there are other parts that are crucial to conciousness/"you" but damage to them is usually irreversible due to the fact that they developed individually as you grew up and not according to a "genetic map"); these parts serve to keep your brain in the awake state (aka "aroused").

    Damage to them usually causes an immediate coma, even though all the higher parts that store your personality and memories are intact. It's like a fuse being burned out in your house; your electronics are fine, but they won't turn on until it's fixed.

    Often brain damage can be permanent, though in less critical areas the brain is more flexible and apt to work around the broken area, which is often filled with scar tissue. Also, the brain has a lot of redundancy, small children for example are capable of losing half of their brain (a surgical procedure known as a hemispherectomy) with minimal permanent damage owing to how adaptable their brains are. Adults can lose small chunks without being permanently damaged depending on the region.

    Anyways, so in the case of a brainstem injury, often it's less recoverably given that you only have one brainstem (unlike the higher brain's two hemispheres), which has less redundancy. However, the body may be able to gradually break down the scar tissue (or scar tissue never forms for some reason), allowing for the damaged neural connections to reform.

    The brain recovers from damage through two important mechanisms:

    A) A group of chemicals called neurotrophic factors. In the presence of these, neurons are encouraged to grow and multiply. Note that the reason that you don't have tons of these seemingly good chemicals floating around your brain all the time is that small groups of neurons dying or breaking off connections with certain other neurons is a normal part of brain function. It's like pruning a shrub, it keeps things tidy.

    B) A chemical called "netrin" that essentially guides "axons" aka "connections" between neurons. This would help the neurons "know" where to grow towards across a gap, as gradients of this would build up in the empty space.

    Finally, the brain may reach a level of connectedness that signalling can begin to occur again in the area, and so the area begins to recover. A person waking up in this manner would not suddenly sit bolt upright; they would experience an a gradual buildup to consciousness, growing increasingly responsive over a period of weeks or months.

    wookinpanub52 A:

    "Coma" is really just a term that describes a prolonged state of unconsciousness. It does not say anything about why a patient is unconscious. So the ability for recovery from a coma really depends on the cause of the coma. Some of these causes are reversible and others are not. For example if a patient suffered a massive stroke causing permanent brain damage then the likelihood for any kind of a meaningful recovery is unlikely. If the patient has an overwhelming infection the infection itself and the physiologic consequences can also cause an acute delirium and sometimes result in a coma. These patients may very well recover once the infection is treated adequately. To make things more complicated the ability for recovery will also depend on any comorbid conditions. A young healthy 18 year old patient will have a much higher chance of recovery than a 70 year old with multiple medical problems.

    Edit: One more thing. What op mentions in his question about being in a coma for years and randomly waking up does not really happen in real life very rarely happens in real life. Patients that are in a coma due to reversible causes will generally have some form of recovery within the first few weeks. If there is no improvement over the course of months, any form of meaningful recovery is unlikely and they may be deemed to be in "persistent vegetative state" or brain dead.

    Edit2: People are citing lots of cases where patients in a coma wake up and remember things that occurred while they were thought to be unconscious. They are also citing cases of patients waking up after years. I obviously cannot speak to these cases specifically because i do not know the details of them. What i will say is that obviously the human body and in particular the brain are incredibly complex. I do not claim to know everything about it. There will always be cases that do not seem to follow the "rules" but for every miracle patient that wakes up there are thousands that do not. I hope with more research we can understand more about the various states of consciousness.

    lk09nni A:

    ITT: I'm not a doctor, but...

    But on topic: I am, actually a doctor and I cannot answer this question. I do believe, though, that cases of patients being in a coma for several years and then suddenly awaking are extremely sparse, and more likely TV-tropes than actual clinical phenomenae.

    A coma can be artificially induced using anesthetics during recovery from, say, trauma. These patients usually wake up and can recover quite well if there was no traumatic brain injury in the first place.

    On the other hand, people in a prolonged coma (= low state of consciousness) following brain damage caused by trauma, stroke or hypoxia have a dismal chance of recovery. If the do recover, they almost always have severe brain damage and need months to years of rehabilitation to function at all. They usually don't just "wake up" from the coma, either - rather they make small gradual steps toward consciousness.

    That being said, googling around can give you cases of people who have fallen into comas and then woken up. But I can't explain them, I have never witnessed such a case in the hospital where I work and know no colleagues who have worked with such cases, either. But that doesn't mean it never happens, I guess.
