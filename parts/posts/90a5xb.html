• Chemistry How does hail manage to stay solid long enough to hit the ground in 100+ degree weather?

    NEW
    Q:

    It's currently hailing in some areas of Las Vegas, and it's also well over 100 degrees.

    freetattoo A:

    It's below freezing high up in the clouds where the hail is formed. When it gets so heavy that the up drafts within the storm can no longer support its weight it falls.

    At that height it only takes a couple of minutes for the hailstones to fall to the ground. It takes far longer than that for the hailstones to completely melt.

    wazoheat A:

    There's a few reasons that all probably have something to do with it.

    1. Solid blocks of ice don't melt all that fast (relatively speaking). This class did an experiment and found that even under a heat lamp it took more than an hour for an ice cube to fully melt! If a storm is strong enough, hail can start at the size of ice cubes or even larger.

    2. The temperature decreases pretty fast as you go up in the atmosphere. In the desert, it is usually close to 5F per 1000 feet, the so-called dry adiabatic lapse rate. Today in Las Vegas, the freezing level is pretty close to 15000 feet (3 miles, 4.5 km) above the ground.

    3. The fall speed of hail is not all that slow. A hailstone only 1 cm (0.4 inches) across falls at around 9 m/s (20 mph). At this speed it would only take 9 minutes to fall from the freezing level. Large hail will fall even faster.

    4. Hail falling all together will tend to cool the air around it, especially as it melts. It may have started at well over 100 degrees F near the ground, but I am sure the temperature of the air around the hail began cooling off quickly in just a few minutes.
