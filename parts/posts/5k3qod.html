    Q:

    Is there much variation in the surface of a black hole? I'd imagine because of its relatively small size and huge mass black holes would be very close to a perfect sphere.

    RobusEtCeleritas A:

    A black hole with zero angular momentum has perfect spherical symmetry. But any realistic black hole will likely have some angular momentum. In that case, it's not longer spherically symmetric but rather axially symmetric about the axis of rotation.

    iHaveAgency A:

    There is almost no such thing as a non-rotating BH. In fact, there is almost no such thing as a non-rotating ANYTHING in the universe, although the universe itself is considered to not spin.

    Non-rotating BH's are possible, just incredibly unlikely. Any time that two objects in space approach each other, they end up giving opposite angular momentums to each other as they collide, or near-miss, or go into orbit with each other. The reason for this is that it is so rare for two objects to approach each other in a perfect head-on collision. Just like when two cars hit in an off-center head-on collision, both cars spin around a time or two before coming to rest. Same thing happens in space, except no one hears the crash.
