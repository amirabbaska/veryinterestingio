    Q:

    How does Vitamin D absorbed from the sun vs. orally supplemented Vitamin D affect the human body?

    well-that-was-fast A:

    The body's process to synthesize vitamin D from the sun is self regulating, consequently you can't synthesize excessive amounts. Supplements bypass this regulation and humans can get hypervitaminosis D -- too much vitamin D. It's rare, but certainly a difference between the two.

    Late_Again68 A:

    If you do not have working kidneys, there is no difference since the body cannot convert Vitamin D to a usable form; that process takes place in the kidneys. Thus you must take a Vitamin D analog - prescription only - that is the usable form.

    It's not an exact science. Vitamin D allows the absorption of calcium but if your serum calcium is already high you can run into hypercalcemia, though you'll likely get sick before that happens and adjust your dose downward. Any kind of Vitamin D supplementation - whether prescription or OTC, such as D3 - is going to affect three things: your calcium, your phosphorus and your parathyroid hormone (PTH). These are all minerals associated with bone metabolism.

    SchwiftyDoc A:

    Orally supplemented Vitamin D usually comes in a D2 and D3 mixture. D2 (ergocalciferol) usually comes from plants, and D3 (cholecalciferol) is what our body & skin makes and what we get from meat/fish/milk.

    Both are absorbed, activated, and used by the body in the same way. So to answer your question specifically: They both affect the body the same way.

    The difference is just that oral supplementation comes with the risk of overdose. which would result in several symptoms associated increased calcium in the blood.
