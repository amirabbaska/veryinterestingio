• Human Body Do bigger people have more nerves than smaller people? If they have, do they have more brain capacity to complement it?

    NEW
    Q:

    If people would have the same nerve density regardless of the amount of tissue, that would mean more sensory information from a bigger person's body. Would that at some point affect brain functions of really large people?

    And if you get fat, would that mean more nerves and information with the added skin and tissue? Your brain still stays the same.

    satellitedoomcannon A:

    Medical student here.

    Everyone has the same general layout of nerves. All nerves arise either from the spinal cord or from the brainstem. What you have to remember is that each area of the body on the outside is innervated (roughly) by a particular nerve. These are called dermatomes.

    But that's at the macro level. Big nerves that arise from the spinal cord branch off into smaller and smaller branches until they send neuronal processes to every place they are supposed to be. When you get fat, you add more tissue, and the existing nerves branch out to innervate the new tissue.

    This wouldn't effect brain function because it's taking place in the Peripheral Nervous System (nerves outside the brain and spinal cord.) New innervation doesn't change the way the brain works, at least not on the sensory side of things. Motor output (i.e. the brain telling the muscles to do stuff) from the brain is tweaked in the cerebellum and several other places.

    Sensory nerves are just there to keep the brain informed of what's going on in the peripheral tissues. If you have more tissue, your nerves branch to innervate that tissue so there isn't a numb place on your body.

    Hope this helps!

    So TL;DR, no. everyone has (mostly) the same BIG nerves, any new tissue would be innervated by branching of existing nerves.

    PhD candidate, cancer research here

    /r/satellitedoomcannon 's conclusion is not that shared by most scientists

    Short answer - yes.

    Long answer - this is a complicated aspect of our biology that appears to be highly variable from person to person.

    /r/satellitedoomcannon has most of the details correct; however, is wrong about the conclusion.

    This wouldn't effect brain function because it's taking place in the Peripheral Nervous System (nerves outside the brain and spinal cord.) New innervation doesn't change the way the brain works, at least not on the sensory side of things.

    He is correct that the architecture of the central nervous system (CNS) is highly constant between individuals; however, this has little to do with nerve number nor neuron number.

    The constancy of the architecture of our CNS does not force a constancy on the number of nerves/neurons.

    In order to maintain the same "sensory resolution" on the skin (for example), a larger person would need more nerves than a smaller person. In order to effectively process that information, the brain (according to what we know) would require more nuerons to process the input.

    It has not been directly proven, but is strongly believed that more sensory input requires a larger brain.
