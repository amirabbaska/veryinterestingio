• Biology Whether grapes, Cacti or geraniums, cloning plants through cuttings has widely contributed to spreading desirable plants through time. What are the longest such cloned lineages and what can they teach us?

    Q:

    Just to be clear - the present discussion only aims artificially propagated clones. Naturally occurring clones, such as megagroups of genetically similar Aspen, are another topic.

    Does the number of times new cuttings are taken have any effect downline? How long have reliably documented lines been maintained?

    Microthrowaway64 A:

    Cannabis clones can see mutations further down the line. I had one strain of Cannabis I was growing for about 5-6 years, to the point I was about 15+ generations in from the Original Seed.

    Started with 1 seed, took 5 clones and then 5 clones of those clones and so on.

    After around 10 generations I started to see mutations like extra growths, weird looking leaves (extra points or less points than a normal leaf) etc.

    Some of these mutations were better than others and I bred those strains specifically and noticed the mutations got better/worse as the next clones grew.

    Can't speak for other plants etc but Cannabis definitely can have mutations via continued cloning over multiple generations.

    willdoc A:

    Citrus clones are interesting in this regard. Most citrus, especially culinary types, are closely related. Being highly self-fertile, it would be relatively easy to make a land race that looked and tasted the same; as opposed to apples, which for uniformity sake, a cultivar is much more practical.

    However, citrus often take a long time to first fruit when propagated from seed. Often a decade or more. When citrus grafts are used the graft is part of a tree much older than the root stock, and has already matured to first fruit. This means the grafted trees start bearing fruit in one to three years, opposed to one to three decades.

    Another interesting clonal plant that humans use is the German iris. The root, known as Orris root is used in perfumes and artificial fruit flavors like grape and raspberry. Some cultivars of it, that are still grown commercially today in Europe, are thousands of years old and go back to Roman empire and ancient Greek times.

    XxsexplorerxX A:

    Not cuttings but Saffron has been propagated asexually for a very long time.

    Asexual propagation is still subject to genetic mutation.

    PS: Cuttings are not artificial. They can happen in nature, a bird can chew off a piece of stem or a branch, some drops down and continues to grow. Similar with a larger animal brushing past and breaking part of the plant off.
