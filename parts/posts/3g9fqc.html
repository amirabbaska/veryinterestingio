    CompletePlague A:

    Well, everything is legal that isn't prohibited.

    So, you have a business that decides that the original venue/ticket seller has underpriced tickets (that is, consumers will be willing to pay more than face value for the tickets, and even at those prices, they'll all sell.

    So, they do the same thing you'd do: they get in line and buy the tickets.

    They're using technology, which is faster than you typing stuff by hand, so in the time you buy your four tickets, they've got 250 computers buying 12 each. After 5 minutes, the ticket brokers have bought all of the tickets (well, a few humans got some too).

    Then, they sell the tickets.

    There's nothing illegal about buying stuff in bulk. There's nothing illegal about selling stuff for any price you'd like.

    Those are fundamental rights in any society that has any kind of free enterprise.

    Scalping is illegal in some markets if done "near" the venue, which is actually really weird.

    SashaTheBOLD A:

    Economist here.

    The ticket prices at the end of the day are dictated by the standard laws of supply and demand. There is a strictly limited supply of the product -- the venue only has a certain number of seats available -- so demand alone determines the final settling price of the product. If demand is unusually high, you don't get to buy more tickets; you just wind up paying more for the same number of tickets.

    The vendors sell their tickets at below-market price mostly as an incentive to convince the resellers to do exactly what they are doing. That way, the vendor (a) doesn't have to waste money advertising, (b) can concentrate their efforts on lining up acts instead of marketing the tickets, and (c) guarantees that they sell every ticket they want, rapidly and with a highly predictable total revenue for the gig.

    If this process were made illegal it would NOT make the tickets cheaper. It would simply adjust who earned the extra revenue. Less would go to the middleman, but prices would remain the same. If the retailer wanted to go through the hassle of marketing and take on the risk of possibly not selling out their tickets, they could do what Ticketmaster and the others do; they prefer it this way.

    You're actually not getting screwed over; you're just paying your money to a different seller.
