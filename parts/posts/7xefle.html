    About 410-290 million years ago the earth's atmosphere was 35% oxygen. If modern man existed in this era could our lungs breathe in this air?

    DrSomeGuy A:

    Yes, oxygen toxicity is a documented problem for divers. It begins to occur when the partial pressure of oxygen being breathed is greater than 1.6 atmospheres. Because the pressure at sea level is only 1 atmosphere, even a pure oxygen atmosphere would be breathable to humans on Earth.

    killdevil A:

    Patients at the hospital are placed on lower levels of supplemental oxygen by nasal cannula, a pronged device that sits in the nares (nostrils). It's the plastic thing you see on every patient's nose in TV shows.

    The conversion is as follows: 1 liter per minute (lowest whole # value of supplemental O2 by nasal cannula, and the lowest typically used for adult patients) = 24% FiO2 (fraction of inspired oxygen)

    • 2lpm = 27% FiO2
    • 3lpm = 30% FiO2
    • 4lpm = 33% FiO2
    • 5lpm = 35% FiO2
    • 6lpm = 38% FiO2

    So, an atmosphere of 35% oxygen is approximated today by 5 liters per minute of supplemental oxygen delivered by nasal cannula. To put this in perspective, this is a rather significant amount of oxygen support and is what a patient with very severe COPD might walk around with chronically on out in the community, without ill effects. In the ED or ICU setting, it's just a bit below the level where you would definitely escalate to nonrebreather-level O2 support (a face-mask system delivering ~70% FiO2 and up) or to positive pressure ventilation with BiPAP or intubation, which can deliver a 100% oxygen atmosphere to the patient.

    With 100% O2 and even well below that, you can experience oxygen toxicity, but as noted above there are people with chronic lung disease on 5L per NC (35% FiO2) for years without problems.

    Source: am physician
