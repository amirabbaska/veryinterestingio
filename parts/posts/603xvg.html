    Q:

    Is it possible to see the American flag on the moon via telescope?

    CrateDane A:

    No, at least not a telescope on Earth or in Earth orbit. Hubble needs objects to be about the size of a football field to be discerned.

    Even the previously proposed 100 meter aperture OWL telescope would not have been able to see the flag, though it might have been able to resolve the landers and rovers.

    GotenXiao A:

    There is no longer an American flag on the moon. Solar exposure (mainly UV) will have bleached them all white by now. Unfortunately, even the Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter (LRO) doesn't have sufficient Resolution at the orbit it's in to see the flags as anything more than a few pixels, but the shadows are clearly visible [1][2]

    BrazenNormalcy A:

    No. It's too far. When thinking of the solar system, our galaxy, and the universe, our own moon seems really close - and it is, in comparison to those things. But space is really, really big, and even the closest things are vast distances away.

    The biggest earth based telescopes can't resolve even the Apollo landers, much less the flags. Neither can the Hubble. Here's an article from almost a decade ago, explaining why in detail.

    Since then, NASA has redirected lunar orbiters (much closer) for photos, but they still are barely close enough to make out the biggest things at the landing sites.

    The entire landing site is a few meters across (say 10) at ~380,000 km. Jupiter is 70 000 km at 588 million km.

    0.01 / 380 000= 0.0000000263157895

    69 911 / 588 000 000= 0.00011889625

    By comparison, Jupiter is 10000 times larger in your telescope, and it only looks like a distant blur or, if you are lucky, like a tiny blob with some orbiting blobs.

    Imagine trying to see the ENTIRE landing site is 10 000 more demanding. Plus the flag stands up straight, so it is effectively a few centimeters across.
