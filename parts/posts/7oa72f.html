    ssaltmine A:

    Your question is a bit strange. Maybe you could tell us the context where you were reading this, and also if you are asking this for a particular reason or application.

    Electromagnetic waves (radiation) are a strange thing. They can be thought of as waves, but if you study them you realize they carry energy in discrete amounts, which are photons.

    Now, static electric and magnetic fields, as studied in a first Physics course in university are not waves, are not radiation; they are not related to photons. Once these two fields are combined, and you get time varying fields, then you start talking about radiation, waves, and photons.

    RobusEtCeleritas A:

    Not all electric and magnetic field configurations contain radiation. For example static fields donâ€™t contain any radiation. Radiation requires time dependence of the fields. For a pointlike source, the radiation parts of the electric and magnetic fields are proportional to 1/r, where r is the distance from the source to the observation point. When you calculate the momentum flux through a surface at infinity, only terms that fall off as 1/r or slower survive. So these are the only parts of the field which can carry energy to infinity.

    We know from Maxwell and Faraday's work that 1) electric current can induce a magnetic field, and 2) a rotating magnet can induce current. The magnitude of the electromagnetic radiation associated with either process can be measured as the frequency of oscillation of the outward particles. However, I don't understand what you mean by "type" of electromagnetic radiation.

    To answer the second question, you are "transmitting" photons every time you turn on a flashlight, and those photons have momentum that imparts a force onto whatever matter the photons encounter.
