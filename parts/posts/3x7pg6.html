• Physics Photons have no mass but are affected by gravity. Do photons themselves affect gravity, e.g. could one make a black hole solely from photons?

    NEW
    Q:

    Experiments have shown that photons are affected by gravity. E.g. if you look at the edge of the moon the light of the stars behind is bent slightly by its gravity. Or in black holes light is bent so hard that it cannot leave the event horizon.

    Now that poses the question if light itself affects gravity. I mean it has energy and it interacts with gravity. But its particles are massless.

    If I were to convert all mass of a black hole into energy and the energy into photons, what would happen in regards to gravity? Would it stay the same or would it simply disappear letting the photons out of their prison?

    crunk_cat A:

    Photons or light are affected by gravity because they are only following the curvature in space-time caused by a large mass and NOT because they are feeling individual gravitational forces on each particle. This was the main difference between Newtonian and Einstein's physics of gravity.

    Photons cannot have rest mass or else quantum mechanics would fall apart through collapse of gauge invariance. The reason photons can have energy per particle without mass is because they obtain relativistic mass which is not mass in the way you are thinking about it. Their E is actually =pc , their momentum x speed of light.

    But you can create a blackhole from photons, by focusing enough energy in one area to warp space time, and in fact this has been hypothesized to have happened in the early universe. You can still create gravity without mass essentially.

    Hope this is understandable.

    PoissonTriumvirate A:

    Gravity actually binds to mass-energy, not just mass. So photons with energy E interact with gravity in the same way as a resting object with mass E/c^2.

    If you get a high enough energy density, it has the same effect as a high enough mass density, and you can create a black hole from photons.

    doctorcoolpop A:

    Light has energy and so creates gravity of its own. Theoretically you can have a ball of light bound into a lump solely by its own gravitation. This is called a Geon and was analyzed by J. A. Wheeler in 1955.

    There seems to be a lot of confusion on Reddit about photons having no mass. They have no REST mass, but they have energy and momentum and interact gravitationally just like other objects.

    DrunkFishBreatheAir A:

    Not only is the answer yes, but carefully focused gravitational waves (not that we're capable of making even close to big enough ones) can also create a black hole. Gravitational waves are ripples in the shape of spacetime that propogate out from moving objects, with the biggest ones (which LIGO Will hopefully detect) coming from pairs of black holes/neutron stars spiraling in toward eachother. So basically waves of gravity could themselves have enough gravity to make a black hole.
