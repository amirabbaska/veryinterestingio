    Q:

    Is a single atom able to cast a shadow?

    seamstress80 A:

    Yes. Sort of. But you can't see it.

    If I shoot one photon to an atom and the photon is absorbed, then, technically speaking, a shadow is cast. Even if the photon is deflected, a shadow is cast. However, a shadow is defined together with the light surrounding it. Our eyes need a few photons to reach our retina in order to see. It's a small number, we can see less than 10 photons already, I believe, but we can't see the shadow cast by one missing photon. And I don't think of any process that can cause a single atom to absorb a large number of photons all at once.
