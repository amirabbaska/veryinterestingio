• Culture Why did some civilisations develop left-to-right writing, whereas some developed right-to-left writing?

    NEW
    Q:

    You often hear left-handed people (in left-to-right countries) complain about the fact that their palms smudge pencil lead on paper, leading to an awkward, hand-unsupported way of writing. Some even complain of hand and wrist pain. I imagine this would have been worse when very wet and smudgeable inks were the main medium of writing.

    Why then, (assuming most civilisations are right-handed) are there some civilisations which write right to left? Wouldn't the development of writing that way be seen as disadvantageous because of the amount of inconvenience?

    I'm less curious about writing horizontally vs vertically because I don't see any drawbacks to either that would lead to one being particularly dominant, so the existence of both isn't very notable to me. I do notice that right-to-left writing does seem to prefer writing vertically down though, so maybe it's important after all.

    Edit: Is the difference between right-to-left and left-to-right whether they started with ink or chisels? That would explain it well I think. Ink-based civilisations would write left-to-right since they're holding the "pen" in their right hand, whereas chisel-based civilisations would write right-to-left because they're holding the chisel in their left hand and the hammer in their right, hammering towards the left (that's how I use chisels and hammers anyway). Chisel writing isn't particularly quick, but around the time of the advent of writing itself I don't think speed was much of a concern.

    Theutates A:

    I can’t speak for other languages, but for Chinese, the tradition is to write in on bamboo slates, vertically from top to bottom. When a slate is filled, it is removed so ink smudges was never a problem. It’s possible that they use their right hand to do both writing and slate-moving, so right-to-left makes it easier.

    AlpineGuy A:

    A lot of countries for a long time considered being right-handed to be "normal" and left-handed to be "wrong". It would make sense if those were the ones writing L-R (which is easier using the right hand).

    However, I believe also arab countries had (maybe even still have) strong feelings against being left-handed and still write R-L.
