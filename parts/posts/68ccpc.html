• Physics What colour would a neutron star be?

    NEW
    Q:

    A neutron star is made up nearly entirely of neutrons, sometimes called Neutronium, so there are basically no electrons at all in the outside of it. Because this is the case, how would light react with it? Would the neutron star be like a mirror of sorts? Completely white?

    Flandardly A:

    Neutron stars are incredibly hot, far hotter than our sun. They appear a blue/violet-white because of the blackbody radiation they emit as a result of their temperature. This is the same reason incandescent lights and our sun emit light. This site is great for showing how the color of light an object emits changes with its temperature.

    blind_cathar A:

    there are basically no electrons at all in the outside of it.

    Actually, this is not quite correct. The exterior layers of neutron stars are composed of (neutron-rich) nuclei and free electrons. This outer layer is called the crust. Only deeper in the interior of neutron stars do the higher pressures make free neutrons stable.

    Since the surface of a neutron star will always be composed of "regular" nuclei and electrons, you never get a direct view of the sea of free neutrons in the interior.
