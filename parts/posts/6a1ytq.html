    Q:

    How do we get Vitamin D from the sun? Is it like photosynthesis in any way?

    seasaltandpepper A:

    Photosynthesis is a process where photon is captured and its energy transported across a chain of molecules for the purpose of storing the said energy. This process is well-controlled at various steps, and organisms have evolved over the years to use the best spectrum of light available.

    In humans the majority of Vitamin D synthesis requires sunlight at one stage, but the UV light just provides the activation energy to convert one isomeric form of provitamin D to another (i.e. there is no active storage of energy). This is a spontaneous process not requiring cellular machinery, and the effect is limited to the substrate and product.

    Tempatroy A:

    In any way, then yes, energy from sunlight allows conformation changes in molecules.

    But the mechanism of action is different.

    With Vitamin D3 synthesis from vitamin D, the UV light turns the precursor molecule, 7-dehydrocholesterol, into another molecule which will turn into Vitamin D3 in a few hours.

    https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK56061/

    Photosynthesis is a more complex reaction carried out in parts of the Chloroplast. It uses electron transfers to create 'sugar' to feed itself, different in many ways.

    https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthesis

    Other similarities might be that (of course) the molecules each have certain wavelengths of light that are most likely to cause this conformational change, "-Dehydrocholesterol absorbs UV light most effectively at wavelengths between 290 and 320 nm "
