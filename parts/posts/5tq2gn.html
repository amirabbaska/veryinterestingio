• Biology Your immune system causes symptoms like fever, swelling, etc, when fighting an infection. I usually get more mild symptoms than my wife when we both get sick. Does this mean my immune system is weaker or stronger?

    NEW
    Q:

    If your immune system is what causes those symptoms, does That mean my immune system can't fight as hard, or that it doesn't have to fight as hard to win.

    Zoten A:

    That's an awesome question, but unfortunately it doesn't have a clear answer, as there are so many variables that can change the response.

    There are 2 components to your immune system: the innate and adaptive. The innate just travels around and freaks out at everything it doesn't recognize. It doesn't matter if it's the common cold or anthrax. (slight exaggeration, since it does matter if it's bacterial or viral). It's going to react the same way. These are responsible for fever, phlegm, swelling, etc. These happen within hours of being infected. The end goal here isn't to kill the infection. It's to fight until your adaptive cells can bring reinforcements.

    You also have an adaptive immune system. These are (primarily) your T and B cells, which take time. They have to 'study' the infection, find a good way to kill it, and then mass produce the antibodies. This can take anywhere from hours (if you've already made antibodies) or days-weeks (if it's something brand new).

    Let's say you get an infection that you've seen before. Your innate system attacks right away, and you start to notice those general signs of being sick. But then, your pre-formed antibodies quickly kill it.

    Or maybe it's a brand new infection, but for some reason, they don't trigger your innate system as strongly as hers. Therefore, you only get mild symptoms until your adaptive* system kills it. It's also possible that your innate response being smaller actually lets the infection grow, causing you to be sick for a longer period of time

    At the end of the day, you can't really say your system is stronger or weaker. The real question is whether it's good enough, and it sounds like both of yours works.

    Doktor_Wunderbar A:

    Great question, but it's complicated, because different infections trigger the immune system to different amounts. Sometimes, what kills a person isn't the infection itself, but the immune response to that infection. In those cases, it's better to have a slightly weaker immune response; the response will still be strong enough to fight the infection, but not so strong as to cause you harm.

    Another thing that will make a difference is how severe the infection gets. A minor infection will trigger a lesser response, but if those bacteria or viruses grow out of control, this will trigger a big response, with worse symptoms. You could be experiencing milder symptoms because you're controlling the infection at an earlier stage...or because your body is taking a more measured, less aggressive response to controlling the infection.

    It's hard for me to say without samples.

    Workacct1484 A:

    So different system respond to different bacteria and trigger different symptoms.

    I'll touch on one, fever.

    Bacteria and viruses are surprisingly temperature sensitive. They have no way good to regulate the temperature inside their cells. So in this case they are dependent on the temperature of the host.

    let's take everyone's least favorite dumpster-diver, the Opossum.

    Opossums are largely not susceptible to the rabies virus, as many people think.

    DISCLAIMER: They can still carry it, as well as other diseases, seek medical attention if bitten / scratched.

    This is because their body temperature is low when compared to other mammals, generally around 90-93 F. The rabies virus cannot sustain itself in this lower temperature, as compared to everyone's favorite dumpster diver, whose body temp averages closer to 100 F.

    Now what does this have to do with fever? Well evolution. If a bacteria, or virus thrives at 98.6 F, but cannot survive temperatures of 100-101F well it would do your body good to "sweat it out".

    So somewhere way way way back one of your ancestors immune responses caused them to heat up. This killed a bacterial or viral infection, and kept them alive to procreate, and thus the trait was passed along, and spread.

    So now fever is a common symptom. Though it is not so much the infection, as it is the body trying to cook out the infection.
