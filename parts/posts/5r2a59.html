    F117Landers A:

    I'm going to address the TV portion as it can be considered separate from movie production and distribution.
     
    To start, let us look at where TV shows come from. A production studio creates the material for a show. As tv production is expensive (2006 estimates are $120 million average for a season), the studio is often funded (and normally owned) by a conglomerate company, such as Comcast-NBCUniversal. Prior to production, the project is greenlighted (ordered) on the premise that it will be exclusive to a certain company (such as the NBC example above).
     
    Airing of the show is negotiated for a time slot on a certain provider. For companies that own the channel (such as NBC), the slots are determined internally based on projected potential viewers and likelihood to draw from competitors' viewers. The channel owner will also sell advertizing slots to offset the cost of production and operation, and negotiate cost-per-subscriber for cable/satellite companies to stream content.
     
    The cable company needs to negotiate with each company that they want to provide on their services. While some of these channels are profitable on their own (based on cost:subscriber), several are not (ESPN, Lifetime, SyFy). Bundling prevents viewers from paying $15 just for ESPN and allows smaller channels to thrive. Cable companies also sell some of the advertizing slots to recuperate broadcasting costs.
     
    Now comes in Netflix. Netflix operates on the same principle as Video-on-Demand (VOD), but uses the internet instead of your media box. In order to display shows through its service, Netflix has to secure rights, the same way that cable companies do.
     
    As most rights are held by TV channels, they are reluctant to lease viewing rights within a certain marketability period, which is why shows may not appear for a certain time following their DVD/BD season release. Some studios refuse to lease content to Netflix; Disney-ABC in particular is notorious for this. They also own (edit: 30% of) Hulu, to compete with Netflix and piracy.
