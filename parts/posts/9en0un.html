    Marzihammer A:

    Opiate overdoses have already been mentioned as causing respiratory depression. I’m an ER nurse, they way I explain it to people is your body doesn’t know it needs to breathe.

    When you hold your breath, you experience the burning lung sensation and eventually, your body will force you to breathe again. (Little kids who say they’re going to hold their breath till they die? Doesn’t work.)

    Opiates block that feedback from happening. Your body, overdosed on opiates, does not recognize that it needs to breathe. So it doesn’t.

    A normal (adult) respiratory rate is 12-20 breaths per minute. I’ve seen people come in at 3-4. Even if we give Narcan (the opiate reversal drug) depending on how long they’ve been at 4 breaths per minute, we might not get them back. Brain damage from lack of oxygen starts at around 3 minutes. Sometimes people have been down for hours before they’re found.

    Cocaine can speed up your heart rate so much that the heart can’t keep up, and will stop. When your heart is between beats, that’s when blood flows to the actual heart itself. The faster your heart rate, the less blood/oxygen is getting to the heart, and eventually the heart can’t go on. (I’ve only seen this happen once, and it was intentional.)

    People ask “What’s your poison” regarding alcohol for a reason, it really is. How you die from alcohol depends on how much you had, and if you mixed with something else, usually. Choking on their own vomit is common. Being exceptionally drunk can suppress the gag reflex. All jokes aside, the gag reflex is there to force you to protect your own airway. If someone comes in drunk, unresponsive and doesn’t have a gag reflex, they almost always get intubated. Long term, your body will process alcohol out but there’s a lot of not fun things that can happen, like liver failure. Another one is alcohol related dementia. Slang term is “wet brain”, but those patients are almost always awful. Nothing will test your nursing compassion like a mean ass drunk. Also with alcohol, it might not be an overdose but people do really, really stupid stuff while drunk. Like jumping off roofs and aiming for the tree next to it, and missing. Or less exciting, driving and wrapping their car around a light pole.

    Recreational drug use aside, there’s plenty of prescription (and over the counter) medications that can kill you/make you sick. Too much Tylenol can put you in liver failure, too much Advil can cause a stomach/intestinal bleed. Blood pressure medications will drop your blood pressure/heart rate to dangerous levels, blood thinners can cause spontaneous bleeding (and lengthen the amount of time it takes for your blood to clot.)

    Wow this got really long. My point is- some things we can save you from. Some things we can’t. Read directions, drink responsibly, if it’s illegal maybe don’t do it. (Except weed, that would get it’s own post, and it’s next to impossible to smoke enough pot to kill yourself.)

    TehWildMan_ A:

    Depends on the drug abused.

    Opiate painkillers and benzodiazepines, especially when combined with each other and/or alcohol, can induce respiratory depression: aka you stop (or significantly slow down) breathing. Obviously if you aren't breathing you won't last very long without medical help.

    Many other drugs may have extremely unpleasant side effects in excess (such as psychosis when abusing stimulants), but they might not affect critical body processes enough to be immediately fatal.

    Another common example is acetaminophen (paracetamol). The metabolism of acetaminophen creates a toxic intermediate that is usually eliminated quickly. However, excess consumption of acetaminophen, and/or concurrent alcohol consumption, can overwhelm that pathway (depleting Glutathione, which both the metabolism of alcohol and acetaminophen require) , causing a build-up of the toxic intermediate which can cause liver damage/failure. Not immediately fatal, but a painful way to die.

    Cocaine and Tricyclic antidepressants are also known for potentially fatal overdoses, as both alter nerve signaling within the body (both inside and outside the brain), which may cause elecated body temperature, heart rate/blood pressure, and the like which may become fatal.

    smmstv A:

    Depends on the drug.

    With opiates, they suppress your breathing, and essentially you end up suffocating.

    With benzos/ alcohol, they slow down your heart rate until it stops.

    This is why mixing opiates, alcohol, and benzos is particularly dangerous, because it slows your body down in different ways.

    Cocaine, meth, and other stimulants speed up your heart to the point you end up having a heart attack and seizures.

    Whether or not they're fatal depends on how much was taken (just enough to render someone unconscious vs enough to take down an elephant) and how quickly after the overdose they can get medical attention.
