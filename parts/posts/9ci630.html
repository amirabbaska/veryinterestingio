    internetboyfriend666 A:

    Any spacecraft launched from Earth is moving along with the Earth in it's direction of motion at considerable speed, 67,000 mph (107,000 kph). In order to reach the outer solar system, a spacecraft only needs to add a little bit to this speed, even less if you want to use gravity assists from the outer planets. To get close to the sun however, a spacecraft has to cancel out almost all of the speed it has from moving with the Earth, meaning it has to cancel out almost all of that 67,000mph velocity around the sun so the sun's gravity can pull it in.

    Loki-L A:

    In space everything comes down to changes in velocity.

    On earth we might be making distinctions between speeding up and breaking to slow down, but in space there isn't really an difference between those concepts other than direction.

    The second thing is obits.

    You can't just point your spaceship in some direction and accelerate in that direction and expect to actually go there. Orbital mechanics are weird that way and usually end up going in a completely different direction that the one you are aiming at.

    To simplify it: to get outward you have to speed up and to get inward you have to slow down.

    This is true for spaceships wanting to go from earth to earth orbit and back but also for spaceships wanting to go from earth's position in the solar system either outward or inward.

    With earth we have the atmosphere that helps slow things down when coming home (at the cost of heating things up), with the sun we don't have anything like that.

    If we want to go to the sun without any tricks we have to get rid of all the velocity we have thanks to the earth orbiting the sun once a year. (it is a lot)

    If we want to go to Pluto we have to add speed instead.

    Since we aren't stupid we do some tricks that help us such as using the gravity of other planets to help us along (slingshot maneuver).

    With the sun we have the problem of lots of velocity to get rid of and not really much to help us a long the way. (The big heavy planets are all out in the other direction).
